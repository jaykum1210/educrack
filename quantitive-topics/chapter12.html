Chapter 12 - Banking & Financial Mathematics

1. Simple & Compound Interest Advanced Problems

i. Simple Interest (SI)

Formula for Simple Interest:

SI = P × R × T / 100

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years

The total amount A after T years is given by:

A = P + SI = P(1 + R × T / 100)

ii. Compound Interest (CI)

Formula for Compound Interest:

A = P(1 + R / 100) ^ T

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years
  • A = Total amount after T years

To find the compound interest, we subtract the principal P from the amount A:

CI = A - P = P(1 + R / 100) ^ T - P

For compound interest compounded more frequently (quarterly, monthly), the formula is adjusted to account for the frequency of compounding.

iii. Advanced Problems: Simple Interest

Problem 1: Find the Time Period for Simple Interest

Problem: A sum of ₹1,500 is invested at 8% per annum simple interest. After 3 years, the interest amount is ₹360. Find the principal.

Solution: Using the formula for Simple Interest:

SI = P × R × T / 100

We know:

  • SI = ₹360
  • R = 8%
  • T = 3 years

Substituting the values:

360 = P × 8 × 3 / 100

360 = 24P / 100

P = (360 × 100) / 24 = ₹1,500

Answer: The principal is ₹1,500.

Problem 2: Find the Interest Earned and Total Amount

Problem: A sum of ₹12,000 is invested for 2 years at a rate of 10% per annum simple interest. Find the total amount at the end of 2 years.

Solution: Using the formula for Simple Interest:

SI = P × R × T / 100

Substitute the values:

  • P = ₹12,000
  • R = 10%
  • T = 2 years

SI = 12,000 × 10 × 2 / 100 = ₹2,400

The total amount A will be:

A = P + SI = ₹12,000 + ₹2,400 = ₹14,400

Answer: The total amount after 2 years is ₹14,400.

iv. Advanced Problems: Compound Interest

Problem 1: Compound Interest with Quarterly Compounding

Problem: A sum of ₹10,000 is invested at 8% per annum interest, compounded quarterly for 2 years. Find the compound interest and the total amount.

Solution: The formula for Compound Interest when interest is compounded quarterly is:

A = P(1 + R / (4 × 100)) ^ (4 × T)

Where:

  • P = ₹10,000
  • R = 8%
  • T = 2 years

Substitute the values:

A = 10,000 × (1 + 8 / (4 × 100)) ^ (4 × 2)

A = 10,000 × (1 + 2 / 100) ^ 8 = 10,000 × (1.02) ^ 8

A ≈ 10,000 × 1.3728 = ₹13,728

Now, to find the Compound Interest:

CI = A - P = ₹13,728 - ₹10,000 = ₹3,728

Answer: The compound interest is ₹3,728, and the total amount is ₹13,728.

2. Inflation & Deflation Calculations

Inflation Calculation

Inflation is generally measured as the percentage change in the Consumer Price Index (CPI) over a specific period of time (usually annually).

The formula to calculate inflation is:

Inflation Rate = (CPI at End of Year - CPI at Beginning of Year) / CPI at Beginning of Year × 100

Where:

  • CPI (Consumer Price Index) is a measure that examines the weighted average of prices of a basket of consumer goods and services.

Example 1: Inflation Calculation

Problem: The Consumer Price Index (CPI) in January was 120 and in December it was 130. What is the inflation rate for the year?

Solution: Using the formula for inflation:

Inflation Rate = (130 - 120) / 120 × 100

Inflation Rate = 10 / 120 × 100 = 8.33%

Answer: The inflation rate is 8.33%.

3. Stocks, Shares & Debentures

Shares and Stocks

Shares and stocks are similar concepts, but they have subtle differences.

  • Share: A share represents a unit of ownership in a company. When you buy a share, you own a portion of the company and are entitled to a share of its profits (dividends) and voting rights at general meetings.
  • Stock: Stock refers to the collective ownership in a company, which consists of multiple shares. It’s often used to refer to ownership in a company without specifying the number of shares.

Types of Shares

  • Equity Shares (Common Shares):
    • Represent ownership in a company.
    • Carry voting rights and entitle the shareholder to dividends.
    • Dividends depend on the company’s profit.
    • Risk is higher, as equity shareholders are paid after debenture holders and preference shareholders in case of liquidation.
  • Preference Shares:
    • Provide a fixed dividend before equity shareholders receive any.
    • Generally, preference shareholders do not have voting rights.
    • In case of liquidation, preference shareholders are paid before equity shareholders.

Key Features of Shares:

  • Face Value: The nominal or original value of a share, typically set by the company.
  • Market Value: The price at which shares are traded in the stock market.
  • Dividend: A share of the company's profits paid to shareholders.
  • Capital Gain: Profit made by selling shares at a higher price than the purchase price.

Debentures

A debenture is a debt instrument issued by companies to raise funds. It represents a loan taken by the company from debenture holders. Unlike shares, debentures do not offer ownership but instead offer fixed interest payments to the debenture holders.

Key Features of Debentures:

  • Debt Instrument: Debentures are a form of borrowing for the company, and they are not ownership in the company.
  • Fixed Interest: Debenture holders receive fixed interest, also known as coupon rates, regularly.

Secured and Unsecured Debentures:

  • Secured Debentures: Backed by the company’s assets as collateral.
  • Unsecured Debentures: Not backed by any specific assets, and hence, considered riskier.

Types of Debentures:

  • Convertible Debentures: Can be converted into a specified number of equity shares after a certain period.
  • Non-Convertible Debentures: Cannot be converted into shares but offer higher interest rates due to the lack of convertibility.
  • Redeemable Debentures: Can be redeemed after a certain period.
  • Irredeemable Debentures: Cannot be redeemed but continue to pay interest.

Comparison: Shares vs. Debentures

Aspect Shares Debentures
Nature Ownership in the company Loan to the company
Returns Dividends, which depend on profit Fixed interest (coupon rate)
Risk Higher risk, as returns depend on company performance Lower risk compared to shares (especially if secured)
Voting Rights Yes, shareholders can vote in company matters No voting rights
Priority in Liquidation Last to be paid (after debt holders) First to be paid (before equity holders)
Convertibility Cannot be converted into other instruments Convertible into equity shares (in the case of convertible debentures)
Repayment No fixed repayment, as it’s ownership-based Must be repaid at maturity or as per terms

Financial Terms Related to Shares and Debentures

Stock Market Terms:

  • Face Value: The nominal value of a share or debenture, set by the issuing company.
  • Market Value: The price at which shares/debentures are bought or sold in the market.
  • Dividend Yield: The annual dividend paid by a company divided by the market price of the share.
  • Capital Gain: Profit from selling shares at a higher price than their purchase price.
  • Coupon Rate (for Debentures): The interest rate paid by the issuer on the debenture’s face value.

Debenture Terms:

  • Redemption Period: The time after which the debenture principal must be repaid.
  • Interest/ Coupon Payment: The fixed interest paid by the company to debenture holders.

Example Problems

Problem 1: Calculation of Dividend for Equity Shares

Problem: A company has issued 1000 equity shares of face value ₹100 each. The company declares a 10% dividend. What is the total dividend earned by a shareholder with 50 shares?

Solution:

  • Dividend per share = 10% of ₹100 = ₹10
  • Total Dividend = 50 shares × ₹10 = ₹500

Answer: The shareholder will earn ₹500 as dividend.
