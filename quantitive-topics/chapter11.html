Chapter 11 - Ratio, Proportion & Averages

1. Basic Ratio & Proportion

Definition

  • Ratio and proportion are fundamental concepts in mathematics used to compare quantities and solve problems involving comparisons and distributions.

Ratio

  • A ratio is a way of comparing two or more quantities, showing the relative size of two values.
  • Formula: The ratio of two quantities a and b is written as:

    • Ratio = a/b or a : b

  • Example 1: If there are 3 apples and 2 oranges, the ratio of apples to oranges is:

    • Ratio = 3/2 or 3 : 2

  • Example 2: The ratio of boys to girls in a class is 4:5, meaning for every 4 boys, there are 5 girls in the class.

Types of Ratios

  1. Simple Ratio

    • When comparing two quantities. Example: The ratio of 5 to 2 is written as 5:2.

  2. Compound Ratio

    • A ratio that involves more than two quantities.
    • For example, the compound ratio of 2:3, 4:5, and 6:7 is:

      • Compound Ratio = (2/3) × (4/5) × (6/7) = 48/105 or 48 : 105

  3. Continued Ratio

    • A ratio involving three or more terms in a continued manner.
    • Example: If A:B = B:C, then A:B:C is the continued ratio.

Proportion

  • A proportion is an equation that expresses the equality of two ratios. It is typically written as:

    • a/b = c/d or a : b = c : d

  • This means the ratio of a to b is equal to the ratio of c to d.
  • Example: If 2/5 = 4/10, then the ratios are in proportion.

Properties of Proportions

  • Cross-multiplication: If a/b = c/d, then a × d = b × c.
  • Example: If 3/5 = x/9, we can solve for x using cross-multiplication:

    • 3 × x = 5 × 9 ⇒ 3x = 45 ⇒ x = 15

Solving Problems Involving Ratios and Proportions

  1. Example 1: A recipe requires 4 cups of flour and 3 cups of sugar. If you want to make a smaller batch using 2 cups of flour, how much sugar do you need?

    • The ratio of flour to sugar is 4:3. You need to find how much sugar is required for 2 cups of flour.

      • Set up the proportion: (4/3) = (2/x)

    • Cross-multiply: 4x = 6 ⇒ x = 1.5

      • Answer: You need 1.5 cups of sugar.

  2. Example 2: In a class, the ratio of boys to girls is 5:7. If there are 35 girls in the class, how many boys are there?

    • The ratio of boys to girls is 5:7. Let the number of boys be x.

      • Set up the proportion: (5/7) = (x/35)

    • Cross-multiply: 5 × 35 = 7 × x ⇒ 175 = 7x ⇒ x = 25

      • Answer: There are 25 boys in the class.

  3. Example 3: The total salary of A and B is $20,000. If the ratio of their salaries is 3:5, what is the salary of B?

    • Let the salary of A be 3x and the salary of B be 5x. The total salary is $20,000:

      • 3x + 5x = 20,000

    • 8x = 20,000 ⇒ x = 2,500

      • The salary of B is 5x = 5 × 2,500 = 12,500.

      Answer: The salary of B is $12,500.

Important Tips for Solving Ratio and Proportion Problems

  • Understand the ratio properly: Ensure that the quantities are being compared correctly.
  • Set up the proportion: Use the relation between the quantities to set up the proportion.
  • Cross-multiply: Helps in solving for the unknown quantity in the proportion.
  • Check your units: Ensure that quantities being compared are in the same unit before forming ratios.

2. Mixtures & Alligations

Definition

  • Mixtures and Alligations are mathematical techniques used to solve problems involving the mixing of two or more substances or quantities, often with different concentrations, values, or qualities.
  • These concepts are frequently used in competitive exams like JEE, NEET, and others.

Mixtures

  • A mixture is a combination of two or more substances in such a way that each substance retains its properties.

    • Types of Mixtures:

    • Homogeneous Mixture: A mixture where the components are uniformly distributed, and you cannot differentiate between them (e.g., salt dissolved in water).
    • Heterogeneous Mixture: A mixture where the components are not uniformly distributed, and you can see the individual substances (e.g., sand and salt mixture).

Alligation (Alligation Rule)

  • Alligation is used to solve problems where two or more substances (like solutions, liquids, or prices) are mixed to form a new mixture. It helps find the ratio in which the substances must be mixed or the mean value of the mixture.

    • Types of Alligation:

    • Alligation Alternate (AA): Used when you know the two concentrations or prices of two substances and want to find the ratio they should be mixed to achieve a desired concentration or price.

Steps for Alligation Alternate:

  1. Step 1: Write the concentration or price of the two components (substances) you are mixing.
  2. Step 2: Find the desired or mean concentration/price of the mixture.
  3. Step 3: Subtract the mean value from the concentration/price of each of the substances.
  4. Step 4: The differences you obtain are the parts in which the two substances must be mixed.

Formula for Alligation Alternate:

If two components with concentrations C₁ and C₂ are mixed to get a mixture with concentration Cₘ, the ratio of mixing is:

Ratio = (C₂ - Cₘ) : (Cₘ - C₁)

Alligation Mean (AM)

  • Alligation Mean is used when the mean (average) value of a mixture is required, based on the concentrations and the quantities of each substance.

Formula for Alligation Mean:

Mean Value = (C₁ × W₁) + (C₂ × W₂) + ... + (Cₙ × Wₙ) / (W₁ + W₂ + ... + Wₙ)

Where:

  • C₁, C₂, ..., Cₙ are the concentrations or prices of the substances.
  • W₁, W₂, ..., Wₙ are the quantities or weights of the substances.

Examples of Mixtures & Alligations Problems

  1. Example 1: Alligation Alternate

    Problem: A chemist mixes two solutions of acid. One contains 30% acid, and the other contains 70% acid. How much of each solution should be mixed to obtain a 50% acid solution?

    Solution:

    1. Step 1: Write the concentrations:
      • First solution = 30% acid
      • Second solution = 70% acid
      • Desired solution = 50% acid
    2. Step 2: Subtract the mean concentration from each of the components:

      3. 70 - 50 = 20 and 50 - 30 = 20

    3. The ratio in which the solutions should be mixed is 20:20, or 1:1.

    Answer: Mix the two solutions in a 1:1 ratio.

3. Weighted Averages & Mean

Mean (Arithmetic Mean)

  • The mean (or average) of a set of numbers is calculated by summing all the numbers and then dividing the sum by the total number of values in the set.

Formula for Arithmetic Mean:

Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of values)

For example, if the numbers are 3, 5, 7, and 9:

Mean = (3 + 5 + 7 + 9) / 4 = 24 / 4 = 6

Weighted Average

  • A weighted average is used when some values in a dataset are more important or have more significance than others. Instead of each value contributing equally to the average, each value is multiplied by a weight that reflects its importance.

Formula for Weighted Average:

Weighted Average = (x₁ × w₁) + (x₂ × w₂) + ... + (xₙ × wₙ) / (w₁ + w₂ + ... + wₙ)

Where:

  • x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ are the values.
  • w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ are the corresponding weights.

The sum of the weights is the total weight, and the formula computes the average considering these weights.

Examples of Weighted Averages

  1. Example 1: Simple Weighted Average

    Problem: A student has the following grades for different subjects:

    • Math: 80 (weight 2)
    • Science: 90 (weight 3)
    • English: 85 (weight 1)

    Find the weighted average of the grades.

    Solution: Using the formula for weighted average:

    Weighted Average = (80 × 2 + 90 × 3 + 85 × 1) / (2 + 3 + 1)

    Weighted Average = (160 + 270 + 85) / 6 = 515 / 6 ≈ 85.83

    Answer: The weighted average grade is approximately 85.83.

  2. Example 2: Weighted Average in Economics

    Problem: A company produces two types of products, A and B. The sales of product A are 300 units at ₹20 each, and the sales of product B are 500 units at ₹15 each. Find the weighted average price of the products sold.

    Solution: Using the weighted average formula:

    Price of A = ₹20, Quantity of A = 300

    Price of B = ₹15, Quantity of B = 500

    Weighted Average Price = (20 × 300 + 15 × 500) / (300 + 500)

    Weighted Average Price = (6000 + 7500) / 800 = 13500 / 800 = ₹16.875

    Answer: The weighted average price of the products sold is ₹16.875.

Key Differences Between Mean and Weighted Average

  • Mean (Arithmetic Mean): Every value contributes equally to the average.
  • Weighted Average: Each value has a different level of importance or weight, and these weights affect the final average.

Applications of Weighted Averages

  • Grades and Scores: When calculating the average grade for a student, where each subject or exam has a different weight.
  • Financial Analysis: Weighted averages are used to calculate the average cost of capital, average portfolio returns, and stock price indices.
  • Economics: Weighted averages help calculate the average price in an economy or for specific goods with varying importance.

4. Partnership & Investments

Partnership

In a partnership, two or more individuals or entities come together to run a business, share profits, and bear the losses according to a mutually agreed ratio. The agreement usually specifies how profits, losses, and capital will be divided among the partners.

Key Concepts in Partnership

  • Capital Contribution: This refers to the amount of money or assets each partner invests in the business. The partnership ratio (profit-sharing ratio) is often based on the amount of capital invested.
  • Profit Sharing Ratio: The ratio in which the partners will share the profits (or losses) of the business. This can be based on their capital contribution or some other agreement.
  • Time Factor: If the partners invest their capital for different periods, the time factor is considered while calculating the profit-sharing ratio. The effective contribution of each partner is their capital multiplied by the time for which it is invested.

Formula for Partnership

If the capital contributions of two partners P₁ and P₂ for different time periods T₁ and T₂ are known, then the share of profits can be calculated using the following formula:

Profit of Partner 1 = (P₁ × T₁) / (P₁ × T₁ + P₂ × T₂) × Total Profit

Profit of Partner 2 = (P₂ × T₂) / (P₁ × T₁ + P₂ × T₂) × Total Profit

Where:

  • P₁ and P₂ are the amounts of capital invested by Partner 1 and Partner 2, respectively.
  • T₁ and T₂ are the time periods for which the capital is invested.
  • Total Profit is the total amount of profit or loss made by the business.

Types of Partnership

  • Fixed Capital Partnership: In this type of partnership, the capital contribution of the partners remains constant throughout the business period.
  • Fluctuating Capital Partnership: Here, the capital contribution of the partners may change during the course of the business, i.e., the partners can add or withdraw capital as per their needs.

Investments

An investment involves putting money or capital into a business or asset with the expectation of receiving a return or profit over time. In partnerships, the initial investment and any subsequent additions to it determine the profit-sharing ratio among the partners.

Key Terms in Investments

  • Principal Amount (P): The initial investment made by a partner.
  • Rate of Interest (R): The percentage of return on the invested amount, usually on an annual basis.
  • Time Period (T): The duration for which the investment is made.

Simple Interest (SI)

The interest earned on the principal over a period of time, calculated using the formula:

SI = (P × R × T) / 100

Where:

  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years

Compound Interest (CI)

Interest that is calculated on both the principal and the accumulated interest. The formula for compound interest is:

A = P(1 + R / 100)^T

Where:

  • A = Amount after interest
  • P = Principal amount
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • T = Time in years

Examples of Partnership & Investments

  1. Example 1: Partnership with Equal Capital

    Problem: Two partners, A and B, invest ₹10,000 and ₹10,000 respectively in a business. If the business earns a total profit of ₹5,000, what will be the share of each partner?

    Solution: Since both partners invested the same amount, the profit will be shared equally.

    Profit share for each partner = ₹5,000 × (10,000 / 20,000) = ₹2,500

    Answer: Both A and B will receive ₹2,500 each.
