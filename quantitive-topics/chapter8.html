Chapter 8 - Mensuration & Geometry

1. Area and Perimeter of 2D Shapes

Definition

  • The area and perimeter of a 2D shape are important measurements that describe its size and boundary.
  • Area refers to the amount of space inside the boundary of the shape.
  • Perimeter refers to the total length around the shape.

Formulas for Common 2D Shapes

1. Square

  • Area: A = s²

    Where s = Length of one side.

  • Perimeter: P = 4s

    Where s = Length of one side.

Example: For a square with side length 5 units:

  • Area = 5² = 25 square units
  • Perimeter = 4 × 5 = 20 units

2. Rectangle

  • Area: A = l × w

    Where l = Length, w = Width of the rectangle.

  • Perimeter: P = 2(l + w)

    Where l = Length, w = Width of the rectangle.

Example: For a rectangle with length 6 units and width 3 units:

  • Area = 6 × 3 = 18 square units
  • Perimeter = 2(6 + 3) = 18 units

3. Triangle

  • Equilateral Triangle
    • Area: A = (s²√3)/4

      Where s = Length of one side.

    • Perimeter: P = 3s

      Where s = Length of one side.

  • Right-Angled Triangle
    • Area: A = ½ × base × height

      Where base = Base length, height = Height.

    • Perimeter: P = a + b + c

      Where a and b are the two legs, and c is the hypotenuse.

Example (Equilateral Triangle): For an equilateral triangle with side length 4 units:

  • Area ≈ 6.93 square units
  • Perimeter = 3 × 4 = 12 units

Example (Right-Angled Triangle): For a right-angled triangle with base 6 units, height 8 units, and hypotenuse 10 units:

  • Area = ½ × 6 × 8 = 24 square units
  • Perimeter = 6 + 8 + 10 = 24 units

4. Circle

  • Area: A = πr²

    Where r = Radius of the circle, π ≈ 3.14159.

  • Perimeter (Circumference): P = 2πr

    Where r = Radius of the circle, π ≈ 3.14159.

Example: For a circle with radius 5 units:

  • Area ≈ 78.54 square units
  • Perimeter ≈ 31.42 units

5. Parallelogram

  • Area: A = b × h

    Where b = Base, h = Height (distance between the parallel sides).

  • Perimeter: P = 2(b + h)

    Where b = Base, h = Height.

Example: For a parallelogram with base 6 units and height 4 units:

  • Area = 6 × 4 = 24 square units
  • Perimeter = 2(6 + 4) = 20 units

6. Trapezium (Trapezoid)

  • Area: A = ½ × (b₁ + b₂) × h

    Where b₁ and b₂ are the lengths of the two parallel sides, h is the height (distance between the parallel sides).

  • Perimeter: P = a + b₁ + b₂ + c

    Where a and c are the non-parallel sides.

Example: For a trapezium with parallel sides 5 units and 7 units, and height 4 units:

  • Area = ½ × (5 + 7) × 4 = 24 square units
  • Perimeter = a + 5 + 7 + c (where a and c are the non-parallel sides)

2. Volume and Surface Area of 3D Shapes

Definition

  • Volume refers to the amount of space occupied by a 3D object.
  • Surface area refers to the total area of all the surfaces of a 3D object.

Formulas for Common 3D Shapes

1. Cube

  • Volume: V = s³

    Where s = Length of one side.

  • Surface Area: A = 6s²

    Where s = Length of one side.

Example: For a cube with side length 4 units:

  • Volume = 4³ = 64 cubic units
  • Surface Area = 6 × 4² = 6 × 16 = 96 square units

2. Rectangular Prism (Cuboid)

  • Volume: V = l × w × h

    Where l = Length, w = Width, h = Height.

  • Surface Area: A = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh

    Where l = Length, w = Width, h = Height.

Example: For a rectangular prism with length 5 units, width 3 units, and height 2 units:

  • Volume = 5 × 3 × 2 = 30 cubic units
  • Surface Area = 2(5 × 3 + 5 × 2 + 3 × 2) = 2(15 + 10 + 6) = 2 × 31 = 62 square units

3. Sphere

  • Volume: V = (4/3)πr³

    Where r = Radius of the sphere.

  • Surface Area: A = 4πr²

    Where r = Radius of the sphere.

Example: For a sphere with radius 3 units:

  • Volume ≈ (4/3)π × 3³ = 36π ≈ 113.1 cubic units
  • Surface Area = 4π × 3² = 36π ≈ 113.1 square units

4. Cylinder

  • Volume: V = πr²h

    Where r = Radius of the base, h = Height of the cylinder.

  • Surface Area: A = 2πr² + 2πrh

    Where r = Radius of the base, h = Height of the cylinder.

Example: For a cylinder with radius 4 units and height 6 units:

  • Volume = π × 4² × 6 = 96π ≈ 301.59 cubic units
  • Surface Area = 2π × 4² + 2π × 4 × 6 = 32π + 48π = 80π ≈ 251.33 square units

5. Cone

  • Volume: V = (1/3)πr²h

    Where r = Radius of the base, h = Height of the cone.

  • Surface Area: A = πr² + πrl

    Where r = Radius of the base, l = Slant height of the cone.

Example: For a cone with radius 3 units and height 5 units:

  • Volume ≈ (1/3)π × 3² × 5 = 15π ≈ 47.12 cubic units
  • Surface Area (if slant height l = 5.83) = π × 3² + π × 3 × 5.83 = 9π + 17.49π = 26.49π ≈ 83.16 square units

6. Pyramid

  • Volume: V = (1/3) × Base Area × h

    Where h = Height of the pyramid.

  • Surface Area: A = b² + 2b × l

    Where b = Length of the side of the base, l = Slant height of the pyramid.

Example: For a square pyramid with base side length 4 units and height 6 units:

  • Volume = (1/3) × 4² × 6 = 32 cubic units
  • Surface Area (if slant height l = 6.4) = 4² + 2 × 4 × 6.4 = 16 + 51.2 = 67.2 square units

7. Torus (Doughnut Shape)

  • Volume: V = 2π²Rr²

    Where R = Major radius, r = Minor radius.

  • Surface Area: A = 4π²Rr

    Where R = Major radius, r = Minor radius.

Example: For a torus with R = 5 units and r = 2 units:

  • Volume = 2π² × 5 × 2² = 40π² ≈ 395.84 cubic units
  • Surface Area = 4π² × 5 × 2 = 40π² ≈ 395.84 square units

3. Coordinate Geometry Basics

Definition

  • Coordinate Geometry (also called Analytical Geometry) uses a coordinate system to represent geometric shapes and solve problems related to lines, curves, and shapes using algebraic equations.
  • It combines algebra and geometry by using coordinates on a plane to represent points and define shapes.

The Cartesian Plane

  • The Cartesian plane is a 2D grid formed by two perpendicular number lines, the X-axis (horizontal) and the Y-axis (vertical).
  • The point of intersection of the X-axis and Y-axis is called the origin, denoted as \( O(0, 0) \).
  • The plane is divided into four quadrants:
    1. Quadrant I: Both \( x \) and \( y \) are positive.
    2. Quadrant II: \( x \) is negative, \( y \) is positive.
    3. Quadrant III: Both \( x \) and \( y \) are negative.
    4. Quadrant IV: \( x \) is positive, \( y \) is negative.

Coordinates of a Point

  • The position of any point in the plane is represented by an ordered pair \( (x, y) \), where:
    • \( x \) is the horizontal distance from the origin (along the X-axis).
    • \( y \) is the vertical distance from the origin (along the Y-axis).
  • Example: Point \( A(3, 4) \) is 3 units to the right and 4 units above the origin.

Distance Formula

  • The distance between two points \( P(x_1, y_1) \) and \( Q(x_2, y_2) \) is given by the formula:

    Formula: \( d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \)

  • Example: The distance between \( A(3, 4) \) and \( B(-2, -5) \) is approximately 10.29 units.

Midpoint Formula

  • The midpoint \( M \) of a line segment joining \( P(x_1, y_1) \) and \( Q(x_2, y_2) \) is given by:

    Formula: \( M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right) \)

  • Example: The midpoint of \( A(3, 4) \) and \( B(-2, -5) \) is \( M(0.5, -0.5) \).

Slope of a Line

  • The slope \( m \) of the line through points \( P(x_1, y_1) \) and \( Q(x_2, y_2) \) is given by:

    Formula: \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)

  • The slope determines the steepness of the line:
    • If \( m > 0 \), the line slopes upwards from left to right.
    • If \( m < 0 \), the line slopes downwards from left to right.
    • If \( m = 0 \), the line is horizontal.
    • If the slope is undefined, the line is vertical.

Equation of a Line

  • The equation of a line can be expressed in different forms:
    • Slope-Intercept Form: \( y = mx + c \), where \( m \) is the slope and \( c \) is the y-intercept.
    • Point-Slope Form: \( y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \), where \( (x_1, y_1) \) is a point on the line and \( m \) is the slope.

Collinearity of Points

  • Three points \( A(x_1, y_1) \), \( B(x_2, y_2) \), and \( C(x_3, y_3) \) are collinear if the area of the triangle formed by these points is zero.

    Area Formula: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \left| x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2) \right| \)

Area of a Triangle

  • The area of a triangle with vertices \( A(x_1, y_1) \), \( B(x_2, y_2) \), and \( C(x_3, y_3) \) is:

    Area Formula: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \left| x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2) \right| \)

General Form of the Equation of a Line

  • The general form of the equation of a line is:

    Formula: \( Ax + By + C = 0 \), where \( A \), \( B \), and \( C \) are constants.

  • To convert from slope-intercept form \( y = mx + c \) to general form, rearrange the equation to \( mx - y + c = 0 \).
4. Properties of Circles & Polygons

Properties of Circles

  • A circle is a collection of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point, called the center. The distance from the center to any point on the circle is called the radius.

Key Properties:

Radius:

  • The distance from the center of the circle to any point on the circle.
  • All radii of a circle are equal.

Diameter:

  • The longest chord of the circle, passing through the center.
  • It is twice the radius:

    Formula: \( D = 2r \), where \( r \) is the radius.

Circumference:

  • The perimeter or the boundary length of the circle.
  • Formula: \( C = 2\pi r \), where \( r \) is the radius of the circle.

Area:

  • The space enclosed by the circle.
  • Formula: \( A = \pi r^2 \), where \( r \) is the radius.

Chord:

  • A line segment joining two points on the circle.
  • The diameter is the longest chord of the circle.

Secant:

  • A straight line that intersects the circle at two points.

Tangent:

  • A straight line that touches the circle at exactly one point (called the point of tangency).
  • The tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency.

Arc:

  • A part of the circumference of the circle.
  • It can be a minor arc (less than 180°) or a major arc (greater than 180°).

Sector:

  • A region bounded by two radii and the arc between them.
  • Formula for Area of Sector: \( A = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times \pi r^2 \), where \( \theta \) is the central angle of the sector.

Segment:

  • A region bounded by a chord and the arc between the two endpoints of the chord.

Central Angle:

  • An angle whose vertex is at the center of the circle, and whose sides pass through two points on the circle.

Inscribed Angle:

  • An angle whose vertex is on the circle and whose sides are chords of the circle.

Properties of Polygons

  • A polygon is a closed, two-dimensional shape with straight sides. Polygons can have any number of sides, and they are classified based on the number of sides they have.

1. Triangle (3 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is always \( 180^\circ \).
  • Types of triangles:
    • Equilateral Triangle: All sides and angles are equal (each angle is \( 60^\circ \)).
    • Isosceles Triangle: Two sides are equal, and the angles opposite those sides are equal.
    • Scalene Triangle: All sides and angles are different.

2. Quadrilateral (4 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is always \( 360^\circ \).
  • Types of quadrilaterals:
    • Square: All sides and angles are equal (each angle is \( 90^\circ \)).
    • Rectangle: Opposite sides are equal, and all angles are \( 90^\circ \).
    • Parallelogram: Opposite sides are equal and parallel, and opposite angles are equal.
    • Rhombus: All sides are equal, but angles are not necessarily \( 90^\circ \).
    • Trapezium: Only one pair of opposite sides is parallel.

3. Pentagon (5 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 540^\circ \).
  • Regular pentagon: All sides and angles are equal.

4. Hexagon (6 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 720^\circ \).
  • Regular hexagon: All sides and angles are equal. The interior angle is \( 120^\circ \).

5. Heptagon (7 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 900^\circ \).

6. Octagon (8 sides)

  • The sum of the interior angles is \( 1080^\circ \).
  • Regular octagon: All sides and angles are equal. The interior angle is \( 135^\circ \).

7. Polygon with n sides

  • The sum of the interior angles of an n-sided polygon is \( (n - 2) \times 180^\circ \).
  • The exterior angles of any polygon always add up to \( 360^\circ \).

Special Properties of Regular Polygons

  • A regular polygon is a polygon where all sides and angles are equal. The most notable properties include:

Interior Angle:

  • The formula for the interior angle of a regular polygon with \( n \) sides is:

    Formula: \( \text{Interior Angle} = \frac{(n - 2) \times 180^\circ}{n} \)

Exterior Angle:

  • The formula for the exterior angle of a regular polygon with \( n \) sides is:

    Formula: \( \text{Exterior Angle} = \frac{360^\circ}{n} \)

Area of Regular Polygon:

  • The area of a regular polygon can be calculated using the formula:

    Formula: \( A = \frac{1}{4}n \times s^2 \times \cot\left(\frac{\pi}{n}\right) \), where \( n \) is the number of sides and \( s \) is the length of each side.

5. Trigonometry Basics

Definition

  • Trigonometry is the branch of mathematics that deals with the relationships between the angles and sides of triangles, particularly right-angled triangles.
  • It involves six primary trigonometric ratios: sine, cosine, tangent, cosecant, secant, and cotangent.

Trigonometric Ratios

  • For a right-angled triangle with an angle 𝜃:
  • Hypotenuse = H (the side opposite the right angle)
  • Opposite side = O (the side opposite the angle 𝜃)
  • Adjacent side = A (the side adjacent to the angle 𝜃)
  • Sine (sin):
    sin𝜃 = O/H
  • Cosine (cos):
    cos𝜃 = A/H
  • Tangent (tan):
    tan𝜃 = O/A
  • Cosecant (csc):
    csc𝜃 = H/O
  • Secant (sec):
    sec𝜃 = H/A
  • Cotangent (cot):
    cot𝜃 = A/O

SI Units

  • There is no specific SI unit for trigonometric ratios as they are dimensionless.

Important Trigonometric Identities

  • Pythagorean Identity:
    sin²𝜃 + cos²𝜃 = 1
  • Tangent and Secant Identity:
    tan²𝜃 + 1 = sec²𝜃
  • Cotangent and Cosecant Identity:
    1 + cot²𝜃 = csc²𝜃

Special Angles in Trigonometry

  • 0°:
    sin0° = 0, cos0° = 1, tan0° = 0
  • 30°:
    sin30° = 1/2, cos30° = √3/2, tan30° = 1/√3
  • 45°:
    sin45° = √2/2, cos45° = √2/2, tan45° = 1
  • 60°:
    sin60° = √3/2, cos60° = 1/2, tan60° = √3
  • 90°:
    sin90° = 1, cos90° = 0, tan90° = ∞ (undefined)

Trigonometric Equations

  • Trigonometric equations involve finding angles that satisfy a given trigonometric expression.
  • Basic strategies for solving include:
    1. Using identities to simplify the equation.
    2. Using known values of trigonometric ratios for special angles.
    3. Applying inverse trigonometric functions to solve for angles.

Applications of Trigonometry

  • Height and Distance Problems: Trigonometry is used to calculate the height of objects and distances between points using right-angled triangles.
  • Solving Triangles: Trigonometric ratios help in solving right-angled and non-right-angled triangles using the sine rule and cosine rule.

Sine and Cosine Rule

  • Sine Rule:
    a/sinA = b/sinB = c/sinC
    Used to solve any triangle (not necessarily right-angled).
  • Cosine Rule:
    c² = a² + b² − 2ab cosC
    Used to solve triangles when two sides and the included angle are known, or when all three sides are known.
