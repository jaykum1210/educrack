Chapter 5 - Time, Speed & Distance

1. Average Speed & Relative Speed

i. Average Speed

Average Speed is defined as the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken to travel that distance.

Formula for Average Speed:

Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time

Where:

  • Total Distance = Sum of all distances traveled
  • Total Time = Sum of all times taken

Important Points about Average Speed:

  • Average speed does not depend on the speed at each individual point, but on the total distance and total time.
  • If a person travels at different speeds over different distances or times, the average speed is calculated by considering the total distance and total time taken.

Example 1:

A car travels 100 km at a speed of 50 km/h and then travels another 100 km at a speed of 100 km/h. What is the average speed of the car for the entire journey?

Solution:

  • Distance 1 = 100 km, Speed 1 = 50 km/h, Time 1 = 100 / 50 = 2 hours
  • Distance 2 = 100 km, Speed 2 = 100 km/h, Time 2 = 100 / 100 = 1 hour
  • Total Distance = 100 + 100 = 200 km
  • Total Time = 2 + 1 = 3 hours
  • Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time = 200 / 3 = 66.67 km/h

Answer: The average speed of the car for the entire journey is 66.67 km/h.

ii. Relative Speed

Relative Speed refers to the speed of one object with respect to another object. It is useful when two objects are moving in the same or opposite directions.

If two objects are moving in the same direction, the relative speed is the difference of their speeds:

Relative Speed = Speed of A - Speed of B (where Speed A is the speed of the faster object and Speed B is the speed of the slower object).

If two objects are moving in opposite directions, the relative speed is the sum of their speeds:

Relative Speed = Speed of A + Speed of B

Example 2:

Two cars are moving towards each other. Car A moves at 60 km/h and Car B moves at 40 km/h. What is their relative speed?

Solution:

  • Since the cars are moving towards each other (opposite directions), the relative speed is:
  • Relative Speed = 60 + 40 = 100 km/h

Answer: The relative speed is 100 km/h.

Example 3:

Two trains are moving in the same direction. Train A moves at 80 km/h and Train B moves at 60 km/h. What is their relative speed?

Solution:

  • Since the trains are moving in the same direction, the relative speed is:
  • Relative Speed = 80 - 60 = 20 km/h

Answer: The relative speed is 20 km/h.

iii. Time Taken to Meet (Relative Speed Approach)

When two objects move towards each other, the time taken for them to meet can be found using the formula:

Time = Distance between them / Relative Speed

Example 4:

Two cars are 200 km apart and are moving towards each other. Car A moves at 60 km/h and Car B moves at 40 km/h. How long will it take for them to meet?

Solution:

  • Relative Speed = 60 + 40 = 100 km/h
  • Distance between them = 200 km
  • Time = 200 / 100 = 2 hours

Answer: It will take 2 hours for the cars to meet.

iv. Time Taken to Cross Each Other (Relative Speed Approach)

When two objects (e.g., trains, cars) are moving in opposite directions and need to pass each other completely, the time taken for them to cross each other is:

Time to Cross = Sum of the lengths of both objects / Relative Speed

Example 5:

Two trains are 100 meters long each and are moving in opposite directions. Train A moves at 60 km/h and Train B moves at 40 km/h. How much time will they take to cross each other?

Solution:

  • Convert the speeds into meters per second:
    • 60 km/h = 60 × 1000 / 3600 = 16.67 m/s
    • 40 km/h = 40 × 1000 / 3600 = 11.11 m/s
  • Relative Speed = 16.67 + 11.11 = 27.78 m/s
  • The total length to be covered is 100 + 100 = 200 meters.
  • Time to Cross = 200 / 27.78 ≈ 7.2 seconds

Answer: The trains will take approximately 7.2 seconds to cross each other.

2. Boats & Streams Problems

Boats & Streams problems are a common type of question in competitive exams involving the concept of relative speed in moving objects. These problems usually involve a boat moving in a river, and the stream’s current influences the boat’s motion. These questions test your ability to calculate the effective speed of the boat and how it is affected by the stream's current.

Key Concepts:

  • Speed of the Boat in Still Water: This is the speed of the boat when there is no current in the river. Denoted as B (speed of boat in still water).
  • Speed of the Stream (Current): This is the speed at which the water of the stream moves. Denoted as S (speed of stream).
  • Effective Speed of the Boat in the Stream:
    • When the boat is moving downstream (in the direction of the current), the current assists the boat, so the effective speed is the sum of the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the stream: Effective Speed Downstream = B + S
    • When the boat is moving upstream (against the current), the current opposes the boat, so the effective speed is the difference between the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the stream: Effective Speed Upstream = B - S

Types of Boats & Streams Problems:

  • Speed of the Boat & Stream: These problems usually ask you to calculate the speed of the boat or the stream when the effective speed downstream or upstream is given.
  • Time Taken to Travel a Certain Distance: These problems involve calculating the time taken by the boat to cover a certain distance, either downstream or upstream.
  • Meeting or Crossing Point: Sometimes, two boats moving in opposite or the same direction are asked to meet or cross each other. The speed of the stream affects their meeting time.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1:

A boat travels 40 km downstream in 4 hours and 40 km upstream in 5 hours. What is the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the stream?

Solution:

Downstream Speed:

  • Distance = 40 km, Time = 4 hours
  • Speed downstream = 40 / 4 = 10 km/h

Upstream Speed:

  • Distance = 40 km, Time = 5 hours
  • Speed upstream = 40 / 5 = 8 km/h

Let the speed of the boat in still water be B km/h, and the speed of the stream be S km/h.

  • Downstream Speed: B + S = 10
  • Upstream Speed: B - S = 8

By solving these two equations:

  • (B + S) = 10
  • (B - S) = 8

Add both equations:

  • 2B = 18 ⇒ B = 9 km/h

Substitute B = 9 into B + S = 10:

  • 9 + S = 10 ⇒ S = 1 km/h

Answer: The speed of the boat in still water is 9 km/h, and the speed of the stream is 1 km/h.

3. Trains & Platform Problems

Trains & Platform problems are another common category of questions in competitive exams. These problems generally involve the motion of trains passing a platform or crossing each other. To solve such problems, you need to apply concepts related to relative speed, time, and distance.

Key Concepts:

  • Speed of the Train: This is the speed at which the train is moving. Denoted as S (Speed of the train).
  • Length of the Train: This is the total length of the train. Denoted as Ltrain (Length of the train).
  • Length of the Platform: This is the total length of the platform. Denoted as Lplatform (Length of the platform).
  • Time Taken to Pass the Platform: This is the time taken by the train to completely pass the platform. Denoted as Tpass (Time taken to pass the platform).

Basic Formulas:

  • Time Taken to Pass a Platform: When a train passes a platform, the total distance the train covers is the sum of the length of the train and the length of the platform. The time taken to pass the platform is given by:
    • Time = (Ltrain + Lplatform) / S
  • Time Taken to Cross Each Other: When two trains pass each other, the total distance they cover is the sum of their lengths, and the relative speed (if they are moving in opposite directions) is the sum of their individual speeds. If they are moving in the same direction, the relative speed is the difference of their individual speeds:
    • Time to Cross = (Sum of the lengths of both trains) / Relative Speed

Types of Trains & Platform Problems:

  • Time Taken to Pass a Platform: These problems involve calculating how much time a train will take to pass a platform given the speed and length of the train and platform.
  • Time Taken to Cross Another Train: In these problems, two trains are moving towards each other or in the same direction. You need to calculate the time taken for them to cross each other.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1:

A train, 100 meters long, is moving at a speed of 36 km/h. How much time will it take to pass a platform that is 200 meters long?

Solution:

Step 1: Convert the speed of the train from km/h to m/s:

36 km/h = (36 × 1000) / 3600 = 10 m/s

Step 2: Calculate the total distance to be covered:

Total Distance = 100 m (train length) + 200 m (platform length) = 300 m

Step 3: Calculate the time taken to pass the platform:

Time = Total Distance / Speed = 300 m / 10 m/s = 30 seconds

Answer: The train will take 30 seconds to pass the platform.

4. Circular Motion & Races Problems

Circular motion problems and race-related problems are commonly found in competitive exams. These problems typically involve understanding concepts related to the motion of objects along circular paths (like a car on a circular track) or the time it takes for different objects to cover specific distances, such as in races.

Circular Motion Problems

Circular motion refers to the motion of an object in a circular path. When objects move in a circle, they are said to undergo uniform circular motion if their speed remains constant. For objects moving in circular paths, key concepts like angular velocity, linear velocity, and centripetal force come into play.

Key Concepts of Circular Motion:

  • Linear Velocity (Tangential Speed): The speed of an object moving along a circular path, denoted by v. It is the distance traveled per unit time along the circumference of the circle.
    • v = r * ω
  • Angular Velocity (ω): The rate at which an object moves through an angle in a circular path. It is measured in radians per second (rad/s).
    • ω = θ / t
  • Centripetal Force: The force that keeps an object moving in a circular path is called the centripetal force. This force is always directed toward the center of the circle.
    • F = m * v² / r
  • Period of Revolution (T): The time taken for one complete revolution of the object around the circular path.
    • T = 2πr / v
  • Frequency (f): The number of revolutions completed per unit time. It is the reciprocal of the period.
    • f = 1 / T

Types of Circular Motion Problems:

  • Time Taken for One Complete Revolution: These problems involve calculating the time taken for an object to complete one full circle along a circular track or path.
  • Speed of an Object in Circular Motion: These problems involve finding the linear speed (tangential speed) or angular velocity of an object moving in a circular path.

Examples and Solutions:

Example 1:

A car is moving along a circular track with a radius of 100 meters. If the car completes one full revolution in 20 seconds, what is the speed of the car?

Solution:

Step 1: The distance covered in one revolution is the circumference of the circle:

Circumference = 2πr = 2π × 100 = 628.31 meters

Step 2: The time taken for one revolution is 20 seconds.

Step 3: The speed v is the distance divided by the time:

v = Circumference / Time = 628.31 / 20 = 31.42 m/s

Answer: The speed of the car is 31.42 m/s.
