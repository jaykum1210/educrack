Chapter 1 - Arithmetic & Number System

1. Basic Operations & BODMAS

The basic operations in mathematics are fundamental processes that form the foundation for most mathematical calculations. These operations include:

1. Addition (+):

The process of combining two or more numbers to get a larger number.

Example:
5 + 3 = 8

2. Subtraction (−):

The process of removing one number from another.

Example:
7 − 4 = 3

3. Multiplication (×):

The process of adding a number to itself multiple times.

Example:
6 × 3 = 18

4. Division (÷):

The process of splitting a number into equal parts.

Example:
12 ÷ 4 = 3

BODMAS Rule

BODMAS stands for Brackets, Orders, Division and Multiplication (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). It is a set of rules that helps in determining the correct order of operations to simplify expressions and avoid confusion.

  • B - Brackets: First, solve expressions inside brackets (parentheses). Brackets include round brackets (), square brackets [], and curly brackets {}.

    • Example:
    (5 + 3) × 2 = 8 × 2 = 16

  • O - Orders: This includes powers or exponents, such as squares, cubes, square roots, etc.

    • Example:
    2³ = 8

  • D and M - Division and Multiplication: After brackets and orders, division and multiplication are performed from left to right. They have equal priority, so they are handled in the order they appear.

    • Example:
    10 ÷ 2 × 5 = 5 × 5 = 25

  • A and S - Addition and Subtraction: Finally, addition and subtraction are performed from left to right. These also have equal priority.

    • Example:
    8 + 2 − 3 = 10 − 3 = 7

Example of BODMAS in Action:

Simplify the following expression:

6 + (5 × 2) − 4 ÷ 2

Step 1: Brackets first
6 + 10 − 4 ÷ 2

Step 2: Division
6 + 10 − 2

Step 3: Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)
16 − 2 = 14

So, the result of the expression is 14.

Following BODMAS ensures that we always perform calculations in the correct order and get the correct results.

2. Factors, Multiples, and Divisibility

Factors

  • A factor of a number is any number that divides that number exactly, without leaving a remainder.
  • Example: Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
  • How to Find Factors:
    • Start with 1 and the number itself.
    • Check for other numbers that divide the given number exactly (i.e., no remainder).
    • List all such numbers.

Multiples

  • A multiple of a number is the product of that number and any integer.
  • Example: Multiples of 5: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, ...
  • How to Find Multiples:
    • Start with the number itself.
    • Multiply that number by different integers (1, 2, 3, 4, etc.).
    • Continue the process to get an infinite list of multiples.

Divisibility

  • A number is divisible by another number if, when divided, there is no remainder.
  • Common Divisibility Rules:
    • Divisibility by 2: A number is divisible by 2 if its last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, 8).
    • Divisibility by 3: A number is divisible by 3 if the sum of its digits is divisible by 3.
    • Divisibility by 4: A number is divisible by 4 if the last two digits form a number divisible by 4.
    • Divisibility by 5: A number is divisible by 5 if its last digit is 0 or 5.
    • Divisibility by 6: A number is divisible by 6 if it is divisible by both 2 and 3.
    • Divisibility by 9: A number is divisible by 9 if the sum of its digits is divisible by 9.

Relation Between Factors and Multiples

  • Factors of a number are the numbers that divide it exactly.
  • Multiples of a number are the numbers you get when you multiply that number by integers.
  • Example:
    • Factors of 6: 1, 2, 3, 6
    • Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24, 30, ...

Summary

  • Factors: Numbers that divide a given number exactly (without a remainder).
  • Multiples: Numbers that are the product of a given number and any integer.
  • Divisibility: A rule that helps determine whether one number can be divided by another without a remainder.

3. LCM (Least Common Multiple) & HCF (Highest Common Factor)

LCM (Least Common Multiple)

  • The LCM of two or more numbers is the smallest number that is a multiple of all the given numbers.
  • It is the smallest common multiple shared by the numbers.
  • How to Find the LCM:
    • List the multiples of each number.
    • Find the smallest multiple that is common to all numbers.
    • Alternatively, the LCM of two numbers can also be found using the formula: LCM ( a , b ) = |a × b| HCF(a, b)
  • Example: Find the LCM of 4 and 6.
    • Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, ...
    • Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24, 30, ...
    • The smallest common multiple is 12, so the LCM of 4 and 6 is 12.

HCF (Highest Common Factor)

  • The HCF of two or more numbers is the largest number that divides all the given numbers exactly, without leaving a remainder.
  • It is also known as the GCD (Greatest Common Divisor).
  • How to Find the HCF:
    • List the factors of each number.
    • Find the largest number that is common to all the factor lists.
    • Alternatively, the HCF of two numbers can also be found using the formula: HCF ( a , b ) = |a × b| LCM(a, b)
  • Example: Find the HCF of 12 and 18.
    • Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
    • Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18
    • The largest common factor is 6, so the HCF of 12 and 18 is 6.

Relation Between LCM and HCF

  • There is an important relationship between the LCM and HCF of two numbers: LCM ( a , b ) × HCF ( a , b ) = |a × b|
  • Example:
    • For 12 and 18, we already know:
    • LCM = 36
    • HCF = 6
    • Now, check the relationship:
      • LCM(12, 18) × HCF(12, 18) = 36 × 6 = 216
      • |12 × 18| = 12 × 18 = 216
    • Since both results are equal, this confirms the relationship between LCM and HCF.

4. Prime Numbers & Composite Numbers

Prime Numbers

  • A prime number is a natural number greater than 1 that has no positive divisors other than 1 and itself.
  • In simple terms, it can only be divided by 1 and the number itself, meaning it cannot be formed by multiplying two smaller natural numbers.

    • Characteristics of Prime Numbers:

    • Prime numbers are greater than 1.
    • They have only two factors: 1 and the number itself.

    Examples of Prime Numbers:

    • 2 (only divisible by 1 and 2)
    • 3 (only divisible by 1 and 3)
    • 5 (only divisible by 1 and 5)
    • 7 (only divisible by 1 and 7)
    • 11 (only divisible by 1 and 11)

    Notice that 2 is the only even prime number. All other even numbers are not prime because they are divisible by 2.

    How to Check if a Number is Prime:

    • Check if the number n is divisible by any smaller number (starting from 2) up to n.
    • If it is divisible by any number other than 1 and itself, it is not a prime number.

Composite Numbers

  • A composite number is a natural number greater than 1 that has more than two factors.
  • In other words, composite numbers can be divided exactly by numbers other than 1 and themselves. These numbers can be formed by multiplying two smaller natural numbers.

    • Characteristics of Composite Numbers:

    • Composite numbers are greater than 1.
    • They have more than two factors.

    Examples of Composite Numbers:

    • 4 (divisible by 1, 2, and 4)
    • 6 (divisible by 1, 2, 3, and 6)
    • 8 (divisible by 1, 2, 4, and 8)
    • 9 (divisible by 1, 3, and 9)
    • 12 (divisible by 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12)

Difference Between Prime and Composite Numbers

Property Prime Numbers Composite Numbers
Divisors Exactly two divisors: 1 and itself More than two divisors
Examples 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17 4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 14
Even Prime Only 2 is even No even composite number is prime
Smallest Prime 2 No smallest composite (smallest is 4)

Special Case: Number 1

  • The number 1 is neither a prime nor a composite number. It only has one divisor (itself), so it doesn't meet the criteria for being prime or composite.
