Chapter 9 - Logical Quantitative Reasoning

1. Number and Letter Series

Number Series

  • In a number series, a sequence of numbers is given, and you need to find the next number or identify the pattern that fits the sequence.

Example 1

  • 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ___
  • Pattern: These are the squares of natural numbers: 1², 2², 3², 4², 5². The next number will be 6² = 36.

Example 2

  • 2, 6, 12, 20, 30, ___
  • Pattern: The difference between consecutive numbers increases by 2 each time:
    • 6 - 2 = 4
    • 12 - 6 = 6
    • 20 - 12 = 8
    • 30 - 20 = 10
    The next difference will be 12, so the next number will be 30 + 12 = 42.

Letter Series

  • In a letter series, a sequence of letters is given, and you need to identify the next letter or the pattern that fits the sequence.

Example 1

  • A, C, E, G, I, ___
  • Pattern: The letters are alternating every two positions in the alphabet: A, C, E, G, I. The next letter will be K.

Example 2

  • Z, X, V, T, R, ___
  • Pattern: The letters are decreasing by two positions in the alphabet: Z, X, V, T, R. The next letter will be P.

2. Coding-Decoding (Numbers & Symbols)

Definition

  • Coding-Decoding is a reasoning technique that involves transforming a given set of numbers, letters, or symbols into codes based on certain rules. The objective is to identify the pattern or logic behind the transformation.

Types of Coding-Decoding

  • Letter Coding
  • Number Coding
  • Symbol Coding

Letter Coding

  • In letter coding, each letter of the alphabet is replaced by another letter according to a fixed rule. The rule can be based on shifting positions, reversing the alphabet, or other manipulations.

Types of Letter Coding Patterns

Shift in Alphabet

  • Example: A → C, B → D, C → E, ___
  • Pattern: Each letter is shifted by +2.
  • Answer: D → F

Reverse Alphabet Coding

  • Example: A → Z, B → Y, C → X, ___
  • Pattern: Reverse the alphabet.
  • Answer: D → W

Positional Shift

  • Example: A → 1, B → 2, C → 3, ___
  • Pattern: The letters are replaced by their position numbers.
  • Answer: D → 4

Number Coding

  • In number coding, a number is replaced by another number based on some mathematical relationship, which could be addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, or powers.

Types of Number Coding Patterns

Simple Arithmetic Operations

  • Example: 3 → 6, 4 → 12, 5 → 20, ___
  • Pattern: The number is multiplied by its position.
  • Answer: 6 × 5 = 30

Squares or Cubes

  • Example: 2 → 4, 3 → 9, 4 → 16, ___
  • Pattern: The number is squared.
  • Answer: 5 → 25

Prime Numbers or Factors

  • Example: 3 → 9, 5 → 25, 7 → 49, ___
  • Pattern: The number is squared.
  • Answer: 11 → 121

Symbol Coding

  • In symbol coding, special symbols like #, $, %, *, &, etc., are used in place of numbers or letters. The key here is to decode the meaning of each symbol and how they relate to numbers or letters.

Types of Symbol Coding Patterns

Symbols Representing Operations

  • Example: # means +, * means -, % means ×.
  • Code: 6 # 3 * 2 % 4 → (6 + 3) - 2 × 4 → 9 - 2 × 4 = 9 - 8 = 1.

Substitution of Symbols with Numbers or Letters

  • Example: * represents multiplication, % represents division.
  • Code: 5 * 4 % 2 → 5 × 4 ÷ 2 = 20 ÷ 2 = 10.

Common Approaches to Solve Coding-Decoding Problems

  • Look for Arithmetic Patterns: Identify if the numbers or letters are related through addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, or exponentiation.
  • Position and Shifting: Sometimes, positions in the alphabet (or the number line) are key. Look for consistent shifting patterns (e.g., shifting a letter by +2 in the alphabet).
  • Reverse and Symmetry: Some codes might use reverse positions, such as reversing the alphabet or number sequences.
  • Understand Symbol Operations: If symbols are involved, carefully analyze the operation they represent, like +, -, ×, ÷.

Practice Examples

Practice Example 1:

  • Code: 4 → 16, 5 → 25, 6 → 36, ___
  • Pattern: The number is squared.
  • Answer: 7 → 49

Practice Example 2:

  • Code: A → Z, B → Y, C → X, ___
  • Pattern: Reverse the alphabet.
  • Answer: D → W

3. Quantitative Puzzles

Definition

  • Quantitative puzzles are designed to test your logical reasoning and numerical abilities. These puzzles typically require solving mathematical problems using reasoning, patterns, or basic arithmetic operations.

Types of Quantitative Puzzles

  • Number Puzzles
  • Age-Based Puzzles
  • Time and Work Puzzles
  • Profit and Loss Puzzles
  • Speed, Distance, and Time Puzzles
  • Mixture and Alligation Puzzles
  • Ratio and Proportion Puzzles
  • Clocks and Calendar Puzzles
  • Blood Relations and Family Puzzles

i. Number Puzzles

  • These puzzles involve numerical sequences, relationships, and arithmetic operations. The goal is often to find the next number in the sequence or solve for a specific number based on given conditions.

Types of Number Puzzles

Arithmetic Sequences

  • Example: Sequence: 3, 7, 11, 15, ___
  • Pattern: The difference between consecutive terms is 4 (7 - 3 = 4, 11 - 7 = 4, etc.).
  • Answer: The next number is 19 (15 + 4).

Geometric Sequences

  • Example: Sequence: 2, 6, 18, 54, ___
  • Pattern: Each number is multiplied by 3.
  • Answer: The next number is 162 (54 × 3).

Sum of Digits

  • Example: Find the number if the sum of its digits equals 10 and the number is a two-digit number.
  • Answer: 19 (1 + 9 = 10).

ii. Age-Based Puzzles

  • These puzzles typically involve relationships between the ages of different people, and you need to figure out the individual ages based on given conditions.

Example 1:

  • John is twice as old as Peter. Five years ago, he was three times as old as Peter. How old are they now?
  • Let Peter’s age be x. John's age will be 2x. Five years ago, Peter’s age was x - 5 and John’s age was 2x - 5.
  • According to the condition: 2x - 5 = 3(x - 5).
  • Solving the equation gives: 2x - 5 = 3x - 15, so x = 10.
  • Answer: Peter is 10 years old, and John is 20 years old.

iii. Time and Work Puzzles

  • These puzzles involve concepts like work rates and time taken to complete a task.

Example 1:

  • A can complete a task in 10 days, and B can complete the same task in 15 days. How long will they take if they work together?
  • Work rate of A = 1/10 (task per day).
  • Work rate of B = 1/15 (task per day).
  • Combined work rate = 1/10 + 1/15 = 1/6.
  • Therefore, together they will complete the task in 6 days.

iv. Profit and Loss Puzzles

  • These puzzles are about calculating profit, loss, cost price, and selling price based on given conditions.

Example:

  • A shopkeeper buys a product for ₹100 and sells it for ₹120. What is the profit percentage?
  • Profit = Selling Price - Cost Price = ₹120 - ₹100 = ₹20.
  • Profit percentage = (Profit / Cost Price) × 100 = (20 / 100) × 100 = 20%.
  • Answer: The profit percentage is 20%.

v. Speed, Distance, and Time Puzzles

  • These puzzles deal with the relationship between speed, distance, and time.

Example:

  • A car travels from City A to City B at 60 km/h and returns from City B to City A at 40 km/h. What is the average speed for the entire journey?
  • Average speed = 2 × (Speed1 × Speed2) / (Speed1 + Speed2) = 2 × 60 × 40 / (60 + 40) = 4800 / 100 = 48 km/h.
  • Answer: The average speed is 48 km/h.

vi. Mixture and Alligation Puzzles

  • These puzzles involve mixing two or more substances and calculating the resulting mixture's properties.

Example:

  • A shopkeeper mixes two types of tea – one costing ₹20 per kg and the other ₹30 per kg – to make 10 kg of a mixture that costs ₹25 per kg. How much of each type of tea is used?
  • The alligation method can be used: Difference between ₹30 and ₹25 = ₹5. Difference between ₹25 and ₹20 = ₹5.
  • This means the two types of tea are mixed in the ratio of 1:1.
  • Answer: 5 kg of each tea.

vii. Ratio and Proportion Puzzles

  • These puzzles involve comparing quantities and solving for unknown values using ratios.

Example:

  • The ratio of the ages of A and B is 3:4. If A is 18 years old, how old is B?
  • Let the age of B be x. Using the ratio: A / B = 3 / 4, so 18 / x = 3 / 4.
  • Cross-multiply to solve for x: x = 18 × 4 / 3 = 24.
  • Answer: B is 24 years old.

viii. Clocks and Calendar Puzzles

  • These puzzles are based on time, days of the week, or months.

Example:

  • If today is Monday, what day will it be 100 days from now?
  • Divide 100 by 7 (the number of days in a week): 100 ÷ 7 = 14 weeks and a remainder of 2.
  • So, 100 days from Monday will be 2 days ahead, which is Wednesday.
  • Answer: Wednesday.

ix. Blood Relations and Family Puzzles

  • These puzzles involve determining the relationship between different family members based on given conditions.

Example:

  • Pointing to a photograph, A said, "I have no brothers, but this boy's father is my father's son." Who is the boy in the photograph?
  • "My father's son" is A himself (since A has no brothers).
  • Therefore, the boy in the photograph is A’s son.

4. Blood Relation & Age Problems

Blood Relation Problems

Definition

  • Blood relation problems involve determining the relationships between different family members based on the given conditions.

Key Terms

  • Father: Male parent.
  • Mother: Female parent.
  • Brother: Male sibling.
  • Sister: Female sibling.
  • Uncle: Brother of one's parent.
  • Aunt: Sister of one's parent.
  • Nephew: Son of one's brother or sister.
  • Niece: Daughter of one's brother or sister.
  • Cousin: A child of one's uncle or aunt.

Examples:

  1. Blood Relation Problem 1: Basic Blood Relation

    Problem: Pointing to a person in a photograph, A said, “He is the son of my father’s only son.” Who is the person in the photograph?

    Solution: "My father’s only son" refers to A (since A is the only son). Hence, the person in the photograph is A’s son.

    Answer: The person in the photograph is A’s son.

  2. Blood Relation Problem 2: Multiple Relations

    Problem: A man is looking at a picture. His friend asks, "Who are you looking at?" The man replies, "Brothers and sisters, I have none. But the father of the person in the picture is my father’s son." Who is in the picture?

    Solution: The man has no brothers, so "my father’s son" must be himself. Therefore, the person in the picture is his son.

    Answer: The person in the picture is his son.

Age Problems

Definition

  • Age problems are based on the relationships between the ages of two or more people, often over time, and require solving using algebra or arithmetic.

Key Terms and Approaches

  • Age Difference: The age difference between two people remains constant.
  • Age Ratio: Relationships between ages are given as ratios.
  • Time Gap: Given conditions about how old someone was or will be at a certain point in time.

Examples:

  1. Age Problem 1: Simple Age Calculation

    Problem: The sum of the ages of A and B is 50 years. A is 10 years older than B. What are their ages?

    Solution: Let B’s age be x. A’s age = x + 10. The sum of their ages is x + (x + 10) = 50.

    Solving: 2x + 10 = 50, so 2x = 40, and x = 20. A’s age = x + 10 = 30.

    Answer: A is 30 years old, and B is 20 years old.

  2. Age Problem 2: Age Difference Over Time

    Problem: Five years ago, A was three times as old as B. In 5 years, A will be twice as old as B. How old are A and B now?

    Solution: Let A’s present age be x and B’s present age be y. The conditions are:

    • Five years ago: A’s age = x - 5, B’s age = y - 5. Hence, x - 5 = 3(y - 5) [Equation 1].
    • In 5 years: A’s age = x + 5, B’s age = y + 5. Hence, x + 5 = 2(y + 5) [Equation 2].

    Solving these two equations gives x = 35 and y = 15.

    Answer: A is 35 years old, and B is 15 years old.

5. Direction Sense & Clock-Based Questions

Direction Sense Questions

Definition

  • Direction sense problems focus on determining the final direction or position after a series of movements or turns.
  • The directions involved are North (N), South (S), East (E), West (W), and sometimes intermediate directions like North-East (NE), South-East (SE), South-West (SW), and North-West (NW).

Important Directions

  • North (N): Upward direction.
  • South (S): Downward direction.
  • East (E): Right direction.
  • West (W): Left direction.
  • North-East (NE): Between North and East.
  • South-West (SW): Between South and West.

Examples:

  1. Direction Sense Problem 1: Basic Movement

    Problem: A person starts walking from point A. He walks 10 meters North, turns right, walks 5 meters, and then turns right again. In which direction is he now from his starting point?

    Solution:

    • He starts walking North.
    • After turning right, he walks East.
    • After another right turn, he walks South.
    • The net movement is 5 meters East and 10 meters South.

    Answer: South-East.

  2. Direction Sense Problem 2: Multiple Turns

    Problem: A man walks 10 meters north, turns left, walks 5 meters, turns left again, and walks 10 meters. What is his final position relative to his starting point?

    Solution:

    • Starts by walking 10 meters North.
    • Turns left (West), and walks 5 meters.
    • Turns left again (South), and walks 10 meters.
    • He is now 5 meters West of his starting point.

    Answer: He is 5 meters West of the starting point.

Clock-Based Questions

Definition

  • Clock-based problems involve finding the position of the hour and minute hands of a clock at a given time or calculating angles between them.

Key Concepts

  • Angle Calculation: The clock has 360°, divided into 12 hours, so each hour represents 30°.
  • The minute hand moves 6° per minute (360° ÷ 60 = 6°).
  • The hour hand moves 0.5° per minute (30° ÷ 60 = 0.5°).

Examples:

  1. Clock-Based Problem 1: Finding the Angle Between Hands

    Problem: What is the angle between the hour and minute hands at 3:15?

    Solution:

    • The hour hand is at 90° (3 × 30° = 90°).
    • At 3:15, the hour hand moves 7.5° further (15 × 0.5°).
    • The minute hand is at 90° (15 × 6°).
    • The angle between them is: |(90° + 7.5°) - 90°| = 7.5°.

    Answer: 7.5°.

  2. Clock-Based Problem 2: When Do the Hands Overlap?

    Problem: When do the hour and minute hands overlap between 3:00 and 4:00?

    Solution:

    • The hour hand is at 90° at 3:00.
    • The minute hand moves faster than the hour hand.
    • The equation for overlap is: 90° + 0.5° × t = 6° × t.
    • Solve for t: 90° = 5.5° × t, so t = 90 ÷ 5.5 = 16.36 minutes.

    Answer: The hands overlap at 3:16:22.

  3. Clock-Based Problem 3: Time When Hands Are 90° Apart

    Problem: When are the hour and minute hands 90° apart between 3:00 and 4:00?

    Solution:

    • Use the formula: θ = |30H - 5.5M| and set θ = 90°.
    • Solve for M (minutes) for two different times within the hour when the hands form a 90° angle.
    • The two times when the hands are 90° apart are around 3:16:22 and 3:49:09.

    Answer: 3:16:22 and 3:49:09.
