Chapter 3 - Indian Polity & Governance

1. Constitution & Political System of India

i. Constitution of India

  • Adopted: 26th November 1949; came into effect on 26th January 1950.
  • Length: The longest written constitution in the world with 448 articles, 12 schedules, and amended 100+ times.
  • Framing: Constituent Assembly, led by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, drafted the constitution.

ii. Preamble

  • The Preamble outlines the goals and values of the constitution.
  • Justice: Social, economic, and political.
  • Liberty: Of thought, expression, belief, faith, and worship.
  • Equality: Of status and opportunity.
  • Fraternity: Promoting dignity and unity.

iii. Fundamental Rights (Part III)

  • Rights guaranteed to all citizens to ensure equality and freedom.
  • Right to Equality: Article 14-18
  • Right to Freedom: Article 19-22
  • Right Against Exploitation: Article 23-24
  • Right to Freedom of Religion: Article 25-28
  • Cultural and Educational Rights: Article 29-30
  • Right to Constitutional Remedies: Article 32

iv. Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV)

  • Guidelines for the government to implement policies to ensure social justice and welfare (e.g., adequate livelihood, education, healthcare).

v. Fundamental Duties (Part IVA)

  • Duties of every citizen, including respect for the Constitution, defend the country, and preserve harmony.

vi. Structure of Government

  • India follows a Federal Parliamentary System, with a separation of powers among three branches:

    • Executive:

    • President: The ceremonial head of state, elected by an electoral college.
    • Prime Minister (PM): The head of government, appointed by the President, leads the Council of Ministers.
    • Council of Ministers: Includes Cabinet Ministers, Ministers of State, and Deputy Ministers.

    Legislature:

    • Parliament: Bicameral, consisting of two houses:
    • Lok Sabha (House of the People): Lower house, directly elected, with 545 members.
    • Rajya Sabha (Council of States): Upper house, indirectly elected, with 245 members.

    Judiciary:

    • Supreme Court of India: The highest judicial body, headed by the Chief Justice of India.
    • High Courts: For each state, dealing with state-specific issues.
    • Lower Courts: District courts handling regional disputes.

vii. Union Territories and States

  • States: India has 28 states and 8 Union Territories.
  • Special Status: Jammu & Kashmir had special status until 2019 (Article 370), now a Union Territory with legislative powers.

viii. Electoral System

  • Lok Sabha Elections: Every 5 years, direct elections by First-Past-the-Post system.
  • Rajya Sabha Elections: Indirect elections, based on proportional representation by State Legislative Assemblies.
  • President Elections: Indirect election by an electoral college.

ix. Amendment of the Constitution

  • Amendments can be made by Parliament with a simple majority, special majority, or ratification by states, depending on the type of change.

x. Political Parties

  • National Parties: BJP, Congress, CPM, etc.
  • Regional Parties: Focused on regional issues (e.g., TMC, DMK, TRS).

xi. Major Institutions

  • Election Commission: Oversees free and fair elections.
  • Finance Commission: Advises on the distribution of financial resources between the Centre and states.
  • Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG): Audits government expenditure.

xii. Important Features

  • Secularism: India has no state religion; all religions are treated equally.
  • Sovereign: India has full control over its territory and decisions without external interference.
  • Democracy: A system of government where the power lies with the people through elected representatives.
  • Republic: Head of the state (President) is elected and not hereditary.

2. Governance & Administration of India

i. Structure of Governance

  • India follows a Federal system of governance, with powers divided between the Central Government (Union) and State Governments.
  • It is based on the Parliamentary System, with the President as the ceremonial head of state and the Prime Minister as the head of government.

ii. Three Branches of Government

Executive:

  • President: The constitutional head of state, with ceremonial powers, acts on the advice of the Prime Minister and Council of Ministers.
  • Prime Minister and Council of Ministers: Head of government and the decision-making body for the country. The PM is appointed by the President.
  • Governors: Represent the President in each state.

Legislature:

  • Parliament: Bicameral (Two Houses):
    • Lok Sabha (House of the People): Lower house, directly elected.
    • Rajya Sabha (Council of States): Upper house, indirectly elected.
  • State Legislature: Respective state assemblies (unicameral or bicameral).

Judiciary:

  • Supreme Court: Highest judicial authority in India.
  • High Courts: Located in each state, they supervise judicial matters within the state.
  • Lower Courts: District courts and sessions courts manage local and regional cases.

iii. Administrative Structure

  • Indiaâ€™s administrative system is based on bureaucracy, headed by Indian Administrative Service (IAS) officers.
  • The administration is divided into central and state tiers:
    • Central Government Administration: Focused on national-level governance, laws, and policies.
    • State Government Administration: Responsible for state laws, welfare, and coordination with the central government.

iv. Key Administrative Officers

  • District Magistrate (DM): Senior-most administrative officer in a district, responsible for law and order, revenue administration, and implementation of policies.
  • Sub-Divisional Magistrate (SDM): Junior to DM, responsible for a subdivision of a district.
  • Block Development Officer (BDO): Responsible for development at the block level (rural areas).

v. Key Governance Institutions

  • Election Commission: An independent body responsible for conducting free and fair elections for Parliament and State Assemblies.
  • Finance Commission: Advises on the distribution of finances between the central government and states.
  • Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG): Audits government accounts and ensures transparency.
  • Central Vigilance Commission (CVC): Monitors corruption in government organizations.
  • Public Service Commissions: Conduct recruitment for central and state government posts (e.g., UPSC for the central level, SPSC for states).

vi. Local Governance

  • Panchayati Raj: Local self-governance in rural areas, established by the 73rd Constitutional Amendment.
    • Gram Panchayat: Village level.
    • Panchayat Samiti: Block level.
    • Zilla Parishad: District level.
  • Urban Governance: Managed through municipal corporations and municipalities:
    • Municipalities: For towns and small cities.
    • Municipal Corporations: For larger cities like Delhi, Mumbai, and Chennai.

vii. Central and State Governments

  • Central Government: The Union Cabinet, led by the Prime Minister, manages national policies, defense, foreign affairs, and finances.
  • State Governments: Each state has a Chief Minister and a Council of Ministers that manage state policies related to education, health, and welfare.

viii. Law and Order

  • Police: Each state has its own police force, and the Central Police Organizations (CPOs) such as CRPF, BSF, and CISF maintain law and order in specialized areas.
  • Judiciary: Ensures the enforcement of laws and resolves disputes through courts.

ix. Public Administration Reforms

  • India continues to evolve its administrative systems to ensure transparency, efficiency, and accountability.
  • E-Governance: The use of digital tools for government services, like Digital India and MyGov.
  • Right to Information (RTI): Ensures transparency in government activities by allowing citizens to seek information from public authorities.

x. Governance Challenges

  • Corruption: Efforts like the Anti-Corruption Act and CVC aim to curb corruption in government.
  • Bureaucratic Red Tape: Complexity in administrative procedures can lead to delays and inefficiencies.
  • Inequality: Economic disparities between states and regions still remain, with some states more developed than others.
  • Regional Autonomy: Issues around the devolution of power and autonomy for states, especially in areas like Jammu & Kashmir and Northeastern states.

3. Important Laws & Acts in India

i. Indian Penal Code (IPC) - 1860

  • The primary criminal code of India, covering a wide range of criminal offenses and penalties.
  • Sections: Defines crimes such as murder, theft, robbery, rape, fraud, etc.

ii. Constitution of India - 1950

  • The supreme law of the land that governs India.
  • It establishes the structure of government, fundamental rights, directive principles, and fundamental duties of citizens.

iii. Indian Contract Act - 1872

  • Governs contracts and agreements in India.
  • Defines the rights and obligations of the parties to a contract, conditions for validity and enforceability of agreements, etc.

iv. Right to Information Act (RTI) - 2005

  • Empowers citizens to seek information from public authorities.
  • Promotes transparency and accountability in government functioning.

v. Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act - 2005

  • Provides protection for women from domestic violence, including physical, emotional, sexual, and economic abuse.
  • Gives women legal recourse through protection orders, residence orders, and monetary relief.

vi. The Goods and Services Tax (GST) Act - 2017

  • Introduced a single tax system for the entire country, replacing multiple indirect taxes like VAT, service tax, and excise duty.
  • Aims for ease of doing business and tax compliance.

vii. Indian Evidence Act - 1872

  • Governs the admissibility of evidence in Indian courts.
  • Defines what can be considered legal evidence in criminal and civil cases.

viii. Hindu Marriage Act - 1955

  • Governs marriage, divorce, and maintenance among Hindus.
  • Provides for monogamy and allows divorce under certain conditions.

ix. Dowry Prohibition Act - 1961

  • Prohibits the demand, acceptance, or giving of dowry in marriages.
  • Punishes those involved in dowry-related harassment or deaths.

x. Right to Education Act (RTE) - 2009

  • Guarantees free and compulsory education for children between the ages of 6 to 14 years.
  • Aims to provide quality education and improve literacy rates.

xi. Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act - 2015

  • Provides for the care, protection, and rehabilitation of children in need.
  • Special provisions for the treatment of juveniles in conflict with law.

xii. The National Food Security Act - 2013

  • Aims to provide food security to over 67% of India's population.
  • Ensures access to subsidized food grains (rice, wheat, and coarse grains).

xiii. The Prevention of Corruption Act - 1988

  • Aims to combat corruption in the government and public sector.
  • Prohibits public servants from accepting bribes and outlines punishments for corrupt practices.

xiv. The National Security Act (NSA) - 1980

  • Allows the detention of individuals deemed a threat to national security or public order.
  • The person can be detained without trial for up to 12 months.

xv. The Companies Act - 2013

  • Governs the formation, operation, and dissolution of companies in India.
  • Provides legal provisions for corporate governance, management, and accountability.

xvi. The Consumer Protection Act - 1986 (Amended in 2019)

  • Protects the rights of consumers against defective goods, services, and unfair trade practices.
  • Established Consumer Disputes Redressal Commissions at district, state, and national levels.

xvii. The Environment Protection Act - 1986

  • Provides a framework for the protection and improvement of the environment.
  • Empowers the government to take preventive and corrective actions for environmental issues.

xviii. The Public Liability Insurance Act - 1991

  • Provides for compensation to victims of accidents or injuries caused by hazardous substances.
  • Enforces that industries handling such substances have insurance to cover liabilities.

xix. The Anti-Defection Law - 1985

  • Prevents politicians from switching parties to avoid instability in the government.
  • Any legislator who defects from the party they were elected from can lose their seat.

xx. The Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act - 1989

  • Aims to prevent discrimination and atrocities against Scheduled Castes (SC) and Scheduled Tribes (ST).
  • Provides for special courts and fast-track trials for cases related to atrocities.

xxi. The Civil Procedure Code (CPC) - 1908

  • Defines the procedures for civil litigation in India, including filing suits, proceedings, and appeals.

xxii. The Criminal Procedure Code (CrPC) - 1973

  • Defines the procedures for criminal justice in India, including the investigation, trial, and punishment for criminal offenses.

xxiii. The Trade Union Act - 1926

  • Regulates the formation and functioning of trade unions in India.
  • Ensures the rights of workers, including the right to organize and negotiate collectively.

xxiv. The Arbitration and Conciliation Act - 1996

  • Provides an alternative dispute resolution mechanism (arbitration) for resolving civil and commercial disputes outside the court.
