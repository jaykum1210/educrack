Chapter 10 - Quantitative Aptitude & Data Interpretation

1. Basic Mathematics

1. Percentage

Definition: A percentage is a way to express a number as a fraction of 100.

Formula:

Percentage = (Part / Whole) × 100

Key Concepts:

  • Increase/Decrease Percentage:

    Percentage Increase = ((New Value - Old Value) / Old Value) × 100

  • Profit Percentage:

    Profit Percentage = (Profit / Cost Price) × 100

    Where, Profit = Selling Price - Cost Price

  • Discount Percentage:

    Discount = (Discount Amount / Marked Price) × 100

2. Profit and Loss

  • Profit: When the selling price (SP) is greater than the cost price (CP).

    • Profit = Selling Price - Cost Price

  • Loss: When the cost price (CP) is greater than the selling price (SP).

    • Loss = Cost Price - Selling Price

  • Profit or Loss Percentage:

    Profit Percentage = (Profit / Cost Price) × 100

    Loss Percentage = (Loss / Cost Price) × 100

  • Selling Price (SP) with Profit or Loss:
    • With Profit: SP = CP × (1 + P / 100)
    • With Loss: SP = CP × (1 - L / 100)

3. Ratios

Definition: A ratio is a relationship between two or more numbers that indicates how many times the first number contains the second.

Formula:

Ratio of A to B = A / B

Types of Ratios:

  • Simplifying Ratios: Divide both terms of the ratio by their greatest common divisor (GCD).
  • Dividing a Quantity in a Given Ratio:

    If a quantity is divided in the ratio A:B, then the parts are:

    • Part 1 = A / (A + B)
    • Part 2 = B / (A + B)
  • Example: Ratio of ages of A and B: 3:4 → If A’s age is 30 years, B’s age = (4 / 3) × 30 = 40 years.

4. Time and Distance

Basic Formula: Distance = Speed × Time

Speed, Time, and Distance:

  • Speed: Speed = Distance / Time
  • Time: Time = Distance / Speed

Relative Speed:

  • When two objects move in opposite directions: Relative Speed = Speed of A + Speed of B
  • When moving in the same direction: Relative Speed = |Speed of A - Speed of B|

Time and Work:

If A can complete a work in x days and B in y days, their combined work time is:

Time taken together = (x * y) / (x + y)

2. Data Interpretation

i. Graphs and Charts

Graphs and charts are visual representations of data that help interpret large amounts of information in a simplified manner.

Types of Graphs and Charts:

  • Bar Chart: A graph that uses bars to represent data. It's useful for comparing quantities across categories.
  • Line Graph: A graph that shows data points connected by a line, useful for displaying trends over time.
  • Pie Chart: A circular chart divided into sectors, representing proportions of a whole.
  • Histogram: Similar to a bar chart but used for frequency distribution of continuous data.
  • Scatter Plot: A chart used to display the relationship between two continuous variables.

Key Concepts:

  • Interpretation: The process of analyzing and extracting meaningful insights from the data displayed in the graph or chart.
  • Scale and Units: Ensure that the scale and units used in the graph or chart are clearly understood before interpreting the data.
  • Trends: Identify increasing, decreasing, or constant trends in data by examining the chart or graph.

ii. Probability

Definition: Probability is the measure of the likelihood of an event occurring. It is expressed as a number between 0 and 1, where 0 means the event will not happen, and 1 means it will definitely happen.

Basic Formula:

Probability (P) = (Number of favorable outcomes) / (Total number of outcomes)

Key Concepts:

  • Complementary Events: The probability of an event not happening is 1 minus the probability of it happening (P(not A) = 1 - P(A)).
  • Independent Events: The occurrence of one event does not affect the occurrence of another event (P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)).
  • Mutually Exclusive Events: Two events cannot happen at the same time (P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)).
  • Conditional Probability: The probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred (P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)).

Common Probability Distributions:

  • Binomial Distribution: A distribution for binary outcomes, where there are two possible outcomes (success/failure).
  • Normal Distribution: A bell-shaped probability distribution often used to model natural occurrences.

iii. Simplifications

Definition: Simplification involves breaking down complex expressions or calculations to make them easier to solve, often using basic arithmetic rules.

Key Concepts:

  • Simplifying Fractions: Reduce fractions to their simplest form by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common divisor (GCD).
  • Decimals to Fractions: Convert decimal numbers to fractions by expressing them as a fraction of 10, 100, etc., depending on the number of decimal places.
  • Percentage Calculations: Use shortcuts for calculating percentages:
    • 10% of a number is the number divided by 10.
    • 20% of a number is the number divided by 5.
    • 50% of a number is the number divided by 2.
  • Multiplication and Division Shortcuts: Use tricks like multiplying by 5, 10, or 50 for quick calculations.

Common Simplification Techniques:

  • Rationalizing Denominators: Convert expressions with roots in the denominator into an equivalent expression without roots by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the conjugate.
  • Approximations: Use rounding or approximations to simplify calculations when exact values are not necessary.
  • Use of Algebraic Identities: Use standard algebraic identities (e.g., (a + b)2, a2 - b2) to simplify complex expressions.
