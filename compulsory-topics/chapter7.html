Chapter 7 - International Relations

1. South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (SAARC)

i. Overview

SAARC stands for South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation, a regional intergovernmental organization and geopolitical union in South Asia.

  • Founded: 1985
  • Headquarters: Kathmandu, Nepal
  • Members: 8 countries
    • Afghanistan
    • Bangladesh
    • Bhutan
    • India
    • Maldives
    • Nepal
    • Pakistan
    • Sri Lanka

ii. Objectives of SAARC

  • Promote Regional Cooperation: Foster economic, social, cultural, and political cooperation among South Asian countries.
  • Improve Living Standards: Enhance the quality of life of the people in South Asia by promoting development in various sectors.
  • Promote Peace and Stability: Foster peace and stability in South Asia through mutual cooperation and respect.
  • Regional Integration: Strengthen the cultural, social, and economic ties among the member countries to build a stronger regional community.

iii. Key Areas of Cooperation

SAARC focuses on various sectors, including:

  • Economic Cooperation: Promoting trade, investment, and economic growth through regional agreements like SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area).
  • Social Development: Cooperation in education, health, poverty reduction, and gender equality.
  • Environmental Protection: Addressing environmental issues such as climate change, deforestation, and natural disasters.
  • Cultural Exchange: Promoting regional cultural understanding and exchange.
  • Agriculture and Rural Development: Improving agriculture productivity and rural development across the region.

iv. SAARC's Major Achievements

  • SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area): Established in 2006 to reduce tariff and non-tariff barriers to trade among member countries.
  • SAARC Development Fund (SDF): Provides financial support for development projects in the region, particularly in areas like education, infrastructure, and poverty alleviation.
  • Regional Centers: SAARC has established various regional centers focused on specific development issues, including the SAARC Forestry Center, SAARC Energy Center, and SAARC Agricultural Center.
  • Health Cooperation: Promoting collaborative efforts in health and healthcare services, including efforts to combat regional diseases such as tuberculosis and HIV/AIDS.

v. SAARC Summits

  • Annual Summits: Heads of state or government from all member countries meet regularly at the SAARC summit. The first summit was held in 1985 in Dhaka, Bangladesh.
  • Agenda: Discuss key regional issues, enhance cooperation, and review the progress of previous initiatives.

vi. Challenges and Criticisms

  • Political Tensions: Political disputes, especially between India and Pakistan, often hinder the functioning and effectiveness of SAARC.
  • Limited Economic Integration: The economic cooperation within SAARC has not reached its full potential due to political differences and protectionist policies.
  • Unequal Development: Economic disparities among member countries pose challenges in implementing cooperative programs and projects.
  • Intra-regional Trade: Despite agreements like SAFTA, intra-regional trade remains low compared to other regional groupings like ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations).

vii. SAARC and Other Regional Organizations

  • SAARC vs. ASEAN: SAARC’s level of integration and cooperation is lower than that of ASEAN, which is often cited as a model of regional cooperation.
  • SAARC and BIMSTEC: The Bay of Bengal Initiative for Multi-Sectoral Technical and Economic Cooperation (BIMSTEC) is another regional organization in South Asia and Southeast Asia that overlaps with SAARC’s goals but has a broader geographic scope.

viii. Recent Developments

  • Bilateral Tensions: SAARC’s functioning has often been hindered by ongoing bilateral tensions, particularly between India and Pakistan, with some members questioning the organization's effectiveness.
  • Regional Connectivity: Efforts have been made to enhance regional connectivity through transport, trade corridors, and energy cooperation, although progress has been slow.
  • China's Influence: China's increasing involvement in South Asia, through initiatives like the Belt and Road Initiative, has raised concerns regarding SAARC’s ability to maintain its relevance.

ix. Future of SAARC

  • Enhancing Connectivity: Increasing economic, trade, and transport connectivity remains a priority for strengthening regional ties.
  • Inclusive Growth: Focus on reducing regional disparities in terms of development, health, and education.
  • Resolving Political Differences: Overcoming political challenges, particularly between India and Pakistan, is crucial for SAARC to realize its full potential.

2. BRICS

i. Overview

BRICS is a group of five major emerging economies: Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa. Initially called BRIC, South Africa joined in 2010, adding the "S" to form BRICS.

Purpose: To promote economic cooperation, enhance trade relations, and contribute to the global governance system.

ii. Formation of BRICS

BRIC was first coined by economist Jim O'Neill in 2001, referring to Brazil, Russia, India, and China as rapidly developing economies. South Africa was added in 2010, reflecting the growing importance of African economies.

The first summit was held in 2009 in Yekaterinburg, Russia.

iii. Objectives of BRICS

  • Economic Growth and Development: Strengthen the economies of the member countries and improve the global economic system.
  • Trade and Investment Cooperation: Increase mutual trade and investment among member states and enhance their global trade standing.
  • Reform Global Governance: Promote reforms in international institutions like the United Nations (UN), International Monetary Fund (IMF), and World Bank to better represent the interests of emerging economies.
  • Sustainable Development: Encourage economic development that balances environmental and social aspects.

iv. Key Areas of Cooperation

A. Economic and Financial Cooperation

  • New Development Bank (NDB): Established in 2014, the NDB provides funding for infrastructure and development projects in BRICS and other emerging economies.
  • Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA): A financial arrangement created in 2014 to provide emergency funding to member countries facing economic crises.

B. Trade and Investment

  • Promoting Trade: Members aim to reduce trade barriers, improve intra-BRICS trade, and encourage economic cooperation.
  • Investment Cooperation: Promoting cross-border investments and facilitating mutual economic partnerships.

C. Political and Security Cooperation

  • Global Governance Reforms: Advocating for reforms in the UN Security Council, IMF, and World Bank to ensure more representation for emerging economies.
  • Political Dialogue: Regular summits and meetings to address global issues, including peace, security, and sustainable development.

D. Sustainable Development

  • Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs): BRICS members are committed to achieving the UN SDGs and promoting sustainable practices in development projects.

v. BRICS Summits

  • Annual Summits: Leaders of BRICS countries meet annually to discuss economic, political, and social issues. The first summit was in 2009 in Russia.
  • Key Themes: Economic cooperation, global governance reforms, trade facilitation, and sustainable development.
  • Host Rotation: The host country of the summit rotates annually among the member countries.

vi. Key Achievements

  • New Development Bank (NDB): The NDB has provided financial support to several large-scale projects, including infrastructure development in member countries.
  • Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA): This financial safety net helps member countries facing financial instability.
  • Increased Trade: BRICS countries have significantly increased intra-group trade and investments.

vii. Challenges and Criticisms

  • Diverse Economies: BRICS consists of countries with different political systems, economic structures, and stages of development, which can lead to differences in priorities.
  • Geopolitical Tensions: Differences between countries like India and China in areas like border disputes can affect cooperation.
  • Unequal Growth: While some members like China and India have seen rapid economic growth, others like Brazil and South Africa face more economic challenges, leading to uneven development within the group.
  • Global Influence: Despite efforts for global reforms, BRICS has not been able to challenge established global institutions like the World Bank and IMF as effectively as planned.

viii. Role of BRICS in Global Governance

  • Reforming Institutions: BRICS advocates for more inclusive global governance, with a stronger voice for emerging economies in institutions like the UN, IMF, and World Bank.
  • Global Trade: As a significant bloc, BRICS countries have been pushing for more equitable trade policies, particularly in the World Trade Organization (WTO).
  • Climate Change: BRICS has committed to addressing climate change through sustainable practices and promoting renewable energy sources.

ix. Future Prospects

  • Expansion: There is potential for expanding the group to include other emerging economies, although this may take time due to the diverse interests of current members.
  • Strengthening the NDB: BRICS may work towards making the NDB a more prominent institution to challenge Western-dominated financial institutions.
  • Deeper Cooperation: There are opportunities for deeper collaboration in areas like digital economy, green energy, and technology innovation.

3. United Nations (UN)

i. Overview

Founded: 24 October 1945, after World War II, to prevent future global conflicts and promote peace and cooperation.

Headquarters: New York City, USA.

Member Countries: 193 member states (as of 2025), including almost every recognized country in the world.

Official Languages: Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian, and Spanish.

ii. Objectives of the UN

  • Promote Peace and Security: Maintain international peace and prevent conflicts.
  • Foster International Cooperation: Facilitate cooperation in areas like health, education, environment, and human rights.
  • Promote Sustainable Development: Achieve the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) to address global challenges such as poverty, inequality, and climate change.
  • Uphold Human Rights: Protect and promote human rights across the globe.
  • Provide Humanitarian Aid: Provide assistance during crises like wars, natural disasters, and famines.

iii. Key Organs of the UN

  • General Assembly: Composed of all 193 member states, each with one vote. Discusses international issues and makes recommendations but has no binding powers. Meets once a year for a regular session.
  • Security Council: Responsible for maintaining international peace and security. Composed of 15 members: 5 permanent (China, France, Russia, UK, USA) with veto power, and 10 rotating members elected for 2-year terms. Can take actions such as imposing sanctions or authorizing military interventions.
  • International Court of Justice (ICJ): Judicial arm of the UN. Resolves legal disputes between states and provides advisory opinions on international legal questions. Located in The Hague, Netherlands.
  • Secretariat: Led by the Secretary-General, who is the chief administrative officer. Provides studies, information, and facilities needed by the UN bodies. Manages peacekeeping operations, humanitarian work, and special political missions.
  • Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC): Coordinates the economic and social work of the UN and its specialized agencies. Oversees development programs, human rights work, and policy formulation.
  • Trusteeship Council: Created to ensure that territories placed under UN trusteeship were governed responsibly and prepared for self-government. Currently inactive, as all Trust Territories have attained self-government.

iv. Specialized Agencies of the UN

  • World Health Organization (WHO): Promotes global health and coordinates health-related issues.
  • United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO): Works to promote peace, education, and cultural exchange.
  • International Labour Organization (ILO): Focuses on improving working conditions and labor rights globally.
  • United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF): Provides humanitarian aid to children around the world.
  • Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO): Aims to eradicate hunger and improve food security worldwide.

v. UN Peacekeeping

  • Peacekeeping Missions: The UN deploys peacekeeping forces to conflict areas to maintain peace, monitor ceasefires, and assist in post-conflict recovery.
  • Blue Helmets: Peacekeepers are often recognized by their blue helmets or berets.
  • Key Missions: UN Peacekeeping Operations have been active in regions like the Middle East, Africa, and the Balkans.

vi. UN and Human Rights

  • Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR): Adopted in 1948, it outlines fundamental human rights that should be protected globally.
  • Human Rights Council (UNHRC): A UN body responsible for promoting and protecting human rights worldwide.
  • High Commissioner for Human Rights: A senior UN official responsible for overseeing human rights activities.

vii. Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

In 2015, the UN adopted 17 SDGs with a target to achieve them by 2030. These include:

  • No Poverty
  • Zero Hunger
  • Good Health and Well-being
  • Quality Education
  • Gender Equality
  • Affordable and Clean Energy
  • Climate Action
  • Life Below Water and Land

The SDGs aim to address the root causes of poverty and inequality and improve the lives of people worldwide.

viii. Funding and Budget

The UN's budget is funded through contributions from its member states. The regular budget is used for the day-to-day running of the UN. The peacekeeping budget is specifically for operations and missions. The contributions are based on the Gross National Income (GNI) of each member country.

ix. Challenges and Criticisms

  • Veto Power in the Security Council: The five permanent members of the Security Council (P5) have veto power, which often leads to gridlocks in decision-making.
  • Inequality in Representation: The UN system has been criticized for not adequately representing the changing global power dynamics, especially in the Security Council.
  • Effectiveness: The UN has often been criticized for its failure to prevent or resolve major conflicts, such as the Rwandan Genocide, the Syrian Civil War, and the ongoing conflicts in Yemen and Ukraine.
  • Political Tensions: The political interests of member states sometimes hinder the UN’s ability to reach consensus or take decisive action.

4. Major Partnerships

i. Bilateral Partnerships

Bilateral partnerships are agreements or relationships between two countries or entities that focus on specific areas of cooperation, such as trade, defense, or development.

  • India-US Partnership: Focuses on defense cooperation, trade relations, technology exchange, and counterterrorism efforts.
  • China-Russia Partnership: A strategic alliance that emphasizes economic collaboration, energy cooperation, and political support on the international stage.
  • USA-Canada Partnership: Centers on trade (USMCA), border security, and energy cooperation.

ii. Regional Partnerships

Regional partnerships are agreements or organizations formed by countries within a specific geographical region to promote political, economic, and social cooperation.

  • European Union (EU): A political and economic union of 27 European countries aiming to ensure regional peace, economic growth, and political integration.
  • ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations): Composed of 10 Southeast Asian countries, this partnership focuses on political, economic, security, and cultural cooperation.
  • South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (SAARC): A regional organization of South Asian nations aiming to promote welfare, economic development, and peace.

iii. Multilateral Partnerships

Multilateral partnerships involve three or more countries working together to address global challenges such as climate change, poverty, and conflicts.

  • BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa): A group of five major emerging economies working together on issues like economic development, global governance reforms, and trade relations.
  • G7/G20: Groups of the world's largest economies that discuss and address global economic issues, international trade, security, and climate change.
  • United Nations (UN): An international organization with 193 member states, focusing on maintaining global peace, promoting sustainable development, and addressing humanitarian crises.

iv. Trade Partnerships

These partnerships focus on trade agreements and economic cooperation, aiming to boost economic growth, reduce tariffs, and enhance regional or global trade relations.

  • Trans-Pacific Partnership (TPP): A trade agreement involving countries from the Asia-Pacific region, designed to promote trade, investment, and regulatory cooperation.
  • World Trade Organization (WTO): A global organization that deals with the rules of trade between nations and helps negotiate trade agreements to facilitate international trade.
  • North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA): A trade agreement between the United States, Canada, and Mexico (now replaced by the USMCA) to facilitate economic cooperation and trade.

v. Defense and Security Partnerships

These partnerships focus on military cooperation, defense alliances, and shared efforts to ensure regional or global security.

  • NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization): A military alliance of 30 countries, primarily from North America and Europe, formed to ensure collective defense and security.
  • US-Japan Security Alliance: A bilateral defense agreement focused on mutual defense, peacekeeping, and cooperation in regional security.
  • Quad (Quadrilateral Security Dialogue): A strategic dialogue between the United States, India, Japan, and Australia, focusing on maintaining a free and open Indo-Pacific region.

vi. Environmental Partnerships

These partnerships aim to address global environmental issues, such as climate change, deforestation, and pollution.

  • Paris Agreement: A global climate accord within the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), aimed at limiting global warming to below 2°C.
  • C40 Cities: A global network of cities committed to tackling climate change through sustainable urban development.
  • International Solar Alliance (ISA): A coalition of countries working to promote solar energy and reduce dependence on fossil fuels.

vii. Development and Humanitarian Partnerships

These partnerships focus on addressing global poverty, inequality, and humanitarian crises through collaborative efforts between countries, organizations, and non-governmental actors.

  • United Nations Development Programme (UNDP): Works with countries to reduce poverty, promote sustainable development, and support governance reforms.
  • Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance: A global health partnership aimed at increasing access to immunization in low-income countries.
  • World Bank and IMF (International Monetary Fund): Provide loans and financial aid to developing countries for infrastructure projects, economic reforms, and poverty alleviation.

viii. Technological and Innovation Partnerships

These partnerships focus on sharing technology, research, and innovation to address challenges in sectors like health, education, and energy.

  • Global Innovation Index: A partnership of governments, universities, and private sectors working together to promote innovation and improve global competitiveness.
  • 5G Partnerships: Collaborations between tech companies and governments to roll out 5G networks, which aim to transform communications and digital economies worldwide.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) Partnerships: Collaborations between countries and companies focused on the development and ethical deployment of AI technologies.

ix. Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs)

These partnerships involve cooperation between governments and private companies to deliver public services and infrastructure.

  • Infrastructure Projects: Governments and private firms partner to develop and maintain critical infrastructure like highways, airports, and power plants.
  • Healthcare Partnerships: Governments collaborate with pharmaceutical companies and health organizations to provide healthcare services, vaccines, and treatments globally.
