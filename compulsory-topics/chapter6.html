Chapter 6 - Environment & Ecology

1. Ecology & Biodiversity

i. Ecology - Definition

Ecology is the study of interactions between organisms and their environment, including both living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) components.

Ecosystem: A system comprising living organisms interacting with each other and their physical environment (air, water, soil).

Levels of Ecological Organization:

  • Individual: A single organism.
  • Population: Group of individuals of the same species.
  • Community: Groups of different species living in a given area.
  • Ecosystem: Interaction of biotic and abiotic components in a particular area.
  • Biosphere: The entire zone of life on Earth, including land, water, and atmosphere.

ii. Components of Ecosystem

A. Biotic Components (Living Organisms)

  • Producers (Autotrophs): Organisms that produce their own food through photosynthesis (e.g., plants, algae).
  • Consumers (Heterotrophs): Organisms that depend on others for food.
    • Primary Consumers: Herbivores that eat producers.
    • Secondary Consumers: Carnivores that eat herbivores.
    • Tertiary Consumers: Apex predators at the top of the food chain.
  • Decomposers: Organisms that break down dead organic matter (e.g., fungi, bacteria).

B. Abiotic Components (Non-living Elements)

  • Climate: Temperature, rainfall, sunlight, etc., that affect living organisms.
  • Soil: Provides nutrients for plants and affects the growth of species.
  • Water: Essential for life; influences species distribution.
  • Air: Provides oxygen and carbon dioxide for respiration and photosynthesis.

iii. Energy Flow in Ecosystem

Food Chain: The sequence of energy flow from producers to consumers. (Example: Sun → Plant → Herbivore → Carnivore)

Food Web: A more complex network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem.

Trophic Levels: The different levels in a food chain/web, from primary producers (level 1) to apex predators (level 4 or higher).

Energy Transfer: Energy decreases as you move up trophic levels, with only 10% of energy being passed from one level to the next (the 10% rule).

iv. Biodiversity - Definition

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life forms, including species diversity, genetic diversity, and ecosystem diversity.

  • Species Diversity: The variety of species in an ecosystem.
  • Genetic Diversity: The variation of genes within species.
  • Ecosystem Diversity: The variety of ecosystems in a region or on Earth.

v. Importance of Biodiversity

  • Ecological Balance: Biodiversity maintains ecosystem functions such as nutrient cycling, pollination, water purification, and climate regulation.
  • Economic Value: Biodiversity supports industries like agriculture, forestry, pharmaceuticals, and tourism.
  • Cultural Importance: Many communities depend on biodiversity for food, shelter, and traditional practices.
  • Medicinal Value: Many modern medicines are derived from natural sources, such as plants and microorganisms.

vi. Threats to Biodiversity

  • Habitat Destruction: Deforestation, urbanization, and agriculture reduce habitats for many species.
  • Pollution: Air, water, and soil pollution harm ecosystems and species.
  • Climate Change: Alters habitats and species distributions, making survival difficult for many organisms.
  • Overexploitation: Overhunting, fishing, and logging threaten species with extinction.
  • Invasive Species: Non-native species that outcompete native species, disrupting ecosystems.

vii. Conservation of Biodiversity

A. In-situ Conservation (On-site conservation)

  • Protected Areas: National parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves that protect ecosystems and species in their natural habitat.
  • Wildlife Corridors: Connecting fragmented habitats to allow species migration and gene flow.
  • Community Conservation: Involving local communities in the protection of biodiversity and sustainable resource use.

B. Ex-situ Conservation (Off-site conservation)

  • Zoos and Aquariums: Breeding and maintaining endangered species in controlled environments.
  • Gene Banks: Storing genetic material (seeds, sperm, eggs) for future restoration.
  • Botanical Gardens: Preserving plant species for research and education.

C. International Conservation Efforts

  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD): An international treaty aimed at conserving biodiversity and ensuring sustainable use of biological resources.
  • CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species): Regulates the international trade of threatened species.
  • WWF (World Wide Fund for Nature): Works to protect the planet's biodiversity through conservation projects and advocacy.

viii. Ecosystem Services

Ecosystem services are the benefits that humans receive from ecosystems:

  • Provisioning Services: Products obtained from ecosystems (e.g., food, water, raw materials).
  • Regulating Services: Benefits from ecosystem processes that regulate climate, disease, water quality, etc.
  • Cultural Services: Non-material benefits like recreation, spiritual enrichment, and aesthetic enjoyment.
  • Supporting Services: Services that support life on Earth, such as nutrient cycling and soil formation.

ix. Major Biomes

  • Tropical Rainforests: High biodiversity, hot and humid climate, and dense vegetation.
  • Deserts: Low biodiversity, extreme temperatures, and low precipitation.
  • Tundra: Cold, dry regions with minimal vegetation and a short growing season.
  • Grasslands: Open, treeless areas with grasses and few trees, found in regions with moderate rainfall.
  • Temperate Forests: Moderate climate, deciduous trees, and seasonal changes.

x. Ecological Footprint

Ecological Footprint refers to the human demand on natural resources and ecosystems. A larger footprint indicates greater resource consumption and environmental degradation.

Sustainability: The goal is to reduce the ecological footprint and ensure that natural resources are available for future generations.

2. Climate Change & Sustainability

i. Climate Change - Definition

Climate Change refers to long-term changes in temperature, precipitation, and other atmospheric patterns over extended periods. It is driven by both natural processes (like volcanic eruptions, solar radiation) and human activities, particularly the release of greenhouse gases (GHGs) like carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O).

ii. Causes of Climate Change

A. Natural Causes

  • Volcanic Eruptions: Release of ash and gases like sulfur dioxide into the atmosphere can cool the planet temporarily.
  • Solar Variations: Changes in the Earth's distance from the sun or solar radiation can influence climate patterns.
  • Ocean Currents: Changes in ocean currents can affect global temperatures and weather patterns.

B. Human-Induced Causes

  • Greenhouse Gas Emissions: The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) for energy, transportation, and industry releases large amounts of CO₂ into the atmosphere.
  • Deforestation: Trees absorb CO₂, so cutting them down reduces the Earth's ability to mitigate climate change.
  • Agriculture: Livestock farming produces methane (CH₄), and rice cultivation releases nitrous oxide (N₂O), both potent GHGs.
  • Industrial Activities: Manufacturing processes and chemical industries emit various GHGs, including chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).

iii. Impact of Climate Change

A. Environmental Impact

  • Rising Global Temperatures: Average temperatures are increasing, leading to more frequent and intense heatwaves.
  • Melting Polar Ice: Polar ice caps and glaciers are melting, contributing to rising sea levels.
  • Rising Sea Levels: Coastal areas are at risk of flooding, affecting millions of people globally.
  • Extreme Weather Events: Increased frequency of hurricanes, floods, droughts, and wildfires.
  • Ocean Acidification: Increased CO₂ in the atmosphere is absorbed by oceans, making them more acidic and harming marine life, particularly coral reefs.

B. Economic Impact

  • Agricultural Losses: Extreme weather, droughts, and floods affect food production, leading to crop failures and price hikes.
  • Damage to Infrastructure: Rising sea levels, flooding, and extreme weather damage buildings, roads, and other infrastructure, leading to significant economic losses.
  • Energy Demand: Warmer temperatures increase energy demand for cooling (air conditioning), straining energy systems.

C. Social Impact

  • Health Risks: Higher temperatures and air pollution exacerbate respiratory diseases and heatstroke.
  • Migration and Displacement: Rising sea levels and extreme weather events force people to leave their homes (climate refugees).
  • Water Scarcity: Changes in precipitation patterns lead to water shortages, affecting billions globally.

iv. Mitigation of Climate Change

A. Reducing Greenhouse Gas Emissions

  • Renewable Energy: Transitioning to solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal energy sources instead of fossil fuels.
  • Energy Efficiency: Improving energy use in buildings, transportation, and industries to reduce energy consumption.
  • Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS): Technologies that capture CO₂ emissions from power plants and industrial processes and store them underground.
  • Sustainable Transportation: Promoting electric vehicles (EVs), public transportation, and cycling over fossil fuel-powered vehicles.

B. Protecting and Restoring Ecosystems

  • Afforestation and Reforestation: Planting trees and restoring forests to absorb CO₂ and maintain biodiversity.
  • Soil Conservation: Preventing soil erosion and enhancing carbon sequestration through sustainable farming practices.
  • Wetland Restoration: Wetlands act as carbon sinks, and restoring them helps mitigate climate change.

v. Adaptation to Climate Change

A. Infrastructure Adaptation

  • Flood Defenses: Building sea walls, levees, and other barriers to protect coastal cities from rising sea levels.
  • Resilient Agriculture: Developing drought-resistant crops and improving irrigation methods to withstand changing weather patterns.
  • Water Conservation: Implementing technologies and policies to manage water resources efficiently and ensure clean water supply.

B. Community Adaptation

  • Climate-Resilient Housing: Building homes with materials that can withstand extreme weather, such as heatwaves or floods.
  • Early Warning Systems: Implementing disaster alert systems to warn communities about upcoming storms, floods, or heatwaves.
  • Public Awareness: Educating the public on climate change, sustainable practices, and emergency preparedness.

vi. Sustainability - Definition

Sustainability refers to meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. It involves the three pillars of sustainability:

  • Environmental Sustainability: Protecting and conserving natural resources, maintaining biodiversity, and reducing pollution.
  • Social Sustainability: Ensuring social equity, promoting fair access to resources, and improving quality of life for all communities.
  • Economic Sustainability: Promoting economic growth that is inclusive, resilient, and reduces inequalities.

vii. Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

The United Nations (UN) has outlined 17 SDGs to achieve by 2030 to promote sustainability, which include:

  • Affordable and Clean Energy (SDG 7)
  • Climate Action (SDG 13)
  • Life Below Water (SDG 14)
  • Life on Land (SDG 15)
  • Responsible Consumption and Production (SDG 12)
  • No Poverty (SDG 1), Quality Education (SDG 4), and Reduced Inequalities (SDG 10)

viii. Role of Individuals in Climate Change and Sustainability

  • Reduce, Reuse, Recycle: Reducing waste and reusing products to lower carbon footprints.
  • Sustainable Eating: Choosing plant-based diets and reducing food waste to reduce emissions from food production.
  • Conserving Energy: Using energy-efficient appliances, turning off unused electronics, and adopting renewable energy.
  • Sustainable Transportation: Using public transportation, walking, cycling, or driving electric vehicles to reduce emissions.

ix. Global Agreements on Climate Change

  • Paris Agreement (2015): An international treaty aiming to limit global warming to below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to limit it to 1.5°C.
  • Kyoto Protocol: An earlier treaty (1997) aimed at reducing GHG emissions, though its effectiveness has been debated.
