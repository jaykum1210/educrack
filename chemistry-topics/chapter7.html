1. Concentration Units

Introduction

  • Concentration is the measure of the amount of solute present in a given amount of solvent or solution.
  • Different units of concentration are used depending on the application (chemistry, biology, industry, etc.).

Mass-Based Concentration Units

Mass Percent (% w/w)

  • Formula: Mass % = Mass of solute Mass of solution × 100
  • Example: A 5% NaCl solution means 5g of NaCl is dissolved in 100g of solution.

Parts per Million (ppm) & Parts per Billion (ppb)

  • Used for very dilute solutions, especially in environmental chemistry.
  • Formula for ppm: ppm = Mass of solute Mass of solution × 10 6
  • Formula for ppb: ppb = Mass of solute Mass of solution × 10 9

Volume-Based Concentration Units

Volume Percent (% v/v)

  • Formula: Volume % = Volume of solute Volume of solution × 100
  • Example: 70% ethanol solution means 70 mL of ethanol in 100 mL of solution.

Mole Fraction (X)

  • Formula: X A = Moles of component A Total moles of all components
  • No units (dimensionless).

Molarity & Molality

Molarity (M)

  • Formula: M = Moles of solute Volume of solution (L)

Molality (m)

  • Formula: m = Moles of solute Mass of solvent (kg)

2. Colligative Properties

Introduction

Colligative properties are physical properties of solutions that depend only on the number of solute particles, not on their identity.

Key Colligative Properties:

  • Relative Lowering of Vapor Pressure
  • Boiling Point Elevation
  • Freezing Point Depression
  • Osmotic Pressure

Relative Lowering of Vapor Pressure

When a non-volatile solute is added to a solvent, the vapor pressure of the solution decreases.

Raoult’s Law:

P solution = X solvent P solvent ^o

Where:

  • P solution = Vapor pressure of solution
  • P solvent ^o = Vapor pressure of pure solvent
  • X solvent = Mole fraction of solvent

Boiling Point Elevation

Adding a solute increases the boiling point of a solvent.

ΔT b = i K b m

Where:

  • ΔT b = Boiling point elevation
  • i = Van’t Hoff factor
  • K b = Boiling point elevation constant
  • m = Molality of solution

Freezing Point Depression

Adding a solute lowers the freezing point of a solvent.

ΔT f = i K f m

Where:

  • ΔT f = Freezing point depression
  • K f = Freezing point depression constant
  • i = Van’t Hoff factor
  • m = Molality of solution

Osmotic Pressure

Osmotic pressure is the pressure required to stop the flow of solvent through a semi-permeable membrane.

π = i M R T

Where:

  • π = Osmotic pressure
  • M = Molarity of solution
  • R = Gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/mol·K)
  • T = Temperature in Kelvin
  • i = Van’t Hoff factor

Van’t Hoff Factor

The Van’t Hoff factor accounts for ionization of solutes.

  • For non-electrolytes: i = 1 (e.g., glucose, urea).
  • For ionic compounds:
    • NaCl → i = 2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
    • CaCl₂ → i = 3 (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻)
