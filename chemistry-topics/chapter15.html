Chapter 15 - Hydrocarbons

1. Alkanes, Alkenes, and Alkynes

Definition

  • Alkanes, alkenes, and alkynes are hydrocarbons, meaning they contain only carbon (C) and hydrogen (H) atoms. They differ based on the types of bonds between carbon atoms.

i. Alkanes (Saturated Hydrocarbons)

  • General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
  • Only single bonds (C–C) → Saturated hydrocarbons
  • Hybridization: sp³ (tetrahedral geometry, 109.5° bond angle)
  • Less reactive due to strong C–C and C–H bonds

Examples of Alkanes

Name Formula Structure
Methane CH₄ CH₄
Ethane C₂H₆ CH₃-CH₃
Propane C₃H₈ CH₃-CH₂-CH₃
Butane C₄H₁₀ CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃

Properties of Alkanes

  • Non-polar, insoluble in water
  • Low boiling points (increase with chain length)
  • Undergo combustion and substitution reactions

Reactions of Alkanes

  • Combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + Energy
  • Halogenation (Substitution reaction): CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (In presence of UV light)

ii. Alkenes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons - Double Bond)

  • General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ
  • Contains at least one C=C double bond → Unsaturated hydrocarbons
  • Hybridization: sp² (trigonal planar geometry, 120° bond angle)
  • More reactive than alkanes due to the presence of π (pi) bonds

Examples of Alkenes

Name Formula Structure
Ethene C₂H₄ CH₂=CH₂
Propene C₃H₆ CH₃-CH=CH₂
Butene C₄H₈ CH₃-CH=CH-CH₃

Properties of Alkenes

  • Non-polar, insoluble in water
  • Boiling points increase with molecular weight
  • More reactive than alkanes due to π bond

Reactions of Alkenes

  • Addition Reactions:
    • Hydrogenation: CH₂=CH₂ + H₂ → CH₃-CH₃ (Ethane)
    • Halogenation: CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ → CH₂Br-CH₂Br
    • Hydration (Addition of H₂O): CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → CH₃-CH₂OH (Ethanol)
  • Combustion: C₂H₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 2H₂O

iii. Alkynes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons - Triple Bond)

  • General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
  • Contains at least one C≡C triple bond → Unsaturated hydrocarbons
  • Hybridization: sp (linear geometry, 180° bond angle)
  • Most reactive due to two π bonds

Examples of Alkynes

Name Formula Structure
Ethyne (Acetylene) C₂H₂ HC≡CH
Propyne C₃H₄ CH₃-C≡CH
Butyne C₄H₆ CH₃-C≡C-CH₃

Properties of Alkynes

  • More reactive than alkanes & alkenes
  • Insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents
  • Burns with a smoky flame due to high carbon content

Reactions of Alkynes

  • Addition Reactions:
    • Hydrogenation: HC≡CH + 2H₂ → CH₃-CH₃
    • Halogenation: HC≡CH + Br₂ → CHBr=CHBr → CHBr₂-CHBr₂
    • Hydration: HC≡CH + H₂O → CH₃CHO (Acetaldehyde)
  • Combustion: 2C₂H₂ + 5O₂ → 4CO₂ + 2H₂O

Key Differences Between Alkanes, Alkenes, and Alkynes

Property Alkanes Alkenes Alkynes
General Formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ CₙH₂ₙ CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
Bond Type Single (C–C) Double (C=C) Triple (C≡C)
Saturation Saturated Unsaturated Unsaturated
Hybridization sp³ sp² sp
Bond Angle 109.5° 120° 180°
Reactivity Least reactive More reactive Most reactive
Reaction Type Substitution Addition Addition
Example Methane (CH₄) Ethene (C₂H₄) Ethyne (C₂H₂)

3. Aromatic Compounds

Definition

  • Aromatic compounds are cyclic, planar molecules with a conjugated π-electron system that follows Hückel’s Rule (4n + 2 π electrons). These compounds exhibit high stability due to resonance.

Examples:

  • Benzene (C₆H₆)
  • Toluene (C₆H₅CH₃)
  • Naphthalene (C₁₀H₈)
  • Phenol (C₆H₅OH)

Hückel’s Rule (4n + 2 Rule)

  • A compound is aromatic if it:
    • Is cyclic (ring structure).
    • Is planar (all atoms in the same plane).
    • Has conjugated π-electrons (alternating single and double bonds).
    • Follows the (4n + 2) rule, where n = 0, 1, 2, 3…
  • Example: Benzene (C₆H₆) has 6 π-electrons (n = 1, 4(1) + 2 = 6), so it is aromatic.

Types of Aromatic Compounds

Benzenoid Aromatic Compounds

  • Contain a benzene ring.
  • Examples: Benzene, Toluene, Phenol, Naphthalene.

Non-Benzenoid Aromatic Compounds

  • Do not contain a benzene ring but still follow Hückel’s Rule.
  • Examples: Cyclopropenyl cation (C₃H₃⁺), Tropolone.

Heterocyclic Aromatic Compounds

  • Contain at least one heteroatom (N, O, S) in the ring.
  • Examples:
    • Pyridine (C₅H₅N) → Contains Nitrogen (N).
    • Furan (C₄H₄O) → Contains Oxygen (O).
    • Thiophene (C₄H₄S) → Contains Sulfur (S).

Properties of Aromatic Compounds

  • Highly stable due to resonance.
  • Undergo substitution reactions instead of addition reactions.
  • Non-polar or slightly polar, insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents.

Reactions of Aromatic Compounds

Electrophilic Substitution Reactions

  • Benzene reacts with electrophiles (E⁺) replacing a hydrogen atom.
  • Nitration: HNO₃ + H₂SO₄ → Nitrobenzene
    • C₆H₆ + HNO₃ → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂O
  • Halogenation: Br₂ + FeBr₃ → Bromobenzene
    • C₆H₆ + Br₂ → C₆H₅Br + HBr
  • Friedel-Crafts Alkylation (Addition of R-group):
    • C₆H₆ + RCl → C₆H₅R + HCl (Catalyst: AlCl₃)
  • Friedel-Crafts Acylation (Addition of CO-R group):
    • C₆H₆ + RCOCl → C₆H₅COR + HCl
  • Sulfonation: SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → Benzene Sulfonic Acid
    • C₆H₆ + SO₃ → C₆H₅SO₃H

Oxidation Reactions

  • Alkyl groups attached to benzene undergo oxidation.
  • Toluene to Benzoic Acid:
    • C₆H₅CH₃ + [O] → C₆H₅COOH (Using KMnO₄)

Difference Between Aromatic & Aliphatic Compounds

Property Aromatic Compounds Aliphatic Compounds
Structure Cyclic, conjugated π-system Open-chain or non-conjugated rings
Stability Highly stable due to resonance Less stable
Reactions Substitution reactions Addition reactions
Examples Benzene, Phenol, Toluene Methane, Ethene, Butane
