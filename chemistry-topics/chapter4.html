Chapter 4 - Chemical Kinametics

1. Rate Laws

Introduction

  • The Rate Law expresses how the rate of a chemical reaction depends on the concentration of reactants.
  • It helps in determining the reaction mechanism and predicting reaction behavior.

General Form of Rate Law

  • For a reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD
  • The rate law is given by:

    Rate = k[A]m[B]n

  • Where:
    • k = Rate constant (depends on temperature).
    • [A], [B] = Concentrations of reactants.
    • m, n = Order of reaction with respect to A and B (determined experimentally).
    • Overall Order = m + n.

Types of Rate Laws

  1. Differential Rate Law

    • Shows how rate depends on reactant concentrations.
    • Example: For a first-order reaction → Rate = k[A]

  2. Integrated Rate Law

    • Relates concentration to time.
    • Useful for determining half-life and predicting concentration changes over time.

Reaction Order & Rate Laws

Order Rate Law Half-Life Formula
Zero Order Rate = k t1/2 = [A]0 / 2k
First Order Rate = k[A] t1/2 = 0.693 / k
Second Order Rate = k[A]2 t1/2 = 1 / k[A]0

Half-Life (t1/2)

  • Half-life is the time taken for a reactant's concentration to reduce to half its initial value.
  • First-order reactions have a constant half-life.
  • Zero and second-order reactions have variable half-life depending on initial concentration.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

  • Concentration: Higher concentration → Faster reaction.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature → Increases rate due to more collisions.
  • Catalyst: Lowers activation energy → Speeds up reaction.
  • Surface Area: More surface area → Faster reaction.

2. Reaction Mechanisms

Introduction

  • A reaction mechanism is the step-by-step sequence of elementary reactions by which an overall chemical reaction occurs.
  • It describes how reactants are converted into products, including the formation of intermediates and the breaking/forming of bonds.

Elementary Reactions & Molecularity

Molecularity Definition Example
Unimolecular Involves a single reactant molecule A → B
Bimolecular Involves two reactant molecules A + B → C
Termolecular Involves three reactant molecules A + B + C → D (Rare)

Reaction Intermediates & Transition States

  • Intermediates: Species that are formed in one step and consumed in another.
  • Transition State: High-energy, unstable configuration of molecules during bond breaking/forming.

Example Mechanism:

For the reaction: 2NO2 → 2NO + O2

Possible mechanism:

  • NO2 + NO2 → NO3 + NO (Slow, Rate-Determining Step)
  • NO3 → NO + O2 (Fast)

NO3 is an intermediate.

Rate-Determining Step (RDS)

  • The slowest step in a reaction mechanism.
  • Overall reaction rate depends on this step.
  • Rate law is determined by the reactants in the RDS.

Example:

For: A + B → C + D

Mechanism:

  • A + B → AB* (Slow, RDS)
  • AB* → C + D (Fast)

Since step 1 is slow, rate law depends only on A & B:

Rate = k[A][B]

Catalysts & Reaction Mechanisms

  • Catalysts provide an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.
  • They are not consumed in the reaction.

Example:

Enzymes in biological reactions.

3. Factors Influencing Reaction Rate

Introduction

  • The rate of a chemical reaction refers to how fast reactants are converted into products.
  • Several factors influence this rate by affecting the frequency and energy of molecular collisions.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

  1. Concentration of Reactants

    • Higher concentration → Faster reaction (more collisions).
    • Example: A concentrated acid reacts faster than a dilute acid with the same reactant.
    • Rate Law Relationship: Rate = k [A] m [B] n

  2. Temperature

    • Higher temperature → Faster reaction (molecules have more energy).
    • More molecules exceed the activation energy (Eₐ) required for reaction.
    • Follows the Arrhenius equation: k = A e - E a R T
    • Example: Food spoils faster at room temperature than in the refrigerator.

  3. Pressure (for Gaseous Reactions)

    • Higher pressure → Faster reaction (molecules are closer, increasing collisions).
    • Applies mainly to gaseous reactions.
    • Example: In the Haber process, increasing pressure speeds up ammonia ( NH 3 ) formation.

  4. Surface Area of Reactants

    • More surface area → Faster reaction (more reactant particles exposed).
    • Example: Finely powdered sugar burns faster than a sugar cube.

  5. Catalyst

    • Lowers activation energy and provides an alternative reaction pathway.
    • Does not get consumed in the reaction.
    • Example: Enzymes in digestion speed up biological reactions.

  6. Nature of Reactants

    • Ionic reactions → Fast (no bond breaking needed).
    • Covalent reactions → Slow (bond breaking required).
    • Example: NaCl dissolves quickly in water, while sugar takes longer.
