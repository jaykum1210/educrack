Chapter 9 - Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure

1. Ionic, Covalent, and Metallic Bonding

Introduction

Chemical bonding occurs when atoms combine to form molecules or compounds. The three main types of bonds are:

  • Ionic Bonding: Transfer of electrons
  • Covalent Bonding: Sharing of electrons
  • Metallic Bonding: Sea of electrons

Ionic Bonding

Definition: Ionic bonding occurs when electrons are transferred from one atom to another, forming oppositely charged ions that attract each other.

  • Occurs between: Metals & Non-metals
  • Example: NaCl (Sodium chloride)

How It Works:

  • Metal atoms (like Na, Mg) lose electrons, forming cations (+ve ions).
  • Non-metal atoms (like Cl, O) gain electrons, forming anions (–ve ions).
  • The opposite charges attract, forming a strong electrostatic force.

Properties of Ionic Compounds:

  • High melting & boiling points (strong ionic bonds)
  • Soluble in water (polar nature)
  • Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions move freely)
  • Brittle (ions shift under force, breaking structure)

Example Reaction: Na + Cl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻

Covalent
Bonding

Definition: Covalent bonding occurs when atoms share electrons to achieve stability.

  • Occurs between: Non-metal & Non-metal
  • Example: H₂O (Water), CO₂ (Carbon dioxide)

How It Works:

  • Each atom shares valence electrons to complete its octet (8 electrons in outer shell).
  • This forms molecules with strong covalent bonds between atoms.

Types of Covalent Bonds:

  • Single Bond (−): H₂, Cl₂ (1 pair of electrons shared)
  • Double Bond (=): O₂, CO₂ (2 pairs shared)
  • Triple Bond (≡): N₂, C₂H₂ (3 pairs shared)

Properties of Covalent Compounds:

  • Low melting & boiling points (weak intermolecular forces)
  • Poor electrical conductivity (no free electrons/ions)
  • Soluble in non-polar solvents
  • Forms gases, liquids, or soft solids

Example Reaction: H + H → H₂

Metallic Bonding

Definition: Metallic bonding occurs when metal atoms share electrons in a "sea of electrons," allowing free movement of electrons.

  • Occurs in: Metals
  • Example: Cu (Copper), Fe (Iron), Al (Aluminum)

How It Works:

  • Metal atoms lose their valence electrons, forming a lattice of positive ions.
  • The delocalized electrons move freely, creating strong attraction between ions.

Properties of Metallic Compounds:

  • High melting & boiling points (strong bonds)
  • Excellent conductivity (free-moving electrons)
  • Malleable & ductile (layers slide easily)
  • Lustrous (shiny) (electrons reflect light)

Example Structure: Cu + e⁻ (sea of electrons)

2. VSEPR Theory (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion Theory)

Introduction

  • VSEPR Theory helps predict molecular shapes based on electron pair repulsion.
  • Electron pairs (bonding & lone pairs) arrange themselves to minimize repulsion.

Types of Electron Pairs

  • Bonding Pairs (BP): Shared electrons forming bonds.
  • Lone Pairs (LP): Non-bonded electrons on the central atom.
  • Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs, affecting bond angles.

VSEPR Notation (AXₙEₘ)

  • A = Central atom
  • Xₙ = Number of bonding atoms
  • Eₘ = Number of lone pairs

Molecular Geometries & Shapes

AX₂E₃ 2 BP, 3 LP Linear 180° XeF₂

AX₅E 5 BP, 1 LP Square Pyramidal <90° BrF₅
AX₄E₂ 4 BP, 2 LP Square Planar 90° XeF₄

Effects of Lone Pairs on Bond Angles

  • Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs, reducing bond angles.
  • Examples:
    • CH₄ (Tetrahedral, 109.5°)
    • NH₃ (Trigonal Pyramidal, 107°)
    • H₂O (Bent, 104.5°)

3. Hybridization & Molecular Orbitals

Hybridization

Definition:

  • Hybridization is the mixing of atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals of equal energy used for bonding.
  • It leads to stronger, more stable bonds due to better orbital overlap.

Types of Hybridization & Molecular Geometry

Hybridization Orbitals Mixed Geometry Bond Angle Example
sp 1 s + 1 p Linear 180° BeCl2, CO2
sp2 1 s + 2 p Trigonal Planar 120° BF3, C2H4
sp3 1 s + 3 p Tetrahedral 109.5° CH4, NH3, H2O
sp3d 1 s + 3 p + 1 d Trigonal Bipyramidal 90°, 120° PCl5, SF4
sp3d2 1 s + 3 p + 2 d Octahedral 90° SF6, XeF4

Example: sp3 Hybridization in CH4 (Methane)

  • Carbon's ground state: 1s2 2s2 2p2
  • It hybridizes to form four sp3 orbitals, creating a tetrahedral shape.

Molecular Orbital (MO) Theory

Definition:

  • MO Theory describes molecular orbitals formed by atomic orbital overlap, considering electrons as delocalized across the molecule.

Types of Molecular Orbitals

  • Bonding Molecular Orbital (BMO)
    • Formed by constructive overlap (in-phase addition).
    • Stabilizes the molecule (lower energy).
    • Example: σ (sigma) & π (pi) bonds.
  • Antibonding Molecular Orbital (ABMO)
    • Formed by destructive overlap (out-of-phase subtraction).
    • Destabilizes the molecule (higher energy).
    • Denoted as σ* and π*.

Molecular Orbital Diagram (O2 & N2)

Molecule Bond Order Formula Bond Order Calculation Stability
O2 (BMO - ABMO) / 2 (10 - 6) / 2 = 2 Stable, paramagnetic (unpaired e-)
N2 (BMO - ABMO) / 2 (10 - 4) / 2 = 3 Strong triple bond, stable

Bond Order Interpretation:

  • Higher bond order = stronger bond
  • If bond order = 0, the molecule doesn’t exist
