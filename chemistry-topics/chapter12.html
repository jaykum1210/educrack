Chapter 12 - d & f-Block Elements

1. Transition Metals

Definition

  • Transition metals are elements found in Groups 3-12 of the periodic table.
  • They exhibit unique properties such as variable oxidation states, formation of colored compounds, and catalytic behavior.

General Characteristics

Property Characteristics
Electronic Configuration Partially filled d-orbitals (e.g., (n-1)d¹⁻¹⁰ ns¹⁻²)
Variable Oxidation States Due to the availability of d-electrons for bonding
Colored Ions & Compounds d-orbital electron transitions absorb light
Magnetism Unpaired electrons cause paramagnetism (Fe, Co, Ni)
Formation of Complexes Act as Lewis acids, forming coordinate bonds
High Melting & Boiling Points Strong metallic bonding and d-orbital interactions
Catalytic Activity Many transition metals (e.g., Pt, Fe) are catalysts
Metallic Character Ductile, malleable, good conductors of electricity

Periodic Trends

Trend Explanation
Atomic Size Decreases slightly across a period due to increased nuclear charge
Ionization Energy Increases across the period but remains lower than p-block elements
Electronegativity Slightly increases across the period
Density High due to strong metallic bonding
Oxidation States Show multiple oxidation states (e.g., Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺)

Important Transition Metals & Their Compounds

Iron (Fe)

  • Common Oxidation States: +2, +3
  • Important Compounds:
    • Iron(II) Sulfate (FeSO₄) – Used in medicine (iron supplement)
    • Iron(III) Oxide (Fe₂O₃) – Used in pigments, rust formation
    • Ferrocene (Fe(C₅H₅)₂) – Organometallic compound

Copper (Cu)

  • Common Oxidation States: +1, +2
  • Important Compounds:
    • Copper(II) Sulfate (CuSO₄) – Used in electroplating, fungicides
    • Cuprous Oxide (Cu₂O) – Red pigment
    • Copper(II) Chloride (CuCl₂) – Catalyst in organic reactions

Zinc (Zn)

  • Common Oxidation State: +2
  • Important Compounds:
    • Zinc Oxide (ZnO) – Used in cosmetics, sunscreen
    • Zinc Sulfide (ZnS) – Used in fluorescent paints

Silver (Ag)

  • Common Oxidation States: +1
  • Important Compounds:
    • Silver Nitrate (AgNO₃) – Used in photography, medicine
    • Silver Halides (AgCl, AgBr, AgI) – Used in photographic films

Gold (Au)

  • Common Oxidation States: +1, +3
  • Important Compounds:
    • Gold(III) Chloride (AuCl₃) – Used in gold plating
    • Colloidal Gold – Used in medicine, electronics

Lanthanides & Actinides (f-block Elements)

  • Lanthanides (4f series): Soft metals, high reactivity, used in magnets and phosphors (e.g., Nd, Ce).
  • Actinides (5f series): Radioactive, used in nuclear energy (e.g., U, Th, Pu).

Key Applications of Transition Metals

  • Catalysts: Fe in Haber process, Pt in catalytic converters
  • Alloys: Stainless steel (Fe + Cr + Ni), Brass (Cu + Zn)
  • Electronics: Cu in wires, Ag in circuits
  • Biological Role: Hemoglobin (Fe), Vitamin B12 (Co)

2. Coordination Chemistry

Introduction to Coordination Chemistry

Coordination chemistry deals with the study of coordination compounds, which consist of a central metal atom/ion surrounded by ligands. These complexes play important roles in biological systems, catalysis, and industrial applications.

Key Terms & Definitions

Term Definition
Coordination Compound A compound containing a metal center bonded to ligands via coordinate bonds.
Central Metal Atom/Ion A metal (usually transition metal) that accepts electrons from ligands.
Ligand A molecule or ion that donates a pair of electrons to the metal center.
Coordination Number (CN) The number of ligand atoms directly bonded to the metal center.
Coordination Sphere The central metal and its directly attached ligands, enclosed in brackets [ ].
Chelation The formation of stable rings when a ligand binds to a metal at multiple sites.
Complex Ion A charged species consisting of a metal center and its ligands.
Counter Ion An ion outside the coordination sphere that balances the charge of the complex.

Types of Ligands

Type of Ligand Definition Examples
Monodentate Binds to metal through a single donor atom H₂O, NH₃, Cl⁻
Bidentate Binds through two donor atoms Ethylenediamine (en), C₂O₄²⁻ (oxalate)
Polydentate Binds through multiple donor atoms EDTA⁴⁻ (hexadentate ligand)
Ambidentate Can bind through different atoms NO₂⁻ (via N or O), SCN⁻ (via S or N)

Coordination Number & Geometries

Coordination Number (CN) Common Geometry Example
2 Linear [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺
4 Tetrahedral [NiCl₄]²⁻
4 Square Planar [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂]
6 Octahedral [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻

Nomenclature of Coordination Compounds

  • Cation named first, then anion.
  • Ligands named in alphabetical order before metal.
  • Neutral ligands use their normal names (e.g., NH₃ = ammine, H₂O = aqua).
  • Anionic ligands end in "-o" (e.g., Cl⁻ = chloro, CN⁻ = cyano).
  • Metal oxidation state is written in Roman numerals.
  • For anionic complexes, metal names end in "-ate" (e.g., Ferrate for Fe).

Examples:

  • [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃ → Hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride
  • [PtCl₂(NH₃)₂] → Diamminedichloroplatinum(II)
  • K₄[Fe(CN)₆] → Potassium hexacyanoferrate(II)

Isomerism in Coordination Compounds

Structural Isomerism

  • Ionization Isomerism: Different ions in solution
    Example: [Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄ vs [Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br
  • Coordination Isomerism: Ligands exchanged between cationic & anionic parts
    Example: [Cr(NH₃)₆][Co(CN)₆] vs [Co(NH₃)₆][Cr(CN)₆]
  • Linkage Isomerism: Ligand binds through different donor atoms
    Example: [Co(NO₂)(NH₃)₅]²⁺ (Nitro) vs [Co(ONO)(NH₃)₅]²⁺ (Nitrito)

Stereoisomerism

  • Geometrical Isomerism: Different spatial arrangements (cis/trans)
    Example: [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂] (Cisplatin – anti-cancer drug)
  • Optical Isomerism: Non-superimposable mirror images (chiral complexes)
    Example: [Co(en)₃]³⁺

Crystal Field Theory (CFT)

  • Explains the splitting of d-orbitals in a coordination complex due to ligand interaction.
  • Octahedral Complex: d-orbitals split into t₂g (low energy) and e₉ (high energy).
  • Tetrahedral Complex: Reverse splitting occurs (e and t₂ levels).
  • High Spin vs Low Spin Complexes:
    • Strong field ligands (CN⁻, CO) → Low spin (large splitting, electrons pair up).
    • Weak field ligands (Cl⁻, H₂O) → High spin (small splitting, electrons remain unpaired).

Applications of Coordination Compounds

  • Biological Systems: Hemoglobin (Fe complex), Vitamin B12 (Co complex).
  • Catalysis: Zeigler-Natta catalyst (TiCl₄ + AlR₃) for polymerization.
  • Medicinal Chemistry: Cisplatin (anti-cancer drug).
  • Extraction of Metals: Cyanide process for gold extraction.

3. Inner Transition Elements

Introduction

  • Inner transition elements are the f-block elements of the periodic table.
  • They include the lanthanides (4f series) and actinides (5f series).
  • Called "inner" transition elements because their f-orbitals are filled inside the d-block elements.

Classification

Series Elements General Electronic Configuration Key Characteristics
Lanthanides (4f series) Ce (58) to Lu (71) [Xe] 4f¹⁻¹⁴ 5d⁰⁻¹ 6s² Rare earth metals, high reactivity, used in electronics & alloys
Actinides (5f series) Th (90) to Lr (103) [Rn] 5f¹⁻¹⁴ 6d⁰⁻¹ 7s² Radioactive, some are synthetic, used in nuclear energy

Lanthanides (4f Series)

  • Includes elements from Cerium (Ce) to Lutetium (Lu).
  • Commonly called "rare earth metals," though they are not rare.

Properties

  • Metallic & highly electropositive.
  • Oxidation State: +3 is the most stable oxidation state.
  • Lanthanide Contraction: Gradual decrease in atomic and ionic radii.
  • Colored Ions due to f-f electronic transitions.

Uses

  • Catalysts in petroleum refining.
  • Magnets in electric vehicles.
  • Glass polishing and UV absorption.

Actinides (5f Series)

  • Includes elements from Thorium (Th) to Lawrencium (Lr).
  • All actinides are radioactive; many are synthetic.

Properties

  • Highly electropositive & radioactive.
  • Multiple Oxidation States: (+3 to +6).
  • Actinide Contraction: Similar to lanthanide contraction.
  • Strong complex formation.

Important Actinides & Their Uses

Element Uses
Thorium (Th) Used in nuclear reactors & gas mantles
Uranium (U) Nuclear fuel for power plants & weapons
Plutonium (Pu) Used in nuclear weapons & space probes
Americium (Am) Used in smoke detectors

Comparison: Lanthanides vs. Actinides

Property Lanthanides (4f Series) Actinides (5f Series)
Occurrence Natural Some natural, mostly synthetic
Oxidation State Mostly +3 Multiple states (+3 to +6)
Radioactivity Non-radioactive (except Pm) All radioactive
Complex Formation Less tendency Strong complex formation
Magnetism Weakly magnetic Strongly magnetic
Density & Hardness Lower Higher
