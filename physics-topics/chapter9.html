Chapter 9 - Waves

1. Wave Motion and Wave Propagation

Definition

A wave is a disturbance that propagates energy and momentum through a medium or space without the transport of matter.

Example: Ripples on water, sound waves, and light waves.

Types of Waves

(A) Based on the Medium of Propagation:

  • Mechanical Waves – Require a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to propagate.

    • Example: Sound waves, water waves, seismic waves.

  • Electromagnetic Waves – Do not require a medium and can travel through a vacuum.

    • Example: Light waves, radio waves, X-rays.

(B) Based on Particle Motion:

  • Transverse Waves – Particles oscillate perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.

    • Example: Light waves, water waves.

  • Longitudinal Waves – Particles oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation.

    • Example: Sound waves, seismic P-waves.

Characteristics of Waves

(A) Wave Parameters

  • Wavelength (λ): Distance between two consecutive crests or troughs.
  • Frequency (f): Number of wave cycles per second (measured in Hz).
  • Time Period (T): Time taken for one complete cycle of the wave.
    • T = 1 f
  • Wave Speed (v): The speed at which a wave propagates.
    • v = f ⋅ λ

(B) Amplitude (A)

Maximum displacement of the wave from its mean position. Determines energy and intensity of the wave.

(C) Phase Difference

Difference in the phase of oscillation between two points on a wave.

Wave Propagation

(A) Propagation of Mechanical Waves

Requires a medium (solid, liquid, gas). Depends on elastic and inertial properties of the medium.

Examples: Sound waves in air, seismic waves in Earth.

(B) Propagation of Electromagnetic Waves

Can travel through a vacuum. Propagates as oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other.

Examples: Light, radio waves, X-rays.

Types of Wave Motion

  • Progressive (Traveling) Waves – Move from one place to another, transferring energy.

    • Example: Sound waves, ocean waves.

  • Stationary (Standing) Waves – Form due to the superposition of two waves traveling in opposite directions, resulting in nodes and antinodes.

    • Example: Vibrations in a stretched string.

Energy and Power in Waves

Wave Energy (E) ∝ Amplitude² (A²). Higher amplitude means greater energy transmission.

Power (P) = Energy per unit time

P ∝ A 2 ⋅ f 2

Reflection, Refraction, and Diffraction of Waves

  • Reflection: When a wave bounces back upon hitting a surface.

    • Example: Echo in sound waves.

  • Refraction: When a wave changes direction due to a change in medium.

    • Example: Light bending in water.

  • Diffraction: The bending of waves around obstacles.

    • Example: Sound bending around corners.

Applications of Wave Motion

  • Communication Systems – Radio, TV, and mobile networks use electromagnetic waves.
  • Medical Imaging – Ultrasound waves are used for scanning.
  • Seismology – Studying earthquake waves to understand Earth's interior.
  • Sonar (Sound Navigation and Ranging) – Used in submarines to detect objects underwater.

2. Superposition of waves

Definition

  • The superposition principle states that when two or more waves overlap in the same medium, the resultant displacement at any point is equal to the algebraic sum of the individual displacements at that point.
  • Example: When two sound waves meet, they interfere to form a new sound wave.

Mathematical Representation

  • If two waves with displacements y 1 ( x,t ) and y 2 ( x,t ) travel through the same medium, then the resultant displacement is given by:
  • y resultant = y 1 + y 2
  • This applies to all types of waves, including mechanical, sound, and electromagnetic waves.

Types of Superposition (Interference of Waves)

(A) Constructive Interference

  • Occurs when two waves meet in phase (crest meets crest, trough meets trough).
  • The resultant amplitude is greater than the individual amplitudes.
  • Condition for constructive interference: Δx = n λ , n = 0 , 1 , 2 ,...
  • Phase difference: Δϕ = 0 , 2π , 4π ,...
  • Example: Brighter light spots in Young’s Double-Slit Experiment.

(B) Destructive Interference

  • Occurs when two waves meet out of phase (crest meets trough).
  • The resultant amplitude is smaller or even zero (complete cancellation).
  • Condition for destructive interference: Δx = ( 2n+1 ) λ 2
  • Phase difference: Δϕ = π , 3π , 5π ,...
  • Example: Noise-canceling headphones use destructive interference to reduce sound.

Special Cases of Wave Superposition

(A) Beats

  • Occurs when two waves of slightly different frequencies interfere.
  • The amplitude of the resultant wave varies periodically with time.
  • Beat Frequency: f beat = | f 1 - f 2 |
  • Example: Tuning musical instruments by listening to beat frequency.

(B) Standing Waves (Stationary Waves)

  • Formed when two waves of the same frequency and amplitude traveling in opposite directions interfere.
  • The wave does not transfer energy but creates fixed points called:
    • Nodes (N): Points of zero displacement.
    • Antinodes (A): Points of maximum displacement.
  • Example: Vibrations in a stretched string, sound waves in pipes.

Energy Transfer in Superposition

  • In constructive interference, energy is reinforced.
  • In destructive interference, energy is redistributed but not destroyed (law of conservation of energy).

Applications of Superposition of Waves

  • Optical Interference: Used in interference patterns (Young’s Double-Slit Experiment).
  • Radio Waves & Antennas: Used in broadcasting and signal modulation.
  • Sound Engineering: Noise-canceling technology uses destructive interference.
  • Musical Instruments: Tuning forks and beats help in fine-tuning instruments.
  • Holography & Lasers: Based on wave interference principles.

3. Wave Motion

Introduction

  • A wave is a disturbance that propagates energy and momentum through a medium or space without transporting matter.
  • Examples: Ripples on water, sound waves, and light waves.

Types of Waves

  1. Based on Medium of Propagation

    • Mechanical Waves: Require a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to propagate.
      Examples: Sound waves, water waves, seismic waves.
    • Electromagnetic Waves: Do not require a medium and can travel through a vacuum.
      Examples: Light waves, radio waves, X-rays.

  2. Based on Particle Motion

    • Transverse Waves: Particles oscillate perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.
      Examples: Light waves, water waves.
    • Longitudinal Waves: Particles oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation.
      Examples: Sound waves, seismic P-waves.

Characteristics of Waves

  • Wavelength (λ): Distance between two consecutive crests or troughs.
  • Frequency (f): Number of wave cycles per second (measured in Hz).
  • Time Period (T): Time taken for one complete cycle of the wave. T = 1 f
  • Wave Speed (v): The speed at which a wave propagates. v = fλ
  • Amplitude (A): Maximum displacement of the wave from its mean position.
  • Phase Difference: Difference in the phase of oscillation between two points on a wave.

Wave Propagation

  • Mechanical Waves: Require a medium and depend on elastic and inertial properties of the medium.
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Can travel through a vacuum and propagate as oscillating electric and magnetic fields.

Types of Wave Motion

  • Progressive (Traveling) Waves: Move from one place to another, transferring energy.
    Examples: Sound waves, ocean waves.
  • Stationary (Standing) Waves: Form due to the superposition of two waves traveling in opposite directions, resulting in nodes and antinodes.
    Examples: Vibrations in a stretched string.

Energy and Power in Waves

  • Wave Energy (E) ∝ A² (Amplitude squared).
  • Power (P) ∝ A²f² (Amplitude squared times Frequency squared).

Wave Phenomena

4. Doppler Effect

Introduction

  • The Doppler Effect refers to the change in frequency or wavelength of a wave when there is relative motion between the source of the wave and the observer.
  • It occurs for sound waves, light waves, and other types of waves.
  • Examples:
    • The sound of a moving ambulance siren appears to change pitch as it approaches and moves away.
    • The redshift and blueshift of light from distant stars indicate their motion relative to Earth.

Doppler Effect in Sound Waves

  • For sound waves, the observed frequency ( f ' ) depends on the motion of the source and the observer relative to the medium.
  • Doppler Effect Formula for Sound:
  • f ' = f ( v+vo v-vs )
  • Where:
    • f' = observed frequency
    • f = actual frequency
    • v = speed of sound in the medium
    • vo = velocity of the observer (+ if moving towards the source, - if moving away)
    • vs = velocity of the source (+ if moving towards the observer, - if moving away)

Special Cases

  • Source Moving Towards Observer: Frequency increases (f' > f). Example: An approaching train sounds higher-pitched.
  • Source Moving Away from Observer: Frequency decreases (f' < f). Example: An ambulance siren sounds lower-pitched after passing by.
  • Observer Moving Towards Source: Observed frequency increases.
  • Observer Moving Away from Source: Observed frequency decreases.

Doppler Effect in Light (Relativistic Doppler Effect)

  • For electromagnetic waves (light), the Doppler effect is given by:
  • f ' = f ∙ 1 - v/c 1 + v/c
  • Where:
    • c = speed of light
    • v = relative velocity between source and observer
    • f' = observed frequency
    • f = actual frequency

Redshift and Blueshift

  • Blueshift: When the source moves toward the observer, light shifts to a higher frequency (blue end of the spectrum).
  • Redshift: When the source moves away, light shifts to a lower frequency (red end of the spectrum).
  • Example: The redshift of galaxies is evidence for the expansion of the universe (Hubble’s Law).

Applications of Doppler Effect

  • Radar Speed Detection: Used by police radar guns to measure vehicle speeds.
  • Medical Applications: Doppler ultrasound measures blood flow in arteries and veins.
  • Astronomy: Used to study the motion of stars, galaxies, and the expansion of the universe.
  • Weather Forecasting: Doppler radar tracks storms and wind patterns.
  • Navigation: Used in sonar and GPS systems.
