Chapter 6 - Gravitation

1. Newton’s Law of Gravitation

Introduction

  • Newton’s Law of Gravitation explains the force of attraction between any two masses in the universe.
  • It is a universal law applicable to all objects, from small particles to massive celestial bodies.

Statement of Newton’s Law of Gravitation

"Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them."

Mathematically,

F = G m1 m2 r 2

where:

  • F = Gravitational force between two masses.
  • G = Universal Gravitational Constant (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ Nm²/kg²).
  • m1, m2 = Masses of the two objects.
  • r = Distance between the centers of the two objects.

Characteristics of Gravitational Force

  • Attractive Force: Gravity always pulls objects toward each other, never repels.
  • Long-Range Force: Gravity acts over infinite distances, though it weakens with increasing distance.
  • Follows Inverse Square Law: The force decreases as the square of the distance increases.
  • Mutual Force: Both objects experience the same magnitude of force.
  • Weakest Fundamental Force: Compared to other fundamental forces, gravity is the weakest.

Universal Gravitational Constant (G)

  • Value: 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ Nm²/kg².
  • It is a fundamental constant of nature, the same everywhere in the universe.
  • Experimentally determined by Henry Cavendish using the Torsion Balance Experiment in 1798.

Applications of Newton’s Law of Gravitation

  • Explains planetary motion (Kepler’s Laws are derived from it).
  • Helps in calculating the weight of objects on Earth and other planets.
  • Used in satellite motion and space missions (e.g., GPS, ISS orbits).
  • Helps in understanding tides caused by the Moon and the Sun.
  • Used in astrophysics to study the motion of stars, galaxies, and black holes.

Variation of Gravitational Force

(i) Effect of Distance

  • If distance is doubled (r' = 2r): F' = F/4 (force reduces to one-fourth).
  • If distance is halved (r' = r/2): F' = 4F (force increases four times).

(ii) Effect of Mass

  • If one mass is doubled (m1' = 2m1): F' = 2F (force doubles).
  • If both masses are doubled (m1' = 2m1, m2' = 2m2): F' = 4F (force increases four times).

Gravitational Force vs. Electrostatic Force

Feature Gravitational Force Electrostatic Force
Nature Always attractive Can be attractive or repulsive
Governing Law Inverse square law Coulomb’s Law
Relative Strength Very weak Much stronger (~10³⁶ times)
Acts Between All masses Charged particles

2. Gravitational Potential and Field - Notes

i. Gravitational Field

Definition:

The region around a mass where it exerts a gravitational force on other masses is called its gravitational field.

Gravitational Field Intensity (g)

It is the force experienced by a unit mass placed in the gravitational field of another mass.

Mathematically,

g = F/m = GM/r²

where:

  • g = Gravitational field intensity.
  • F = Gravitational force.
  • m = Test mass.
  • G = Universal gravitational constant.
  • M = Mass of the object creating the field.
  • r = Distance from the center of mass.

Properties of Gravitational Field:

  • Vector Quantity: It has both magnitude and direction.
  • Always Directed Toward the Mass: The field lines point inward toward the source mass.
  • Follows Inverse Square Law: g ∝ 1/r².
  • Independent of the Test Mass: Only depends on the source mass M.

Variation of g with Height and Depth:

(a). Above the Earth's Surface:

g' = g (R / (R + h))²

As height h increases, g decreases.

(b). Below the Earth's Surface:

g' = g (1 - d/R)

At the Earth's center (d = R), g = 0.

ii. Gravitational Potential

Definition:

Gravitational potential at a point is the work done per unit mass in bringing a test mass from infinity to that point in the gravitational field.

Mathematically,

V = -GM/r

where:

  • V = Gravitational potential.
  • G = Universal gravitational constant.
  • M = Mass of the body creating the field.
  • r = Distance from the center of the mass.

Properties of Gravitational Potential:

  • Scalar Quantity: It has magnitude but no direction.
  • Always Negative: Since work is done against gravity.
  • Decreases with Distance: As r increases, V approaches zero.

Gravitational Potential Difference:

The potential difference between two points is given by:

ΔV = V₂ - V₁ = -GM (1/r₂ - 1/r₁)

where r₁ and r₂ are the distances of the two points from the mass M.

iii. Gravitational Potential Energy (U)

Definition:

The energy possessed by a mass due to its position in a gravitational field is called gravitational potential energy.

Mathematically,

U = mV = -GMm/r

where:

  • U = Gravitational potential energy.
  • m = Test mass.
  • V = Gravitational potential.

Key Points:

  • Negative Value: Because work is required to separate masses against gravity.
  • At Infinity, U = 0: Objects at an infinite distance have no gravitational potential energy.
  • If r decreases, U becomes more negative, meaning stronger binding energy.

iv. Relationship Between Gravitational Field and Potential

The gravitational field (g) and gravitational potential (V) are related as:

g = -dV/dr

This means:

  • The gravitational field is the negative gradient of gravitational potential.
  • It shows how potential changes with distance.

v. Equipotential Surfaces

Definition:

A surface where gravitational potential is the same at every point.

Characteristics:

  • No work is done when moving along an equipotential surface.
  • Equipotential surfaces are perpendicular to gravitational field lines.
  • The surfaces become farther apart as distance from the mass increases.

vi. Escape Velocity (vₑ)

The minimum velocity required for an object to escape the gravitational field of a planet without further propulsion.

Derived using gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy:

vₑ = √(2GM/R)

For Earth, vₑ ≈ 11.2 km/s.

3. Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Introduction

Kepler’s Laws of Planetary Motion describe how planets move around the Sun. These laws were formulated by Johannes Kepler in the early 17th century based on the observations of Tycho Brahe.

i. Kepler’s First Law (Law of Ellipses)

Statement:

A planet moves around the Sun in an elliptical orbit, with the Sun at one of the two foci of the ellipse.

Explanation:

  • Planets do not move in perfect circles but in elliptical paths.
  • An ellipse has two foci, and the Sun is located at one of these foci.
  • The closest point in the orbit to the Sun is called the Perihelion.
  • The farthest point in the orbit from the Sun is called the Aphelion.

Mathematical Representation:

The equation of an ellipse is:

x 2 a 2 + y 2 b 2 = 1

where:

  • a = semi-major axis (longest radius).
  • b = semi-minor axis (shortest radius).

Implication:

  • The distance between a planet and the Sun changes as it moves in its orbit.
  • Not all orbits are highly elliptical; some are nearly circular (like Earth's orbit).

ii. Kepler’s Second Law (Law of Equal Areas)

Statement:

A line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time.

Explanation:

  • When a planet is closer to the Sun (Perihelion), it moves faster.
  • When a planet is farther from the Sun (Aphelion), it moves slower.
  • The area swept per unit time remains constant.

Mathematical Representation:

dA dt = constant

where A is the area swept and t is time.

Implication:

  • Planets move faster when closer to the Sun and slower when farther.
  • This is due to conservation of angular momentum ( L = mvr ).

iii. Kepler’s Third Law (Law of Harmonies)

Statement:

The square of the orbital period of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit.

Mathematical Representation:

T 2 ∝ r 3

or

T 2 r 3 = constant

where:

  • T = Time period of revolution.
  • r = Semi-major axis of orbit.

For planetary motion around the Sun:

T 2 = 4 π 2 r 3 G M

where G is the gravitational constant and M is the mass of the Sun.

Implication:

  • Planets farther from the Sun take longer to complete one orbit than planets closer to the Sun.
  • The law applies to all celestial bodies, including moons orbiting planets.

Key Takeaways

  • First Law: Planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.
  • Second Law: Planets move faster when closer to the Sun and slower when farther.
  • Third Law: The farther a planet is from the Sun, the longer its orbital period.

Kepler’s Laws were later explained using Newton’s Law of Gravitation, which showed that these motions result from gravitational forces.

4. Satellite Motion - Notes

Definition

A satellite is an object that revolves around a planet or a star due to gravitational attraction. It can be natural (e.g., Moon around Earth) or artificial (e.g., communication satellites).

Types of Satellites

  • Natural Satellites: Objects like the Moon, which naturally orbit planets.
  • Artificial Satellites: Man-made objects launched into space for various purposes (GPS, communication, weather monitoring).

Orbital Motion of a Satellite

A satellite moves in a circular or elliptical orbit due to the gravitational force exerted by the planet. The required centripetal force is provided by the gravitational attraction of the planet.

Mathematical Formulation:

GMm r 2 = mv 2 r

Orbital Velocity

v = GM r

Time Period of a Satellite

T 2 ∝ r 3

From derivation:

T = 2 π r 3 GM

Energy of a Satellite

Kinetic Energy (KE)

KE = 1 2 mv 2 = GMm 2 r

Gravitational Potential Energy (PE)

PE = - GMm r

Total Energy (E)

E = KE + PE = - GMm 2 r

Escape Velocity

v e = 2 GM R

For Earth:

v e ≈ 11.2 km/s

Types of Orbits

  • Geostationary Orbit: Satellite appears stationary relative to Earth. Located at an altitude of 35,786 km above Earth. Used for communication (e.g., TV, GPS).
  • Polar Orbit: Passes over both poles of Earth. Covers entire Earth in one day. Used for weather monitoring and Earth observation.

Key Takeaways

  • A satellite stays in orbit due to the balance between gravitational force and centripetal force.
  • Orbital velocity depends on the mass of the planet and the radius of the orbit.
  • Escape velocity is the minimum speed required to break free from gravitational pull.
  • Different orbits serve different purposes (geostationary for communication, polar for surveillance).
