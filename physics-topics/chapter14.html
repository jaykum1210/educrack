Chapter 14 - Electromagnetic Waves

1. Electromagnetic Spectrum

Introduction

  • The Electromagnetic Spectrum is the complete range of electromagnetic waves arranged according to their wavelength or frequency.
  • These waves do not require a medium to travel and can propagate through a vacuum at the speed of light (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s).

Properties of Electromagnetic Waves

  • Transverse waves – Consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and the direction of propagation.
  • Travel at the speed of light in a vacuum.
  • Obey the wave equation: c = fλ

    where c = Speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s), f = Frequency (Hz), λ = Wavelength (m).

  • Carry energy and momentum and can exert pressure.

Electromagnetic Spectrum Classification

Type of Wave Wavelength Range Frequency Range Main Uses
Radio Waves > 1 m < 10⁹ Hz Communication, broadcasting (FM, AM), satellites
Microwaves 1 mm – 1 m 10⁹ − 10¹² Hz Radar, cooking (microwave ovens), mobile networks
Infrared (IR) Rays 700 nm – 1 mm 10¹² − 10¹⁴ Hz Thermal imaging, remote controls, night vision
Visible Light 400 – 700 nm 10¹⁴ − 10¹⁵ Hz Human vision, photography, optical fibers
Ultraviolet (UV) Rays 10 nm – 400 nm 10¹⁵ − 10¹⁷ Hz Sterilization, sun tanning, fluorescence
X-Rays 0.01 – 10 nm 10¹⁷ − 10²⁰ Hz Medical imaging, security scanners
Gamma Rays < 0.01 nm > 10²⁰ Hz Cancer treatment, nuclear reactions, astrophysics

Explanation of Each Region

  • Radio Waves: Longest wavelength, lowest frequency. Used in communication (TV, radio, mobile networks, Wi-Fi, satellites).
  • Microwaves: Used in microwave ovens, GPS, weather forecasting, and radar.
  • Infrared (IR) Radiation: Emitted by warm objects. Used in thermal cameras, remote controls, and fiber optic communication.
  • Visible Light: The only part of the spectrum visible to the human eye. Different colors correspond to different wavelengths.
  • Ultraviolet (UV) Rays: Present in sunlight, used for sterilization, fluorescent lights, and tanning beds.
  • X-Rays: Highly penetrating, used in medical imaging, security scanners, and crystallography.
  • Gamma Rays: Highest frequency, shortest wavelength. Used in cancer treatment, sterilization, and nuclear energy.

Applications of the Electromagnetic Spectrum

  • Communication – Radio waves and microwaves in TV, mobile phones, and satellites.
  • Medical Uses – X-rays in diagnostics, gamma rays in cancer treatment.
  • Security – X-ray scanners at airports.
  • Energy Production – Infrared in solar panels.
  • Scientific Research – Gamma rays and X-rays in astronomy and nuclear physics.

2. Properties of Electromagnetic Waves

Key Properties of Electromagnetic Waves

i. Transverse Nature

  • Electromagnetic waves are transverse waves, meaning the electric field (E) and magnetic field (B) oscillate perpendicular to each other and to the direction of wave propagation.

ii. Travel at the Speed of Light

  • In a vacuum, all EM waves travel at the speed of light:
  • c = 3.0 × 108 m/s
  • In a medium, the speed decreases depending on the refractive index (n):
  • v = c/n

iii. Do Not Require a Medium

  • Unlike sound or water waves, EM waves do not need a medium to propagate and can travel through the vacuum of space.

iv. Obey the Wave Equation

  • The relationship between wavelength (λ), frequency (f), and speed (c) is:
  • c = fλ
  • where:
    • f = Frequency (Hz)
    • λ = Wavelength (m)

v. Carry Energy and Momentum

  • Electromagnetic waves carry energy and momentum, which can exert radiation pressure on objects.
  • Energy per photon is given by:
  • E = hf
  • where h = 6.626 × 10-34 J·s (Planck’s constant).

vi. Can Be Polarized

  • Since EM waves are transverse, they can undergo polarization, meaning the electric field oscillations are restricted to a single plane.

vii. Exhibit Reflection, Refraction, Diffraction, and Interference

  • Reflection: EM waves bounce off surfaces (e.g., mirrors).
  • Refraction: EM waves change direction when passing through different mediums (e.g., light bending in water).
  • Diffraction: EM waves spread out when passing through narrow openings.
  • Interference: Two EM waves can superpose, leading to constructive or destructive interference.

viii. Governed by Maxwell’s Equations

  • EM waves obey Maxwell’s equations, which describe how electric and magnetic fields generate each other and propagate through space.
