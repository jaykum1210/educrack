Chapter 3 - Laws of Motion

1. Motion in One Dimension

Introduction

Newton’s Laws of Motion, formulated by Sir Isaac Newton in 1687, describe the relationship between the motion of an object and the forces acting on it. These laws are fundamental in understanding classical mechanics and apply to everything from everyday movements to planetary motion.

Newton’s First Law of Motion – Law of Inertia

Statement:

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion continues in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external unbalanced force."

Explanation:

  • If no net external force acts on an object, its state of motion remains unchanged.
  • This means that an object will remain at rest if it was initially at rest, and it will continue moving at a constant velocity if it was already in motion.
  • This tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion is called inertia.

Examples:

  • A book on a table remains at rest unless someone pushes it.
  • A moving bus stops suddenly, and passengers jerk forward due to inertia.
  • A coin placed on a card above a glass falls straight into the glass when the card is quickly pulled away.

Mathematical Form:

Since no external force is acting, acceleration is zero:

         F=0⇒a=0
         v=constant

Inertia and Mass:

  • Inertia is the property of an object that resists changes in its motion.
  • Mass is a measure of inertia; heavier objects have greater inertia than lighter ones.

Newton’s Second Law of Motion – Law of Force and Acceleration

Statement:

"The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force."

Mathematical Expression:

          F = d p d t

Since momentum ( p ) is given by:

          p = m v

Differentiating with respect to time:

          F = d ( m v ) d t

For constant mass:

          F = m d v d t = m a

Explanation:

  • A force is required to change an object’s velocity (i.e., to accelerate it).
  • The greater the force applied, the greater the acceleration.
  • A larger mass requires a larger force to achieve the same acceleration.

Examples:

  1. Pushing a car vs. a bicycle: A car requires a greater force to accelerate than a bicycle.
  2. Kicking a football: A harder kick (greater force) results in a greater acceleration of the ball.
  3. Stopping a fast-moving vehicle: A larger force (stronger brakes) is required to bring a speeding vehicle to rest.

Special Cases:

  1. If force is zero, then acceleration is zero, meaning the object will move with constant velocity (First Law).
  2. If mass is constant, then acceleration is directly proportional to force:
                                          a∝F
  3. If force is constant, then acceleration is inversely proportional to mass:
                                          a ∝ 1 m

Newton’s Third Law of Motion – Action and Reaction

Statement:

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

Explanation:

  • When one object exerts a force on another, the second object exerts an equal force in the opposite direction.
  • These action and reaction forces always occur in pairs and act on different objects.
  • They are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

Examples:

  • Walking: When we push the ground backward with our feet (action), the ground pushes us forward (reaction).
  • Recoil of a gun: When a bullet is fired, the gun moves backward due to the reaction force.
  • Rocket propulsion: The exhaust gases push downward (action), and the rocket moves upward (reaction).
  • Swimming: A swimmer pushes water backward, and water pushes them forward.

Mathematical Form:

If F₁ is the force exerted by object A on object B, and F₂ is the force exerted by B on A, then:

                                      F 1 = - F 2

Applications of Newton’s Laws of Motion

  1. In Daily Life:
    • Seatbelts in cars (First Law – inertia).
    • Cycling and skating (Second Law – force and acceleration).
    • Jumping off a boat (Third Law – action and reaction).
  2. In Engineering & Technology:
    • Bridges and buildings are designed considering Newton’s laws.
    • Vehicle braking systems apply Newton’s Second Law.
    • Rocket launch works based on the Third Law.
  3. In Space and Astronomy:
    • Satellites stay in orbit due to inertia and gravitational force.
    • Planets revolve around the Sun due to Newton’s laws and gravitational force.

2. Friction

Introduction

Friction is a force that opposes the relative motion or the tendency of motion between two surfaces in contact. It acts parallel to the contact surface and is opposite to the direction of motion. Friction plays a crucial role in everyday life, enabling walking, driving, and even writing.

Types of Friction

Friction is classified into different types based on the nature of motion:

  1. Static Friction f s

    • The force that prevents an object from moving when a force is applied.
    • It acts up to a maximum limit known as limiting friction.
    • When the applied force exceeds this limit, motion starts.
                                            f s ≤ μ s N

      • where,

      μ s = Coefficient of static friction,

      N = Normal reaction force.

    Example: A heavy box on the floor does not move until a certain force is applied.

  2. Kinetic (Sliding) Friction f k

    • The force that acts when an object is already in motion.
    • It is always less than static friction.
                                            f k = μ k N

      • where,

      μ k = Coefficient of kinetic friction.

    Example: A moving book on a table slows down due to friction.

  3. Rolling Friction f r

    • The force that opposes the rolling motion of an object.
    • It is much smaller than sliding friction.
                                            f r = μ r N

      • where,

      μ r = Coefficient of rolling friction.

    Example: Rolling a ball on the ground is easier than sliding a book.
  4. Fluid Friction (Viscous Force)
    • The resistance offered by fluids (liquids or gases) to the motion of an object moving through them.
    • Depends on velocity, shape, and viscosity of the fluid.

    Example: Swimming in water or air resistance on a moving car.

Causes of Friction

  1. Irregularities of Surfaces – Microscopic roughness interlocks surfaces.
  2. Molecular Adhesion – When surfaces come into contact, molecular forces cause attraction.

Laws of Friction

  • Friction depends on the nature of surfaces in contact.
  • Friction is independent of the area of contact.
  • Static friction is greater than kinetic friction.
  • Friction is directly proportional to normal force (N):
                                         f=μN

Advantages of Friction

  • Helps in walking without slipping.
  • Essential for vehicles to move and stop.
  • Allows us to write using a pen or pencil.
  • Provides grip between objects.

Disadvantages of Friction

  • Causes wear and tear in machines.
  • Produces unwanted heat and energy loss.
  • Reduces efficiency in mechanical systems.
  • Opposes motion, requiring extra energy.

Methods to Reduce Friction

  • Lubrication – Oil or grease reduces surface roughness.
  • Polishing – Smoother surfaces reduce friction.
  • Use of Ball Bearings – Rolling friction is lower than sliding friction.
  • Streamlining Shapes – Reduces air/water resistance in vehicles and aircraft.

3.Circular Motion Dynamics

Introduction

Circular motion refers to the motion of an object along a circular path. The study of forces and dynamics involved in circular motion is called Circular Motion Dynamics.

Types of Circular Motion

Circular motion can be categorized based on speed and external forces:

  1. Uniform Circular Motion (UCM)
    • The motion of an object in a circular path with a constant speed.
    • Although speed is constant, velocity changes due to the continuous change in direction.
    • The acceleration responsible for this motion is called centripetal acceleration.
                                            a c = v 2 r

    where,

    v = speed of the object,

    r = radius of the circular path.

    Example: A satellite orbiting the Earth.

  2. Non-Uniform Circular Motion
    1. The speed of the object changes while moving in a circular path.
    2. The object experiences both centripetal acceleration and tangential acceleration.

Kinematics of Circular Motion

In circular motion, displacement, velocity, and acceleration can be expressed in angular form:

  1. Angular Displacement: Angle covered by the object in radians.
                                          θ = s r

    where,

    s = arc length,

    r = radius.

  2. Angular Velocity: Rate of change of angular displacement.

                                          ω = d θ d t

    For uniform circular motion,

                                          ω = 2 π T

    where T is the time period.

  3. Angular Acceleration (α): Rate of change of angular velocity.

                                          α = d ω d t

    For non-uniform circular motion, α ≠ 0.

Dynamics of Circular Motion

  1. Centripetal Force
    • A force directed towards the center of the circular path that keeps an object moving in a circle.
    • Without it, the object would move in a straight line due to inertia.

      F c = m v 2 r

      or,

      F c = m ω 2 r

      where,

      m = mass of the object,

      v = linear speed,

      ω = angular velocity,

      r = radius.

      Examples:

      • Gravitational force acts as a centripetal force in planetary motion.
      • Friction provides centripetal force when a car takes a turn.
    • Centrifugal Force (Pseudo Force)
      • An apparent force observed in a rotating frame of reference.
      • Acts outward away from the center of rotation.

                                              F cf = m v 2 r

    Example: Feeling pushed outward when taking a sharp turn in a car.

Applications of Circular Motion

  1. Banking of Roads – Roads are inclined at an angle to provide necessary centripetal force for vehicles.
  2. Motion of Satellites – Satellites remain in orbit due to gravitational centripetal force.
  3. Roller Coasters – Centripetal force keeps the ride on track.
  4. Centrifuges – Used in laboratories to separate substances based on mass.
