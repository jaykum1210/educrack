Chapter 15 - Ray Optics

1. Reflection and Refraction of Light

Reflection of Light

Definition

  • Reflection is the phenomenon where light bounces off a surface instead of passing through it.

Laws of Reflection

  • The angle of incidence (i) is equal to the angle of reflection (r): i = r
  • The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the surface all lie in the same plane.

Types of Reflection

  • Specular Reflection: Occurs on smooth surfaces like mirrors; reflected rays remain parallel.
  • Diffuse Reflection: Occurs on rough surfaces like paper; reflected rays scatter in different directions.

Applications of Reflection

  • Mirrors (plane, concave, convex)
  • Periscopes and Kaleidoscopes
  • Optical instruments like telescopes and microscopes

ii. Refraction of Light

Definition

  • Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed.

Laws of Refraction (Snell’s Law)

  • Light bends when moving between two media of different refractive indices (n):
  • n1 sin i = n2 sin r

    • where n1, n2 are the refractive indices, i is the angle of incidence, and r is the angle of refraction.

  • The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.

Refractive Index (n)

  • Given by: n = c/v

    • where c = Speed of light in vacuum, v = Speed of light in the medium.

  • Higher n → Light slows down → Bends towards normal.
  • Lower n → Light speeds up → Bends away from normal.

Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

  • Occurs when light travels from a denser to a rarer medium at an angle greater than the critical angle, causing total reflection.
  • Used in optical fibers, diamonds, and periscopes.

Applications of Refraction

  • Lenses (Convex & Concave)
  • Prisms (Dispersion of Light)
  • Optical fibers (Telecommunications)
  • Mirages (Light bending due to air temperature differences)

2. Mirrors and Lenses

i. Mirrors

A mirror is a smooth, polished surface that reflects light according to the laws of reflection.

Types of Mirrors

  • Plane Mirror
    • Forms a virtual, upright, and same-size image as the object.
    • Used in household mirrors, periscopes, and kaleidoscopes.
  • Spherical Mirrors
    • Concave Mirror (Converging Mirror)
      • Reflecting surface curved inward.
      • Forms real and inverted images when the object is beyond the focal point.
      • Forms a virtual and magnified image when the object is between the focus and the mirror.
      • Used in telescopes, shaving mirrors, headlights, and solar concentrators.
    • Convex Mirror (Diverging Mirror)
      • Reflecting surface curved outward.
      • Always forms a virtual, upright, and diminished image.
      • Used in rear-view mirrors of vehicles and security mirrors.

Mirror Formula

1 f = 1 v + 1 u

ii. Lenses

A lens is a transparent optical device that bends light rays by refraction.

Types of Lenses

  • Convex Lens (Converging Lens)
    • Thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges.
    • Converges light rays to a point.
    • Forms real and inverted images for objects beyond the focal point.
    • Forms a virtual, magnified, and upright image when the object is within the focal length.
    • Used in magnifying glasses, cameras, and human eyes.
  • Concave Lens (Diverging Lens)
    • Thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges.
    • Diverges light rays outward.
    • Always forms a virtual, upright, and diminished image.
    • Used in spectacles for myopia (nearsightedness) and peepholes.

Lens Formula

1 f = 1 v - 1 u

iii. Power of a Lens

The power ( P ) of a lens is given by:

P = 1 f  (in meters)

  • Unit: Diopter (D)
  • Convex lens has positive power (+D).
  • Concave lens has negative power (-D).

iv. Applications of Mirrors and Lenses

  • Mirrors: Used in rear-view mirrors, telescopes, microscopes, and makeup mirrors.
  • Lenses: Used in glasses, cameras, magnifying glasses, projectors, and optical instruments.

3. Prisms

Definition

  • A prism is a transparent optical element with flat, polished surfaces that refract light.
  • The most common type is a triangular prism, which has two triangular bases and three rectangular sides.

Refraction Through a Prism

  • When light passes through a prism, it bends (refracts) due to the change in speed as it moves between different media.
  • The angle of deviation (D) is the angle between the incident and emergent ray.

Prism Formula

  • The deviation angle is given by:
  • D = i 1 + i 2 - A

    where \( i_1 \) = Angle of incidence, \( i_2 \) = Angle of emergence, \( A \) = Angle of the prism.

Dispersion of Light

  • When white light enters a prism, it splits into its component colors due to different refractive indices.
  • This phenomenon is called dispersion.
  • The spectrum follows the order: VIBGYOR (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red).
  • Violet bends the most (higher refractive index), and red bends the least.

Example: A rainbow is a natural example of dispersion caused by raindrops acting as tiny prisms.

Types of Prisms

  1. Triangular Prism

    • Most common type, used for dispersion and deviation of light.

  2. Reflecting Prisms

    • Used to change the direction of light by total internal reflection (TIR).
    • Examples:
      • Right-Angle Prism (90°) – Used in periscopes and binoculars.
      • Pentaprism – Used in DSLR cameras to correct image orientation.

  3. Polarizing Prisms

    • Used to polarize light (filter specific light waves).
    • Example: Nicol Prism (used in microscopes and optical instruments).

Applications of Prisms

  • Spectroscopy – Analyzing the composition of light from stars and materials.
  • Cameras & Binoculars – Reflecting prisms adjust image orientation.
  • Periscopes & Optical Instruments – Used in submarines and microscopes.
  • Laser Technology – Prisms control and direct laser beams.
  • Holography & 3D Imaging – Used to manipulate light waves for holograms.
