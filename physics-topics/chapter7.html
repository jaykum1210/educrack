Chapter 7 - Properties of Solids and Liquids

1. Introduction to Elasticity

Elasticity is the property of a material to regain its original shape and size after the removal of an external force.

When an external force is applied, the material deforms; once the force is removed, it tries to return to its original shape.

The study of elasticity is essential in engineering, construction, and material science.

Example:

A rubber band stretches when pulled but regains its original shape after release.

Stress and Strain

(a) Stress (σ)

Stress is the internal restoring force per unit area within a material due to an applied external force.

Stress = Force (F) Area (A)

Unit: Pascal (Pa) or N/m2

Types of Stress:

  • Tensile Stress: When the object is stretched.
  • Compressive Stress: When the object is compressed.
  • Shear Stress: When the object is twisted or sheared.

(b) Strain (ε)

Strain is the deformation per unit original dimension due to applied stress.

Strain = Change in dimension Original dimension

It has no units (dimensionless) because it is a ratio.

Types of Strain:

  • Longitudinal Strain: Change in length per unit original length.
  • Volumetric Strain: Change in volume per unit original volume.
  • Shear Strain: Change in shape due to tangential stress.

Hooke’s Law

Hooke’s law states that stress is directly proportional to strain, within the elastic limit.

Stress = Elastic Modulus × Strain

Limitations of Hooke’s Law:

  • Valid only within the elastic limit.
  • Does not hold for plastic or brittle materials.

Elastic Moduli

(a) Young’s Modulus (E)

Measures elasticity in length (Tensile/Compressive stress).

E = Longitudinal Stress Longitudinal Strain

(b) Bulk Modulus (K)

Measures elasticity in volume (Volumetric stress).

K = Volumetric Stress Volumetric Strain

(c) Shear Modulus (G or η)

Measures elasticity in shape (Shear stress).

G = Shear Stress Shear Strain

Elastic Limit and Plastic Deformation

Elastic Limit: The maximum stress a material can withstand and still return to its original shape.

Plastic Deformation: If stress exceeds the elastic limit, the material deforms permanently.

Example:

A rubber band stretches elastically up to a point.

If stretched too much, it permanently deforms and does not return to its original shape.

Applications of Elasticity

  • Construction: Bridges, buildings, and beams must withstand stress without permanent deformation.
  • Automobile Industry: Car springs and tires use elasticity for impact absorption.
  • Medical: Tendons and bones exhibit elastic behavior to support movement.
  • Sports: Rackets, bows, and trampolines depend on elastic materials.

2. Viscosity

Definition

  • Viscosity is the property of a fluid (liquid or gas) that describes its resistance to flow.
  • It occurs due to internal friction between different layers of the fluid moving at different velocities.
  • Fluids with high viscosity flow slowly (e.g., honey), while fluids with low viscosity flow easily (e.g., water).

Example

  • Honey has a higher viscosity than water, which means it flows slower.
  • Air has a lower viscosity than oil, so it moves more freely.

Causes of Viscosity

  • Viscosity arises due to the cohesive forces (intermolecular forces) between molecules in a fluid.
  • In liquids, viscosity is mainly due to intermolecular attraction.
  • In gases, viscosity is caused by collisions between fast-moving molecules.

Coefficient of Viscosity (η)

The coefficient of viscosity measures a fluid’s resistance to deformation. It is defined by Newton’s Law of Viscosity:

F = η A (dv/dx)

  • F = force applied on the liquid layer
  • A = area of the layer
  • dv/dx = velocity gradient (change in velocity per unit distance)
  • η = coefficient of viscosity

Unit of Viscosity

  • SI Unit: Pascal-second (Pa·s) or N·s/m²
  • CGS Unit: Poise (P), where 1 Poise = 0.1 Pa·s

Dimensional Formula

η = [M L⁻¹ T⁻¹]

Types of Fluids Based on Viscosity

  1. Newtonian Fluids
    • Obey Newton’s Law of Viscosity (constant viscosity at all flow conditions).
    • Examples: Water, air, alcohol.
  2. Non-Newtonian Fluids
    • Do not obey Newton’s law (viscosity changes with applied force).
    • Examples: Blood, ketchup, quicksand.

Factors Affecting Viscosity

Temperature

  • In liquids, viscosity decreases with increasing temperature (molecules move faster, reducing attraction).
  • In gases, viscosity increases with increasing temperature (molecular collisions become more frequent).

Pressure

  • In liquids, viscosity increases with pressure.
  • In gases, viscosity is not significantly affected by pressure.

Molecular Composition

  • Fluids with larger molecules tend to have higher viscosity.
  • Example: Oils are more viscous than water due to larger molecular size.

Stokes' Law (For a Sphere Moving in a Viscous Fluid)

When a small sphere moves through a viscous fluid, it experiences a drag force given by Stokes' Law:

F = 6πηrv

  • F = viscous drag force
  • η = viscosity of the fluid
  • r = radius of the sphere
  • v = velocity of the sphere

Example

  • A raindrop falling through the air slows down due to air viscosity.

Terminal Velocity

When an object falls through a viscous fluid, it reaches a constant velocity called terminal velocity (vt).

It happens when the gravitational force is balanced by the viscous drag and buoyant force. Given by:

vt = (2r2 (ρ - σ)g) / (9η)

  • r = radius of the object
  • ρ = density of the object
  • σ = density of the fluid
  • g = acceleration due to gravity
  • η = coefficient of viscosity

Examples

  • A raindrop stops accelerating after reaching terminal velocity.
  • A parachutist slows down due to air resistance.

Poiseuille’s Law (Flow of Liquid Through a Pipe)

Describes the flow rate of a viscous liquid through a cylindrical pipe:

Q = (πr4(P1 - P2)) / (8ηl)

  • Q = volume flow rate
  • r = radius of the pipe
  • P1 - P2 = pressure difference
  • l = length of the pipe
  • η = viscosity of the fluid

Applications

  • Blood flow in veins and arteries.
  • Oil flow through pipelines.

Applications of Viscosity

  • Lubrication: Engine oils reduce friction between machine parts.
  • Blood Flow: Blood viscosity affects circulation and heart health.
  • Paints and Inks: Proper viscosity ensures smooth application.
  • Aviation: Air viscosity affects aircraft aerodynamics.
  • Polymer Manufacturing: Determines flow behavior in plastic molding.

3. Surface Tension

Definition

  • Surface tension is the property of a liquid that makes its surface behave like a stretched elastic sheet.
  • It arises due to cohesive forces (attractive forces between molecules) at the surface of a liquid.
  • It is responsible for the formation of droplets, bubbles, and capillary action.

Example

  • Water droplets on a surface appear spherical due to surface tension.
  • A small insect like a water strider can walk on water without sinking.

Cause of Surface Tension

  • Molecules inside a liquid experience equal cohesive forces in all directions.
  • Molecules at the surface experience a net inward force, as there are no liquid molecules above them.
  • This inward force pulls surface molecules together, minimizing surface area.

Analogy: Surface tension is like a stretched rubber sheet on the surface of water.

Surface Tension Formula & Unit

(a) Surface Tension (T)

Surface tension is defined as the force per unit length acting along the surface of a liquid:

T = F / L

  • T = Surface tension
  • F = Force acting on the surface
  • L = Length along which the force acts

(b) Unit of Surface Tension

  • SI Unit: Newton per meter (N/m)
  • CGS Unit: dyne/cm, where 1 dyne/cm = 0.001 N/m

(c) Dimensional Formula

[T] = [M T⁻²]

Factors Affecting Surface Tension

(a) Temperature

  • Surface tension decreases with increase in temperature because molecular motion increases.
  • At boiling point, surface tension becomes very low.

(b) Impurities

  • Soluble impurities (like soap, detergents) reduce surface tension.
  • Insoluble impurities (like oil, grease) increase surface tension.

(c) Nature of Liquid

  • Liquids with strong intermolecular forces (like mercury) have high surface tension.
  • Liquids with weak forces (like alcohol) have low surface tension.

(d) Presence of Surfactants

  • Surfactants (like detergents, soaps) reduce surface tension, helping in cleaning.

Work Done in Increasing Surface Area

To increase the surface area of a liquid film, work must be done against surface tension.

The work done (W) is given by:

W = T ⋅ ΔA

  • T = Surface tension
  • ΔA = Increase in surface area

Example

  • Blowing a soap bubble requires work to expand the surface.

Angle of Contact

The angle of contact (θ) is the angle between the liquid surface and the solid surface at the point of contact. It determines whether a liquid will wet a surface or not.

  • If θ < 90°: Liquid wets the surface (e.g., water on glass).
  • If θ > 90°: Liquid does not wet the surface (e.g., mercury on glass).

Example

  • Water spreads on glass because the angle of contact is small.
  • Mercury forms droplets on glass because the angle of contact is large.

Capillary Action (Capillarity)

Capillary action is the rise or fall of a liquid in a narrow tube due to surface tension and adhesive forces.

Formula for Capillary Rise (h)

h = (2T cos θ) / (rρg)

  • h = Height of liquid rise
  • T = Surface tension
  • θ = Angle of contact
  • r = Radius of the capillary tube
  • ρ = Density of liquid
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity

Examples of Capillary Action

  • Ink rising in a pen nib.
  • Water moving through plant roots and stems.
  • Blotting paper absorbing ink.

Applications of Surface Tension

  • Soap and Detergents: Reduce surface tension, helping in cleaning.
  • Rainproof Coatings: Water does not wet surfaces due to high contact angle.
  • Insect Movement on Water: Insects like water striders use surface tension to float.
  • Capillary Action in Plants: Helps in water transport in trees.
  • Medicine: Drops of liquid medicines maintain shape due to surface tension.

4.Fluid Pressure

Definition

  • Fluid pressure is the pressure exerted by a fluid (liquid or gas) at any point within it.
  • It is caused by molecular collisions and depends on depth, density, and gravity.
  • Fluids exert pressure in all directions.

Examples

  • A diver feels more pressure as they go deeper underwater.
  • Air pressure decreases as we move to higher altitudes.

Formula for Fluid Pressure

The pressure exerted by a fluid at depth (h) is given by:

P = P0 + ρgh

  • P = Total fluid pressure
  • P0 = Atmospheric pressure at the surface
  • ρ = Density of the fluid
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
  • h = Depth of the fluid

Special Cases

(a) If the fluid is open to the atmosphere (e.g., a lake)

P = Patm + ρgh

(b) If the fluid is enclosed (e.g., inside a tank)

P = ρgh

(c) If pressure is the same at different depths in a connected fluid

P1 = P2

Units of Pressure

(a) SI Unit

Pascal (Pa): 1 Pa = 1 N/m²

(b) Other Common Units

  • Atmosphere (atm): 1 atm = 101,325 Pa
  • Torr (mmHg): 1 atm = 760 mmHg
  • Bar: 1 bar = 10⁵ Pa

Example

The pressure at sea level is 1 atm = 101.3 kPa.

Properties of Fluid Pressure

  • Increases with Depth: Pressure increases as we go deeper in a fluid.
  • Same in All Directions: A fluid exerts pressure equally in all directions at a given depth.
  • Independent of Shape: The shape of the container does not affect pressure.
  • Depends on Density: A denser fluid exerts more pressure.

Examples

  • Water exerts more pressure than air at the same height because water is denser.
  • A dam is thicker at the bottom because the pressure is highest at greater depths.

Pascal’s Law

Pascal’s law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions.

Pin = Pout

Applications

  • Hydraulic brakes: Used in cars to amplify force.
  • Hydraulic lifts: Used in garages to lift heavy vehicles.
  • Syringes: Pressing the plunger transmits pressure equally in all directions.

Applications of Fluid Pressure

  • Blood Pressure: Measures the force of blood against artery walls.
  • Scuba Diving: Divers experience increased pressure underwater.
  • Airplane Cabin Pressure: Maintained to keep passengers comfortable.
  • Weather Patterns: High and low-pressure systems affect climate.
  • Dams and Reservoirs: Designed to withstand high water pressure at the base.

5. Archimedes' Principle

Definition

Archimedes' principle states that:

"Any object completely or partially submerged in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object."

Examples

  • A ship floats because the buoyant force balances its weight.
  • A stone feels lighter in water due to the upward buoyant force.

Buoyant Force

When an object is placed in a fluid (liquid or gas), it experiences an upward force called buoyant force (Fb). This force is responsible for making objects appear lighter in water.

Formula for Buoyant Force

Fb = ρfluid ⋅ g ⋅ Vdisplaced

  • Fb = Buoyant force (N)
  • ρfluid = Density of the fluid (kg/m³)
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
  • Vdisplaced = Volume of fluid displaced (m³)

Conditions for Floating and Sinking

The buoyant force determines whether an object will float or sink:

  • Object Floats: Buoyant force ≥ weight of the object.
  • Object Sinks: Buoyant force < weight of the object.
  • Object is Neutrally Buoyant: Buoyant force = weight, the object remains suspended.

Examples

  • A wooden log floats because its weight is less than the buoyant force.
  • A stone sinks because its weight is greater than the buoyant force.

Applications of Archimedes' Principle

  • Ships and Boats: Ships are designed with hollow hulls to displace enough water to balance their weight.
  • Submarines: Control their buoyancy by adjusting the water inside ballast tanks.
  • Hot Air Balloons: Use the principle to float in air by reducing their density compared to the surrounding air.
  • Hydrometers: Measure the density of liquids based on how much they float.
  • Icebergs: Float because ice is less dense than water.

Mathematical Proof

From Newton’s second law, the net force acting on a submerged object is:

Fnet = Buoyant Force − Weight of Object

  • If Fnet > 0 → The object rises (floats).
  • If Fnet < 0 → The object sinks.
  • If Fnet = 0 → The object remains suspended.

Relation with Density

  • If density of object < density of fluid → The object floats.
  • If density of object > density of fluid → The object sinks.

Examples

  • Oil floats on water because oil has a lower density than water.
  • Iron sinks in water because it has a higher density than water.
