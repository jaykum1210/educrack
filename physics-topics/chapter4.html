Chapter 4 - Work, Energy, and Power

1.Work-Energy Theorem

Introduction

The Work-Energy Theorem is a fundamental principle in physics that establishes a direct relationship between the work done on an object and its kinetic energy. It states that the net work done by all forces acting on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy.

Statement of Work-Energy Theorem

The Work-Energy Theorem states:

                  Wnet = ΔKE = KEf − KEi

where,

  • Wnet = Net work done on the object
  • KEf = Final kinetic energy
  • KEi = Initial kinetic energy

This theorem implies that if a force does work on an object, it changes the object’s kinetic energy.

Derivation of Work-Energy Theorem

From Newton’s Second Law of Motion,
                   F=ma

Multiplying both sides by displacement dx,
                   Fdx=madx

Using the kinematic equation vdv=adx, we replace adx, in the equation,
                   Fdx = mvdv

Now, integrating both sides from initial velocity vi to final velocity vf, we get:

                   ∫xixf F dx = ∫vivf m v dv

Since work done is given by,
                   W=∫Fdx

And, kinetic energy is defined as:
                  KE = (1/2) m v2

So, the equation simplifies to:
                  Wnet = (1/2) m vf2 − (1/2) m vi2
                  Wnet = KEf − KEi

Understanding Work and Energy

  1. Work (W): The transfer of energy by a force acting over a displacement.
    • If work is positive, kinetic energy increases.
    • If work is negative, kinetic energy decreases.
  2. Kinetic Energy (KE): The energy possessed by a body due to its motion.
    • Formula: KE = (1/2) m v2
    • Kinetic energy depends on mass (m) and velocity (v).

Key Points of Work-Energy Theorem

  • The net work done by all forces on an object results in a change in kinetic energy.
  • If work is done by external forces, kinetic energy increases or decreases.
  • If no work is done, kinetic energy remains constant (conservation of energy).
  • It applies to both constant and variable forces.

Applications of Work-Energy Theorem

  1. Free-Fall Motion
    • When an object falls under gravity, gravitational force does positive work, increasing its kinetic energy.
  2. Braking in Vehicles
    • The brakes apply a force opposite to motion, doing negative work and decreasing kinetic energy.
  3. Projectile Motion
    • The kinetic energy changes as the speed of the projectile varies with height.
  4. Roller Coasters
    • Work done by gravity and external forces determines the speed at different points.
  5. Frictional Force
    • Friction does negative work, reducing kinetic energy and bringing objects to rest.

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

If only conservative forces (like gravity) act on an object, mechanical energy is conserved:

                  KEi + PEi = KEf + PEf

where,

  • KE = Kinetic Energy
  • PE = Potential Energy

This principle is an extension of the Work-Energy Theorem, showing that energy is transformed but remains conserved in an isolated system.

2. Potential and Kinetic Energy

Kinetic Energy (KE)

  • Definition: Energy possessed by a body due to its motion.
  • Formula:
                      

    KE = (1/2) m v2

    where m = mass, v = velocity.

  • Characteristics:
    • Increases with speed.
    • Directly proportional to mass and square of velocity.
    • Completely lost if the object comes to rest.
  • Examples:
    • A moving car.
    • A rolling ball.
    • Flowing water in a river.

Potential Energy (PE)

  • Definition: Energy stored in an object due to its position or configuration.
  • Types:
    1. Gravitational Potential Energy
      1. Energy due to height in a gravitational field.
      2. Formula: PE=mgh

        where m = mass, g = gravity, h = height.

      3. Example: Water stored in a dam.
    2. Elastic Potential Energy
      • Energy stored in stretched or compressed objects (springs, rubber bands).
      • Formula:

        PE = (1/2) k x2

        where k = spring constant, x = deformation.

      • Example: A stretched bow before releasing an arrow.

Relation Between KE and PE

  • Interconvertible: In a closed system, energy transforms between KE and PE.
  • Example:
    • A falling ball: PE → KE.
    • A projectile at highest point: KE → PE.
  • Total Mechanical Energy (E)= E=KE+PE
    • Conserved in the absence of external forces (e.g., gravity, friction).

Applications

  • Hydroelectric Dams: Convert PE (stored water) into KE (flowing water) to generate electricity.
  • Pendulum Motion: PE at the highest point converts to KE at the lowest point.
  • Roller Coasters: Gain PE when going up, convert to KE when going down.

3. Power

Power is the rate at which work is done or the rate at which energy is transferred. It tells us how fast a machine or a person can do work.

Definition:

Power is defined as the amount of work done per unit time. Mathematically:

P = W / t

where:

  • P = Power (Watt, W)
  • W = Work done (Joule, J)
  • t = Time taken (seconds, s)

SI Unit: Watt (W) (1 Watt = 1 Joule/second)

Other units:

  • Kilowatt (kW): 1 kW = 1000 W
  • Horsepower (HP): 1 HP ≈ 746 W

Power in Terms of Force and Velocity

From the work formula: W = F ⋅ d

Since velocity is displacement per unit time: v = d / t

Power can also be written as: P = F ⋅ v

where:

  • F = Force (Newton, N)
  • v = Velocity (m/s)

Application: This formula is useful for moving objects, engines, and vehicles.

Types of Power

(A) Mechanical Power

Power used in mechanical systems involving forces and motion.

P = F ⋅ v

Used in motors, vehicles, and mechanical work.

(B) Electrical Power

Power used in electrical circuits. Given by:

P = V ⋅ I

where:

  • V = Voltage (Volts)
  • I = Current (Amperes)

Using Ohm’s Law (V = I ⋅ R), power can be rewritten as:

P = I² ⋅ R = V² / R

Used in household appliances, power plants, and electrical devices.

(C) Thermal Power

Power in terms of heat energy:

P = Q / t

where:

  • Q = Heat energy transferred (J)
  • t = Time (s)

Used in heat engines, radiators, and thermal systems.

(D) Rotational Power

Power in rotating systems:

P = τ ⋅ ω

where:

  • τ = Torque (N·m)
  • ω = Angular velocity (rad/s)

Used in turbines, car engines, and rotating machinery.

Average Power and Instantaneous Power

(A) Average Power

Power over a period of time:

Pavg = Total work done / Total time taken

Used when power changes over time (e.g., machines with variable loads).

(B) Instantaneous Power

Power at a specific moment:

P = limΔt → 0 (ΔW / Δt)

Used in dynamic systems like electric circuits and engines.

Efficiency and Power Output

Power is never fully converted into useful work due to losses (e.g., heat, friction).

Efficiency (η)

η = (Useful Power Output / Total Power Input) × 100

Efficiency is always less than 100% due to energy losses.

Examples:

  • Electric motors: ~85–95% efficiency
  • Combustion engines: ~25–40% efficiency
  • Incandescent bulbs: ~5% efficiency (most energy lost as heat)

Applications of Power

  • Electric Power Supply: Used to measure household electricity usage (kWh).
  • Automobiles & Engines: Horsepower is used to measure engine performance.
  • Renewable Energy: Power output of solar panels and wind turbines.
  • Industrial Machines: Power consumption in factories and motors.
  • Human Body Power Output: A human can generate ~100-200 W of power while cycling.

4.Collision

Definition

  • A collision is an event where two or more bodies interact with each other for a very short duration, exchanging energy and momentum.

Types of Collisions

  1. Elastic Collision

    • Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.
    • No loss of energy in the form of heat or deformation.
    • Example: Collision between billiard balls, atomic and molecular collisions.
    • Formulas:

      Momentum conservation:

      m 1 u 1 + m 2 u 2 = m 1 v 1 + m 2 v 2

      Kinetic energy conservation:

      1 2 m 1 u 1 2 + 1 2 m 2 u 2 2 = 1 2 m 1 v 1 2 + 1 2 m 2 v 2 2

  2. Inelastic Collision

    • Momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not conserved (some energy is lost as heat, sound, or deformation).
    • Example: Car crashes, clay hitting the floor.

  3. Perfectly Inelastic Collision

    • Objects stick together after collision and move as one body.
    • Maximum kinetic energy is lost.
    • Example: Two pieces of clay colliding and sticking together.

Coefficient of Restitution (e)

  • e measures how "elastic" a collision is:
  • Formula: e = Relative velocity after collision / Relative velocity before collision
  • For elastic collision: e = 1
  • For inelastic collision: 0 < e < 1
  • For perfectly inelastic collision: e = 0

Applications of Collisions

  • Physics & Engineering: Designing car crash safety.
  • Astronomy: Understanding asteroid collisions.
  • Sports: Collision of balls in games like cricket, football, and billiards.
