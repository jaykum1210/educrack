Chapter 8 - Oscillations

1. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Introduction to SHM

  • Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a type of periodic motion where an object moves back and forth about a fixed point under a restoring force proportional to its displacement.

Examples

  • Motion of a pendulum ⏳
  • Vibration of a spring
  • Motion of atoms in a crystal lattice

Conditions for SHM

  • The restoring force is proportional to the displacement: F ∝ -x
  • Acceleration is proportional to displacement and directed towards the equilibrium position: a = -ω²x
  • The object oscillates around a stable equilibrium position.

Equation of Motion for SHM

  • General Equation: x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)
  • Velocity: v = -Aω sin(ωt + φ)
  • Acceleration: a = -Aω² cos(ωt + φ)

Time Period and Frequency

  • Time period (T): T = 2π / ω
  • Frequency (f): f = 1 / T

Energy in SHM

  • Kinetic Energy (KE): KE = (1/2) mω² (A² - x²)
  • Potential Energy (PE): PE = (1/2) mω² x²
  • Total Energy: E = (1/2) mω² A²

Examples of SHM

  • Simple Pendulum – A bob oscillating under gravity.
  • Spring-Mass System – A mass attached to a spring.
  • Vibrating Strings – Motion of musical instrument strings.

2. Damped and Forced Oscillations

Damped Oscillations

  • In real-world situations, oscillations do not continue forever because of resistive forces like friction or air resistance.
  • The amplitude decreases over time due to energy loss.
  • The damping force is proportional to velocity.
  • The motion eventually stops if damping is strong enough.

Equation of Damped Motion

m d 2 dt x + b d dt x + k x = 0

Types of Damping

  • Underdamping: Oscillations continue but gradually reduce in amplitude.
  • Critical Damping: The system returns to equilibrium in the shortest time without oscillating.
  • Overdamping: The system returns to equilibrium very slowly without oscillating.

Forced Oscillations

When an external periodic force is applied to a system, it undergoes forced oscillations.

Equation of Forced Motion

m d 2 dt x + b d dt x + k x = F0 cos ( ωt )

Resonance in Forced Oscillations

  • When the frequency of the external force matches the natural frequency of the system, resonance occurs.
  • Resonance leads to maximum amplitude and can cause structural damage.
  • Real-life applications include musical instruments, radio tuning, and earthquake-resistant buildings.

Comparison: Damped vs. Forced Oscillations

Feature Damped Oscillations Forced Oscillations
Cause Energy loss due to resistance External periodic force applied
Amplitude Decreases over time May increase if resonance occurs
Example A pendulum in air slows down Tuning a radio to a specific frequency

Applications

  • Car Shock Absorbers – Use damping to reduce vibrations.
  • Seismographs – Detect earthquakes using damping principles.
  • Musical Instruments – Strings undergo forced oscillations when plucked.
  • Buildings & Bridges – Designed to avoid resonance effects.

Conclusion

Damping helps control oscillations, while forced oscillations are useful in engineering, music, and physics!

3. Resonance

Definition

Resonance is a phenomenon that occurs when a system is subjected to an external periodic force with a frequency equal to its natural frequency. This leads to a large increase in amplitude of oscillation.

Example: When you push a swing at just the right moments (matching its natural frequency), the swing moves higher.

Conditions for Resonance

  • The system must have a natural frequency of oscillation.
  • An external periodic force must be applied.
  • The frequency of the external force must match the natural frequency of the system.

Explanation Using Forced Oscillation Equation

The equation governing forced oscillations is:

m d 2 x d t 2 + b d x d t + k x = F0 cos(ωt)

where:

  • m = Mass of the system
  • b = Damping coefficient
  • k = Spring constant
  • F0 = Amplitude of external force
  • ω = Frequency of external force

When ω matches the natural frequency, the amplitude becomes maximum, leading to resonance.

Types of Resonance

  • Mechanical Resonance – Occurs in mechanical systems like bridges, pendulums, and vibrating plates.
    Example: The collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge due to wind-induced resonance.
  • Acoustic Resonance – Occurs when sound waves match the natural frequency of an object.
    Example: Soundboards in musical instruments amplify certain frequencies.
  • Electrical Resonance – Occurs in circuits where inductance and capacitance create oscillations.
    Example: Tuning a radio to a specific frequency.
  • Optical Resonance – Occurs in lasers and optical cavities.
    Example: Laser light amplification.

Applications of Resonance

  • Musical Instruments – Amplify sound at resonant frequencies.
  • Microwave Ovens – Use electromagnetic resonance to heat food.
  • Radio and TV Transmission – Tuning circuits use resonance to select specific frequencies.
  • Seismology – Helps understand the resonance of buildings during earthquakes.

Disadvantages of Resonance

  • Structural Damage – Bridges and buildings can collapse if resonance occurs.
  • Excessive Vibrations – Machines and engines can fail due to uncontrolled resonance.
  • Unwanted Noise – Can cause loud and disturbing sounds in mechanical systems.

How to Reduce Harmful Resonance?

  • Damping – Adding resistive forces to reduce amplitude.
  • Changing Mass or Stiffness – Altering natural frequency to avoid resonance.
  • Shock Absorbers – Used in cars and buildings to minimize resonance effects.
