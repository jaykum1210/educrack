Chapter 16 - Wave Optics

1. Huygens' Principle

Statement of Huygens’ Principle

“Every point on a wavefront acts as a secondary source of new wavelets, which spread out in all directions at the same speed as the original wave. The new wavefront is found by drawing a surface tangent to all these secondary wavelets.”

This principle helps in understanding reflection, refraction, diffraction, and interference of waves.

Explanation of Wave Propagation

  • Wavefront: A surface connecting points of a wave that oscillate in the same phase.
  • Wavelets: Secondary waves emerging from every point of a wavefront.
  • New Wavefront: Formed by the envelope (tangent) of secondary wavelets.

Types of Wavefronts

  • Spherical Wavefront: Produced by a point source.
  • Cylindrical Wavefront: Produced by a line source.
  • Plane Wavefront: Formed by a distant source like the Sun.

Applications of Huygens’ Principle

i. Reflection of Light (Huygens' Explanation)

  • When a wavefront strikes a mirror, each point acts as a secondary source.
  • The reflected wavefront follows the law of reflection:
  • θi = θr (Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection)

ii. Refraction of Light (Huygens' Explanation)

  • When light enters a new medium, wavelets slow down or speed up, bending the wavefront.
  • The bending follows Snell’s Law:
  • i r = v 1 v 2 = n

    • where v1, v2 are the wave speeds in different media and n is the refractive index.

iii. Diffraction of Light

  • When waves pass through a small opening, they spread out.
  • Huygens’ Principle explains why light bends around obstacles instead of traveling in straight lines.

iv. Interference of Light

  • When two wavefronts overlap, the constructive and destructive interference patterns are explained using Huygens’ Principle.

2. Interference & Young’s Double-Slit Experiment

What is Interference?

  • Interference occurs when two or more waves overlap, forming a resultant wave.
  • Constructive Interference: Waves reinforce each other (bright fringes).
  • Destructive Interference: Waves cancel each other out (dark fringes).

Conditions for Interference

  • Sources must be coherent (same frequency and constant phase difference).
  • Sources must be monochromatic (single wavelength).

Young’s Double-Slit Experiment (YDSE)

Young’s Experiment (1801) demonstrated the wave nature of light through an interference pattern.

Experimental Setup

  • A monochromatic light source illuminates a single slit to ensure coherence.
  • The light reaches two closely spaced slits (S1 and S2), acting as coherent sources.
  • Interference patterns appear as alternating bright and dark fringes on the screen.

Mathematical Analysis of Interference

Path Difference & Fringe Formation

  • Constructive Interference (Bright Fringe): Δx = nλ, where n = 0,1,2,...
  • Destructive Interference (Dark Fringe): Δx = (2n+1)λ/2, where n = 0,1,2,...

Fringe Width

β = λD/d

  • β = Fringe width
  • λ = Wavelength of light
  • D = Distance between slits and screen
  • d = Distance between the two slits

Key Observations of YDSE

  • Fringes are equally spaced.
  • Fringe width increases with higher λ or larger D.
  • Fringe width decreases with greater d (slit separation).
  • Using white light produces colored fringes due to different wavelengths.

3. Diffraction of Light

Definition

  • Diffraction is the bending or spreading of waves when they pass through a narrow slit or around an obstacle.
  • More noticeable when the size of the obstacle or slit is comparable to the wavelength of the wave.
  • Occurs with all types of waves, including sound, water, and light waves.
  • Larger wavelength results in more diffraction.

Types of Diffraction

  1. Fresnel Diffraction (Near-Field Diffraction)
    • Occurs when the light source or screen is relatively close to the obstacle/slit.
    • The wavefronts are spherical or cylindrical.
    • The diffraction pattern varies with distance.
  2. Fraunhofer Diffraction (Far-Field Diffraction)
    • Occurs when the light source and screen are far from the slit.
    • The wavefronts are parallel (plane waves).
    • A well-defined diffraction pattern is formed, which can be easily analyzed mathematically.

Single-Slit Diffraction Experiment

When light passes through a single narrow slit, it spreads out, forming a diffraction pattern with a central bright fringe and alternating dark and bright fringes.

Key Features of the Pattern

  • Central maximum: The brightest and widest fringe at the center.
  • Secondary maxima: Fainter bright fringes on both sides.
  • Minima (dark fringes): Regions where destructive interference occurs.

Mathematical Analysis

  • For dark fringes (minima):

    a sin θ = m λ

    where:

    • a = Width of the slit
    • λ = Wavelength of light
    • θ = Angle of diffraction
    • m = Order of minimum (except central maximum)
  • The width of the central maximum is:

    W = 2 λ D a

    where D = Distance between slit and screen.

Difference Between Interference & Diffraction

Property Interference Diffraction
Number of slits Two or more Single slit or obstacle
Pattern Alternating bright and dark fringes Central maximum with decreasing intensity fringes
Fringe width Almost equal Varies, with wider central fringe

4. Polarization of Light

What is Polarization?

  • Polarization is the phenomenon in which the vibrations of light waves are restricted to a single plane.
  • Light waves are transverse waves, meaning their vibrations occur perpendicular to the direction of propagation.
  • Unpolarized light has vibrations in all possible directions perpendicular to the wave motion.
  • Polarized light has vibrations restricted to one direction only.

Types of Polarization

  1. Linear Polarization
    • The vibrations are confined to a single plane.
    • Achieved using a polaroid filter or reflection.
  2. Circular Polarization
    • The electric field vector rotates in a circular motion as the wave propagates.
    • Requires two perpendicular waves of equal amplitude with a 90° phase difference.
  3. Elliptical Polarization
    • The electric field traces an elliptical shape.
    • Happens when two perpendicular waves have different amplitudes and a phase difference.

Methods of Polarization

  1. Polarization by Selective Absorption (Polaroid Filter)
    • Special materials (Polaroids) absorb one plane of vibrations while allowing only one direction of light to pass.
    • Used in sunglasses, camera filters, and LCD screens.
  2. Polarization by Reflection (Brewster’s Law)
    • Light reflected at a special angle (Brewster’s angle) is completely polarized.
    • Brewster’s Law:
      tan θB = n2 n1

      where θB is Brewster’s angle and n1, n2 are refractive indices of the two media.

    • Used in anti-glare coatings on glasses and camera lenses.
  3. Polarization by Scattering
    • When light passes through a medium, it gets scattered, and the scattered light is partially polarized.
    • The blue sky is partially polarized due to scattering by air molecules.
    • Used in polarized sunglasses to reduce glare.
  4. Polarization by Double Refraction (Birefringence)
    • Some crystals (e.g., calcite, quartz) split light into two perpendicularly polarized rays:
      • Ordinary ray (O-ray): Follows normal refraction laws.
      • Extraordinary ray (E-ray): Does not follow normal refraction laws.
    • Used in optical instruments and stress analysis of materials.
