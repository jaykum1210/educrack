Chapter 12 - Magnetic Effects of Current and Magnetism

1. Magnetic Field Due to a Current

Magnetic Field Around a Straight Current-Carrying Conductor

When an electric current flows through a conductor, it generates a circular magnetic field around it.

Direction of Magnetic Field:

  • Right-Hand Thumb Rule: If you point your right-hand thumb along the current direction, your curled fingers show the direction of the magnetic field.

Magnetic Field Formula (Biot-Savart Law):

At a distance r from a straight wire:

B = (μ0 I) / (2πr)

Where:

  • B = Magnetic field strength (Tesla, T)
  • μ0 = Permeability of free space (4π × 10⁻⁷ T⋅m/A)
  • I = Current (A)
  • r = Distance from the wire (m)

Magnetic Field of a Circular Current Loop

A circular loop of current produces a magnetic field similar to a small bar magnet.

Magnetic Field at Center of Loop:

B = (μ0 I) / (2R)

Where:

  • R = Radius of the loop

Right-Hand Thumb Rule: Curl your fingers along the current direction, and your thumb points to the magnetic field direction.

Magnetic Field Inside a Long Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire with closely spaced turns.

Magnetic Field Formula:

B = μ0 n I

Where:

  • n = Number of turns per unit length (N/L)
  • I = Current (A)

Properties:

  • The magnetic field inside a solenoid is uniform and parallel.
  • Solenoids act like bar magnets with north and south poles.

Magnetic Field Inside a Toroid

A toroid is a solenoid bent into a circular shape.

Magnetic Field Formula:

B = (μ0 N I) / (2πr)

Where:

  • N = Total number of turns
  • r = Radius of the toroid

Properties:

  • The magnetic field inside a toroid is strong and confined within the core.

2. Ampere's Law

Statement of Ampere’s Law

Ampere’s Law relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the electric current passing through it.

Mathematical Expression:

∮ B ⋅ dl = μ0 Ienc

Where:

  • ∮ B ⋅ dl = Line integral of the magnetic field around a closed loop
  • μ0 = Permeability of free space (4π × 10⁻⁷ T⋅m/A)
  • Ienc = Net current enclosed by the loop

The circulation of the magnetic field around a closed path is proportional to the total current enclosed.

Applications of Ampere’s Law

(a) Magnetic Field Due to a Long, Straight Current-Carrying Wire

For a straight wire carrying current I, the magnetic field at a distance r from the wire is:

B = (μ0 I) / (2πr)

Properties:

  • The magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire.
  • Follows the Right-Hand Thumb Rule.

(b) Magnetic Field Inside a Long Solenoid

For a solenoid (a coil of wire), Ampere’s Law gives:

B = μ0 n I

Where:

  • n = N / L (turns per unit length)
  • I = Current (A)

Properties:

  • The magnetic field inside a solenoid is uniform and parallel.
  • Solenoids behave like bar magnets with north and south poles.

(c) Magnetic Field Inside a Toroid

For a toroid (a solenoid bent into a circular shape), Ampere’s Law gives:

B = (μ0 N I) / (2πr)

Where:

  • N = Total number of turns
  • r = Radius of the toroid

Properties:

  • The magnetic field is confined inside the toroid.
  • Stronger and more concentrated than in a solenoid.

3. Magnetic Properties of Materials

Magnetic materials respond to external magnetic fields in different ways, depending on their atomic structure and electron configuration. These properties are crucial for designing electromagnets, transformers, motors, and data storage devices.

Types of Magnetic Materials

(a) Diamagnetic Materials

Definition: Weakly repelled by a magnetic field.

Cause: No permanent magnetic dipoles; induced dipoles oppose the applied field.

Examples: Copper, Gold, Water, Bismuth.

Properties:

  • Negative magnetic susceptibility (χm < 0).
  • No permanent magnetism.
  • Weak and opposite induced magnetic moment.

(b) Paramagnetic Materials

Definition: Weakly attracted by a magnetic field.

Cause: Presence of unpaired electrons that align with the external field.

Examples: Aluminium, Platinum, Oxygen.

Properties:

  • Positive but small magnetic susceptibility (χm > 0).
  • Magnetism disappears when the external field is removed.
  • Follows Curie's Law: χm = C/T, where C is Curie’s constant and T is temperature.

(c) Ferromagnetic Materials

Definition: Strongly attracted to a magnetic field and retain magnetism.

Cause: Permanent atomic magnetic moments align parallel due to exchange interaction.

Examples: Iron (Fe), Nickel (Ni), Cobalt (Co).

Properties:

  • Very high magnetic susceptibility (χm ≫ 0).
  • Can be permanently magnetized.
  • Exhibit hysteresis (retain magnetism after field removal).
  • Have a Curie temperature (TC), above which they become paramagnetic.

(d) Antiferromagnetic Materials

Definition: Neighboring atomic dipoles align oppositely, canceling out magnetism.

Examples: Manganese oxide (MnO), Nickel oxide (NiO).

Properties:

  • Zero net magnetization.
  • Transition to paramagnetic behavior above the Néel temperature.

(e) Ferrimagnetic Materials

Definition: Similar to antiferromagnetic materials, but opposing moments are unequal, leading to net magnetism.

Examples: Magnetite (Fe3O4), Ferrites.

Properties:

  • Net magnetization but weaker than ferromagnetic materials.
  • Used in magnetic storage and transformers.

Hysteresis Curve (Magnetization vs. Magnetic Field)

Hysteresis: The lag between magnetization and applied magnetic field.

Key Points:

  • Retentivity: Residual magnetization after removing the field.
  • Coercivity: Reverse field needed to demagnetize.

Hard vs. Soft Magnetic Materials:

  • Hard magnets (steel) retain magnetism (high coercivity).
  • Soft magnets (iron) lose magnetism easily (low coercivity).

Applications of Magnetic Materials

  • Diamagnetic: Magnetic levitation, superconductors.
  • Paramagnetic: MRI scanning, oxygen detection.
  • Ferromagnetic: Permanent magnets, transformers, electric motors.
  • Ferrimagnetic: Data storage, microwave devices.
  • Antiferromagnetic: Spintronics, magnetic recording.

4. Forces in Magnetic Fields

When a charged particle or a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force. These forces play a fundamental role in electric motors, generators, and various electromagnetic applications.

1. Force on a Moving Charge in a Magnetic Field

Lorentz Force

When a charge q moves with velocity v in a magnetic field B, it experiences a force given by:

F = q (v × B)

Right-hand rule for direction:

  • Point fingers in the direction of v.
  • Curl fingers towards B.
  • Thumb points in the direction of F (for a positive charge, opposite for a negative charge).

Magnitude of the force:

F = qvB sinθ, where θ is the angle between v and B.

Special Cases:

  • If θ = 0° or 180°, then F = 0 (No force, charge moves straight).
  • If θ = 90°, then F = qvB (Maximum force, charge moves in a circular path).

Motion of a Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

Circular Motion: If v is perpendicular to B, the force acts as a centripetal force, leading to circular motion.

Radius of circular path:

r = (mv) / (qB)

Time period of motion:

T = (2πm) / (qB)

Frequency (cyclotron frequency):

f = (qB) / (2πm)

Helical Motion: If v has a component along B, the charge moves in a helical path.

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

When a conductor of length l carrying current I is placed in a uniform magnetic field B, it experiences a force:

F = I (l × B)

Magnitude: F = IlB sinθ

Right-hand rule for direction:

  • Thumb = direction of current I.
  • Fingers = direction of B.
  • Palm = direction of force F (for a positive charge).

Special Cases:

  • If θ = 0° or 180°, then F = 0 (No force).
  • If θ = 90°, then F = IlB (Maximum force).

Force Between Two Parallel Current-Carrying Conductors

Two parallel wires carrying currents I1 and I2 experience a force due to their magnetic fields.

Force per unit length:

F = (μ0 / 2π) * (I1 I2 / d)

where d is the separation between the wires and μ0 is the permeability of free space.

Direction of Force:

  • If currents are in the same direction, the wires attract.
  • If currents are opposite, the wires repel.

Torque on a Current-Carrying Loop in a Magnetic Field

A current loop in a uniform magnetic field experiences torque, given by:

τ = NIAB sinθ

where:

  • N = Number of turns.
  • A = Area of the loop.
  • θ = Angle between the normal to the loop and B.

This principle is used in electric motors and galvanometers.

Applications of Magnetic Forces

  • Electric motors: Uses force on a current-carrying loop.
  • Particle accelerators: Uses Lorentz force to guide charged particles.
  • Mass spectrometers: Identifies elements by bending ion paths.
  • Magnetic levitation (Maglev trains): Uses repulsion between superconducting magnets.
  • Electromagnetic braking: Uses eddy currents to slow down moving objects.
