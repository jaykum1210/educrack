Chapter 10 - Electrostatics

1. Electric Charges and Fields

Introduction

Electric charges are fundamental properties of matter that cause electromagnetic interactions. The study of electric charges and their effects in space is called Electrostatics.

Types of Electric Charges

  • Positive Charge (+q): Carried by protons.
  • Negative Charge (-q): Carried by electrons.
  • Neutral Objects: Have an equal number of positive and negative charges.

Basic Properties of Charge

  • Quantization of Charge: Charge exists in discrete packets as an integral multiple of the elementary charge.
  • Formula: q = ± n e where e = 1.6 × 10 -19 C .
  • Charge Conservation: The total charge in an isolated system remains constant.
  • Charge is Additive: The total charge of a system is the algebraic sum of individual charges.

Coulomb’s Law

It describes the force between two stationary point charges.

F = k × | q1 q2 | r 2

where:

  • F: Electrostatic force (N)
  • k: Coulomb’s constant = 9 × 10 9 N m 2 / C 2
  • q1, q2: Magnitudes of charges (C)
  • r: Distance between charges (m)

Electric Field (E)

The electric field at a point is the force experienced per unit positive charge placed at that point.

E = F q

For a point charge:

E = k | Q | r 2

Electric Dipole

A system of two equal and opposite charges (+q and -q) separated by a distance 2a.

Dipole Moment (p):

p = q × 2 a

SI Unit: Coulomb-meter (C·m)

Electric Field of a Dipole

On Axial Line:

E = 2 k p r 3

On Equatorial Line:

E = k p r 3

Torque on a Dipole in a Uniform Electric Field

τ = p E sin θ

Gauss's Law

Gauss’s law states that the total electric flux through a closed surface is proportional to the total charge enclosed by the surface.

∯ E ⋅ dA = Qenc ε0

where:

  • Qenc: Charge enclosed inside the surface (C)
  • ε0: Permittivity of free space = 8.85 × 10 -12 C2/N m2

2. Gauss's Law

  • Electric flux represents the number of electric field lines passing through a surface.
  • Formula: Φ E = ∯ E ⋅ dA = E A cos θ

    where ΦE = Electric flux, E = Electric field, A = Area, θ = Angle between E and A.

  • If E is perpendicular to the surface, θ = 0° and ΦE = EA.
  • If E is parallel to the surface, θ = 90° and ΦE = 0.

Gauss's Law

  • Gauss’s Law states that the total electric flux through a closed surface is proportional to the charge enclosed.
  • Formula: ∯ E ⋅ dA = Q ε0

    where Q = Charge enclosed, ε0 = Permittivity of free space.

Applications of Gauss’s Law

  1. Electric Field Due to an Infinitely Long Line Charge

    • Charge density: λ (charge per unit length).
    • Using a cylindrical Gaussian surface, the electric field is:
    • E = λ 2 π ε0 r

      • where r = Distance from the line charge.

  2. Electric Field Due to an Infinite Plane Sheet of Charge

    • Surface charge density: σ (charge per unit area).
    • Using a pillbox Gaussian surface, the electric field is:
    • E = σ 2 ε0

      • The field is uniform and does not depend on distance.

  3. Electric Field Due to a Spherical Shell

    • Outside the sphere (r > R): The sphere acts as a point charge.
    • E = 1 / 4 π ε0 Q / r2

      • Similar to Coulomb’s Law.

    • Inside a conductor (r < R): E = 0 (Electrostatic Shielding).

Importance of Gauss’s Law

  • Simplifies electric field calculations for symmetrical charge distributions.
  • Explains why electric fields inside conductors are zero.
  • Used in capacitor design and electrostatic shielding.

3. Electric Potential and Electric Potential Energy

Introduction

  • Electric potential and electric potential energy describe how charges interact in an electric field.
  • Electric potential (V): The amount of electric potential energy per unit charge.
  • Electric potential energy (U): The energy stored due to a charge's position in an electric field.

Electric Potential (V)

Definition

  • Electric potential at a point is the work done per unit charge to move a small test charge from infinity to that point without acceleration.
  • Formula: V = W/q

    where V = Electric potential (V), W = Work done (J), q = Charge (C).

  • SI unit: Volt (V)
  • 1 Volt = 1 Joule per Coulomb (1V = 1 J/C)

Electric Potential Due to a Point Charge

  • Formula: V = kQ/r

    where k = 9×109 Nm2/C2, Q = Source charge (C), r = Distance from charge (m).

  • Potential is positive for a positive charge and negative for a negative charge.
  • Electric potential is a scalar quantity.

Electric Potential Due to Multiple Charges

  • For multiple charges, total potential at a point is:

    Vnet = V1 + V2 + V3 + ...

    or Vnet = ∑(kQi/ri)

  • Unlike electric fields, electric potentials add algebraically since they are scalars.

Potential Difference (ΔV)

  • Potential difference between two points A and B is:

    ΔV = VB - VA = WAB/q

  • Electric field and potential difference relation: E = -dV/dr
  • Electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential.

Electric Potential Energy (U)

Definition

  • Electric potential energy is the work done in assembling a charge system or moving a charge in an electric field.
  • Formula: U = qV
  • SI unit: Joule (J)

Potential Energy of a System of Two Charges

  • Formula: U = kq1q2/r
  • If q1 and q2 have the same sign, U is positive (repulsion).
  • If q1 and q2 have opposite signs, U is negative (attraction).
  • If r → ∞, then U → 0.

Work Done in Moving a Charge

  • Work done in moving charge q from A to B:

    • W = q(VB - VA) = qΔV

  • Positive work: Work done against the field (energy stored).
  • Negative work: Work done by the field (energy released).

Equipotential Surfaces

  • Surfaces where electric potential is constant.
  • No work is required to move a charge along an equipotential surface.
  • Electric field is perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.
  • In a uniform field, equipotential surfaces are parallel planes.

Relation Between Electric Field and Electric Potential

  • Electric field is the negative gradient of potential:

    • E = -dV/dr

  • Stronger electric fields correspond to greater potential differences over shorter distances.

4. Capacitors and Dielectrics

Definition

  • A capacitor is an electrical component that stores electric charge and energy in an electric field.
  • It consists of two conductors (plates) separated by an insulating material (dielectric).
  • Symbol in circuit diagrams: Two parallel lines ( || ).

Capacitance (C)

  • Capacitance is the ability of a capacitor to store charge per unit voltage.
  • Formula: C = Q/V

    • where C = Capacitance (Farad, F), Q = Charge stored (Coulomb, C), V = Potential difference (Volt, V).

  • SI Unit: Farad (F) → 1F = 1 C/V.
  • Capacitors are usually measured in μF, nF, or pF.
  • Higher capacitance means more charge storage for the same voltage.

Parallel Plate Capacitor

  • Capacitance formula: C = (ε₀ A) / d

    • where ε₀ = permittivity of free space (8.85×10⁻¹² F/m), A = Plate area (m²), d = Separation (m).

  • Increased plate area → Higher capacitance.
  • Increased separation → Lower capacitance.

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

  • Energy stored formula: U = (1/2) C V²

    • where U = Energy stored (J), C = Capacitance (F), V = Voltage (V).

  • Energy is stored in the electric field between the plates.
  • Applications: Camera flashes, power backups, oscillators.

Capacitors in Series and Parallel

i Series Connection

  • Formula: 1/C_eq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + …
  • Same charge on all capacitors.
  • Total voltage is the sum of individual voltages.
  • Total capacitance is smaller than the smallest capacitor.

ii. Parallel Connection

  • Formula: C_eq = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + …
  • Same voltage across all capacitors.
  • Total charge is the sum of individual charges.
  • Total capacitance is larger than any single capacitor.

Effect of Dielectrics

  • Dielectric: An insulating material placed between plates to increase capacitance.
  • Common dielectrics: Glass, mica, paper, plastic, ceramic.
  • Formula: C' = K C

    • where C' = Capacitance with dielectric, K = Dielectric constant (K > 1).

  • Higher dielectric constant → Higher capacitance.
  • Applications: Memory storage, signal processing, energy storage.
