Chapter 21 - Thermodynamics

1. Laws of Thermodynamics

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

  • If two systems are in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other.
  • Defines the concept of temperature and thermal equilibrium.

First Law of Thermodynamics

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or converted.
  • Formula: ΔQ = ΔU + W

    where ΔQ = Heat supplied, ΔU = Change in internal energy, W = Work done by the system.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

  • Heat flows from hot to cold, and entropy always increases.
  • Formula: ΔS = ΔQ / T

    where ΔS = Change in entropy, ΔQ = Heat transfer, T = Temperature in Kelvin.

Third Law of Thermodynamics

  • As temperature approaches absolute zero (0K), entropy approaches zero.
  • Absolute zero is practically impossible to achieve.

Comparison of Thermodynamic Laws

Law Main Concept Key Idea
Zeroth Law Thermal Equilibrium Defines temperature
First Law Energy Conservation Energy is neither created nor destroyed
Second Law Entropy Increase Heat flows hot → cold, entropy increases
Third Law Absolute Zero Perfect order at 0K, impossible to reach

Applications of Thermodynamics

  • Heat engines (Car engines, steam turbines).
  • Refrigeration (Using the second law to remove heat).
  • Power plants (Converting heat into electricity).
  • Air conditioners (Thermodynamic cycles for cooling).

2. Thermodynamic Processes

Definition

  • A thermodynamic process refers to the change in the state of a system due to heat, work, or both.
  • These processes describe how gases behave under changes in pressure, volume, and temperature.

Types of Thermodynamic Processes

Isothermal Process (Constant Temperature, T = Constant)

  • Temperature remains constant throughout the process.
  • Heat exchange occurs to maintain a constant temperature.
  • Equation: PV = constant (Boyle’s Law)
  • Work done: W = nRT ln(Vf / Vi)
  • Example: Slow gas expansion or compression in a heat reservoir.

Adiabatic Process (No Heat Exchange, Q = 0)

  • No heat is exchanged with the surroundings.
  • Energy change is due to work done on/by the system.
  • Equation: PVγ = constant (Poisson’s Law)
  • Work done: W = (PiVi - PfVf) / (γ - 1)
  • Example: Sudden compression or expansion of gas in an insulated container.

Isobaric Process (Constant Pressure, P = Constant)

  • Pressure remains constant while volume and temperature change.
  • Work done: W = PΔV
  • Heat transfer: Q = ΔU + W
  • Example: Heating water in an open container.

Isochoric Process (Constant Volume, V = Constant)

  • Volume remains constant, meaning no work is done (W = 0).
  • Heat transfer changes only the internal energy: Q = ΔU
  • Example: Heating gas in a rigid, closed container.

Cyclic and Non-Cyclic Processes

  • Cyclic Process: System returns to its initial state after a series of thermodynamic changes (e.g., heat engines).
  • Non-Cyclic Process: System does not return to its initial state (e.g., water heating and evaporating).

Quasistatic and Irreversible Processes

  • Quasistatic Process: Occurs infinitely slowly, keeping the system in equilibrium.
  • Irreversible Process: Happens rapidly, causing disequilibrium and energy dissipation (e.g., sudden gas expansion).

Comparison of Thermodynamic Processes

Process Constant Quantity Equation Example
Isothermal Temperature (T) PV = constant Slow gas expansion
Adiabatic No heat (Q = 0) PVγ = constant Sudden gas compression
Isobaric Pressure (P) W = PΔV Heating water in an open container
Isochoric Volume (V) Q = ΔU Heating gas in a rigid container

Applications of Thermodynamic Processes

  • Heat engines – Use cyclic processes to convert heat into work.
  • Refrigerators – Use isothermal and adiabatic expansions.
  • Internal combustion engines – Utilize adiabatic and isobaric processes.

3. Kinetic Theory of Gases

Assumptions of Kinetic Theory

  • Gas molecules are in continuous random motion.
  • The size of gas molecules is negligible compared to the distance between them.
  • Molecules undergo perfectly elastic collisions (no energy loss).
  • No intermolecular forces exist except during collisions.
  • The average kinetic energy of gas molecules is proportional to temperature in Kelvin.
  • The time spent in collisions is much smaller than the time spent moving freely.

Pressure Exerted by a Gas

  • When gas molecules collide with container walls, they exert pressure.
  • Equation: P V = 1 3 mN v rms

Root Mean Square (RMS) Speed

  • Formula: v rms = 3 k B T m

Kinetic Energy of Gas Molecules

  • Formula: KE avg = 3 2 k B T

Degrees of Freedom and Energy Distribution

  • Monoatomic gases (e.g., He, Ne, Ar) → 3 degrees (translational motion only).
  • Diatomic gases (e.g., O₂, N₂) → 5 degrees (translational + rotational motion).
  • Polyatomic gases → 6 or more degrees of freedom.

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

  • Describes how the speeds of gas molecules are spread out in a sample.
  • Higher temperatures shift the distribution rightward, increasing molecular speeds.

Mean Free Path (λ)

  • Formula: λ = k B T 2 π d 2 P
  • Higher pressure → Shorter mean free path.
  • Lower pressure → Longer mean free path.
