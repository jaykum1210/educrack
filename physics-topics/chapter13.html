Chapter 13 - Electromagnetic Induction and Alternating Current

1. Faraday’s Law and Lenz’s Law

Faraday’s Laws of Electromagnetic Induction

Michael Faraday discovered that a changing magnetic field induces an electromotive force (EMF) in a conductor. This is the principle behind electromagnetic induction.

First Law (Law of Induction)

An EMF (electromotive force) is induced in a conductor whenever there is a change in the magnetic flux linked with it.

Magnetic flux (ΦB) is given by:

ΦB = B A cosθ

where:

  • B = Magnetic field strength
  • A = Area of the loop
  • θ = Angle between the magnetic field and the normal to the loop

Ways to Change Magnetic Flux:

  • Changing the magnetic field (B)
  • Changing the area of the loop (A)
  • Changing the angle (θ) between the loop and the field

Second Law (Mathematical Formulation of Induction)

The induced EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux:

E = − (dΦB / dt)

where:

  • E = Induced EMF
  • (dΦB / dt) = Rate of change of magnetic flux

The negative sign represents Lenz’s Law (explained next).

For a coil with N turns:

E = − N (dΦB / dt)

Lenz’s Law

Lenz’s Law states:

The direction of the induced current is such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that caused it.

Explanation:

  • If the magnetic flux increases, the induced current flows in a direction that opposes the increase.
  • If the magnetic flux decreases, the induced current flows in a way that tries to maintain the flux.

Mathematical Representation:

The negative sign in Faraday’s Law:

E = − N (dΦB / dt)

ensures that the induced current always opposes the change in flux.

Right-Hand Rule for Lenz’s Law:

  • Point your thumb in the direction of the increasing magnetic field.
  • Curl your fingers – they show the direction of the induced current.

Applications of Faraday’s and Lenz’s Laws

  • Electric Generators: Converts mechanical energy to electrical energy by rotating a coil in a magnetic field.
  • Transformers: Uses changing flux to transfer energy between coils.
  • Eddy Currents: Used in braking systems (e.g., magnetic brakes in roller coasters).
  • Induction Stoves: Uses electromagnetic induction to heat cookware.
  • Wireless Charging: Transfers power via induced EMF.

2. Self and Mutual Inductance

Self-Inductance

Self-inductance is the property of a coil (or circuit) to oppose the change in current flowing through it by inducing an EMF in itself.

Definition:

When the current in a coil changes, the magnetic field around it also changes. This changing magnetic field induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the same coil, opposing the change in current.

Formula for Self-Inductance (L)

The induced EMF (E) is proportional to the rate of change of current:

E = −L (dI / dt)

where:

  • L = Self-inductance (Henry, H)
  • dI / dt = Rate of change of current

The negative sign follows Lenz’s Law (opposing change in current).

Expression for Self-Inductance

Self-inductance is given by:

L = (N ΦB) / I

where:

  • N = Number of turns in the coil
  • ΦB = Magnetic flux linked with the coil
  • I = Current flowing through the coil

For a solenoid of length l, area A, and turns per unit length n:

L = μ0 μr n² A / l

where:

  • μ0 = Permeability of free space
  • μr = Relative permeability of the core material

Example: An inductor in a circuit (like a coil in a transformer).

Mutual Inductance

Mutual inductance is the property of two coils where a changing current in one coil induces an EMF in the other coil.

Definition:

If two coils are placed close to each other, a change in current in the first coil produces a changing magnetic field, which induces an EMF in the second coil.

Formula for Mutual Inductance (M)

The induced EMF in the second coil due to a changing current in the first coil:

E2 = −M (dI1 / dt)

Similarly, the EMF induced in the first coil due to a changing current in the second coil:

E1 = −M (dI2 / dt)

where:

  • M = Mutual inductance (Henry, H)
  • I1, I2 = Currents in the coils
  • E1, E2 = Induced EMFs in coils

Expression for Mutual Inductance

Mutual inductance depends on the flux linkage:

M = (N2 ΦB2) / I1 = (N1 ΦB1) / I2

where:

  • N1, N2 = Number of turns in each coil
  • ΦB1, ΦB2 = Magnetic flux linked with the coils
  • I1, I2 = Currents in the coils

For two long solenoids:

M = μ0 μr N1 N2 A / l

Example: Transformers use mutual inductance to transfer energy between primary and secondary coils.

Difference Between Self and Mutual Inductance

Property Self-Inductance (L) Mutual Inductance (M)
Definition Induction of EMF in the same coil Induction of EMF in a second coil
Formula E = −L (dI / dt) E = −M (dI / dt)
Depends On Coil’s own magnetic field and turns Coupling between two coils
Applications Inductors, solenoids Transformers, coupled circuits

Applications of Inductance

  • Transformers: Uses mutual inductance to step up/down voltage.
  • Motors & Generators: Work on electromagnetic induction principles.
  • Inductors in Circuits: Store energy in magnetic fields.
  • Wireless Power Transfer: Uses mutual inductance for wireless charging.

3. Alternating Current (AC) Circuits

Introduction to AC Circuits

  • An Alternating Current (AC) circuit is a circuit where the current changes direction periodically.
  • Unlike Direct Current (DC), which flows in one direction, AC varies in magnitude and direction over time.
  • General form of AC voltage: V = V0 sin(ωt)
  • General form of AC current: I = I0 sin(ωt)

Root Mean Square (RMS) Value of AC

  • Since AC voltage and current vary over time, the RMS value represents an equivalent DC value.
  • Voltage (RMS): Vrms = V0 / √2
  • Current (RMS): Irms = I0 / √2

AC Circuit Elements

i. AC Through a Resistor

  • Voltage and current are in phase.
  • Ohm’s Law: Vrms = Irms R
  • Phase Difference = 0°

ii. AC Through an Inductor

  • Current lags voltage by 90°.
  • Inductive Reactance: XL = 2πfL
  • Phase Difference = -90°

iii. AC Through a Capacitor

  • Current leads voltage by 90°.
  • Capacitive Reactance: XC = 1 / (2πfC)
  • Phase Difference = +90°

Series RLC Circuit

  • Contains a resistor (R), an inductor (L), and a capacitor (C) connected in series.
  • Impedance (Z) = √(R² + (XL - XC)²)
  • Phase angle: tanϕ = (XL - XC) / R

Resonance in AC Circuits

  • Occurs when XL = XC.
  • Resonant Frequency: f0 = 1 / (2π√(LC))
  • At resonance, impedance is minimum and current is maximum.

Power in AC Circuits

  • Instantaneous Power: P = VI
  • Average Power: Pavg = Vrms Irms cosϕ
  • Power Factor = cosϕ (determines efficiency).

Applications of AC Circuits

  • Power Transmission – AC is used in electrical grids due to easy voltage transformation.
  • Transformers – Work on mutual inductance for voltage regulation.
  • Tuned Circuits – Used in radio, TV, and wireless communication.
  • Electric Motors & Generators – Operate on AC principles.

4. AC Generators and Transformers

i. AC Generator (Alternator)

Introduction

  • An AC Generator (or Alternator) converts mechanical energy into electrical energy using electromagnetic induction.

Principle of Operation

  • Based on Faraday's Law: "A changing magnetic flux through a coil induces an electromotive force (EMF) in it."

Construction

  • Armature (Coil) – A rectangular coil of wire wound on a metal core.
  • Magnetic Field – A permanent magnet or electromagnet provides the magnetic field.
  • Slip Rings – Two metal rings connected to the coil, rotating with it.
  • Brushes – Carbon brushes transfer current to the external circuit.
  • Shaft – Rotates the coil inside the magnetic field.

Working

  • The rotating coil in the magnetic field changes the magnetic flux.
  • According to Faraday’s Law, an EMF (voltage) is induced.
  • The direction of induced EMF follows Lenz’s Law.
  • The induced voltage is alternating (AC), changing direction periodically.
  • Current is collected through slip rings and brushes.

Equation of Induced EMF

  • Formula: E = E0 sin(ωt)

    • where E0 = Maximum EMF, ω = Angular frequency (2πf), t = Time.

Frequency of AC Output

  • Formula: f = (N × P) / 120

    • where N = Speed of rotation (RPM), P = Number of poles.

Advantages

  • Simple construction – No commutators needed.
  • Efficient power transmission – Easily transformed using transformers.
  • Low maintenance – Uses slip rings instead of a commutator.
  • Widely used – In power plants, wind turbines, hydroelectric plants, etc.

ii. Transformers

Introduction

  • A transformer is an electrical device that changes AC voltage while keeping the frequency constant, based on mutual induction.

Principle

  • Works on Faraday's Law – A changing current in the primary coil induces a voltage in the secondary coil.

Construction

  • Primary Coil – Connected to the AC power supply.
  • Secondary Coil – Provides transformed output voltage.
  • Iron Core – Guides magnetic flux efficiently.

Working

  • AC voltage in the primary coil creates a changing magnetic field.
  • The flux induces an EMF in the secondary coil.
  • Voltage can be increased (step-up) or decreased (step-down) depending on the turns ratio.

Transformer Equation

  • Formula: Vs / Vp = Ns / Np

    • where Vp, Vs are voltages, and Np, Ns are coil turns.

  • Step-Up Transformer: Ns > Np → Increases voltage.
  • Step-Down Transformer: Ns < Np → Decreases voltage.

Power in a Transformer

  • Ideal transformers conserve power: Pp = Ps
  • Formula: Vp Ip = Vs Is

    • If voltage increases, current decreases and vice versa.

Efficiency

  • Formula: η = (Output Power / Input Power) × 100%
  • Practical efficiency is above 95%, but losses occur due to:
    • Copper Loss – Due to resistance in wires.
    • Iron Loss – Due to hysteresis and eddy currents.

Applications

  • Power Transmission – Steps up voltage for long distances.
  • Voltage Regulation – Used in stabilizers and power adapters.
  • Electronics – Found in chargers, appliances, and radio circuits.
