Chapter 1 - Physics and Measurement

1. SI Units & Dimensions

i. What are SI Units?

SI (International System of Units) is a system of standard measurements used all over the world. It ensures that measurements are the same everywhere, making it easier for scientists, engineers, and even students to understand and use.

For example, if you measure your height in India using meters, someone in the USA will understand it the same way because they also use meters in science.

Why Do We Need SI Units?

Before SI units, different countries used different measurement systems. This caused confusion! Imagine someone using inches, another using centimeters, and another using cubits (an ancient unit)!

Benefits of SI Units:

  • Universal - The same everywhere.
  • Easy to Use - Based on the number 10
  • Helps in Science - Used in physics, chemistry, and everyday life.
  • Prevents Mistakes – Avoids confusion in measurements.

Example:

  • A scientist in Japan and a scientist in the USA can understand each other's work easily because they both use SI units.

ii. The 7 Fundamental SI Units

The SI system is built on seven basic units, known as fundamental units. These units are used to measure different things like length, weight, and time.

Quantity SI Unit Symbol What It Measures Example
Length Meter m Distance Height of a tree
Time Second s Duration How long a song lasts
Temperature Kelvin K Hot or cold The temperature of the sun
Electric Current Ampere A Flow of electricity Power in a TV
Amount of Substance Mole mol Number of particles Number of atoms in water
Luminous Intensity Candela cd Brightness Light from a bulb

Fun Fact:

  • The meter was originally defined as one ten-millionth of the distance from the equator to the North Pole!
  • The second is measured using atomic clocks that count vibrations of cesium atoms!

iii. Derived SI Units – Making New Units from Basic Ones!

Derived units are created by combining two or more fundamental SI units.

Pressure Pascal Pa Force ÷ Area Air pressure in a tire
Density Kilogram per Cubic Meter kg/m³ Mass ÷ Volume Density of water

Electric Charge Coulomb C Current × Time Charge stored in a battery

Fun Fact:

  • The unit Newton (N) is named after Sir Isaac Newton, who discovered the laws of motion!

iv. What is a Dimension?

A dimension tells us what a quantity is made of. It is expressed using fundamental units (like meters, kilograms, and seconds).

Example of Dimensions:

  • Length = [L]
  • Mass = [M]
  • Time = [T]

When we combine units, we can express the dimensions of other physical quantities.

Quantity Formula Dimensions
Speed Distance ÷ Time [L][T]⁻¹
Acceleration Speed ÷ Time [L][T]⁻²
Force Mass × Acceleration [M][L][T]⁻²
Energy Force × Distance [M][L]²[T]⁻²
Power Energy ÷ Time [M][L]²[T]⁻³

v. Why are Dimensions Important?

  1. Checks if equations are correct - Helps scientists verify if a formula is correct.
  2. Converts units easily - Helps convert from one unit to another.
  3. Understands relationships - Shows how different physical quantities are related.

vi. Applications of SI Units & Dimensions in Daily Life

SI units are everywhere in our daily lives! Here’s how:

  • In School – Measuring length with rulers, weighing things in kilograms.
  • In Sports – Timing races in seconds, measuring distances in meters.
  • In Cooking – Measuring weight in grams and liters for liquids.
  • In Science – Experiments, physics, and chemistry use SI units.
  • In Medicine – Dosages in milligrams, body temperature in Kelvin or Celsius.
  • In Space – Astronomers use SI units to measure distances in light-years!

vii. Fun Facts About SI Units!

  • The kilogram is the only SI unit that is still defined by a physical object – a platinum-iridium cylinder kept in France!
  • The speed of light in a vacuum is exactly 299,792,458 meters per second.
  • The candela is based on the brightness of a candle!
  • The Mole is used to count atmos - 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (that’s a huge number!).

2. Errors in Measurement

i. What are Errors in Measurement?

When we measure something, we try to be as accurate as possible. But sometimes, small mistakes (errors) happen due to various reasons, like faulty instruments, human mistakes, or environmental conditions.

For example:

  • If you measure a table’s length with a ruler and get 99 cm, but the actual length is 100 cm, then your measurement has a 1 cm error.

Why Do Errors Happen?

  • The measuring instrument may not be perfect.
  • The person taking the measurement might make a mistake.
  • External factors like temperature or wind may affect the result.

ii. Types of Errors in Measurement

Errors in measurement can be classified into three main types:

  1. Systematic Errors

    These errors happen again and again in the same way. They are caused by faulty instruments, wrong methods, or external factors.

    Example:

    If a clock runs 5 minutes fast, it will always show the wrong time, leading to a systematic error.

    Causes of Systematic Errors:

    1. Instrumental Errors – Faulty or improperly calibrated measuring instruments.
      • Example: A weighing scale that always shows 2 kg extra.
    2. Environmental Errors – External factors like temperature, humidity, or pressure affect the measurement.
      • Example: A thermometer showing incorrect temperature due to strong sunlight.
    3. Observational Errors – Mistakes made by the person measuring.
      • Example: Misreading the level of liquid in a measuring cylinder.
    How to Reduce Systematic Errors?

    • Use properly calibrated instruments.
    • Avoid measuring under extreme conditions.
    • Be careful while taking readings.

  2. Random Errors

    These errors occur by chance and are unpredictable. They happen due to sudden, small, uncontrollable factors.

    Example:

    If you measure a pencil's length three times and get different values like 14.8 cm, 15.0 cm, and 15.2 cm, this is a random error.

    Causes of Random Errors:

    1. Sudden changes in conditions (e.g., wind affecting balance scales).
    2. The person taking the measurement may not use the instrument correctly.
    3. Tiny unpredictable variations in the instrument.

    How to Reduce Random Errors?

    • Take multiple measurements and calculate the average value.
    • Use high-quality measuring instruments.
    • Take readings carefully and under stable conditions.

  3. Gross Errors

    These errors happen due to big human mistakes like reading the wrong number or using the wrong instrument.

    Example:

    If someone records 10 cm instead of 100 cm, this is a gross error.

    Causes of Gross Errors:

    • Recording mistakes – Writing the wrong number.
    • Using the wrong instrument – Measuring length with a clock instead of a ruler!
    • Not following proper procedure – Forgetting to set an instrument to zero before using it.

    How to Reduce Gross Errors?

    • Double-check readings before recording them.
    • Be careful while using instruments.
    • Have another person verify the measurement.

iii. Absolute Error, Relative Error & Percentage Error

To understand errors better, we use three important types of calculations:

  1. Absolute Error (How Much Off?)

    It tells us how much the measured value differs from the actual (true) value.

    Formula:

                  Absolute Error=∣Measured Value−Actual Value∣

    Example:

    If the actual weight of a book is 2.0 kg, but a scale shows 2.2 kg, then:

                  Absolute Error=∣2.2−2.0∣=0.2 kg

  2. Relative Error (Comparison with Actual Value)

    It tells us how big the absolute error is compared to the actual value.

    Formula:

                  Relative Error = (Absolute Error ÷ Actual Value)

    Example:

    For a book with an actual weight of 2.0 kg and an absolute error of 0.2 kg:

                  Relative Error = 0.2 ÷ 2.0 = 0.1

  3. Percentage Error (Error in % Form)

    This shows the error as a percentage, making it easier to understand.

    Formula:

                  Percentage Error = Relative Error × 100%

    Example:

    Using the relative error 0.1:

                  Percentage Error = 0.1 × 100 = 10%

    So, the measurement has a 10% error.

iv. How to Minimize Errors in Measurement?

  • Use High-Quality Instruments Avoid External Influences.
  • Check for Zero Error – Always set the instrument to zero before using it.
  • Repeat Measurements – Take multiple readings and use the average value.
  • Use Proper Techniques – Follow the correct method for measuring.
  • Avoid External Influences – Try to measure in a controlled environment.

v. Fun Facts About Measurement Errors!

  • NASA once lost a $125 million Mars orbiter because one team used miles while another used kilometers!
  • A tiny 0.5 cm measurement mistake in a bridge design can cause a huge structural failure!
  • In 1999, a 500-meter error caused a space probe to crash into Mars instead of orbiting it!

3. Significant Figures

i. What are Significant Figures?

Significant figures (also called significant digits) are the digits in a number that carry meaning and contribute to its precision. They help us express numbers in a way that reflects the accuracy of measurements.

For example:

  • 12.30 cm has 4 significant figures(1, 2, 3, and 0).
  • 0.0045 kg has 2 significant figures(4 and 5).

ii. Why are Significant Figures Important?

  1. They Show Measurement Accuracy – More significant figures mean a more precise measurement.
  2. They Help in Scientific Calculations – Prevents exaggerated accuracy in answers.
  3. They Ensure Consistency – Used in physics, chemistry, and engineering calculations.

iii. Rules for Counting Significant Figures

  1. All nonzero digits are always significant

    • Example: 1234 has 4 significant figures (1, 2, 3, 4).
    • Example: 56.78 has 4 significant figures (5, 6, 7, 8).

  2. Zeros between nonzero digits are significant

    • Example: 204 has 3 significant figures (2, 0, 4).
    • Example: 1002 has 4 significant figures (1, 0, 0, 2).

  3. Leading zeros (zeros before nonzero digits) are NOT significant

    • Example: 0.0034 has 2 significant figures (3 and 4).
    • Example: 0.000056 has 2 significant figures (5 and 6).

  4. Trailing zeros (zeros at the end) are significant IF there is a decimal point

    • Example: 45.00 has 4 significant figures (4, 5, 0, 0).
    • Example: 3.7800 has 5 significant figures (3, 7, 8, 0, 0).

  5. Trailing zeros in a whole number without a decimal point are NOT significant

    • Example: 2500 has 2 significant figures (2 and 5).
    • Example: 300000 has 1 significant figure (3).

  6. In scientific notation, all digits in the coefficient are significant

    • Example: 4.50 × 10³ has 3 significant figures (4, 5, 0).
    • Example: 2.340 × 10⁶ has 4 significant figures (2, 3, 4, 0).

iii. Significant Figures in Mathematical Operations

  1. Addition & Subtraction

    The result should have the same number of decimal places as the number with the least decimal places.

    • Example: 12.345 + 1.2 = 13.545 → 13.5 (rounded to 1 decimal place).
    • Example: 15.02 - 0.678 = 14.342 → 14.34 (rounded to 2 decimal places).
  2. Multiplication & Division

    The result should have the same number of significant figures as the number with the fewest significant figures.

    • Example: 3.42 × 1.2 = 4.104 → 4.1 (rounded to 2 significant figures).
    • Example: 25.0 ÷ 4.2 = 5.952 → 6.0 (rounded to 2 significant figures).

Rounding Off Significant Figures

Rules for Rounding:

  1. If the digit after the last significant figure is less than 5, drop it.

    • Example: 4.732 rounded to 3 significant figures → 4.73.

  2. If the digit after the last significant figure is 5 or greater, increase the last significant figure by 1

    • Example: 5.678 rounded to 3 significant figures → 5.68.

  3. If rounding a number like 3.500 to 2 significant figures, it becomes 3.5 (the trailing zero is removed).

Examples of Significant Figures in Real Life

  1. Science & Engineering - When measuring the length of an object, the number of significant figures tells how precise the measurement is.
  2. Finance & Economy – Prices, exchange rates, and financial figures use significant digits for accuracy.
  3. Physics & Chemistry Experiments – Measuring chemicals or force requires proper rounding to avoid errors.
