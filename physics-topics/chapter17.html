Chapter 17 - Dual Nature of Matter and Radiation

1. Photoelectric Effect

What is the Photoelectric Effect?

  • The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when light of a sufficient frequency falls on it.
  • Discovered by Heinrich Hertz in 1887.
  • Explained by Albert Einstein in 1905 using the concept of photons.
  • Proved that light behaves as particles (photons), supporting the quantum theory of light.

Experimental Setup

  • Light Source – Emits photons towards the metal plate.
  • Metal Surface (Cathode) – Releases electrons when light hits it.
  • Collector (Anode) – Attracts emitted electrons, creating current.
  • Ammeter – Measures the photoelectric current.
  • Battery – Provides a variable stopping potential to analyze electron energy.

Einstein’s Photoelectric Equation

  • Energy of a photon:

    E = hν

    where h = Planck’s constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s), ν = Frequency of incident light

  • Work Function (ϕ) – The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the metal surface.

    ϕ = hν₀

    where ν₀ is the threshold frequency.

  • Kinetic Energy of Emitted Electrons:

    Kmax = hν − ϕ

    If ν < ν₀, no electrons are emitted, regardless of light intensity.

Key Observations and Laws

  • Threshold Frequency (ν₀): Below this frequency, no photoelectrons are emitted.
  • Instantaneous Emission: Electrons are emitted immediately when light falls on the surface.
  • Increasing Light Intensity: Increases the number of photoelectrons but does not affect their energy.
  • Increasing Light Frequency: Increases the kinetic energy of emitted electrons.

Graphical Representation

  • Stopping Potential vs. Frequency:

    A straight-line graph showing that kinetic energy increases with frequency.

    Intercepts the x-axis at threshold frequency (ν₀).

  • Photoelectric Current vs. Light Intensity:

    A linear graph showing that current increases with intensity.

Applications of the Photoelectric Effect

  • Solar Panels – Convert sunlight into electricity.
  • Photocells – Used in automatic doors, light meters, and streetlights.
  • X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy – Analyzes material surfaces.
  • TV and Camera Sensors – Use photoelectric sensors to detect light.

2. De Broglie Hypothesis

Introduction

  • In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that particles, like electrons, exhibit wave-like properties, similar to light.
  • This idea is called Wave-Particle Duality – meaning that matter can behave both as a particle and a wave.
  • This hypothesis laid the foundation for Quantum Mechanics.

De Broglie Wavelength

  • De Broglie stated that any moving particle has an associated wavelength (λ):
  • Formula: λ = h p

    • where:

    • λ = Wavelength of the particle
    • h = Planck’s constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)
    • p = Momentum of the particle (p = mv)
  • For an electron or any microscopic particle:
  • λ = h m v
  • For macroscopic objects (e.g., a football), the wavelength is extremely small and negligible.

Significance of De Broglie’s Hypothesis

  • Explains Electron Diffraction – Experiments like the Davisson-Germer experiment confirmed that electrons show wave-like properties.
  • Basis of Quantum Mechanics – Helped develop the Schrödinger wave equation.
  • Explains Stability of Atoms – Bohr’s quantization of electron orbits in atoms can be understood using de Broglie waves.

Experimental Confirmation

  • Davisson-Germer Experiment (1927): Electrons were fired at a crystal, and a diffraction pattern was observed, proving their wave nature.
  • Electron Microscopes: Work on the principle that electrons behave as waves, allowing higher resolution than light microscopes.
