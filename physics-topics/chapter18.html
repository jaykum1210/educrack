Chapter 18 - Atoms and Nuclei

1. Atomic Models: Bohr Model

Introduction

Before Bohr’s model, Thomson’s "Plum Pudding" model and Rutherford’s Nuclear model attempted to explain atomic structure. However, they failed to explain atomic stability and spectral lines.

In 1913, Niels Bohr proposed a new model of the atom based on quantum theory, which successfully explained the hydrogen spectrum.

Postulates of Bohr's Model

  • 1st Postulate: Electrons move in fixed circular orbits (energy levels) around the nucleus without radiating energy.
  • 2nd Postulate: Only certain discrete orbits are allowed, where the angular momentum of an electron is quantized:

    m v r = n h 2 π

  • 3rd Postulate: Electrons absorb or emit energy when they jump between energy levels:

    ΔE = E 2 - E 1 = h ν

Energy Levels and Hydrogen Spectrum

Energy of an Electron in the nth Orbit:

E n = - 13.6 n 2   eV

Radius of nth Orbit:

r n = n 2 × 0.529   Å

Hydrogen Spectral Series

Series Name Transition (n → m) Region
Lyman n → 1 UV
Balmer n → 2 Visible
Paschen n → 3 Infrared
Brackett n → 4 Infrared
Pfund n → 5 Infrared

Success of Bohr’s Model

  • Explained the stability of atoms.
  • Accurately predicted hydrogen’s emission spectrum.
  • Introduced quantization in atomic structure.

Limitations of Bohr’s Model

  • Couldn’t explain spectra of multi-electron atoms.
  • Didn’t consider electron wave nature (later explained by Schrödinger’s wave equation).
  • Failed to explain Zeeman and Stark effects (splitting of spectral lines in magnetic/electric fields).

2. Radioactivity

1Introduction

  • Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of particles or radiation from the nucleus of an unstable atom.
  • Discovered by Henri Becquerel (1896) and further studied by Marie and Pierre Curie.

Types of Radioactive Decay

Type Symbol Charge Mass Penetration Power
Alpha (α) 4He2+ +2 Heavy Low (stopped by paper)
Beta (β) e⁻ or e⁺ -1 or +1 Light Medium (stopped by aluminum)
Gamma (γ) - 0 No mass High (stopped by thick lead)
  • Alpha decay (α): A nucleus emits a helium nucleus (4He).
  • Beta decay (β): A neutron converts into a proton, emitting an electron (β⁻) or positron (β⁺).
  • Gamma decay (γ): Nucleus releases high-energy electromagnetic waves.

Radioactive Decay Law

The decay of radioactive substances follows an exponential law:

N = N0 e - λt

  • N0: Initial number of nuclei
  • N: Remaining nuclei after time t
  • λ: Decay constant

Half-life and Mean Life

  • Half-life (T1/2): The time taken for half the radioactive atoms to decay.

    T 1/2 = 0.693 λ

  • Mean life (τ): The average lifetime of a radioactive atom.

    τ = 1 λ

Applications of Radioactivity

  • Carbon Dating – Determines the age of fossils using C-14 isotope.
  • Nuclear Medicine – Used in cancer treatment (radiotherapy).
  • Nuclear Power – Generates electricity from nuclear fission.
  • Industrial Use – Detects pipeline leaks using radioactive tracers.

Hazards of Radioactivity

  • Causes DNA mutations and cancer.
  • Leads to radiation sickness (e.g., Chernobyl, Fukushima disasters).
  • Requires proper disposal to prevent environmental damage.

3. Nuclear Reactions

Introduction

  • A nuclear reaction occurs when the nucleus of an atom changes due to interactions with other nuclei or particles, leading to the formation of new elements and energy release.
  • Unlike chemical reactions, nuclear reactions involve changes in the nucleus and are governed by the principles of mass-energy equivalence (E = mc²).

Types of Nuclear Reactions

(A) Nuclear Fission

  • In fission, a heavy nucleus (e.g., uranium-235) splits into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy and neutrons.
  • Example: Uranium-235 Fission

92235U + 01n → 3692Kr + 56141Ba + 3 01n + Energy

  • Features of Fission:
    • Releases a huge amount of energy (used in nuclear power plants).
    • Produces chain reactions (used in nuclear bombs).
    • Controlled in nuclear reactors using moderators (like graphite or heavy water).

(B) Nuclear Fusion

  • In fusion, two light nuclei (e.g., hydrogen isotopes) combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing even more energy than fission.
  • Example: Hydrogen Fusion in the Sun

11H + 12H → 23He + γ (Energy)

  • Features of Fusion:
    • Occurs in stars and hydrogen bombs.
    • Requires extremely high temperature and pressure (millions of °C).
    • Clean energy source (no harmful radiation or waste).

Mass-Energy Equivalence (Einstein’s Equation)

  • E = mc²
  • Energy (E) is obtained by converting a small amount of mass (m) into energy.
  • This principle explains the huge energy release in both fission and fusion.

Controlled vs. Uncontrolled Nuclear Reactions

Type Example Application
Controlled Reaction Nuclear Reactors Energy production
Uncontrolled Reaction Nuclear Bombs Weapons of mass destruction

Applications of Nuclear Reactions

  • Nuclear Power Plants – Electricity generation.
  • Medical Uses – Cancer treatment (radiotherapy).
  • Space Exploration – Nuclear propulsion in spacecraft.
  • Weapons – Atomic and hydrogen bombs.

Dangers and Safety Measures

  • Hazards:
    • Radiation Exposure – Causes mutations and cancer.
    • Radioactive Waste – Requires safe disposal.
  • Safety Measures:
    • Proper Shielding – Use of lead or concrete.
    • Controlled Environment – Nuclear reactors use moderators and control rods.

4. Nuclear Fission and Fusion

Introduction

  • Nuclear fission and fusion are processes that release vast amounts of energy by altering atomic nuclei.
  • They are based on Einstein’s equation: E = m c 2 , which describes mass-energy equivalence.

Nuclear Fission

  • Fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy and neutrons.
  • Example:
    U 235 + n 1 → Kr 92 + Ba 141 + 3 n 1 + Energy

Nuclear Fusion

  • Fusion is the combination of two light nuclei into a heavier nucleus, releasing tremendous energy.
  • Example (Hydrogen Fusion in the Sun):
    H 1 + H 2 → He 3 + γ + Energy

Comparison: Fission vs. Fusion

Feature Nuclear Fission Nuclear Fusion
Definition Splitting of a heavy nucleus Combining of two light nuclei
Example Uranium-235 fission Hydrogen fusion in the Sun
Energy Released High Very High
Reaction Conditions Can occur at room temperature with neutron bombardment Requires extreme temperature & pressure
Byproducts Radioactive waste No harmful waste
Applications Nuclear power plants, atomic bombs Sun’s energy, hydrogen bombs, future power plants

Applications of Nuclear Reactions

  • Nuclear Power Plants: Generates electricity through controlled fission.
  • Medical Uses: Used in radiotherapy for cancer treatment.
  • Weapons: Used in atomic and hydrogen bombs.
  • Space Exploration: Nuclear propulsion systems for deep-space missions.

Dangers and Safety Measures

  • Radiation Exposure: Can cause mutations and cancer.
  • Radioactive Waste: Needs proper disposal and long-term management.
  • Safety Measures: Use of shielding (lead, concrete) and controlled environments.
