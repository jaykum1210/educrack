Chapter 5 - Rotational Motion

1. Center of Mass

Definition

  • The center of mass is the point where the entire mass of a system can be considered to be concentrated for motion analysis.
  • It moves as if all external forces act at that single point.

Center of Mass Formula

For a system of n particles:

X CM = m 1 x 1 + m 2 x 2 + ... + m n x n m 1 + m 2 + ... + m n

Similar formulas hold for YCM and ZCM.

Center of Mass for Continuous Bodies

X CM = ∫ x d m ∫ d m

Similar formulas apply for YCM and ZCM.

Center of Mass of Common Objects

  • Uniform Rod (length L): Center is at midpoint.
  • Ring/Sphere: Center of mass is at geometric center.
  • Right-Angled Triangle: X CM = b 3 , Y CM = h 3

Motion of Center of Mass

The center of mass moves as if all external forces act on it.

Velocity of COM:

V CM = m 1 v 1 + m 2 v 2 + ... + m n v n m 1 + m 2 + ... + m n

Acceleration of COM:

a CM = F net M

Applications

  • Rocket Motion: COM shifts as mass ejects.
  • Balancing Objects: Finding COM helps in stability.
  • Astronomy: Planets orbit around the COM of their system.

Key Points

  • The COM follows Newton’s Laws of Motion.
  • If no external force acts, the COM moves with constant velocity.
  • In collisions, COM plays a crucial role in momentum conservation.

2. Moment of Inertia

Definition

  • The Moment of Inertia (I) is the rotational equivalent of mass in linear motion.
  • It represents the resistance of a body to changes in its rotational motion.
  • It depends on the mass distribution and the axis of rotation.

Moment of Inertia Formula

  • For a system of discrete particles: I = ∑ i m i r i 2
  • For a continuous body: I = ∫ dm r 2

Parallel and Perpendicular Axis Theorems

  • Parallel Axis Theorem: I = I CM + M d 2
  • Perpendicular Axis Theorem (for 2D bodies): I z = I x + I y

Moment of Inertia of Common Objects

Shape Axis Moment of Inertia
Rod Perpendicular to length through center 1 12 ML L 2
Rod Perpendicular to length through one end 1 3 ML L 2
Ring About its central axis MR R 2
Disc About its central axis 1 2 MR R 2

Rotational Kinetic Energy

  • Formula: KE = 1 2 I ω 2

Torque and Angular Momentum

  • Torque: τ = I α
  • Angular Momentum: L = I ω
  • Conservation of Angular Momentum: I 1 ω 1 = I 2 ω 2

Applications

  • Flywheels store rotational energy.
  • Planetary motion follows angular momentum conservation.
  • Athletes & Gymnasts adjust their MOI to control rotation.

Key Points

  • Larger MOI → Harder to rotate.
  • MOI depends on mass and distance from axis squared.
  • Conservation of angular momentum is crucial in space physics.

3. Torque and Angular Momentum

i. Torque (τ)

Definition

  • Torque is the rotational equivalent of force.
  • It measures the tendency of a force to cause rotational motion about an axis.

Formula

  • Formula: τ = r × F = rFsinθ

    where τ = Torque (Nm), r = Position vector (distance from axis of rotation), F = Applied force, θ = Angle between r and F.

Key Points

  • If θ = 0° or 180°, then τ = 0 (force acts along the line of motion).
  • If θ = 90°, then τ is maximum.
  • The direction of torque is given by the right-hand rule:
    • Curl fingers in the direction of rotation caused by force.
    • Thumb points in the direction of torque.

Relation between Torque and Angular Acceleration

  • Formula: τ = Iα

    where I = Moment of Inertia, α = Angular acceleration.

ii. Angular Momentum (L)

Definition

  • Angular momentum is the rotational analog of linear momentum.
  • It is the product of the moment of inertia and angular velocity.

Formula

  • Formula: L = r × p = Iω

    where L = Angular momentum (kg·m²/s), p = Linear momentum (mv), ω = Angular velocity.

Key Points

  • If no external torque acts on a system, angular momentum remains conserved.
  • Direction is determined by the right-hand rule (same as torque).

iii. Conservation of Angular Momentum

  • Formula: Linitial = Lfinal
  • or I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂
  • If the moment of inertia decreases, angular velocity increases (e.g., a spinning ice skater pulling arms in).
  • Important in planetary motion, rotating bodies, and gyroscopes.

iv. Relation Between Torque and Angular Momentum

  • Formula: τ = dL/dt
  • Torque is the rate of change of angular momentum.
  • If net torque is zero, angular momentum is conserved.

v. Applications

  • Ice skaters & Gymnasts: Change body shape to control spin speed.
  • Planets and Satellites: Orbit stability due to angular momentum conservation.
  • Flywheels: Store rotational energy in engines.
  • Gyroscopes: Used in navigation systems.

vi. Key Differences Between Torque and Angular Momentum

Torque (τ) Angular Momentum (L)
Causes rotational motion Describes rotational motion
τ = r × F L = Iω
Vector quantity Vector quantity
Measured in Nm Measured in kg·m²/s
Depends on force Depends on mass and speed

vii. Summary

  • Torque initiates rotation, Angular Momentum maintains it.
  • No external torque → Angular momentum is conserved.
  • Used in spinning objects, planetary motion, and machines.

4. Conservation of Angular Momentum

Definition

  • The conservation of angular momentum states that if no external torque acts on a system, its angular momentum remains constant.
  • It follows from Newton's First Law for rotational motion (an object in rotational motion continues unless acted upon by an external torque).

Mathematical Expression

  • Formula: Linitial = Lfinal
  • Or: I1ω1 = I2ω2

    • where:

    • L = Angular Momentum
    • I = Moment of Inertia
    • ω = Angular Velocity
  • If I decreases, ω increases and vice versa.

Explanation

  • Since L = Iω, any change in moment of inertia (I) will affect angular velocity (ω) to keep L constant.
  • This principle explains why a skater spins faster when pulling in arms or a planet speeds up when moving closer to the Sun.

Applications of Conservation of Angular Momentum

  1. Spinning Ice Skater or Dancer

    • When a skater pulls their arms and legs inward, moment of inertia decreases.
    • To conserve angular momentum, angular velocity increases (they spin faster).

  2. Planetary Motion (Kepler’s 2nd Law)

    • Planets move faster when closer to the Sun and slower when farther due to conservation of angular momentum.
    • Angular momentum of planets remains constant as they orbit.

  3. Neutron Stars and Black Holes

    • When a massive star collapses, its radius decreases, leading to an increase in angular velocity, making it spin rapidly.
    • This is why neutron stars and pulsars rotate at high speeds.

  4. Rotating Chair Experiment

    • A person sitting on a freely rotating chair holding weights can change their speed by pulling the weights closer or stretching arms outward.

  5. Satellites and Spacecraft Motion

    • Artificial satellites adjust their orientation using the principle of angular momentum conservation (e.g., gyroscopes in space telescopes like Hubble).

Relation Between Torque and Angular Momentum

  • Formula: τ = dL/dt
  • If net external torque is zero, τ = 0 → dL/dt = 0 → L is constant.
  • If torque is applied, angular momentum changes over time.

Rolling Motion

Definition

  • Rolling motion is a combination of translational and rotational motion of a body.
  • A rolling object moves forward while also rotating about its own axis.
  • Examples:
    • A rolling wheel, ball, or cylinder.
    • Motion of a car’s tires on the road.

Types of Rolling Motion

  1. Pure Rolling
    • No slipping occurs between the rolling object and the surface.
    • The velocity of the point of contact with the ground is zero.
  2. Rolling with Slipping
    • Occurs when friction is insufficient, causing the object to slide.
    • The velocity of the point of contact with the surface is not zero.

Condition for Pure Rolling

  • For a rolling object to be in pure rolling motion, the relation between translational velocity and rotational velocity is:
  • Formula: v = Rω

    where v = Linear velocity of the center of mass, R = Radius of the rolling object, ω = Angular velocity.

    • If v > Rω, the object is slipping forward.
    • If v < Rω, the object is slipping backward.

Kinetic Energy in Rolling Motion

  • Rolling motion has both translational and rotational kinetic energy.
  • Formula: KEtotal = KEtranslational + KErotational
  • KEtotal = (1/2)Mv² + (1/2)Iω²

    • where M = Mass of the object, I = Moment of inertia about the center of mass, ω = Angular velocity.

  • For pure rolling, substituting v = Rω:
  • KEtotal = (1/2)Mv² + (1/2)I(v²/R²)

Friction in Rolling Motion

  • Static friction plays a key role in rolling motion.
  • It prevents slipping and helps in the rolling motion.
  • Rolling friction is usually small compared to kinetic friction (slipping case).
  • If no friction → pure rolling cannot occur.
  • More friction → higher resistance to rolling.

Acceleration in Rolling Motion

  • The acceleration of the rolling body is influenced by torque and friction.
  • Formula: a = F/M
  • For an object rolling down an incline, the acceleration is:
  • a = (g sinθ) / (1 + I/MR²)

    • where g = Acceleration due to gravity, θ = Inclination angle, I = Moment of inertia.

Rolling Motion on an Inclined Plane

  • When a body rolls down an incline, both translation and rotation contribute to motion.
  • The time taken to reach the bottom depends on moment of inertia.
  • Objects with a larger moment of inertia roll slower.
  • Example:
    • A solid sphere reaches the bottom faster than a ring of the same mass and radius because a ring has a higher moment of inertia.
