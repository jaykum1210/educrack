Chapter 2 - Kinematics

1. Motion in One Dimension

What is Motion in One Dimension?

Motion in one dimension (1D) refers to movement along a straight line. The position of an object changes with time along a single axis (x-axis or y-axis).

Important Terms

  1. Distance (d) & Displacement (s)

    • Distance: Total path covered (scalar, always positive).
    • Displacement:Shortest path from start to end (vector, can be positive or negative).

  2. Speed & Velocity

    • Speed (v) = Distance/Time (scalar, no direction).
    • Velocity (v)= Displacement/Time (vector, has direction).

  3. Acceleration (a)

    • Rate of change of velocity.
    • a = (Final velocity - Initial velocity) / Time
    • Unit: m/s²

Equations of Motion

For constant acceleration (v, u, a, s, t are used):

  1. v = u + at
  2. s = ut + (1/2)at²
  3. v² = u² + 2as

Where:

  • u = Initial velocity
  • v = Final velocity
  • a = Acceleration
  • s = Displacement
  • t = Time

Motion Under Gravity (Free Fall)

  • When an object falls freely under gravity, acceleration a = g = 9.8 m/s² (downward).
  • Equations of motion remain the same but with a = g.

If dropped from height h:

  • v = u + gt
  • h = ut + (1/2)gt²
  • v² = u² + 2gh
  • At highest point, v = 0 in upward motion.

Types of Motion in One Dimension

  1. Uniform Motion – Constant velocity, a = 0.
  2. Non-uniform Motion – Changing velocity, a ≠ 0.
  3. Freely Falling Motion – Motion under gravity with g = 9.8 m/s².

Graphical Representation of Motion

  1. Distance-Time Graph
    • Straight Line → Uniform motion (constant speed).
    • Curved Line → Accelerated motion.
  2. Velocity-Time Graph
    • Horizontal Line → Constant velocity.
    • Sloped Line → Acceleration or deceleration.
    • Area under v-t graph → Displacement.

Relative Velocity

The velocity of one object with respect to another.

If two objects move in the same direction with velocities v₁ and v₂:

Relative velocity = v₁ - v₂

If two objects move in opposite directions:

Relative velocity = v₁ + v₂

2. Motion in Two Dimensions

Introduction

  • Motion in Two Dimensions occurs when an object moves in a plane, meaning it has motion along both the x-axis and y-axis.
  • It is described using vector quantities such as displacement, velocity, and acceleration.
  • Examples include projectile motion, circular motion, and motion of a car on a curved path.

  1. Scalars and Vectors

    Scalar Quantity:

    • A quantity that has only magnitude (no direction).
    • Examples: Mass, time, speed, energy, temperature.

    Vector Quantity:

    • A quantity that has both magnitude and direction.
    • Examples: Displacement, velocity, acceleration, force.

    Vector Representation:

    • Represented using an arrow, where:
      • Length of the arrow represents magnitude.
      • Direction of the arrow shows the direction of the vector.

  2. Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration in Two Dimensions

    Displacement in Two Dimensions:

    • Displacement is a vector quantity that represents the shortest path between two points.
    • If an object moves from A(x1, y1) to B(x2, y2)
      Δr = ( x 2 − x 1 ) 2 + ( y 2 − y 1 ) 2

      Velocity in Two Dimensions:

      • Velocity is the rate of change of displacement.
      • It has two components: v x = d x d t , v y = d y d t

      • Resultant Velocity:

        v = v x 2 + v y 2

      • Direction of Velocity:

        θ = tan -1 v y v x

Acceleration in Two Dimensions:

  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity.
  • It has two components:
               a x = d v x d t , a y = d v y d t
  • Resultant acceleration:
               a = a x 2 + a y 2
  • Direction of acceleration:
               θ = tan -1 a y a x

Motion in a Plane with Constant Acceleration

If acceleration is constant, motion can be analyzed using kinematic equations separately in the x and y directions:

Equations of Motion in Two Dimensions

For x-axis:

            x = x 0 + v 0 x t + 1 2 a x t 2

           v x = v 0 x + a x t

For y-axis:

           y = y 0 + v 0 y t + 1 2 a y t 2

           v y = v 0 y + a y t

  • These equations describe motion when acceleration is constant, such as in projectile motion.

Projectile Motion

  • Projectile motion is the motion of an object thrown into the air, subject to gravitational acceleration.
  • The path followed by a projectile is called a parabola.
  • Examples:
    • A ball thrown at an angle.
    • A bullet fired from a gun.

Key Assumptions of Projectile Motion:

  • The only force acting on the object is gravity (g = 9.8 m/s² downward).
  • No air resistance.
  • Motion occurs in two perpendicular directions (horizontal and vertical) independently.

Components of Velocity in Projectile Motion:

Horizontal velocity ( v x ):

  • Remains constant because there is no acceleration in the x-direction.

    v x = v 0 ⋅ cos θ

Vertical velocity ( v y ):

  • Affected by gravity (g).

    v y = v 0 ⋅ sin θ - g t

Uniform Circular Motion

  • An object moving in a circular path with constant speed is said to be in Uniform Circular Motion.
  • Example: Motion of a satellite around Earth.

Characteristics of Circular Motion:

  1. Speed remains constant but velocity changes due to direction change.
  2. There is always an acceleration towards the center (centripetal acceleration).
  3. The force responsible for circular motion is called centripetal force.

Centripetal Acceleration:

a c = v 2 r

where:

  • v = velocity of the object
  • r = radius of the circular path

Centripetal Force:

F c = m v 2 r

where:

  • m = mass of the object
  • v = velocity
  • r = radius of the circle

Relative Motion in Two Dimensions

When two objects move relative to each other, their velocities are compared using vector addition or subtraction.

Relative Velocity Formula:

v AB = v A - v B

where:

  • v AB = velocity of A relative to B

  • v A = velocity of A

  • v B = velocity of B

Example of Relative Motion:

  • A boat crossing a river when water is flowing with velocity v r .
  • A plane flying in wind currents.

3. Relative Velocity

i. Introduction

Relative velocity is the velocity of one object with respect to another. It helps in understanding motion when both observer and the object are moving.

ii. Concept of Relative Velocity

  • Velocity is always measured relative to a reference frame.
  • If two objects are moving, the velocity of one object appears different when observed from the other moving object.

iii. Formula for Relative Velocity

(A) When Two Objects Move in the Same Direction

If two objects A and B move with velocities VA and VB in the same direction:

Relative velocity of A with respect to B:

VAB = VA − VB

Relative velocity of B with respect to A:

VBA = VB − VA

  • If VA > VB, A appears to move forward relative to B.
  • If VA < VB, A appears to move backward relative to B.

(B) When Two Objects Move in Opposite Directions

If A and B move in opposite directions:

VAB = VA + VB

(C) Relative Velocity in Two Dimensions (Vector Form)

If two objects move at an angle to each other:

⃗VAB = VA − VB

Here, the velocities are represented as vectors, and subtraction is done using vector components.

iv. Special Cases of Relative Velocity

Objects Moving at the Same Speed in the Same Direction

If VA = VB, then VAB = 0.

Example: Two trains moving side by side at the same speed appear stationary to passengers inside.

Objects Moving Towards Each Other

VAB = VA + VB

Example: If two cars are approaching each other with speeds 50 km/h and 30 km/h:

50 + 30 = 80 km/h

They appear to approach each other at 80 km/h.

Objects Moving Perpendicularly

When two objects move at right angles:

VAB = √(VA2 + VB2)

v. Applications of Relative Velocity

  • Boats Crossing a River: The effective velocity is the vector sum of the boat’s speed and the river’s flow.
  • Airplane Motion in Wind: Pilots adjust speed and direction to compensate for wind to maintain the correct course.
  • Collisions and Accidents: Relative velocity helps determine the impact speed between vehicles.

vi. Example Problems

Example 1: Two Cars Moving in the Same Direction

Car A moves at 60 km/h, and Car B moves at 40 km/h in the same direction.

VAB = 60 − 40 = 20 km/h

Car A appears to move forward at 20 km/h relative to car B.

Example 2: Two Cyclists Moving Towards Each Other

Cyclist A moves at 10 m/s and Cyclist B at 5 m/s in opposite directions.

VAB = 10 + 5 = 15 m/s

They approach each other at 15 m/s.
