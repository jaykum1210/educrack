Chapter 11 - Current Electricity

1.Electric Current and Drift Velocity

Definition

  • Electric current (I) is the rate of flow of charge through a conductor.
  • Formula: I = Q t

    • where I = Current (A), Q = Charge (C), t = Time (s).

SI Unit

  • The SI unit of electric current is the Ampere (A).
  • 1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb per second (1 A = 1 C/s).
  • Conventional current flows from positive to negative.
  • Electron flow (real charge movement) is from negative to positive.

Types of Current

  • Direct Current (DC) → Charges flow in one direction (e.g., batteries).
  • Alternating Current (AC) → Charges change direction periodically (e.g., household electricity).

Drift Velocity (vd)

  • Electrons in a conductor move randomly, but under a voltage, they drift slowly in the opposite direction of the electric field.
  • Formula: v d = I nAe

    • where vd = Drift velocity (m/s), I = Current (A), n = Free electrons per unit volume, A = Cross-sectional area, e = Charge of an electron (1.6 × 10-19 C).

  • Drift velocity is very small (~10-4 m/s), but current flows nearly instantly due to many electrons moving simultaneously.

Relation Between Current and Drift Velocity

  • Since I = nAe v d , we observe:
  • More free electrons (higher n) → More current.
  • Larger cross-section A → More current flow.
  • Higher drift velocity vd → More current.

Mobility (μ)

  • Mobility measures how quickly electrons move in response to an electric field.
  • Formula: μ = v d E

    • where μ = Mobility (m2/Vs), vd = Drift velocity (m/s), E = Electric field (V/m).

  • Higher mobility = Faster electron movement.
  • Metals have high mobility → Good conductors.

2. Ohm’s Law and Resistance

Definition

  • Ohm’s Law states that the current (I) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage (V) applied across it, provided the temperature and physical conditions remain constant.
  • Formula: V = IR

    • where:

    • V = Voltage (Volts, V)
    • I = Current (Amperes, A)
    • R = Resistance (Ohms, Ω)
  • If voltage increases, current increases (for a constant resistance).
  • If resistance increases, current decreases (for a constant voltage).

Graphical Representation of Ohm’s Law

  • A graph of V vs I is a straight line passing through the origin.
  • The slope of the graph gives the resistance (R).
  • Non-ohmic materials (e.g., diodes, filament bulbs) do not follow a straight-line graph.

Resistance (R)

  • Resistance is the opposition offered by a material to the flow of electric current.
  • SI Unit: Ohm (Ω)
  • 1 Ohm: A conductor has 1 Ω resistance if 1 Volt produces 1 Ampere of current.

Factors Affecting Resistance

  • The resistance of a conductor depends on its length, cross-sectional area, and material.
  • Formula: R = ρ (L / A)

    • where:

    • R = Resistance (Ω)
    • ρ = Resistivity (Ω⋅m) (depends on material)
    • L = Length of the conductor (m)
    • A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
  • Longer wire → More resistance
  • Thicker wire → Less resistance
  • Higher resistivity (ρ) → More resistance

Resistivity (ρ)

  • Resistivity is a material property that determines how strongly it resists current flow.
  • SI Unit: Ω⋅m
  • Metals have low resistivity → Good conductors.
  • Insulators have high resistivity → Poor conductors.
  • Order of resistivity: Metals < Semiconductors < Insulators

Conductance and Conductivity

  • Conductance (G) = Reciprocal of resistance.
  • Formula: G = 1/R
  • Unit: Siemens (S) or Ω⁻¹
  • Conductivity (σ) = Reciprocal of resistivity.
  • Formula: σ = 1/ρ
  • Unit: S/m
  • Higher conductivity → Better conductor.

Temperature Dependence of Resistance

  • Metals: Resistance increases with temperature.
  • Semiconductors: Resistance decreases with temperature.
  • Superconductors: At very low temperatures, resistance becomes zero!
  • Formula: RT = R0 (1 + αT)

    • where:

    • RT = Resistance at temperature T
    • R0 = Resistance at reference temperature
    • α = Temperature coefficient of resistance
  • Positive α: Resistance increases with temperature (metals).
  • Negative α: Resistance decreases with temperature (semiconductors).

3. Kirchhoff’s Laws

i. Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL) – Junction Rule

Statement: The sum of all currents entering a junction is equal to the sum of all currents leaving the junction.

Mathematical Form: ∑Iin = ∑Iout

  • Based on the Law of Conservation of Charge.
  • A junction (or node) is a point where two or more conductors meet.

Example:

If at a junction:

  • I1 = 5A (entering)
  • I2 = 3A (entering)
  • I3 (leaving)

Then by KCL:

I1 + I2 = I3

5A + 3A = I3

I3 = 8A (leaving the junction)

ii. Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL) – Loop Rule

Statement: The algebraic sum of all voltage drops and EMFs in a closed loop is always zero.

Mathematical Form: ∑V = 0

  • Based on the Law of Conservation of Energy.
  • The total energy gained by charges in a loop must be equal to the total energy lost.

Sign Convention in KVL:

  • Voltage rise (battery, EMF) → Positive (+)
  • Voltage drop (resistors, loads) → Negative (-)

Example:

Consider a simple circuit loop with:

  • A battery of 12V
  • Resistor R1 = 4Ω with a current of 2A
  • Resistor R2 = 2Ω with a current of 2A

Using KVL:

Vbattery - VR1 - VR2 = 0

12V - (2A × 4Ω) - (2A × 2Ω) = 0

12V - 8V - 4V = 0

The equation holds, so the circuit follows Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law.

iii. Applications of Kirchhoff’s Laws

  • Analyzing complex circuits (with multiple loops and junctions).
  • Finding unknown currents and voltages in electrical networks.
  • Used in Wheatstone bridge, mesh analysis, and nodal analysis.

4. Wheatstone Bridge & Potentiometer

i. Wheatstone Bridge

The Wheatstone Bridge is an electrical circuit used to measure an unknown resistance with high accuracy. It works on the principle of balanced bridge circuits.

Principle:

When the bridge is balanced, the ratio of resistances in one branch is equal to the ratio in the other branch.

Circuit Components:

  • R1 and R2 form one branch.
  • R3 (known resistance) and Rx (unknown resistance) form the second branch.
  • A galvanometer (G) is connected between the two branches.
  • A battery provides power.

Balanced Condition:

When no current flows through the galvanometer, the bridge is balanced:

R1 / R2 = R3 / Rx

Solving for Rx:

Rx = (R3 × R2) / R1

Applications:

  • Used in resistance measurement.
  • Found in strain gauges (to measure small changes in resistance).
  • Used in sensor circuits.

ii. Potentiometer

A potentiometer is a device used to measure unknown voltage by comparing it with a known reference voltage. It is more accurate than a voltmeter since it does not draw current from the circuit.

Principle:

It works on the principle that the potential drop across a wire is proportional to its length, given a uniform current flow.

Construction:

  • A long uniform resistance wire is connected to a battery.
  • A sliding jockey moves along the wire to measure voltage.
  • A galvanometer detects the null point (zero deflection).

Formula for Voltage Measurement:

If the null point is found at length l, then:

Vx = k ⋅ l

where k is the potential gradient (V/L).

Applications:

  • Measuring EMF of a cell.
  • Comparing two voltages.
  • Measuring internal resistance of a battery.

iii. Comparison of Wheatstone Bridge & Potentiometer

Feature Wheatstone Bridge Potentiometer
Measures Resistance (Rx) Voltage (EMF)
Accuracy Moderate Very High
Power Source Battery required Battery required
Current Flow Uses a galvanometer to detect balance No current flows at balance point
Applications Resistance measurement, strain gauges Voltage measurement, battery testing
