Chapter 20 - Thermal Properties of Matter

1. Heat and Temperature

Introduction

  • Heat (Q): A form of energy transfer due to temperature difference. Measured in Joules (J).
  • Temperature (T): A measure of the average kinetic energy of molecules. Measured in Kelvin (K), Celsius (°C), or Fahrenheit (°F).

Difference Between Heat and Temperature

Property Heat (Q) Temperature (T)
Definition Energy transfer due to temperature difference Measure of average kinetic energy
Symbol Q T
Unit Joule (J), Calorie (cal) Kelvin (K), Celsius (°C), Fahrenheit (°F)
Device for Measurement Calorimeter Thermometer

Heat Transfer Mechanisms

  • Conduction: Heat transfer through direct contact (e.g., metal rod heating up).
  • Convection: Heat transfer in fluids due to motion of molecules (e.g., boiling water).
  • Radiation: Heat transfer without a medium, through electromagnetic waves (e.g., Sun's heat).

Specific Heat Capacity (c)

The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C.

Formula: Q = mcΔT

  • Q = Heat energy (J)
  • m = Mass (kg)
  • c = Specific heat capacity (J/kg·K)
  • ΔT = Change in temperature (°C or K)

Thermal Expansion

When a substance is heated, its particles move faster, causing expansion.

  • Linear Expansion: Increase in length of a solid. Formula: ΔL = αL0ΔT
  • Area Expansion: Increase in surface area. Formula: ΔA = βA0ΔT
  • Volume Expansion: Increase in volume of solids, liquids, and gases. Formula: ΔV = γV0ΔT

Change of State (Phase Transitions)

  • Melting (Solid → Liquid)
  • Freezing (Liquid → Solid)
  • Evaporation (Liquid → Gas)
  • Condensation (Gas → Liquid)
  • Sublimation (Solid → Gas)

Latent Heat: The heat required to change state without changing temperature.

2. Thermal Expansion

Introduction

  • Thermal expansion is the increase in size (length, area, or volume) of a material when its temperature increases.
  • This occurs because atoms and molecules vibrate more when heated, increasing the average separation between them.
  • Key Concept: Most substances expand when heated and contract when cooled.

Types of Thermal Expansion

Linear Expansion (Increase in Length)

  • Occurs in solids when heated.
  • Formula: ΔL = αL0ΔT

    • where:

    • ΔL = Change in length (m)
    • L0 = Original length (m)
    • α = Coefficient of linear expansion (K⁻¹)
    • ΔT = Change in temperature (°C or K)
  • Example: Metal rods expand when heated, so gaps are left in railway tracks to prevent bending.

Area Expansion (Increase in Surface Area)

  • Occurs in thin sheets and plates.
  • Formula: ΔA = βA0ΔT

    • where:

    • ΔA = Change in area (m²)
    • A0 = Initial area (m²)
    • β = Coefficient of area expansion (K⁻¹)
    • ΔT = Change in temperature (°C or K)
  • Relation: β = 2α
  • Example: Metal plates expand when heated, which is why overheated car engines can deform.

Volume Expansion (Increase in Volume)

  • Applies to solids, liquids, and gases.
  • Formula: ΔV = γV0ΔT

    • where:

    • ΔV = Change in volume (m³)
    • V0 = Initial volume (m³)
    • γ = Coefficient of volume expansion (K⁻¹)
    • ΔT = Change in temperature (°C or K)
  • Relation: γ = 3α
  • Example: Liquids expand more than solids, which is why water overflows when heated in a container.

Thermal Expansion in Different States of Matter

  • Solids: Expand the least due to strong intermolecular forces.
  • Liquids: Expand more than solids because their molecules are loosely packed.
  • Gases: Expand the most because their molecules move freely.

Special Case - Water (Anomalous Expansion)

  • Water contracts from 0°C to 4°C instead of expanding.
  • This is why ice floats on water and why aquatic life can survive in cold climates.

3. Heat Transfer (Conduction, Convection, Radiation)

Introduction

  • Heat transfer is the process of thermal energy moving from a hotter object to a cooler one.
  • There are three main methods of heat transfer:
    1. Conduction – Direct transfer through solids.
    2. Convection – Heat transfer in liquids and gases due to fluid motion.
    3. Radiation – Transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves.

Conduction

  • Definition: Transfer of heat through direct contact in solids.
  • Heat moves from a region of high temperature to a region of low temperature without the movement of the material itself.
  • Formula:
    Q = kA(T₁−T₂) d

Examples:

  • Metal spoon getting hot in tea.
  • Cooking pans made of metal.

Convection

  • Definition: Heat transfer through fluid movement (liquids and gases).
  • Types:
    • Natural Convection – Occurs due to density differences.
    • Forced Convection – Uses external forces like fans.

Examples:

  • Boiling water.
  • Land and sea breezes.

Radiation

  • Definition: Transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves (infrared radiation) without needing a medium.
  • Formula:
    Q = σAT⁴

Examples:

  • Sun’s heat reaching Earth.
  • Black clothes absorbing more heat.

Comparison of Heat Transfer Methods

Feature Conduction Convection Radiation
Medium Required? Yes (solids) Yes (fluids) No
Speed Slow Moderate Fast (Speed of light)
Heat Transfer Mechanism Molecular vibrations Fluid movement Electromagnetic waves

Applications of Heat Transfer

  • Cooking utensils (Conduction: Metal pans, Convection: Water boiling).
  • Refrigerators and ACs (Forced convection to cool air).
  • Solar panels (Radiation to absorb heat from the sun).
  • Thermos flasks (Minimizing all three heat transfer methods).
