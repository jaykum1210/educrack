Chapter 4 - Animal Tissues and Body Organization

1. Animal Tissues and Their Functions

Animal tissues are classified into four main types based on their structure and function: epithelial, connective, muscular, and nervous tissues. Each tissue type performs specialized functions essential for the body's structure, function, and survival.

i. Epithelial Tissue

Epithelial tissue forms the outer layer of body surfaces and lines internal cavities and organs. It acts as a protective barrier and is involved in absorption, secretion, and sensation.

Characteristics:

  • Cells are closely packed with little intercellular space.
  • No blood vessels (avascular).
  • Has a high rate of regeneration.

Types of Epithelial Tissue:

  • Squamous Epithelium:
    • Structure: Flattened, scale-like cells.
    • Function: Facilitates diffusion and filtration.
    • Examples: Lining of blood vessels (endothelium), alveoli of lungs.
  • Cuboidal Epithelium:
    • Structure: Cube-shaped cells.
    • Function: Secretion and absorption.
    • Examples: Kidney tubules, glands (e.g., thyroid).
  • Columnar Epithelium:
    • Structure: Tall, column-like cells.
    • Function: Absorption and secretion (often with cilia or microvilli).
    • Examples: Lining of the digestive tract, respiratory tract.
  • Ciliated Epithelium:
    • Structure: Columnar or cuboidal cells with cilia on the surface.
    • Function: Movement of substances like mucus or eggs.
    • Examples: Lining of the trachea, Fallopian tubes.
  • Stratified Epithelium:
    • Structure: Multiple layers of cells.
    • Function: Protection (from mechanical stress and abrasion).
    • Examples: Skin (epidermis), mouth lining.

Functions:

  • Protection (from mechanical injury, pathogens, and dehydration).
  • Secretion (produces mucus, enzymes, and hormones).
  • Absorption (e.g., nutrients in the intestines).
  • Sensation (e.g., in the skin).

ii. Connective Tissue

Connective tissue supports, binds, and connects different parts of the body. It is the most diverse tissue type in animals.

Characteristics:

  • Cells are widely spaced and embedded in a matrix (extracellular material).
  • Vascular (except for cartilage).

Types of Connective Tissue:

  • Loose Connective Tissue:
    • Structure: Fibroblasts (cells that produce fibers), with collagen and elastin fibers in a gel-like matrix.
    • Function: Support, flexibility, and cushioning.
    • Examples: Areolar tissue, adipose tissue (fat).
  • Dense Connective Tissue:
    • Structure: Dense fibers, mainly collagen, arranged in parallel or irregular patterns.
    • Function: Strength and support.
    • Examples: Tendons (connect muscles to bones), ligaments (connect bones to bones).
  • Cartilage:
    • Structure: Chondrocytes (cartilage cells) embedded in a matrix of collagen and elastin fibers.
    • Function: Support, flexibility, and cushioning of joints.
    • Examples: Hyaline cartilage (found in joints), fibrocartilage (intervertebral discs), elastic cartilage (ears).
  • Bone:
    • Structure: Osteocytes (bone cells) embedded in a rigid matrix made of calcium salts.
    • Function: Support, protection of internal organs, mineral storage, and blood cell production (in the bone marrow).
    • Examples: Bones of the skeleton.
  • Blood:
    • Structure: Composed of cells (red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets) suspended in plasma.
    • Function: Transport of gases (O₂, CO₂), nutrients, waste products, and immune responses.
    • Examples: Blood in blood vessels.

Functions:

  • Support: Provides structural support to organs and tissues.
  • Transport: Transports nutrients, gases, and waste materials.
  • Protection: Provides cushioning (e.g., cartilage in joints) and defense (e.g., blood in immunity).
  • Storage: Stores energy in the form of fat and minerals in bones.

iii. Muscular Tissue

Muscular tissue is responsible for movement and contraction in the body. It is composed of muscle fibers (long, contractile cells).

Characteristics:

  • Specialized for contraction and generating force.
  • Has a rich blood supply.

Types of Muscular Tissue:

  • Skeletal Muscle:
    • Structure: Long, cylindrical, multinucleate cells with striations (stripes).
    • Function: Voluntary movement of the body, helps in locomotion and posture.
    • Examples: Muscles attached to bones (e.g., biceps, quadriceps).
  • Cardiac Muscle:
    • Structure: Branched cells with a single nucleus, striated, and connected by intercalated discs (for synchronized contraction).
    • Function: Involuntary contraction of the heart to pump blood.
    • Examples: Heart muscle (myocardium).
  • Smooth Muscle:
    • Structure: Spindle-shaped cells with a single nucleus and no striations.
    • Function: Involuntary movement of internal organs (contraction and relaxation).
    • Examples: Walls of hollow organs like the stomach, intestines, blood vessels.

Functions:

  • Movement: Skeletal muscles help in voluntary movement; smooth and cardiac muscles control involuntary functions.
  • Contraction: Muscles contract to produce force, maintain posture, and pump blood.

iv. Nervous Tissue

Nervous tissue is responsible for receiving stimuli, processing information, and transmitting electrical impulses throughout the body.

Characteristics:

  • Composed of neurons (nerve cells) and neuroglia (supporting cells).
  • Neurons are highly specialized for electrical communication.

Types of Nervous Tissue:

  • Neurons:
    • Structure: Consists of a cell body, dendrites (receive signals), and an axon (transmits signals).
    • Function: Transmit electrical impulses for communication within the body (e.g., from sensory receptors to the brain).
    • Examples: Motor neurons, sensory neurons, interneurons.
  • Neuroglia:
    • Structure: Supporting cells that do not transmit impulses but assist neurons in their function.
    • Function: Support, nourish, and protect neurons.
    • Examples: Astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, Schwann cells, microglia.

Functions:

  • Communication: Transmits electrical signals for sensory perception, movement coordination, and cognitive functions.
  • Coordination: Helps coordinate responses to stimuli and controls body activities.
  • Protection: Neuroglial cells provide support and protection to neurons.

Summary of Animal Tissues

Tissue Type Structure Function
Epithelial Tissue Closely packed cells; forms protective layers Protection, secretion, absorption, and sensation
Connective Tissue Cells scattered in an extracellular matrix Support, binding, transport, protection, and storage
Muscular Tissue Specialized cells (fibers) that can contract Movement, contraction, and force generation
Nervous Tissue Neurons and neuroglia for communication Sensory input, motor output, and processing information

2. Morphology and Anatomy of Earthworm, Cockroach, and Frog

The morphology and anatomy of organisms are studied to understand their external features (morphology) and the internal structure (anatomy). In this context, we will explore the morphology and anatomy of the earthworm, cockroach, and frog, focusing on their external features, internal organs, and physiological functions.

i. Earthworm (Genus: Lumbricus)

Morphology:

  • Shape: Cylindrical, segmented body.
  • Size: Varies from a few centimeters to over 1 meter.
  • Segments: The body is divided into numerous segments called metameres, each separated by septae (internal walls).
  • Body Covering: The body is covered with a slimy cuticle that helps in moisture retention and movement.
  • Setae: Small bristle-like structures on each segment, aiding in locomotion and anchoring the earthworm in soil.
  • Mouth: Located at the anterior end; used for ingesting soil and organic matter.
  • Anus: Located at the posterior end for the expulsion of waste.

Anatomy:

Digestive System:

Mouth → Pharynx → Esophagus → Crop (storage) → Gizzard (grinding) → Intestine → Anus.

Earthworms feed on organic matter in the soil, digesting it through their digestive system.

Circulatory System:

Closed circulatory system with blood vessels (dorsal and ventral vessels). Five pairs of aortic arches act as hearts to pump blood.

Respiratory System:

Respiration occurs through the skin (cutaneous respiration), aided by the presence of moisture on the skin.

Excretory System:

Nephridia (kidney-like structures) in each segment filter waste from the body and expel it through pores.

Nervous System:

A dorsal nerve cord runs along the body with a brain at the anterior end (paired ganglia). Segmental ganglia control each segment’s movements.

Reproductive System:

Earthworms are hermaphrodites (both male and female organs). They cross-fertilize, with sperm passing through spermathecae.

ii. Cockroach (Genus: Periplaneta)

Morphology:

  • Shape: Flattened, oval-shaped body.
  • Size: Approximately 2 to 4 cm in length.
  • Color: Brownish to black.
  • Body Segments: Divided into three main regions: head, thorax, and abdomen.
  • Antennae: Long, threadlike antennae for sensory perception.
  • Wings: Two pairs—forewings (protective, leathery) and hindwings (membranous, used for flight).
  • Legs: Three pairs of legs (used for running).
  • Cerci: Pair of sensory structures at the posterior end of the abdomen.

Anatomy:

Digestive System:

Mouth → Pharynx → Esophagus → Crop → Gizzard → Midgut → Hindgut → Anus. Food is digested with the help of digestive enzymes, and absorption occurs in the midgut.

Circulatory System:

Open circulatory system; blood flows in body cavities. Hemolymph (blood) circulates, but no distinct blood vessels exist.

Respiratory System:

Tracheal system for gas exchange. Air enters through small openings called spiracles, which branch into tracheae and carry oxygen directly to the cells.

Excretory System:

Malpighian tubules collect waste from the blood and excrete it as uric acid, which is passed into the hindgut for expulsion.

Nervous System:

Dorsal brain and ventral nerve cord with segmental ganglia control movements. Sensory organs: Compound eyes, antennae, and cerci detect environmental stimuli.

Reproductive System:

Cockroaches are dioecious (separate sexes). Females lay eggs in a protective ootheca (egg case), which contains multiple eggs.

iii. Frog (Genus: Rana)

Morphology:

  • Shape: Compact, streamlined body with well-developed limbs for jumping.
  • Size: Varies from 5 cm to over 30 cm in length.
  • Skin: Moist, glandular skin that facilitates respiration and absorption of water.
  • Eyes: Large, prominent eyes that aid in vision above water.
  • Limbs:
    • Hind limbs: Long and muscular for jumping.
    • Forelimbs: Shorter, with four digits.
  • External Nares: Two small openings on the snout for breathing.
  • Mouth: Large and wide, used for capturing prey.

Anatomy:

Digestive System:

Mouth → Esophagus → Stomach → Small intestine → Large intestine → Cloaca → Anus. Frogs are carnivores and swallow prey whole, with digestion occurring in the stomach and absorption in the intestines.

Circulatory System:

Closed circulatory system with a three-chambered heart (two atria and one ventricle). Blood is pumped to both the lungs (for oxygenation) and the rest of the body.

Respiratory System:

Lungs for pulmonary respiration and skin for cutaneous respiration (breathing through the skin). Frogs also breathe through their mouth (buccal respiration).

Excretory System:

Kidneys filter waste from the blood. Ureters carry urine to the bladder, and it is expelled through the cloaca. Frogs excrete uric acid (semi-solid) to conserve water.

Nervous System:

A brain with a well-developed cerebrum for sensory processing and coordination. A spinal cord extends along the body, controlling movements. Sensory organs include large eyes, tympanic membrane (eardrum), and lateral line system (detects vibrations in water).

Reproductive System:

Frogs are dioecious (separate sexes). Male frogs call to attract females during the breeding season. Fertilization is external, and females lay eggs in water, where they hatch into tadpoles that later metamorphose into adult frogs.

Comparison of Earthworm, Cockroach, and Frog

Organism Earthworm Cockroach Frog
Body Shape Cylindrical, segmented Flattened, oval-shaped Compact, streamlined, limbs adapted for jumping
Body Segments Divided into metameres Divided into head, thorax, abdomen Divided into head, trunk, limbs
Circulatory System Closed (blood vessels) Open (hemolymph in body cavity) Closed (three-chambered heart)
Respiratory System Cutaneous respiration (skin) Tracheal system (spiracles) Lungs and skin (cutaneous respiration)
Excretory System Nephridia Malpighian tubules Kidneys (uric acid excretion)
Reproductive System Hermaphroditic (cross-fertilization) Dioecious (separate sexes) Dioecious (external fertilization)
Locomotion Peristalsis (using setae) Running and flying Jumping (hind limbs)
