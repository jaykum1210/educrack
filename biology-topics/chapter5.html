Chapter 5 - Cell Basics

1. Cell Theory

The Cell Theory is one of the fundamental principles in biology that explains the structure and function of cells in living organisms. It provides a conceptual framework for understanding the organization and functioning of life at the cellular level. The theory has three main principles:

All living organisms are made up of cells.

This principle states that all forms of life, from the simplest bacteria to the most complex multicellular organisms, are composed of one or more cells. Cells are considered the basic structural and functional units of life.

The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living organisms.

The cell is the smallest unit of life capable of carrying out all necessary life functions such as metabolism, reproduction, response to stimuli, and homeostasis. In multicellular organisms, cells work together to form tissues, organs, and systems, but each cell still maintains its individual functions and characteristics.

All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

This principle, formulated by Rudolf Virchow in 1855, states that cells cannot spontaneously arise. Instead, they are produced by the division of existing cells. This concept disputes the theory of spontaneous generation (the idea that living organisms can arise from non-living matter) and supports the idea of cell division as the basis for new cell formation.

Historical Development of Cell Theory

  • Robert Hooke (1665): First observed and coined the term “cell” after examining a thin slice of cork under a microscope. He saw small, box-like structures and referred to them as "cells" because they resembled the small rooms (cells) in a monastery.
  • Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1674): Using his improved microscope, he observed living cells, such as bacteria and protozoa, in pond water, making significant contributions to the understanding of cellular life.
  • Matthias Schleiden (1838): Proposed that all plant tissues are composed of cells.
  • Theodor Schwann (1839): Extended Schleiden's work to animals, stating that all living organisms, both plants and animals, are made up of cells.
  • Rudolf Virchow (1855): Stated that cells arise from pre-existing cells, adding the final principle to the cell theory.

Importance of the Cell Theory

  • It forms the foundation of modern biology and provides a unifying concept for the study of living organisms.
  • It emphasizes the essential role of cells in biological functions, including metabolism, reproduction, and genetic inheritance.
  • Understanding the cellular basis of life has led to advances in areas such as medicine, genetics, and biotechnology.

Exceptions to Cell Theory

While cell theory is a universally accepted principle in biology, there are a few exceptions, including:

  • Viruses: Viruses are not made up of cells. They consist of a protein coat and genetic material (either DNA or RNA). They cannot carry out metabolic processes on their own and require a host cell to reproduce.
  • Mitochondria and Chloroplasts: These organelles within eukaryotic cells have their own DNA and can replicate independently, suggesting they may have originated from independent prokaryotic cells through an evolutionary process known as endosymbiosis.

2. Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic Cells

Cells are the basic building blocks of life, and they come in two main types: prokaryotic and eukaryotic. These two types of cells differ in their structure, function, and complexity. Below is a comparison of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells:

Structure

Feature Prokaryotic Cells Eukaryotic Cells
Size Smaller (usually 0.1–5 micrometers) Larger (typically 10–100 micrometers)
Nucleus No defined nucleus (DNA is free in the cytoplasm) Has a defined nucleus (DNA enclosed in nuclear membrane)
Genetic Material Single, circular DNA molecule (genophore) Linear DNA inside the nucleus, in chromosomes
Organelles Lacks membrane-bound organelles Has membrane-bound organelles (e.g., mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus)
Cell Wall Present (made of peptidoglycan in bacteria) Present in plants and fungi (cellulose in plants, chitin in fungi)
Plasma Membrane Present Present
Cytoplasm Simple, with few structures Complex, with various organelles suspended in cytosol
Ribosomes Small (70S ribosomes) Larger (80S ribosomes in cytoplasm, 70S in mitochondria and chloroplasts)

Genetic Material and Cell Division

Feature Prokaryotic Cells Eukaryotic Cells
DNA Structure Circular, free in the cytoplasm (nucleoid region) Linear, found in the nucleus
Plasmids Present (extra-chromosomal DNA) Absent (except in mitochondria and chloroplasts)
Cell Division Binary fission (asexual) Mitosis (asexual) and meiosis (sexual)
Reproduction Asexual (by binary fission) Both sexual and asexual (e.g., mitosis)

Metabolism and Energy Production

Feature Prokaryotic Cells Eukaryotic Cells
Metabolism Can be aerobic or anaerobic Primarily aerobic (some can perform anaerobic respiration)
Respiratory Enzymes In the cell membrane In the mitochondria
Energy Production Occurs in the cell membrane and cytoplasm Occurs in mitochondria (eukaryotes) and chloroplasts (plants)

Examples

Feature Prokaryotic Cells Eukaryotic Cells
Examples Bacteria (e.g., Escherichia coli, Streptococcus) Animals, plants, fungi, and protists (e.g., human cells, plant cells, yeast)

Key Differences:

  • Nucleus: Prokaryotic cells do not have a membrane-bound nucleus. Their genetic material is found in a region called the nucleoid. Eukaryotic cells have a membrane-bound nucleus that contains the cell's genetic material (DNA).
  • Organelles: Prokaryotes lack membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, and Golgi apparatus. Eukaryotes have complex membrane-bound organelles, allowing for compartmentalization of cellular functions.
  • DNA: Prokaryotic cells contain a single, circular DNA molecule that floats freely in the cytoplasm. Eukaryotic cells have multiple linear chromosomes contained within the nucleus.
  • Cell Division: Prokaryotes reproduce through binary fission, where the DNA is copied and the cell divides into two identical daughter cells. Eukaryotes divide through mitosis for growth or asexual reproduction and meiosis for sexual reproduction.
  • Size: Prokaryotic cells are smaller (1–10 micrometers) and simpler in structure. Eukaryotic cells are larger (10–100 micrometers) and more complex, containing many specialized organelles.

Similarities Between Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells:

  • Both contain ribosomes for protein synthesis.
  • Both have a plasma membrane that regulates the movement of substances in and out of the cell.
  • Both have cytoplasm that holds cell components.
  • Both contain genetic material (DNA) that carries information for cell function and reproduction.

3. Structure of Plant and Animal Cells

Both plant and animal cells are eukaryotic, meaning they have membrane-bound organelles and a defined nucleus. However, they differ in their structures and certain organelles due to their specific functions. Here's a comparison of the structures found in plant and animal cells:

Common Features of Plant and Animal Cells

  • Plasma Membrane: Both plant and animal cells have a plasma membrane that encloses the cell and regulates the movement of substances in and out of the cell.
  • Cytoplasm: Both cell types contain cytoplasm, a jelly-like substance where various cell organelles are suspended. It aids in the movement of materials around the cell.
  • Nucleus: Both have a nucleus that contains the cell's DNA. The nucleus controls the cell's activities, including growth, metabolism, and reproduction. It is enclosed by a nuclear membrane and contains nucleolus, which is involved in ribosome production.
  • Mitochondria: Both cells have mitochondria, the powerhouse of the cell, where cellular respiration occurs to produce energy (ATP).
  • Ribosomes: Ribosomes are present in both plant and animal cells, responsible for protein synthesis.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): Both have rough ER (with ribosomes) and smooth ER (without ribosomes). Rough ER is involved in protein synthesis, while smooth ER is involved in lipid synthesis and detoxification.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Both plant and animal cells contain a Golgi apparatus, which modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for transport.
  • Lysosomes: Lysosomes are present mainly in animal cells and help break down waste material. They contain digestive enzymes.

Plant Cell Structure

In addition to the common organelles shared with animal cells, plant cells have certain specialized structures:

  • Cell Wall: The cell wall is present in plant cells, providing structural support and rigidity. It is made of cellulose and helps maintain the shape of the plant cell. It also acts as a protective barrier.
  • Chloroplasts: Plant cells contain chloroplasts, which are involved in photosynthesis. Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll, a green pigment that captures light energy for converting carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.
  • Central Vacuole: The vacuole is large and central in plant cells. It contains cell sap, which is a mixture of water, salts, sugars, and other substances. The vacuole helps maintain turgor pressure, provides structural support, and stores nutrients and waste products.
  • Plasmodesmata: Plasmodesmata are channels between plant cells that allow for the exchange of materials and communication between adjacent cells.

Animal Cell Structure

In addition to the common organelles shared with plant cells, animal cells have certain specialized structures:

  • Lysosomes: Lysosomes in animal cells contain digestive enzymes that break down waste materials, cellular debris, and foreign particles.
  • Centrioles: Centrioles are found in animal cells and are involved in cell division (mitosis and meiosis). They help in organizing microtubules to form the spindle fibers during cell division.
  • Cilia and Flagella: Some animal cells have cilia or flagella, which are hair-like structures used for movement or for moving substances along the surface of the cell.
