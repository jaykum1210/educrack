Chapter 11 - Digestive and Respiratory Systems

1. Human Digestive System and Disorders

The human digestive system is responsible for the breakdown of food into nutrients, which the body uses for energy, growth, and repair. It consists of a series of organs that work together to process food. Let's dive into the structure and function of the digestive system, followed by common digestive disorders.

Structure of the Human Digestive System

The human digestive system consists of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and accessory digestive organs. The GI tract is a continuous tube from the mouth to the anus, while accessory organs help in the digestion process.

Main Parts of the Digestive System:

  • Mouth:
    • Function: The mouth is the entry point for food. Teeth break down food into smaller pieces (mechanical digestion), and saliva, which contains the enzyme amylase, begins the process of starch digestion (chemical digestion).
  • Esophagus:
    • Function: The esophagus is a muscular tube that connects the mouth to the stomach. Food is moved through the esophagus by peristalsis, a wave-like muscle contraction.
  • Stomach:
    • Function: The stomach secretes gastric juices, including hydrochloric acid and enzymes like pepsin, to break down proteins. The stomach churns food to form a semi-liquid substance called chyme.
  • Small Intestine:
    • Function: The small intestine is the primary site for digestion and absorption. It has three parts:
      • Duodenum: Receives bile from the liver and digestive enzymes from the pancreas to digest fats, proteins, and carbohydrates.
      • Jejunum: Main site for the absorption of nutrients.
      • Ileum: Absorbs remaining nutrients and connects to the large intestine.
  • Liver:
    • Function: The liver produces bile, which is stored in the gallbladder and helps in the emulsification (breakdown) of fats.
  • Gallbladder:
    • Function: Stores and concentrates bile from the liver and releases it into the small intestine to aid in fat digestion.
  • Pancreas:
    • Function: The pancreas produces digestive enzymes (like amylase, lipase, proteases) and bicarbonate to neutralize stomach acid, aiding digestion in the small intestine.
  • Large Intestine (Colon):
    • Function: The large intestine absorbs water and salts from the remaining indigestible food matter and forms feces. It includes the cecum, ascending colon, transverse colon, descending colon, and rectum.
  • Rectum and Anus:
    • Function: The rectum stores feces until it is ready to be excreted through the anus during defecation.

Digestive Process

  • Ingestion: Food enters the mouth.
  • Mechanical digestion: Food is physically broken down by chewing and churning in the stomach.
  • Chemical digestion: Enzymes and digestive juices break down complex molecules into simpler ones. For example, starches are broken down into sugars, proteins into amino acids, and fats into fatty acids and glycerol.
  • Absorption: Nutrients are absorbed primarily in the small intestine, passing into the bloodstream or lymphatic system.
  • Excretion: Indigestible food materials and waste are eliminated from the body in the form of feces.

Common Digestive Disorders

Several digestive disorders can affect the proper functioning of the digestive system. Here are some common ones:

i. Acid Reflux (GERD - Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease)

  • Cause: Occurs when stomach acid or bile irritates the food pipe (esophagus).
  • Symptoms: Heartburn, regurgitation, chest pain, difficulty swallowing.
  • Treatment: Antacids, proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), lifestyle changes (e.g., avoiding spicy foods, eating smaller meals).

ii. Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

  • Cause: A functional disorder of the digestive system, with no underlying damage to the intestines.
  • Symptoms: Abdominal pain, bloating, diarrhea, constipation.
  • Treatment: Dietary changes, stress management, medications (fiber supplements, antispasmodics).

iii. Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

  • Types: Includes Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.
  • Cause: Chronic inflammation of the digestive tract, often due to an autoimmune response.
  • Symptoms: Severe diarrhea, weight loss, fatigue, abdominal pain.
  • Treatment: Anti-inflammatory drugs, immune suppressants, antibiotics, surgery in severe cases.

iv. Lactose Intolerance

  • Cause: The body lacks sufficient levels of the enzyme lactase, which is needed to break down lactose (a sugar found in milk).
  • Symptoms: Bloating, diarrhea, gas, abdominal pain after consuming dairy products.
  • Treatment: Lactose-free products, lactase supplements.

v. Gallstones

  • Cause: Solid particles (stones) form in the gallbladder, which can block the bile ducts.
  • Symptoms: Sudden and intense pain in the upper right abdomen, nausea, vomiting.
  • Treatment: Surgery (cholecystectomy) to remove the gallbladder, dietary changes.

vi. Celiac Disease

  • Cause: An autoimmune disorder in which the ingestion of gluten (a protein found in wheat, barley, and rye) damages the small intestine lining.
  • Symptoms: Diarrhea, weight loss, abdominal pain, malnutrition.
  • Treatment: Strict gluten-free diet for life.

vii. Peptic Ulcers

  • Cause: Sores that develop on the lining of the stomach, small intestine, or esophagus, often caused by Helicobacter pylori infection or the prolonged use of NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs).
  • Symptoms: Stomach pain, bloating, heartburn, nausea.
  • Treatment: Antibiotics (for H. pylori), proton pump inhibitors, antacids.

viii. Gastroenteritis (Stomach Flu)

  • Cause: Infection caused by viruses (like norovirus) or bacteria (like Salmonella or E. coli), leading to inflammation of the stomach and intestines.
  • Symptoms: Diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, fever.
  • Treatment: Hydration, rest, antibiotics (in bacterial cases).

2. Mechanism of Breathing and Respiratory Disorders

Breathing is a vital process that allows oxygen (O₂) to be brought into the body and carbon dioxide (CO₂) to be expelled. The respiratory system involves the lungs, airways, and other structures responsible for inhaling and exhaling gases. Let’s break down how breathing works and some common respiratory disorders.

Mechanism of Breathing

Breathing is divided into two main phases: inhalation (inspiration) and exhalation (expiration). These processes are governed by the pressure changes in the lungs and the action of respiratory muscles, particularly the diaphragm and intercostal muscles.

Inhalation (Inspiration):

Process:

  • During inhalation, the diaphragm contracts and moves downward, and the intercostal muscles between the ribs contract, raising the rib cage.
  • This increases the volume of the thoracic cavity (the chest area) and decreases the pressure inside the lungs (below atmospheric pressure).
  • As a result, air is drawn into the lungs from the outside environment due to the pressure difference (high to low pressure).

Key Points:

  • Diaphragm moves downward.
  • Rib cage expands.
  • Air enters the lungs.

Exhalation (Expiration):

Process:

  • During exhalation, the diaphragm relaxes and moves upward, and the intercostal muscles relax, causing the rib cage to move downward and inward.
  • This decreases the volume of the thoracic cavity and increases the pressure inside the lungs.
  • As a result, air is pushed out of the lungs and expelled from the body.

Key Points:

  • Diaphragm moves upward.
  • Rib cage contracts.
  • Air is expelled from the lungs.

Gas Exchange:

The exchange of gases occurs in the alveoli, which are tiny air sacs in the lungs. Oxygen from the inhaled air diffuses into the blood in the surrounding capillaries, while carbon dioxide (a waste product of metabolism) diffuses from the blood into the alveoli to be exhaled.

Respiratory Disorders

Several respiratory disorders can affect the efficiency of breathing and gas exchange. Here are some common respiratory conditions:

i. Asthma

  • Cause: Asthma is a chronic condition that causes inflammation and narrowing of the airways, making it difficult to breathe. It can be triggered by allergens, exercise, cold air, or respiratory infections.
  • Symptoms: Wheezing, shortness of breath, coughing, tightness in the chest.
  • Treatment: Use of bronchodilators (to open the airways) and inhaled corticosteroids (to reduce inflammation). Avoiding triggers is also crucial.

ii. Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

  • Cause: COPD is a progressive lung disease, often caused by long-term smoking, that obstructs airflow in and out of the lungs. It includes conditions like emphysema and chronic bronchitis.
  • Symptoms: Chronic cough, shortness of breath, wheezing, and difficulty in performing physical activities.
  • Treatment: There is no cure for COPD, but treatments like bronchodilators, steroids, oxygen therapy, and pulmonary rehabilitation can help manage symptoms.

iii. Pneumonia

  • Cause: Pneumonia is an infection of the lungs caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi. The infection causes inflammation and fluid buildup in the alveoli, impairing gas exchange.
  • Symptoms: Fever, cough, chest pain, difficulty breathing, fatigue.
  • Treatment: Antibiotics (for bacterial pneumonia), antiviral medications (for viral pneumonia), and sometimes oxygen therapy.

iv. Tuberculosis (TB)

  • Cause: Tuberculosis is a bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which primarily affects the lungs but can spread to other organs.
  • Symptoms: Persistent cough, chest pain, weight loss, night sweats, coughing up blood.
  • Treatment: A combination of antibiotics for several months. Drug-resistant TB requires more aggressive treatment.

v. Pulmonary Edema

  • Cause: Pulmonary edema occurs when fluid accumulates in the lungs, often due to heart failure. This prevents the proper exchange of gases in the alveoli.
  • Symptoms: Shortness of breath, coughing, wheezing, difficulty breathing while lying down.
  • Treatment: Diuretics to remove excess fluid, oxygen therapy, and sometimes heart medications if heart failure is the cause.

vi. Emphysema

  • Cause: A type of COPD, emphysema involves the destruction of the alveolar walls, leading to the loss of surface area for gas exchange.
  • Symptoms: Shortness of breath, chronic cough, wheezing, and fatigue.
  • Treatment: Smoking cessation, bronchodilators, oxygen therapy, and sometimes surgery (lung volume reduction or transplant).

vii. Sleep Apnea

  • Cause: Sleep apnea is a sleep disorder where a person's breathing repeatedly stops and starts during sleep due to an obstruction in the airway or failure of the brain to signal the muscles to breathe.
  • Symptoms: Snoring, choking or gasping for air during sleep, excessive daytime sleepiness, morning headaches.
  • Treatment: Use of a CPAP machine (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure) during sleep, lifestyle changes like weight loss, or surgery in some cases.

viii. Cystic Fibrosis

  • Cause: Cystic fibrosis is a genetic disorder that affects the respiratory, digestive, and reproductive systems. It causes thick, sticky mucus to build up in the lungs, leading to chronic respiratory infections and difficulty breathing.
  • Symptoms: Persistent cough, wheezing, frequent lung infections, difficulty breathing, poor growth.
  • Treatment: Regular chest physiotherapy, inhaled medications, enzyme replacement therapy, and lung transplants in severe cases.

ix. Bronchitis

  • Cause: Bronchitis is the inflammation of the bronchial tubes (airways) that carry air to and from the lungs. It can be acute (often due to a viral infection) or chronic (caused by long-term irritation from smoking).
  • Symptoms: Coughing, mucus production, wheezing, and shortness of breath.
  • Treatment: Acute bronchitis usually resolves on its own, while chronic bronchitis may require medications like bronchodilators and steroids.
