Chapter 2 - Classification of Plants and Animals

1. Plant Kingdom: Algae, Bryophytes, Pteridophytes, Gymnosperms, Angiosperms

i. Algae

Characteristics:

  • Simple, aquatic plants.
  • Non-vascular, meaning they do not have specialized tissue (xylem and phloem) for transporting water and nutrients.
  • Contain chlorophyll and can perform photosynthesis.
  • Unicellular or multicellular organisms.
  • Reproduction can be sexual or asexual.

Examples:

  • Green Algae (e.g., Chlamydomonas, Spirogyra).
  • Red Algae (e.g., Polysiphonia).
  • Brown Algae (e.g., Fucus, Laminaria).

Importance:

  • Primary producers in aquatic ecosystems.
  • Provide oxygen and serve as food for aquatic organisms.

ii. Bryophytes

Characteristics:

  • Non-vascular plants, meaning they lack vascular tissues (xylem and phloem).
  • Small, mostly found in moist environments.
  • Body made of simple tissues.
  • Reproduction is through spores, requiring water for fertilization (sperm must swim to the egg).
  • Have rhizoids instead of true roots for anchorage.

Examples:

  • Mosses (e.g., Polytrichum).
  • Liverworts (e.g., Marchantia).
  • Hornworts (e.g., Anthoceros).

Importance:

  • Help in soil formation.
  • Provide a habitat for small animals and serve in the ecosystem as moisture regulators.

iii. Pteridophytes

Characteristics:

  • Vascular plants (have xylem and phloem).
  • Seedless and reproduce by spores (via sporangia).
  • The plant body is differentiated into roots, stems, and leaves.
  • Require water for fertilization (sperm swim to the egg).
  • Have true roots, stems, and leaves, making them more complex than bryophytes.

Examples:

  • Ferns (e.g., Nephrolepis).
  • Horsetails (e.g., Equisetum).
  • Club mosses (e.g., Lycopodium).

Importance:

  • Contribute to soil formation.
  • Used for ornamental purposes and have medicinal uses.

iv. Gymnosperms

Characteristics:

  • Seed-producing plants, but unlike angiosperms, their seeds are not enclosed in a fruit.
  • Vascular plants with specialized tissues (xylem and phloem).
  • Reproduction is sexual; they produce seeds in cones (e.g., male cones for pollen and female cones for seeds).
  • Have needle-like leaves and are usually adapted to dry environments.

Examples:

  • Conifers (e.g., Pines, Firs, Spruces).
  • Cycads (e.g., Cycas).
  • Ginkgo (e.g., Ginkgo biloba).

Importance:

  • Provide timber, paper, and resins.
  • Serve as habitat for wildlife.

v. Angiosperms

Characteristics:

  • Also known as flowering plants.
  • Seed-producing plants whose seeds are enclosed within a fruit (derived from the ovary).
  • Vascular plants with well-differentiated tissues.
  • Reproduction involves flowers (male and female organs for pollination) and fruits (develop from the fertilized ovary).
  • Can be monocots (e.g., grasses, lilies) or dicots (e.g., roses, beans), based on the number of seed leaves (cotyledons).

Examples:

  • Monocots: Maize (corn), Rice, Wheat.
  • Dicots: Rose, Sunflower, Pea.

Importance:

  • Food crops for humans and animals (e.g., wheat, rice, fruits).
  • Source of medicine, fibers, and wood.

Summary Table:

Group Characteristics Examples Importance
Algae Simple, aquatic, non-vascular, photosynthetic Green, Red, and Brown Algae Oxygen production, primary producers in aquatic systems
Bryophytes Non-vascular, small, spore-reproducing, need water for fertilization Mosses, Liverworts, Hornworts Soil formation, habitat for small organisms
Pteridophytes Vascular, seedless, spore-reproducing, need water for fertilization Ferns, Horsetails, Club mosses Soil stabilization, ornamental uses

2. Animal Kingdom: Levels of Organization, Symmetry, Germ Layers

1. Levels of Organization

The level of organization refers to the complexity of structure and function in an organism. In the Animal Kingdom, organisms are classified into different levels based on how their cells, tissues, organs, and systems are arranged.

Levels of Organization:

  • Cellular Level: The simplest level of organization where organisms consist of individual cells that perform all necessary functions. Example: Porifera (sponges).
  • Tissue Level: Groups of similar cells work together to perform specific functions. Example: Cnidaria (jellyfish, corals).
  • Organ Level: Tissues combine to form organs, which are specialized structures to perform specific functions. Example: Platyhelminthes (flatworms).
  • Organ System Level: Groups of organs work together to perform complex functions, maintaining homeostasis and performing life functions. Example: Humans, Mammals, and other complex organisms.

Symmetry

Symmetry refers to the arrangement of body parts around a central axis. Animals can exhibit different types of symmetry based on how their body parts are arranged.

Types of Symmetry:

  • Asymmetry: No symmetry in the body; the body does not have a balanced shape or corresponding parts. Example: Porifera (sponges).
  • Radial Symmetry: Body parts are arranged around a central axis, where any plane passing through the central point divides the body into mirror-image halves. Example: Cnidaria (jellyfish, sea anemones), Echinodermata (starfish).
  • Bilateral Symmetry: The body can be divided into two identical halves by a single plane passing through the midline (left and right sides are mirror images). Example: Mammals, Arthropods (insects), Chordates (vertebrates).

Germ Layers

Germ layers are the three primary layers of cells in an embryo that give rise to different tissues and organs in the body. Based on the number of germ layers, animals are classified into two main categories:

Types of Germ Layers:

  • Diploblastic (2 Germ Layers): Organisms with two embryonic germ layers: ectoderm (outer layer) and endoderm (inner layer). These animals usually have a simple body plan and lack complex organs. Example: Cnidaria (jellyfish, corals), Ctenophora (comb jellies).
  • Triploblastic (3 Germ Layers): Organisms with three embryonic germ layers: ectoderm, mesoderm (middle layer), and endoderm. The mesoderm forms muscles, circulatory systems, and internal organs. Triploblastic animals have more complex body structures and organ systems. Example: Platyhelminthes (flatworms), Arthropods, Molluscs, Chordates (vertebrates).

Summary Table:

Feature Description Examples
Levels of Organization Classification based on the complexity of structure and function: cellular, tissue, organ, system Porifera (cellular), Humans (organ system)
Symmetry Body arrangement around a central point or axis: asymmetry, radial, bilateral Porifera (asymmetry), Mammals (bilateral)
Germ Layers Embryonic layers from which different tissues develop: diploblastic (2 layers), triploblastic (3 layers) Cnidaria (diploblastic), Humans (triploblastic)

3. Salient Features of Non-Chordates and Chordates

Non-Chordates

Non-chordates are animals that do not possess a notochord (a rod-like structure) during any stage of development. They are more primitive and are classified into various phyla based on their body structure, symmetry, and other characteristics.

Salient Features of Non-Chordates:

  • Lack of Notochord: Non-chordates do not have a notochord, which is a flexible, rod-like structure that supports the body.
  • Body Symmetry:
    • Radial Symmetry: Found in organisms like Cnidaria (jellyfish, corals), where the body is arranged around a central axis.
    • Bilateral Symmetry: Found in organisms like Arthropoda (insects, crustaceans), where the body can be divided into two identical halves along a central plane.
  • Development:
    • Diploblastic (2 germ layers): Cnidarians (jellyfish, corals) have two embryonic layers: ectoderm and endoderm.
    • Triploblastic (3 germ layers): Phyla like Platyhelminthes (flatworms) and Arthropoda (insects) have three embryonic layers: ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm.
  • Presence of Coelom: Non-chordates may be acoelomate (e.g., Platyhelminthes), pseudocoelomate (e.g., Nematoda), or coelomate (e.g., Annelida).
  • Circulatory System: Open Circulatory System in many non-chordates like Arthropoda and Mollusca; Closed Circulatory System in some, like Annelida.
  • Nervous System: Typically simple, often with nerve nets (e.g., Cnidaria) or ganglia in more complex non-chordates (e.g., Arthropoda).
  • Respiration: Respiratory structures like gills, tracheae, or even skin (e.g., Earthworms).
  • Reproduction: Generally sexual with external or internal fertilization. Some exhibit asexual reproduction (e.g., Hydra).

Examples of Non-Chordates:

  • Porifera (sponges)
  • Cnidaria (jellyfish, corals)
  • Platyhelminthes (flatworms)
  • Nematoda (roundworms)
  • Annelida (earthworms, leeches)
  • Arthropoda (insects, crustaceans)
  • Mollusca (snails, clams)
  • Echinodermata (starfish, sea urchins)

Chordates

Chordates are animals that possess a notochord at some stage in their development, along with other defining characteristics. Chordates are more advanced and are further classified into invertebrates (non-vertebrates) and vertebrates (animals with a backbone).

Salient Features of Chordates:

  • Presence of Notochord: A flexible rod-like structure that provides support to the body, present at some stage of development (may be replaced by a vertebral column in vertebrates).
  • Dorsal Hollow Nerve Cord: The nerve cord is located along the dorsal side of the body, and it is hollow. It develops into the brain and spinal cord in vertebrates.
  • Pharyngeal Slits/Pouches: These are openings in the pharynx (throat area) found in embryos. In vertebrates, they may develop into gills or parts of the ear and other structures.
  • Post-anal Tail: Chordates have a tail that extends beyond the anus at some point in their development. In many vertebrates, the tail is present only in the embryonic stage.
  • Body Symmetry: Bilateral Symmetry: Found in most chordates, where the body can be divided into two mirror-image halves.
  • Presence of Coelom: Chordates are generally coelomate, meaning they have a true coelom (body cavity) that is lined with mesoderm.
  • Endoskeleton: Chordates, especially vertebrates, have an internal skeleton made of bone or cartilage, which supports the body.
  • Circulatory System: Typically closed circulatory systems, with a heart that pumps blood through blood vessels.
  • Nervous System: A well-developed brain and spinal cord (in vertebrates), along with complex sense organs.
  • Reproduction: Primarily sexual reproduction, with internal or external fertilization, depending on the species.

Examples of Chordates:

Non-Vertebrate Chordates:

  • Urochordata (sea squirts)
  • Cephalochordata (lancelets)

Vertebrate Chordates (with a backbone):

  • Fish (e.g., sharks, bony fish)
  • Amphibians (e.g., frogs, salamanders)
  • Reptiles (e.g., snakes, lizards)
  • Birds (e.g., sparrows, eagles)
  • Mammals (e.g., humans, lions, whales)
