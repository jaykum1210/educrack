Chapter 16 - Biology and Human Welfare

1. Health and Disease

Definition

  • Health is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.
  • Disease refers to an abnormal condition affecting the body or mind, causing discomfort or dysfunction.

Factors Affecting Health

Genetic Factors

  • Inherited genetic traits play a crucial role in determining an individual's susceptibility to certain diseases.
  • Example: Genetic predispositions to heart disease, diabetes, or cancer.

Environmental Factors

  • Environment, including air quality, water, sanitation, and exposure to toxins, influences health.
  • Example: Poor living conditions can increase the risk of respiratory diseases.

Lifestyle Factors

  • Lifestyle choices such as diet, physical activity, smoking, and alcohol consumption affect health.
  • Example: A balanced diet and exercise reduce the risk of diseases like obesity and diabetes.

Healthcare

  • Access to medical care, including preventive, diagnostic, and treatment services, is crucial for maintaining health.
  • Example: Regular checkups and vaccinations help maintain better health.

Types of Diseases

Infectious Diseases

  • Caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites).
  • Examples: Tuberculosis, Typhoid, Influenza, COVID-19, Malaria.

Non-Infectious Diseases

  • Caused by genetic, environmental, or lifestyle factors.
  • Examples: Heart disease, diabetes, hypertension, asthma.

Deficiency Diseases

  • Caused by a lack of essential nutrients.
  • Examples: Vitamin A deficiency (night blindness), Iron deficiency (anemia).

Autoimmune Diseases

  • Conditions where the immune system attacks healthy cells.
  • Examples: Rheumatoid arthritis, Type 1 diabetes, Multiple sclerosis.

Cancer

  • Uncontrolled growth of abnormal cells forming tumors, may spread to other parts.
  • Examples: Lung cancer, Breast cancer, Leukemia.

Disease Prevention

Vaccination

  • Vaccines stimulate the immune system to prevent infectious diseases.
  • Examples: Polio vaccine, Measles vaccine.

Hygiene and Sanitation

  • Proper hygiene practices prevent the spread of infectious diseases.

Healthy Diet and Exercise

  • A balanced diet and regular physical activity strengthen the immune system and reduce disease risk.

Regular Health Checkups

  • Routine screenings help detect diseases early and improve outcomes.
  • Examples: Blood pressure check, Mammogram for cancer detection.

Avoiding Risky Behaviors

  • Avoid smoking, excessive alcohol, and unprotected sexual activity to reduce disease risk.

The Immune System and Disease Defense

Components of the Immune System

  • White blood cells, antibodies, and the lymphatic system work together to protect the body.

Types of Immunity

  • Innate Immunity: The body’s first line of defense.
  • Acquired Immunity: Immunity developed after exposure to pathogens or through vaccination.

Common Diseases and Their Symptoms

Respiratory Diseases

  • Asthma: Wheezing, shortness of breath.
  • Pneumonia: Fever, chest pain, difficulty breathing.

Cardiovascular Diseases

  • Hypertension: High blood pressure, dizziness.
  • Heart Attack: Chest pain, shortness of breath, nausea.

Diabetes

  • Symptoms: Frequent urination, increased thirst, blurred vision.

Digestive Disorders

  • IBS: Abdominal pain, bloating, diarrhea.
  • Ulcer: Stomach pain, nausea, vomiting.

Treatment of Diseases

Medicines

  • Antibiotics, antivirals, and other medicines treat infections and manage chronic conditions.

Surgery

  • Necessary for conditions like tumors or organ failure.

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Changes like diet, exercise, and stress management improve health outcomes in chronic diseases.

Alternative Medicine

  • Acupuncture, herbal medicine, and homeopathy are used by some individuals for treatment.

Mental Health

  • Mental health is important for emotional, psychological, and social well-being.
  • Common disorders: Depression, anxiety, schizophrenia.

2. Microbes in Human Welfare

Definition

  • Microorganisms (microbes) are tiny organisms that are not visible to the naked eye but play a crucial role in human welfare.
  • They include bacteria, fungi, viruses, protozoa, and algae.
  • While many microbes cause diseases, many others are beneficial and are used in various industries, medicine, agriculture, and environmental management.

Microbes in Medicine

i. Production of Antibiotics

  • Antibiotics are substances produced by microorganisms that help in treating infections caused by harmful microbes.
  • Example: Penicillium fungus produces penicillin, the first antibiotic, which has saved millions of lives by curing bacterial infections like pneumonia and tuberculosis.

ii. Production of Vaccines

  • Vaccines are made using weakened or inactivated forms of microorganisms, which help the immune system build immunity against specific diseases.
  • Example: The polio vaccine is derived from weakened strains of the polio virus to prevent polio in humans.

iii. Production of Insulin

  • Recombinant DNA technology allows the production of human insulin using bacteria (E. coli), which is used for managing diabetes.
  • Example: Escherichia coli bacteria are engineered to produce insulin, which has revolutionized diabetes treatment.

iv. Probiotics

  • Probiotics are live microorganisms that provide health benefits when consumed in adequate amounts. They help in maintaining a healthy balance of beneficial microbes in the digestive system.
  • Example: Lactobacillus species are used in yogurts and other dairy products to promote gut health.

Microbes in the Food Industry

i. Fermentation

  • Microorganisms are widely used in the fermentation process to produce various foods and beverages.
  • Example:
    • Baker’s yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) is used to leaven bread and produce alcohol.
    • Lactic acid bacteria are used to ferment milk products like yogurt, cheese, and buttermilk.

ii. Production of Alcohol

  • Yeasts are used in the production of alcoholic beverages such as beer, wine, and spirits by fermenting sugars.
  • Example: Saccharomyces cerevisiae is used in brewing beer and making wine from grapes.

iii. Fermented Foods

  • Fermented foods not only taste better but are often more nutritious and have longer shelf lives.
  • Example: Fermented products like pickles, sauerkraut, tempeh, and kimchi involve the action of microbes such as bacteria and fungi.

Microbes in Agriculture

i. Nitrogen Fixation

  • Certain bacteria, known as nitrogen-fixing bacteria, convert atmospheric nitrogen into a form that plants can use for growth.
  • Example: Rhizobium bacteria, which form symbiotic relationships with leguminous plants like peas and beans, fix nitrogen in the soil, enriching it for plant growth.

ii. Biofertilizers

  • Microorganisms are used as biofertilizers to improve soil fertility. These biofertilizers help in providing essential nutrients to plants.
  • Example: Azotobacter and Azospirillum are nitrogen-fixing bacteria used to enhance soil fertility.

iii. Biopesticides

  • Biopesticides are microbial-based products used to control pests and diseases in crops, reducing the need for chemical pesticides.
  • Example: Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) is a bacterium that produces a toxin harmful to certain pests but safe for humans and animals.

iv. Mycorrhizal Fungi

  • These fungi form symbiotic relationships with plant roots, helping them absorb nutrients, especially phosphorus, from the soil.
  • Example: Glomus species help in enhancing the nutrient uptake by plants, improving plant growth and resistance to drought.

Microbes in Environmental Management

i. Wastewater Treatment

  • Microorganisms are used in the treatment of wastewater and sewage to break down organic matter and remove harmful pollutants.
  • Example: E. coli, Aeromonas, and other bacteria degrade organic waste in sewage treatment plants, purifying the water.

ii. Bioremediation

  • Bioremediation involves using microorganisms to clean up pollutants in the environment, such as oil spills, heavy metals, and pesticides.
  • Example: Pseudomonas bacteria degrade hydrocarbons in oil spills, reducing pollution.

iii. Composting

  • Microorganisms decompose organic waste into compost, which is a valuable organic fertilizer that improves soil quality.
  • Example: Fungi and bacteria break down plant residues and animal waste into compost.

Microbes in Biotechnology

i. Genetic Engineering

  • Microbes, especially bacteria and fungi, are used in genetic engineering to produce desired products like proteins, hormones, and enzymes.
  • Example: Escherichia coli is commonly used in recombinant DNA technology to produce human proteins like insulin and growth hormones.

ii. Enzyme Production

  • Microorganisms are used to produce enzymes for various industrial processes, including food processing, textile manufacturing, and detergents.
  • Example: Aspergillus and Trichoderma fungi are used to produce enzymes like amylase and cellulase.

iii. Biogas Production

  • Microorganisms such as methanogens in anaerobic conditions help produce biogas, which can be used as a renewable energy source.
  • Example: Biogas plants use anaerobic bacteria to convert organic waste into methane gas, which can be used for cooking and electricity generation.

Microbes in Research

i. Model Organisms

  • Microorganisms like E. coli, yeast, and Drosophila (fruit fly) are used as model organisms in genetic and biomedical research to understand basic biological processes.

ii. Gene Cloning

  • Microbes are used in gene cloning techniques to replicate DNA for research and therapeutic purposes.
