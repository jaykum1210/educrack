Chapter 12 - Circulatory and Excretory Systems

1. Human Circulatory System and Blood Composition

The human circulatory system is responsible for transporting blood, nutrients, oxygen, carbon dioxide, hormones, and other substances throughout the body. It is essential for maintaining homeostasis and supporting the functions of various organs and tissues.

Human Circulatory System

The circulatory system is a complex network of blood vessels and organs that ensure the distribution of blood to various parts of the body. It consists of two major circuits: the pulmonary circuit and the systemic circuit.

Major Components of the Circulatory System:

Heart:

Function: The heart is the central organ of the circulatory system. It acts as a pump to circulate blood through the body. The heart consists of four chambers:

  • Right Atrium: Receives deoxygenated blood from the body.
  • Right Ventricle: Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs for oxygenation (pulmonary circulation).
  • Left Atrium: Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs.
  • Left Ventricle: Pumps oxygenated blood to the rest of the body (systemic circulation).

The heart is divided by a septum into two halves, each with an atrium and a ventricle.

Blood Vessels:

  • Arteries: Carry oxygenated blood away from the heart (except the pulmonary artery, which carries deoxygenated blood). Arteries have thick muscular walls to withstand high pressure.
  • Veins: Carry deoxygenated blood back to the heart (except the pulmonary vein, which carries oxygenated blood). Veins have thinner walls than arteries and contain valves to prevent the backflow of blood.
  • Capillaries: Tiny blood vessels that connect arteries and veins. They are the sites of gas and nutrient exchange between the blood and body tissues. The walls of capillaries are one cell thick, allowing for easy diffusion of gases (oxygen and carbon dioxide), nutrients, and waste products.

Blood:

Blood is the medium through which substances are transported throughout the body. It is made up of cells and plasma, and its composition plays a vital role in the body's overall function.

Circulation Pathways

i. Pulmonary Circulation:

This circuit involves the movement of blood from the right ventricle of the heart to the lungs, where it is oxygenated, and then back to the left atrium of the heart.

Path: Right Ventricle → Pulmonary Artery → Lungs → Pulmonary Vein → Left Atrium.

ii. Systemic Circulation:

This circuit involves the movement of oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the rest of the body and the return of deoxygenated blood to the right atrium.

Path: Left Ventricle → Aorta → Arteries → Capillaries (in tissues) → Veins → Superior/Inferior Vena Cava → Right Atrium.

Blood Composition

Blood is composed of two main components: Plasma (the liquid part) and Formed Elements (the cellular part). These components perform different functions crucial for the body's health.

i. Plasma (55% of blood volume):

Plasma is a yellowish liquid that serves as a medium for transporting blood cells, nutrients, gases, hormones, and waste products.

Composition of Plasma:

  • Water (about 90%): It is the primary solvent in plasma and helps in the transport of nutrients, gases, and waste products.
  • Proteins (about 7-8%): Key proteins include:
    • Albumin: Helps maintain osmotic pressure and blood volume.
    • Globulins: Involved in immune responses (e.g., antibodies).
    • Fibrinogen: Involved in blood clotting.
  • Electrolytes: Sodium, potassium, calcium, chloride, and bicarbonate help in maintaining the body’s acid-base balance and osmotic pressure.
  • Nutrients: Glucose, amino acids, vitamins, and fatty acids are transported to cells.
  • Gases: Oxygen and carbon dioxide are transported, though most oxygen is carried by red blood cells.
  • Waste Products: Urea, creatinine, and bilirubin are carried to the kidneys for excretion.

ii. Formed Elements (45% of blood volume):

The formed elements of blood consist of cells and cell fragments.

Red Blood Cells (RBCs) (Erythrocytes):

  • Function: RBCs are primarily responsible for transporting oxygen from the lungs to tissues and carbon dioxide from tissues to the lungs.
  • Structure: RBCs are biconcave discs that lack a nucleus, which allows more space for hemoglobin (the protein that binds oxygen).
  • Life Span: Approximately 120 days; they are produced in the bone marrow and broken down in the liver and spleen.

White Blood Cells (WBCs) (Leukocytes):

Function: WBCs are involved in immune responses and defense against infections and foreign invaders.

Types of WBCs:

  • Neutrophils: First responders to infections.
  • Lymphocytes: Involved in the immune response (B-cells and T-cells).
  • Monocytes: Turn into macrophages and help in phagocytosis (engulfing pathogens).
  • Eosinophils: Fight parasitic infections and are involved in allergic reactions.
  • Basophils: Release histamine during inflammatory responses.

Life Span: Varies depending on the type of WBC, from a few days to years (in the case of memory cells).

Platelets (Thrombocytes):

  • Function: Platelets are involved in blood clotting. They help form a plug at the site of injury to prevent blood loss.
  • Structure: Platelets are small, irregularly shaped cell fragments derived from megakaryocytes in the bone marrow.
  • Life Span: About 7-10 days.

Blood Clotting Mechanism

When a blood vessel is injured, a blood clot is formed to stop bleeding. This process involves several steps:

  • Vasoconstriction: Blood vessels constrict to reduce blood flow.
  • Platelet Plug Formation: Platelets adhere to the site of injury and release chemicals to attract more platelets.
  • Coagulation: Clotting factors (proteins in plasma) form a fibrin mesh that traps blood cells and forms a stable clot.
  • Clot Retraction and Repair: The clot contracts to help reduce the wound size, and tissue repair begins.

2. Excretory System: Urine Formation and Kidney Function

The excretory system is responsible for removing metabolic waste products and maintaining homeostasis within the body, particularly in terms of water balance, ion concentration, and pH. The kidneys play a central role in this system by filtering blood, removing waste, and producing urine.

1. Overview of the Excretory System

The excretory system includes several organs and structures that work together to eliminate waste products from the body and regulate water and electrolyte balance. These organs include:

  • Kidneys: Main organs that filter blood and form urine.
  • Ureters: Tubes that carry urine from the kidneys to the bladder.
  • Bladder: Stores urine until it is excreted.
  • Urethra: The tube that carries urine from the bladder to the outside of the body.

2. Kidney Structure and Function

Each kidney is a bean-shaped organ located in the abdominal cavity, and each kidney consists of two main parts:

  • Outer cortex: The outer region where blood filtration begins.
  • Inner medulla: Contains structures like the renal pyramids that help in the final stages of urine formation.

The functional unit of the kidney is the nephron. Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons, which are responsible for filtering blood and producing urine.

Structure of a Nephron:

The nephron is composed of:

  • Renal corpuscle:
    • Bowman's capsule: A cup-like structure that encases the glomerulus.
    • Glomerulus: A network of capillaries where filtration of blood occurs.
  • Renal tubule:
    • Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT): The first segment of the renal tubule, where most reabsorption of water, ions, and nutrients occurs.
    • Loop of Henle: A U-shaped tube that plays a key role in creating a concentration gradient in the kidney.
    • Distal convoluted tubule (DCT): Further reabsorption of ions occurs here, and the final adjustment of urine composition is made.
    • Collecting duct: Receives urine from multiple nephrons and moves it to the renal pelvis.

3. Urine Formation

Urine formation involves three main processes: filtration, reabsorption, and secretion.

1. Glomerular Filtration:

Location: Takes place in the glomerulus.

Process: Blood entering the kidney through the renal artery is filtered under high pressure as it passes through the glomerulus. The filtered fluid, called glomerular filtrate, consists of water, ions, glucose, amino acids, and metabolic waste products (e.g., urea). However, large proteins and blood cells do not pass through the filtration barrier.

Filtration Rate: The rate at which the kidneys filter blood is called the glomerular filtration rate (GFR). A healthy GFR is crucial for maintaining proper kidney function.

2. Tubular Reabsorption:

Location: Occurs mainly in the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT), the loop of Henle, and the distal convoluted tubule (DCT).

Process: After filtration, most of the water and useful substances (such as glucose, amino acids, sodium, potassium, and bicarbonate) are reabsorbed into the blood. This occurs through active and passive transport mechanisms in the renal tubules.

  • The proximal convoluted tubule reabsorbs the majority of these substances.
  • The loop of Henle creates a concentration gradient that allows the kidneys to conserve water.
  • The distal convoluted tubule fine-tunes the reabsorption of ions, like sodium and calcium, and helps regulate pH.

Selective Reabsorption: Not all filtrates are reabsorbed. For example, urea and creatinine remain in the filtrate and are excreted as waste.

3. Tubular Secretion:

Location: Occurs mainly in the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct.

Process: Certain substances are actively secreted from the blood into the filtrate, such as hydrogen ions, potassium ions, ammonium, and creatinine. This helps regulate the pH of the blood and remove additional waste products that are not filtered at the glomerulus.

4. Water Reabsorption and Concentration of Urine:

Location: Occurs in the loop of Henle, distal convoluted tubule, and collecting duct.

Process: As the filtrate moves through the nephron, water is reabsorbed based on the body's needs. In the loop of Henle, water is reabsorbed by the countercurrent mechanism, which helps concentrate the urine and conserve water. The final concentration of urine is determined in the collecting duct, where water reabsorption is regulated by hormones like antidiuretic hormone (ADH).

5. Excretion of Urine:

Once the urine is formed, it flows from the collecting ducts into the renal pelvis, then into the ureter, and is stored in the bladder until excretion through the urethra.

4. Kidney Functions

The kidneys perform several important functions to maintain homeostasis:

  • Filtration of Blood: The kidneys filter about 180 liters of blood daily, removing excess substances, waste products, and toxins while retaining essential nutrients like glucose and amino acids.
  • Regulation of Fluid and Electrolyte Balance: The kidneys help maintain the balance of water and electrolytes (like sodium, potassium, calcium) in the body. This regulation is essential for nerve function, muscle contraction, and maintaining blood pressure.
  • Acid-Base Balance: The kidneys help maintain the body's pH by excreting hydrogen ions (H⁺) and reabsorbing bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) to keep the blood's pH within the optimal range (around 7.4).
  • Regulation of Blood Pressure: Through the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), the kidneys help regulate blood pressure by adjusting the amount of sodium and water excreted. This controls blood volume and vascular resistance.
  • Erythropoiesis Regulation: The kidneys produce the hormone erythropoietin (EPO), which stimulates the production of red blood cells in the bone marrow in response to low oxygen levels in the blood.
  • Detoxification: The kidneys filter and excrete metabolic waste products like urea, creatinine, and uric acid, as well as drugs and toxins, helping to detoxify the body.

5. Disorders of the Excretory System

Several disorders can affect kidney function and the formation of urine:

  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD): Progressive loss of kidney function due to conditions like diabetes, hypertension, or glomerulonephritis.
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Sudden loss of kidney function, often caused by infection, dehydration, or drug toxicity.
  • Kidney Stones: Hard deposits of minerals and salts that form in the kidneys, causing pain and blockage.
  • Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs): Infections of the urinary system, including the kidneys, bladder, and urethra.
  • Nephrotic Syndrome: A condition where the kidneys leak excessive protein into the urine, leading to swelling and other complications.
