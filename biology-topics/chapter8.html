Chapter 8 - Transport in Plants

1. Absorption and Transport of Water and Nutrients in Plants

The absorption and transport of water and nutrients in plants are vital processes for their survival, growth, and development. These processes occur through the roots, vascular tissues, and transport systems to distribute water, minerals, and nutrients to various parts of the plant.

Absorption of Water and Nutrients

Water Absorption:

Roots are primarily responsible for absorbing water from the soil.

  • Water enters the root through the root hairs, which are extensions of the epidermal cells. These root hairs increase the surface area for absorption.
  • Water absorption occurs via two main mechanisms:
  • Osmosis: Water moves from an area of lower solute concentration (in the soil) to an area of higher solute concentration (inside the root cells) through a semipermeable membrane.
  • Capillary Action: This is the upward movement of water through the small spaces between the soil particles and into the root system due to the cohesive and adhesive properties of water molecules.

Nutrient Absorption:

Mineral nutrients (such as nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, calcium, magnesium, and sulfur) are absorbed from the soil through active and passive transport mechanisms.

  • Active Transport: This requires energy (ATP) to move nutrients against a concentration gradient (from soil to root cells).
  • Passive Transport: Nutrients move down a concentration gradient, through diffusion or facilitated diffusion.
  • Mycorrhizal Fungi: These symbiotic fungi extend the surface area for nutrient absorption, especially phosphorus, and help in the exchange of nutrients between the plant and soil.

Transport of Water and Nutrients

Water Transport:

Water absorbed by the roots is transported upward through the plant via the xylem.

The process of water transport in plants is mainly driven by the following mechanisms:

  • Root Pressure: This is a small upward pressure exerted by the roots that pushes water into the stem. It occurs due to the active transport of minerals into the roots, which causes water to follow passively via osmosis.
  • Capillary Action: Water moves upward through the narrow xylem vessels due to cohesive and adhesive forces of water molecules.
  • Transpiration: This is the evaporation of water from the stomata in leaves. The loss of water through transpiration creates a negative pressure in the leaf, which pulls more water from the roots through the xylem to replace the lost water. This is the most significant force for water movement in plants.

Nutrient Transport:

Minerals and other nutrients absorbed by the roots are transported via the xylem alongside water.

Translocation: The movement of dissolved nutrients in water from the roots to various plant parts occurs through the xylem. The nutrients are transported upward towards stems, leaves, and flowers where they are utilized.

Transport of Sugars (Phloem):

After water and nutrients are transported via the xylem, phloem is responsible for transporting organic molecules, particularly sugars produced during photosynthesis.

  • Source to Sink Transport: Sugars are produced in the source (typically leaves), and they move through the phloem to the sink (areas of growth like roots, fruits, and flowers).
  • Pressure Flow Hypothesis: This theory explains how sugars move through the phloem. At the source (leaves), sugars are actively loaded into the phloem, creating a high concentration. This high concentration causes water to enter from the xylem, increasing pressure and pushing the sugary solution towards the sink, where the sugars are unloaded.

Mechanisms Involved in Water and Nutrient Transport

Cohesion-Tension Theory (Water Transport):

  • Cohesion: Water molecules stick to each other due to hydrogen bonds.
  • Adhesion: Water molecules also adhere to the walls of xylem vessels.
  • The tension (negative pressure) created by transpiration pulls water upwards from the roots through the xylem, aided by cohesion and adhesion.

Root Pressure:

  • When minerals are actively transported into the root cells, water follows by osmosis, creating positive pressure in the roots, which helps push water up into the stem.

Transpiration:

  • The evaporation of water from the leaf surface creates a vacuum that pulls water from the xylem vessels into the leaves, facilitating nutrient and water movement.

Factors Affecting Water and Nutrient Transport

  • Temperature: High temperatures increase the rate of transpiration, causing more water to be pulled up from the roots.
  • Humidity: Low humidity increases transpiration rates, causing more water loss.
  • Soil Moisture: Sufficient moisture in the soil is necessary for proper water absorption.
  • Root Health: Damaged or unhealthy roots can affect water and nutrient absorption.
  • Mineral Concentration: High concentrations of certain minerals can affect the uptake of other nutrients due to competition or toxicity.
  • Water Potential: The ability of water to move from areas of higher to lower potential affects the flow of water in the plant.

Summary of Water and Nutrient Transport

Process Description Role
Water Absorption Water enters the roots through osmosis and capillary action. Provides hydration and a medium for nutrient transport.
Nutrient Absorption Active and passive transport of nutrients from soil to roots. Provides essential minerals for growth and metabolism.
Xylem Transport Movement of water and dissolved minerals upward through the plant. Transports water and minerals to all plant cells.
Transpiration Evaporation of water from the stomata of leaves. Creates a negative pressure that pulls water through the plant.
Phloem Transport Movement of sugars and other organic compounds from source to sink. Delivers energy in the form of sugars to various plant parts.

2. Transpiration and Stomatal Regulation in Plants

Transpiration is the process by which water is absorbed by the roots, moves through the plant, and is released as water vapor through the stomata in the leaves. Stomatal regulation plays a crucial role in controlling transpiration, water loss, and gas exchange. Let’s delve deeper into these processes.

i. Transpiration

Definition:

Transpiration is the loss of water vapor from the aerial parts of a plant, particularly through the stomata in the leaves. It is primarily a passive process driven by environmental factors.

Types of Transpiration:

  • Stomatal Transpiration: This is the main form of transpiration, occurring through small pores called stomata on the surface of leaves.
  • Cuticular Transpiration: This occurs through the cuticle (a waxy covering) on the surface of the leaves and stems.
  • Lenticular Transpiration: This occurs through lenticels (small openings in the bark) in woody plants.

Process of Transpiration:

  • Water is absorbed from the soil by the roots and moves up through the plant’s vascular system, particularly through the xylem.
  • Once the water reaches the leaves, it evaporates into the intercellular spaces in the leaf and diffuses out of the leaf through the stomata.
  • The loss of water from the stomata creates a negative pressure that helps pull more water upward from the roots through the plant, maintaining a continuous flow of water (also known as the transpiration stream).

Functions of Transpiration:

  • Cooling Effect: Transpiration helps in cooling the plant, especially during high temperatures. The evaporation of water requires energy, which cools the plant tissues.
  • Water and Nutrient Transport: It helps in the movement of water and dissolved minerals from the roots to the leaves.
  • Maintaining Turgidity: Water loss through transpiration maintains the turgor pressure in plant cells, which helps in keeping the plant rigid and upright.

ii. Stomatal Regulation

Stomata are tiny pores located on the surface of plant leaves and stems that allow for gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ out) and water vapor loss. The opening and closing of stomata are controlled by specialized cells called guard cells.

Guard Cells:

  • Structure: Guard cells are kidney-shaped cells that surround each stoma. They contain chloroplasts and can change shape depending on turgor pressure.
  • Function: They regulate the opening and closing of stomata based on water availability and environmental factors.

Mechanism of Stomatal Opening and Closing:

Opening of Stomata:

  • When guard cells absorb water and become turgid (swollen), they bend outward, opening the stomatal pore.
  • The process of water uptake into the guard cells occurs through osmosis, where water moves from the surrounding epidermal cells into the guard cells due to a concentration gradient of ions.
  • This process is generally stimulated by light, as photosynthesis requires CO₂, and plants open their stomata to allow its entry.

Closing of Stomata:

  • When the guard cells lose water and become flaccid (shrunken), the stomatal pore closes.
  • This is often triggered by darkness, low humidity, water stress, or high temperatures, to prevent excessive water loss.

Factors Affecting Stomatal Regulation:

  • Light: During the day (light), stomata generally open to allow CO₂ entry for photosynthesis. At night (dark), stomata typically close as photosynthesis ceases.
  • Water Availability: In water stress conditions (drought), stomata close to conserve water. In well-watered conditions, stomata remain open to allow gas exchange.
  • Humidity: High humidity reduces the rate of transpiration, and stomata may close to prevent water loss. Low humidity increases transpiration, and stomata may remain open to facilitate the movement of water vapor out of the plant.
  • Temperature: High temperatures increase transpiration and may lead to stomatal closure to reduce water loss. Low temperatures may cause stomata to close, as the plant doesn't need to lose water when metabolic activity is low.
  • CO₂ Concentration: High CO₂ levels inside the leaf signal to the plant that it does not need more CO₂, leading to stomatal closure. Low CO₂ levels prompt stomata to open to allow more CO₂ in for photosynthesis.

iii. Transpiration Rates and Stomatal Behavior

Influence of Stomatal Density:

  • Plants that are adapted to dry environments, such as cacti and other xerophytes, typically have fewer stomata or have stomata that are sunken or covered by hairs to reduce water loss.
  • Hydrophytes (plants that grow in water) have stomata that are more abundant and may open easily as they don't need to conserve water as much.

Environmental Factors and Transpiration:

  • Wind: Wind increases the rate of transpiration by removing the water vapor surrounding the leaf, thus increasing the concentration gradient for water to diffuse out of the plant.
  • Air Movement: Strong air currents can also increase transpiration rates by enhancing the evaporation of water from the leaf surface.

iv. Importance of Stomatal Regulation and Transpiration

  • Water Conservation: Stomatal regulation helps plants conserve water during periods of drought or high heat. When the plant senses water stress, stomata close to prevent excessive water loss.
  • Photosynthesis and Growth: Open stomata allow CO₂ to enter for photosynthesis, which is essential for the plant’s growth. By regulating stomatal opening, plants optimize both water use and CO₂ uptake.
  • Temperature Regulation: Transpiration helps cool the plant, preventing overheating and enabling it to maintain an optimal temperature for enzyme activity and overall growth.
  • Nutrient Transport: Transpiration creates a pulling force that drives the movement of water, minerals, and nutrients from the roots through the xylem to the leaves and other parts of the plant.

v. Summary of Transpiration and Stomatal Regulation

Aspect Description
Transpiration Loss of water vapor from aerial parts, mainly through stomata.
Stomata Small pores on the leaf surface that regulate gas exchange and water loss.
Guard Cells Cells that control the opening and closing of stomata.
Opening Mechanism Stomata open when guard cells absorb water and become turgid.
Closing Mechanism Stomata close when guard cells lose water and become flaccid.
Environmental Factors Light, temperature, humidity, CO₂ concentration, and water availability regulate stomatal behavior.
Significance Transpiration aids in cooling, nutrient transport, and maintaining turgidity, while stomatal regulation optimizes water conservation and photosynthesis.
