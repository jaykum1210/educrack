Chapter 6 - Biomolecules and Enzymes

1. Macromolecules: Carbohydrates, Proteins, Lipids, Nucleic Acids (DNA & RNA)

These are the four major macromolecules essential for the structure and function of living organisms. They play vital roles in metabolism, structure, and genetic information transfer. Below is a detailed overview of each of these biomolecules:

i. Carbohydrates

Definition: Carbohydrates are organic compounds made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. They are a primary source of energy for the body.

Types of Carbohydrates:

  • Monosaccharides: Simple sugars (e.g., glucose, fructose, galactose).
  • Disaccharides: Formed by the combination of two monosaccharides (e.g., sucrose, lactose, maltose).
  • Polysaccharides: Complex carbohydrates made up of many monosaccharides (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose).

Functions:

  • Energy Source: Carbohydrates, especially glucose, provide energy for cellular activities.
  • Energy Storage: Glycogen (in animals) and starch (in plants) are storage forms of carbohydrates.
  • Structural Role: Cellulose in plants provides structural support in cell walls.

Structure:

  • Monosaccharides have a simple ring-like or chain structure.
  • Polysaccharides are long chains of monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bonds.

ii. Proteins

Definition: Proteins are large, complex molecules made up of chains of amino acids. They are essential for the structure, function, and regulation of the bodyâ€™s cells, tissues, and organs.

Amino Acids:

Proteins are made up of 20 different amino acids, each with an amino group (-NH2) and a carboxyl group (-COOH). Amino acids are joined by peptide bonds to form polypeptide chains.

Types of Proteins:

  • Structural Proteins: Provide support (e.g., collagen in skin, keratin in hair).
  • Enzymes: Speed up biochemical reactions (e.g., amylase, lactase).
  • Transport Proteins: Carry molecules (e.g., hemoglobin).
  • Defensive Proteins: Involved in immune responses (e.g., antibodies).
  • Contractile Proteins: Involved in movement (e.g., actin, myosin).

Functions:

  • Catalyzing Reactions: Enzymes speed up chemical reactions.
  • Structural Support: Proteins like collagen form the structural components of tissues.
  • Immune Response: Proteins such as antibodies protect the body from pathogens.

Structure:

  • Primary Structure: Sequence of amino acids.
  • Secondary Structure: Folding into alpha-helices or beta-pleated sheets.
  • Tertiary Structure: 3D folding due to interactions between R-groups.
  • Quaternary Structure: Multiple polypeptide chains form a functional protein.

iii. Lipids

Definition: Lipids are hydrophobic or amphiphilic molecules made up of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. They are primarily involved in energy storage, insulation, and forming cell membranes.

Types of Lipids:

  • Fats: Composed of glycerol and three fatty acids (triglycerides).
  • Phospholipids: Consist of a glycerol backbone, two fatty acid tails, and a phosphate group, forming the structure of cell membranes.
  • Steroids: Include cholesterol and hormones like testosterone and estrogen.
  • Waxes: Provide waterproofing (e.g., on plant leaves).

Functions:

  • Energy Storage: Lipids store more energy per gram than carbohydrates.
  • Cell Membrane: Phospholipids form the bilayer of cell membranes.
  • Insulation and Protection: Lipids serve as thermal insulation and protect organs.
  • Hormonal Function: Steroids and fat-soluble vitamins are essential for hormone production.

Structure:

  • Fats and oils are made of triglycerides, where glycerol is attached to three fatty acids.
  • Phospholipids have two hydrophobic fatty acid tails and a hydrophilic phosphate head, which is important for the formation of cellular membranes.

iv. Nucleic Acids (DNA & RNA)

Definition: Nucleic acids are macromolecules made of nucleotides. They store and transmit genetic information. The two main types are DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) and RNA (Ribonucleic Acid).

Nucleotides:

A nucleotide is made up of a phosphate group, a sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA), and a nitrogenous base (adenine, thymine, cytosine, guanine for DNA; adenine, uracil, cytosine, guanine for RNA).

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid):

  • Structure: Double helix made of two complementary strands of nucleotides.
  • Function: Stores genetic information that is passed down during reproduction. It contains instructions for making proteins.
  • Location: Found in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells and the cytoplasm of prokaryotic cells.

RNA (Ribonucleic Acid):

  • Structure: Single-stranded molecule.
  • Types:
    • mRNA (Messenger RNA): Carries genetic information from DNA to ribosomes for protein synthesis.
    • tRNA (Transfer RNA): Transfers amino acids to the ribosome during protein synthesis.
    • rRNA (Ribosomal RNA): Forms part of the ribosome and assists in protein synthesis.

Functions:

  • DNA: Stores and transmits genetic information.
  • RNA: Helps in protein synthesis and gene expression.

Summary of Functions and Structures:

Macromolecule Function Structure
Carbohydrates Energy source, storage, structural support Monosaccharides, disaccharides, polysaccharides
Proteins Catalyze reactions, structural support, immune defense, transport Amino acid chains (primary to quaternary structure)
Lipids Energy storage, insulation, cell membrane formation, hormones Glycerol backbone with fatty acids, phospholipids, steroids
Nucleic Acids Genetic information storage and transfer DNA: Double helix; RNA: Single strand

2. Enzymes: Properties, Mechanism of Action

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up the rate of chemical reactions in living organisms without being consumed in the process. They are highly specific, work efficiently under mild conditions, and are essential for numerous biochemical processes.

1. Properties of Enzymes

  • Biological Catalysts: Enzymes accelerate the rate of biochemical reactions by lowering the activation energy required for the reaction to proceed.
  • Specificity: Enzymes are highly specific to their substrates. This specificity arises due to the lock and key model or induced fit model, which ensures that only a particular substrate fits into the enzyme's active site.
  • Efficiency: Enzymes increase reaction rates by a factor of millions, allowing biochemical reactions to occur quickly under normal physiological conditions.
  • Regulation: Enzyme activity can be regulated by factors such as temperature, pH, and the presence of activators or inhibitors. This regulation ensures that metabolic processes occur when needed.
  • Reusability: Enzymes are not consumed in the reaction and can be reused multiple times to catalyze the same type of reaction.
  • Sensitivity to Environmental Conditions: Enzymes are sensitive to temperature and pH. Each enzyme has an optimal temperature and pH at which it works most efficiently. Extreme conditions can lead to denaturation, where the enzyme loses its functional shape.

2. Mechanism of Action

Enzymes work by lowering the activation energy required for a reaction to proceed, making the reaction faster. The process involves the following steps:

(a) Formation of Enzyme-Substrate Complex (ES Complex):

  • Substrate Binding: The substrate (the molecule that is going to be transformed in the reaction) binds to the enzyme's active site. The active site is a specific region on the enzyme where the substrate binds.
  • Induced Fit: In the induced fit model, the enzyme undergoes a slight conformational change upon substrate binding, which helps in properly orienting the substrate for the reaction.

(b) Catalysis:

Once the enzyme-substrate complex is formed, the enzyme facilitates the conversion of the substrate into the product by lowering the activation energy. This is achieved through several mechanisms:

  • Proximity and Orientation: Enzymes bring substrates into close proximity and in the correct orientation, which increases the likelihood of a successful reaction.
  • Strain: Enzymes may apply physical strain on the substrate, weakening certain bonds and facilitating their breaking or formation.
  • Microenvironment: Enzymes can create a specific environment (e.g., acidic or basic conditions) at the active site that promotes the reaction.
  • Covalent Catalysis: Some enzymes form temporary covalent bonds with the substrate, which helps in breaking or forming bonds.

(c) Formation of Product:

After the substrate undergoes the catalytic process, the enzyme releases the products. The enzyme itself is unchanged and can bind to new substrate molecules.

(d) Release of Product:

The enzyme-product complex dissociates, and the product(s) are released from the enzyme's active site. The enzyme is free to bind to a new substrate and repeat the process.

3. Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

  • Temperature: Each enzyme has an optimal temperature at which its activity is maximized. Higher temperatures can cause enzymes to denature (lose their shape), while lower temperatures slow down enzyme activity.
  • pH: Enzymes also have an optimal pH range. Extreme pH levels can disrupt the ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds in the enzyme, leading to denaturation.
  • Substrate Concentration: As substrate concentration increases, enzyme activity increases, but only up to a point. When the enzyme becomes saturated with substrate, the rate of reaction levels off.
  • Enzyme Concentration: Increasing enzyme concentration leads to an increase in reaction rate, provided there is enough substrate present.
  • Inhibitors: Enzyme inhibitors are molecules that reduce the enzyme's activity. They can be classified into:
    • Competitive Inhibitors: These bind to the active site of the enzyme, competing with the substrate.
    • Non-Competitive Inhibitors: These bind to a site other than the active site, causing a conformational change in the enzyme that reduces its activity.
    • Allosteric Inhibitors: These bind to a site other than the active site and change the enzyme's shape, decreasing its affinity for the substrate.
  • Activators: Some enzymes require the presence of certain molecules or ions to be activated, called cofactors or coenzymes (e.g., vitamins, metal ions).

4. Types of Enzymes Based on Function

  • Hydrolases: Catalyze hydrolysis reactions, where water is used to break bonds (e.g., amylase, lipase).
  • Oxidoreductases: Catalyze oxidation-reduction reactions (e.g., dehydrogenases, oxidases).
  • Transferases: Transfer functional groups (e.g., kinases, transaminases).
  • Ligases: Catalyze the joining of two molecules, often with the hydrolysis of ATP (e.g., DNA ligase).
  • Isomerases: Catalyze the rearrangement of atoms within a molecule (e.g., glucose-6-phosphate isomerase).
  • Lyases: Break bonds without the involvement of water (e.g., decarboxylases).

5. Enzyme Inhibition and Regulation

  • Competitive Inhibition: The inhibitor resembles the substrate and competes for the active site. The inhibition can be overcome by increasing the substrate concentration.
  • Non-Competitive Inhibition: The inhibitor binds to an allosteric site (a site other than the active site), causing a conformational change in the enzyme that decreases its activity. This type of inhibition cannot be overcome by adding more substrate.
  • Allosteric Regulation: Some enzymes are regulated by molecules that bind to an allosteric site, which can either increase (activators) or decrease (inhibitors) enzyme activity.
  • Feedback Inhibition: In metabolic pathways, the end product may inhibit the activity of an enzyme early in the pathway to prevent overproduction of the product.
