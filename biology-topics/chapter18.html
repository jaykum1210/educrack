Chapter 18 - Ecology and Environment

1. Organisms and Population - Short Notes

i. Organism

An organism is any individual living entity that can carry out all life processes independently. It can range from unicellular organisms like bacteria to multicellular organisms like plants and animals.

Types of Organisms:

  • Unicellular: Made of a single cell (e.g., bacteria, yeast).
  • Multicellular: Composed of many cells with specialized functions (e.g., humans, plants, animals).

Adaptations:

  • Organisms have evolved specific structures, behaviors, or physiological processes to survive in their environment.
  • Example: The thick fur of polar bears helps them survive cold climates.

Interactions:

  • Organisms interact with each other and the environment through various ecological processes such as predation, competition, and symbiosis.

ii. Population

A population is a group of individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time, capable of interbreeding and producing fertile offspring.

Population Characteristics:

  • Density: Number of individuals per unit area or volume. Example: The density of deer in a forest.
  • Dispersion: The pattern of spacing of individuals within a population (clumped, uniform, or random). Example: Trees in a forest can be randomly dispersed, while wolves in a pack may have uniform spacing.
  • Age Structure: The distribution of individuals in different age groups (juvenile, adult, and senile) affects the population's growth and reproduction.
  • Sex Ratio: The proportion of males to females in a population, which can influence reproductive rates.
  • Growth Rate: The rate at which a population increases in size, determined by birth rates, death rates, immigration, and emigration.

Growth Models:

  • Exponential Growth: Occurs when resources are abundant, and the population grows rapidly.
  • Logistic Growth: Occurs when resources become limited, and the growth rate slows down as the population approaches the carrying capacity.

iii. Factors Affecting Population Dynamics

Abiotic Factors:

  • Non-living factors such as temperature, humidity, light, and soil type that influence the survival and distribution of organisms.

Biotic Factors:

  • Living factors such as competition, predation, and symbiosis that affect the population size and structure.

Carrying Capacity:

  • The maximum number of individuals an environment can support without degrading the ecosystem.

Birth and Death Rates:

  • Influence the population size and growth rate. High birth rates and low death rates result in population growth.

Immigration and Emigration:

  • The movement of individuals into (immigration) or out of (emigration) a population affects its size.

iv. Population Interactions

Organisms within a population do not live in isolation. They interact with each other in several ways, which affect their survival and reproduction.

Competition:

  • Occurs when individuals of the same or different species compete for limited resources like food, water, and shelter.

Predation:

  • One organism (the predator) kills and eats another (the prey). This interaction regulates population sizes of both predators and prey.

Symbiosis:

  • A long-term interaction between two species that may benefit one or both.
  • Mutualism: Both organisms benefit (e.g., bees and flowers).
  • Commensalism: One organism benefits, and the other is neither helped nor harmed (e.g., barnacles on whales).
  • Parasitism: One organism benefits at the expense of the other (e.g., ticks on mammals).

v. Population Growth Models

Exponential Growth:

  • The population grows at a constant rate, doubling in size over each time period.
  • Example: Bacterial growth under ideal conditions.

Logistic Growth:

  • The population growth slows down as the population reaches the environment's carrying capacity.
  • Example: A deer population in a forest will stop growing once resources (food, space) become limited.

2. Ecosystem and Energy Flow - Short Notes

i. Ecosystem

An ecosystem is a dynamic community of living organisms (biotic components) interacting with their physical environment (abiotic components). These interactions create a flow of energy and cycling of nutrients within the ecosystem.

Components of an Ecosystem:

  • Abiotic Components: Non-living factors such as air, water, soil, sunlight, and temperature.
  • Biotic Components: Living organisms in an ecosystem, including producers, consumers, and decomposers.

Types of Organisms in an Ecosystem:

  • Producers (Autotrophs): Organisms that produce their own food through photosynthesis (e.g., plants, algae).
  • Consumers (Heterotrophs): Organisms that consume other organisms for food. They are classified into:
    • Primary Consumers: Herbivores that eat plants (e.g., deer).
    • Secondary Consumers: Carnivores that eat herbivores (e.g., wolves).
    • Tertiary Consumers: Apex predators that eat secondary consumers (e.g., lions).
  • Decomposers (Detritivores): Organisms like fungi and bacteria that break down dead organisms and recycle nutrients into the environment.

ii. Energy Flow in Ecosystems

Energy in an ecosystem flows in one direction, from the sun to producers and then to various consumers, and ultimately to decomposers. Energy cannot be recycled and is lost at each trophic level.

Source of Energy:

  • The primary source of energy for most ecosystems is sunlight.

Photosynthesis:

  • Plants, algae, and some bacteria capture solar energy and convert it into chemical energy in the form of glucose.

Trophic Levels:

  • Producers: The first trophic level, where plants and algae convert sunlight into energy.
  • Consumers: Organisms that consume producers or other consumers.
    • Primary Consumers: Herbivores that eat producers.
    • Secondary Consumers: Carnivores that eat herbivores.
    • Tertiary Consumers: Top predators.
  • Decomposers: Organisms that break down dead material, returning nutrients to the soil.

iii. Food Chain and Food Web

Food Chain:

  • A linear sequence showing the flow of energy from one organism to another. Each step in the food chain is called a "trophic level."
  • Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake.

Food Web:

  • A more complex and realistic model showing how multiple food chains are interconnected in an ecosystem. It shows the various feeding relationships between organisms.
  • Example: In a forest, multiple species of plants, herbivores, carnivores, and decomposers form an intricate food web.

iv. Energy Pyramid

An Energy Pyramid represents the energy available at each trophic level in an ecosystem. Primary Producers (plants) at the base have the highest energy, while energy decreases as you move up each trophic level.

10% Rule:

  • Only about 10% of the energy is passed on to the next trophic level, while the rest is lost as heat or used for metabolism.

Energy Pyramid Example:

  • Trophic Level 1: Primary producers (plants) → highest energy.
  • Trophic Level 2: Primary consumers (herbivores) → 10% of the energy.
  • Trophic Level 3: Secondary consumers (carnivores) → 1% of the energy.
  • Trophic Level 4: Tertiary consumers (top predators) → 0.1% of the energy.

v. Nutrient Cycling

Nutrients like carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus cycle through ecosystems in a process known as biogeochemical cycling. Decomposers play a key role in breaking down organic matter and recycling nutrients back into the environment for use by producers.

Types of Nutrient Cycles:

  • Carbon Cycle: Involves the movement of carbon through the atmosphere, producers, consumers, and decomposers.
  • Nitrogen Cycle: Involves the transformation of nitrogen in various forms through soil, plants, animals, and microorganisms.
  • Phosphorus Cycle: Moves phosphorus through soil, water, and living organisms, with limited atmospheric involvement.

vi. Efficiency of Energy Transfer

Energy transfer between trophic levels is inefficient. Only about 10% of the energy is passed to the next level, while the rest is used by organisms for metabolism, movement, or is lost as heat.

Biomass:

  • The total mass of living matter at each trophic level decreases as you go up the food chain due to energy loss.

vii. Ecological Pyramids

Pyramid of Numbers:

  • Represents the number of organisms at each trophic level. It can sometimes be inverted (e.g., one tree may support thousands of insects).

Pyramid of Biomass:

  • Represents the total dry weight of organisms at each trophic level. Generally, it’s upright, showing that biomass decreases as you move up trophic levels.

Pyramid of Energy:

  • Depicts the amount of energy available at each trophic level. It is always upright as energy decreases at higher trophic levels.

3. Biodiversity and Conservation - Short Notes

i. Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life forms on Earth, including different species of plants, animals, microorganisms, and ecosystems. It encompasses the genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity.

Levels of Biodiversity:

  • Genetic Diversity: Variation in the genetic material within and between populations of species.
    • Example: Different breeds of dogs, variations in crop species.
  • Species Diversity: The variety of species within a given habitat or region.
    • Example: A tropical rainforest containing numerous species of plants and animals.
  • Ecosystem Diversity: Variety of ecosystems (forests, deserts, wetlands, oceans, etc.) that support different species.
    • Example: Coral reefs, forests, and wetlands.

Importance of Biodiversity:

  • Ecological Services: Biodiversity supports ecosystem functions like pollination, soil fertility, water purification, and climate regulation.
  • Economic Value: Many industries, including agriculture, forestry, and pharmaceuticals, depend on biodiversity.
  • Cultural Significance: Many cultures depend on plants and animals for food, medicine, and spiritual values.
  • Health: Genetic diversity within species helps with resilience to diseases and environmental changes.

ii. Conservation of Biodiversity

Conservation refers to the protection, preservation, management, and restoration of biodiversity. It aims to prevent the loss of species, habitats, and ecosystems.

Types of Conservation:

  • In-situ Conservation: Protecting species in their natural habitats.
    • Examples: National parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves.
    • Key Focus: Protecting entire ecosystems and maintaining the natural processes that sustain them.
  • Ex-situ Conservation: Conservation outside the natural habitat.
    • Examples: Zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks, and gene banks.
    • Key Focus: Preserving species by removing them from their natural habitats for breeding or genetic storage.

iii. Threats to Biodiversity

Biodiversity is under threat due to human activities and natural factors. The major threats include:

  • Habitat Destruction: Deforestation, urbanization, and agricultural expansion lead to the loss of natural habitats.
    • Example: Clearing of forests for agriculture or infrastructure development.
  • Pollution: Chemical pollutants, plastic waste, and industrial emissions negatively affect air, water, and soil quality, harming species.
    • Example: Water pollution from industrial runoff affecting aquatic life.
  • Over-exploitation: Overhunting, fishing, logging, and mining reduce species populations.
    • Example: Overfishing depletes marine species populations.
  • Climate Change: Global warming, habitat alteration, and changing weather patterns disrupt ecosystems and affect species’ survival.
    • Example: Coral bleaching due to ocean temperature rise.
  • Invasive Species: Non-native species introduced into ecosystems can outcompete native species, leading to their decline.
    • Example: The spread of zebra mussels in North American lakes.

iv. Conservation Strategies

Protected Areas:

  • Establishing national parks, wildlife reserves, and biosphere reserves to protect natural habitats and species.
  • Example: Sundarbans mangrove forest reserve for protecting the Bengal tiger.

Wildlife Protection Acts:

  • Legislation to protect endangered species and regulate activities that threaten biodiversity.
  • Example: The Wildlife Protection Act (1972) in India.

Sustainable Use of Resources:

  • Ensuring that resources like forests, fisheries, and wildlife are used in a way that meets present needs without compromising future generations.
  • Example: Sustainable forestry practices that allow trees to regenerate.

Reforestation and Afforestation:

  • Planting trees in deforested areas or creating new forests to restore biodiversity and combat climate change.
  • Example: The Green Belt Movement in Kenya.

Captive Breeding and Reintroduction:

  • Breeding endangered species in captivity and reintroducing them into their natural habitats.
  • Example: The successful reintroduction of the Arabian Oryx into the wild after breeding in captivity.

Public Awareness and Education:

  • Educating the public about the importance of biodiversity and how individuals can contribute to its conservation.
  • Example: Campaigns to reduce plastic use and promote recycling.

Biodiversity Hotspots:

  • Protecting areas with high levels of biodiversity but also facing significant threats. These areas are prioritized for conservation.
  • Example: The Western Ghats and Sundarbans are considered biodiversity hotspots in India.

v. International Efforts in Biodiversity Conservation

  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD): An international treaty aimed at preserving biodiversity, promoting sustainable development, and ensuring the fair sharing of genetic resources.
  • CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species): An international agreement to ensure that trade in wild animals and plants does not threaten their survival.
  • Global Environment Facility (GEF): Provides financial support to developing countries for projects related to environmental conservation and sustainable development.

vi. Role of Individuals in Biodiversity Conservation

Individuals can play a key role in protecting biodiversity by:

  • Reducing their carbon footprint and conserving energy.
  • Supporting sustainable and eco-friendly products.
  • Participating in local conservation efforts such as tree planting and wildlife protection.
  • Raising awareness about biodiversity and its importance.

4. Environmental Issues - Short Notes

i. Pollution

Pollution refers to the introduction of harmful substances or products into the environment that cause adverse effects on living organisms and the ecosystem.

Types of Pollution:

  • Air Pollution: Contamination of the atmosphere with harmful gases like carbon dioxide (CO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), and particulate matter.
    • Sources: Vehicle emissions, industrial processes, burning of fossil fuels.
    • Effects: Respiratory diseases, global warming, acid rain.
  • Water Pollution: Contamination of water bodies (rivers, lakes, oceans) by harmful chemicals, waste, and pathogens.
    • Sources: Industrial discharge, agricultural runoff, untreated sewage.
    • Effects: Waterborne diseases, harm to aquatic life, and loss of biodiversity.
  • Soil Pollution: The presence of toxic substances in soil, often from industrial activities, improper waste disposal, or excessive use of pesticides and fertilizers.
    • Sources: Chemical spills, improper waste disposal, agricultural chemicals.
    • Effects: Reduced soil fertility, contamination of food, harm to soil organisms.
  • Noise Pollution: Excessive noise levels from traffic, industries, and human activities that disturb human and animal life.
    • Sources: Vehicles, construction sites, industrial machines.
    • Effects: Hearing loss, stress, disturbance to wildlife.
  • Light Pollution: Excessive artificial light in urban areas that interferes with natural light patterns.
    • Sources: Streetlights, advertising lights, and other artificial sources.
    • Effects: Disruption of ecosystems, disturbance to nocturnal wildlife, and human health issues like sleep disorders.

ii. Global Warming and Climate Change

Global warming refers to the increase in Earth's average temperature due to the accumulation of greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide in the atmosphere.

Causes of Global Warming:

  • Greenhouse Gases: Burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas), deforestation, and agricultural practices release greenhouse gases that trap heat in the atmosphere.
  • Deforestation: Trees absorb carbon dioxide; when forests are destroyed, more carbon is released into the atmosphere, exacerbating global warming.
  • Industrialization: Factories and industries emit large quantities of greenhouse gases.

Effects of Global Warming:

  • Rising sea levels due to the melting of polar ice caps and glaciers.
  • Extreme weather events like heatwaves, floods, droughts, and hurricanes.
  • Disruption of ecosystems and habitats, leading to the extinction of species.
  • Changes in precipitation patterns affecting agriculture and water resources.

iii. Ozone Layer Depletion

The ozone layer, located in the Earth's stratosphere, protects life by absorbing the majority of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

Causes of Ozone Depletion:

  • CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons): Chemicals used in refrigeration, air conditioning, and aerosol propellants that break down ozone molecules in the stratosphere.
  • Halons, Methyl Bromide: Other chemicals that contribute to ozone depletion.

Effects of Ozone Layer Depletion:

  • Increased UV radiation reaching the Earth's surface, leading to higher rates of skin cancer and cataracts in humans.
  • Disruption of marine ecosystems, especially affecting phytoplankton which forms the base of the food chain.
  • Damage to crops, especially those sensitive to UV radiation.

iv. Deforestation

Deforestation is the large-scale removal of forests, often to make way for agriculture, urbanization, or logging.

Causes of Deforestation:

  • Agricultural Expansion: Clearing land for farming, including crops like soy, palm oil, and cattle ranching.
  • Urbanization: Expansion of cities and infrastructure.
  • Logging: Harvesting trees for timber and paper production.
  • Mining: Extraction of minerals and oil leads to forest destruction.

Effects of Deforestation:

  • Loss of biodiversity as many species depend on forests for habitat.
  • Increased greenhouse gas emissions due to the burning or decay of trees, contributing to global warming.
  • Soil erosion and loss of fertile land.
  • Disruption of the water cycle, leading to changes in rainfall patterns.

v. Loss of Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth, including species diversity, genetic diversity, and ecosystem diversity. Its loss is a critical environmental issue.

Causes of Biodiversity Loss:

  • Habitat Destruction: Destruction of forests, wetlands, and coral reefs.
  • Pollution: Air, water, and soil pollution disrupt ecosystems and harm species.
  • Climate Change: Rising temperatures and extreme weather events force species to adapt or face extinction.
  • Over-exploitation: Overhunting, overfishing, and illegal wildlife trade.
  • Invasive Species: Non-native species can outcompete and displace native species.

Effects of Biodiversity Loss:

  • Disruption of ecosystems and the essential services they provide, such as pollination, water purification, and nutrient cycling.
  • Reduced resilience of ecosystems to environmental changes and natural disasters.
  • Decreased food security and loss of medicinal resources.

vi. Overpopulation

Overpopulation occurs when the number of individuals in a population exceeds the carrying capacity of its environment.

Causes of Overpopulation:

  • High Birth Rates: In some regions, birth rates remain high due to cultural and economic factors.
  • Declining Mortality Rates: Advances in healthcare, sanitation, and nutrition have led to lower death rates.

Effects of Overpopulation:

  • Depletion of natural resources (water, fossil fuels, arable land).
  • Increased pollution and waste generation.
  • Greater demand for food, leading to deforestation and overfarming.
  • Strain on healthcare, education, and infrastructure systems.

vii. Solid Waste Management

Solid waste refers to garbage, refuse, or trash that is produced by human activity, including industrial, residential, and commercial waste.

Types of Solid Waste:

  • Organic Waste: Food scraps, yard waste, etc.
  • Inorganic Waste: Plastics, metals, glass, etc.
  • Hazardous Waste: Chemicals, batteries, and e-waste.

Issues in Solid Waste Management:

  • Landfills: Accumulation of waste in landfills leads to environmental pollution and waste of valuable resources.
  • Plastic Waste: Non-biodegradable plastics contribute significantly to pollution, especially in oceans.
  • Recycling and Waste Reduction: Improper recycling practices and insufficient waste management systems lead to more waste entering landfills.

Solutions:

  • Reduce, Reuse, and Recycle.
  • Improve waste segregation and composting.
  • Promote sustainable product design to reduce waste.
