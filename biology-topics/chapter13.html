Chapter 13 - Muscular, Nervous, and Endocrine Systems

1.Muscle Types and Movement

Muscles are specialized tissues responsible for producing movement in the body. They are divided into three main types: skeletal muscles, smooth muscles, and cardiac muscles. Each muscle type has unique characteristics, functions, and locations within the body. Understanding these types and how they produce movement is essential in human physiology.

i. Skeletal Muscle

Structure: Skeletal muscles are striated (striped) because they have a regular arrangement of muscle fibers. They are long, cylindrical fibers with multiple nuclei located at the periphery of the cells.

Location: These muscles are attached to bones by tendons and are responsible for voluntary movements.

Control: Skeletal muscles are voluntary, meaning they are controlled consciously by the central nervous system (CNS).

Function: Skeletal muscles enable the body to move, maintain posture, and generate heat. They work in pairs or groups to produce coordinated movement.

Examples: Biceps, triceps, quadriceps, and hamstrings.

Movement: When skeletal muscles contract, they generate force, pulling bones at the joints. For example, when the biceps contract, they cause flexion of the elbow, while the triceps cause extension.

ii. Smooth Muscle

Structure: Smooth muscles are non-striated (lack the striped appearance). The muscle fibers are spindle-shaped, and each cell has a single nucleus. They contract slowly and rhythmically.

Location: Found in the walls of internal organs like the stomach, intestines, bladder, blood vessels, and airways.

Control: Smooth muscles are involuntary, meaning they are controlled automatically by the autonomic nervous system, hormones, and local chemical signals.

Function: Smooth muscles regulate the movement of substances through hollow organs (e.g., food through the digestive system) and help control blood flow and air passage.

Examples: Muscles in the intestines (peristalsis), muscles in blood vessels (vasoconstriction/vasodilation), and muscles in the bladder.

Movement: Smooth muscle contractions are slow and sustained. For example, in the digestive system, smooth muscles help move food along the gastrointestinal tract through a process called peristalsis.

iii. Cardiac Muscle

Structure: Cardiac muscles are striated like skeletal muscles but are branched and interconnected. Each cardiac muscle cell contains a single nucleus, and the fibers are joined by intercalated discs, which help synchronize contractions.

Location: Found exclusively in the walls of the heart.

Control: Cardiac muscles are involuntary and controlled by the autonomic nervous system as well as pacemaker cells in the heart.

Function: Cardiac muscles are responsible for pumping blood throughout the body by contracting and relaxing in a coordinated rhythm.

Examples: The heart muscle (myocardium).

Movement: The heart contracts rhythmically to pump blood. This contraction, called a heartbeat, ensures blood is pumped to the lungs and the rest of the body.

Types of Muscle Movements

Movement in the body occurs when muscles contract and shorten, pulling on bones or tissues. Muscle movements can be categorized into the following:

Flexion and Extension:

  • Flexion: A bending movement that decreases the angle between two body parts (e.g., bending the elbow).
  • Extension: A straightening movement that increases the angle between two body parts (e.g., straightening the elbow).

Abduction and Adduction:

  • Abduction: Movement of a body part away from the midline of the body (e.g., raising the arms sideways).
  • Adduction: Movement of a body part toward the midline of the body (e.g., lowering the arms back to the sides).

Rotation:

  • Medial rotation: Turning a body part inward (e.g., rotating the arm so the palm faces backward).
  • Lateral rotation: Turning a body part outward (e.g., rotating the arm so the palm faces forward).

Pronation and Supination:

  • Pronation: Rotating the forearm so that the palm faces downward.
  • Supination: Rotating the forearm so that the palm faces upward.

Plantar Flexion and Dorsiflexion:

  • Plantar flexion: Pointing the toes downward (e.g., pressing the gas pedal in a car).
  • Dorsiflexion: Pulling the toes upward (e.g., standing on heels).

Circumduction:

  • Circular movement: Circular movement at a joint, such as moving the arm in a circular motion.

Muscle Contraction Mechanism

The process of muscle contraction is explained through the sliding filament theory. Here's how it works:

  • Activation: A nerve impulse from the central nervous system triggers the release of acetylcholine at the neuromuscular junction, which then stimulates the muscle fiber.
  • Calcium Release: The impulse travels into the muscle fiber, causing the release of calcium ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum.
  • Cross-Bridge Formation: Calcium binds to troponin on the actin filament, causing a shift in the tropomyosin and allowing the myosin heads to attach to actin, forming cross-bridges.
  • Sliding of Filaments: The myosin heads pull the actin filaments towards the center of the sarcomere, causing the muscle to contract.
  • Relaxation: After the impulse stops, calcium ions are pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum, and the muscle fiber relaxes.

Summary of Muscle Types

Feature Skeletal Muscle Smooth Muscle Cardiac Muscle
Appearance Striated, long fibers Non-striated, spindle-shaped Striated, branched fibers
Control Voluntary Involuntary Involuntary
Location Attached to bones Internal organs Heart
Function Movement, posture Movement of substances Heartbeat, pumping blood
Examples Biceps, quadriceps Intestinal muscles, blood vessels Heart muscle (myocardium)

2. Neurons, Synapse, and Reflex Action

The nervous system is responsible for coordinating and regulating all body functions, allowing an organism to respond to internal and external stimuli. The neurons, synapse, and reflex action are crucial components of this system. Below is a breakdown of these concepts:

i. Neurons (Nerve Cells)

Neurons are the functional units of the nervous system, responsible for transmitting electrical impulses. They can be classified based on their function and structure.

Structure of Neuron:

A typical neuron consists of three main parts:

  • Cell Body (Soma): Contains the nucleus and other organelles, responsible for the metabolic activities of the neuron.
  • Dendrites: Branched extensions that receive electrical signals from other neurons and transmit them towards the cell body.
  • Axon: A long, cylindrical extension that carries electrical impulses away from the cell body to other neurons, muscles, or glands. The axon is often covered by a fatty insulating layer called the myelin sheath that speeds up the transmission of nerve impulses.

Types of Neurons:

  • Sensory Neurons (Afferent Neurons): Transmit sensory information from sensory receptors to the central nervous system (CNS).
  • Motor Neurons (Efferent Neurons): Carry impulses from the CNS to muscles and glands, triggering responses.
  • Interneurons: Located in the CNS, they transmit impulses between sensory and motor neurons, enabling communication within the nervous system.

Function of Neurons:

Neurons transmit electrical signals (nerve impulses) through action potentials, which move along the axon and are transmitted across synapses to other neurons or target cells. The transmission of these signals enables quick responses to stimuli, integration of sensory inputs, and regulation of bodily functions.

ii. Synapse

A synapse is the junction or gap between two neurons, where communication occurs through the transmission of chemical signals.

Structure of a Synapse:

  • Presynaptic Terminal: The end of the axon of the transmitting neuron, which contains neurotransmitters (chemical messengers).
  • Synaptic Cleft: The small gap between the presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons.
  • Postsynaptic Membrane: The membrane of the receiving neuron or target cell (e.g., muscle or gland) that contains receptors for neurotransmitters.

Function of Synapse:

When an electrical impulse (action potential) reaches the axon terminal, it triggers the release of neurotransmitters (such as dopamine, serotonin, acetylcholine) into the synaptic cleft. These neurotransmitters cross the synapse and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, generating a new electrical signal in the receiving neuron. Synapses allow communication between neurons, which is essential for processes like reflexes, thought, and memory formation.

Types of Synapses:

  • Chemical Synapse: The most common type, where neurotransmitters are released to transmit signals across the synaptic cleft.
  • Electrical Synapse: Less common, where neurons are connected by gap junctions, allowing direct electrical signal transmission.

iii. Reflex Action

A reflex action is an automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that does not require the involvement of the brain. Reflex actions help protect the body from harm and are faster than voluntary responses.

Structure of Reflex Arc:

The reflex arc is the pathway taken by the nerve impulses during a reflex action. It involves the following components:

  • Receptor: A sensory organ or nerve ending that detects a stimulus (e.g., heat, pain).
  • Sensory Neuron: Carries the impulse from the receptor to the spinal cord (CNS).
  • Interneuron: In the spinal cord, the interneuron processes the sensory information and sends a response to the motor neuron.
  • Motor Neuron: Carries the impulse from the spinal cord to an effector (e.g., a muscle or gland).
  • Effector: The muscle or gland that responds to the stimulus (e.g., muscle contraction, gland secretion).

Example of Reflex Action:

Withdrawal Reflex: If you touch something hot, sensory receptors in your skin detect the heat and send an impulse via a sensory neuron to the spinal cord. The impulse is processed, and a motor neuron sends a signal to the muscles in your arm to contract, pulling your hand away from the hot object. This entire process happens before the pain sensation reaches the brain.

Types of Reflexes:

  • Monosynaptic Reflex: Involves only one synapse between the sensory and motor neuron, e.g., the knee-jerk reflex.
  • Polysynaptic Reflex: Involves more than one synapse, including interneurons, e.g., the withdrawal reflex.

Significance of Reflex Actions:

  • Reflexes provide a rapid response to a stimulus, helping the body protect itself from injury.
  • Reflex actions can also help maintain balance, posture, and certain autonomic functions like heart rate and digestion.

3. Hormones and Endocrine Glands (Hypothalamus, Pituitary, Thyroid, Pancreas)

The endocrine system plays a crucial role in regulating various physiological processes through the release of hormones, which are chemical messengers that travel through the bloodstream to target organs. These hormones control growth, metabolism, reproduction, and mood. The major endocrine glands involved in this process include the hypothalamus, pituitary gland, thyroid gland, and pancreas.

i. Hypothalamus

The hypothalamus is a small but crucial part of the brain located just above the brainstem. It is the link between the nervous system and the endocrine system.

Functions of Hypothalamus:

  • It regulates homeostasis by controlling temperature, hunger, thirst, and sleep patterns.
  • It produces hormones that control the release of hormones from the pituitary gland.
  • It connects the nervous system with the endocrine system via the pituitary gland, allowing for coordination of body functions.

Hormones Secreted by the Hypothalamus:

  • Thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH): Stimulates the pituitary gland to release thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH).
  • Gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH): Stimulates the pituitary gland to release gonadotropins (LH and FSH).
  • Corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH): Stimulates the release of ACTH from the pituitary gland.
  • Growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH): Stimulates the release of growth hormone (GH) from the pituitary.
  • Somatostatin: Inhibits the release of growth hormone (GH) and thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH).
  • Dopamine: Inhibits the release of prolactin from the pituitary gland.

ii. Pituitary Gland

The pituitary gland is often called the "master gland" because it controls other endocrine glands. It is located at the base of the brain just beneath the hypothalamus.

Divisions of Pituitary Gland:

  • Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis): It produces and secretes several hormones that regulate other glands.
  • Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis): It stores and releases hormones produced by the hypothalamus.

Hormones Secreted by the Pituitary Gland:

Anterior Pituitary:

  • Growth Hormone (GH): Stimulates growth and cell reproduction.
  • Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH): Stimulates the thyroid gland to release thyroid hormones.
  • Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH): Stimulates the adrenal glands to release cortisol.
  • Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH): Stimulates the growth of follicles in the ovaries and sperm production in males.
  • Luteinizing Hormone (LH): Stimulates ovulation and the production of sex hormones.
  • Prolactin (PRL): Stimulates milk production in females after childbirth.

Posterior Pituitary:

  • Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): Regulates water balance by promoting water reabsorption in the kidneys.
  • Oxytocin: Stimulates uterine contractions during labor and milk ejection during breastfeeding.

iii. Thyroid Gland

The thyroid gland is a butterfly-shaped gland located in the neck. It plays a vital role in regulating metabolism, growth, and development.

Hormones Secreted by the Thyroid Gland:

  • Thyroxine (T4): Increases the metabolic rate of the body, influencing growth, energy production, and protein synthesis.
  • Triiodothyronine (T3): Similar to thyroxine but more potent in its action.
  • Calcitonin: Lowers blood calcium levels by promoting calcium deposition in bones.

Functions of Thyroid Hormones:

  • Regulation of metabolism.
  • Control of energy production.
  • Regulation of growth and development during childhood.
  • Influencing the nervous system's function.

iv. Pancreas

The pancreas is both an endocrine and exocrine gland located behind the stomach. It plays a major role in digestion and blood sugar regulation.

Endocrine Functions of the Pancreas:

The pancreas contains clusters of cells known as islets of Langerhans, which secrete hormones involved in regulating blood sugar levels.

Hormones Secreted by the Pancreas:

  • Insulin: Lowers blood glucose levels by promoting the uptake of glucose into cells for energy or storage.
  • Glucagon: Raises blood glucose levels by stimulating the liver to release glucose into the bloodstream.
  • Somatostatin: Inhibits the release of insulin and glucagon, helping to regulate the balance between these hormones.
  • Pancreatic Polypeptide: Regulates the exocrine functions of the pancreas and appetite.

Functions of Pancreatic Hormones:

  • Insulin: Reduces blood sugar levels by enabling cells to absorb glucose.
  • Glucagon: Raises blood sugar by stimulating the liver to convert stored glycogen into glucose.
  • Somatostatin: Acts as a regulatory hormone that balances insulin and glucagon levels.
