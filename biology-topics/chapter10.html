Chapter 10 - Growth and Development

1. Plant Hormones: Auxins, Gibberellins, Cytokinins, and Ethylene

Plant hormones are chemical messengers that regulate various aspects of plant growth, development, and response to environmental stimuli. Four key plant hormones are Auxins, Gibberellins, Cytokinins, and Ethylene. These hormones play vital roles in processes such as cell division, elongation, fruit ripening, and response to stress.

1. Auxins

Definition:

Auxins are a group of plant hormones that promote cell elongation and are involved in many growth and developmental processes.

Sources:

  • Primarily produced in apical meristems (tips of roots and shoots), young leaves, and developing seeds.

Functions:

  • Cell elongation: Auxins promote the elongation of cells, particularly in the stems and roots.
  • Apical dominance: Inhibits the growth of lateral buds to promote the growth of the main stem.
  • Root initiation: Auxins play a key role in root development and adventitious rooting.
  • Phototropism and gravitropism: Auxins help plants grow towards light (phototropism) and respond to gravity (gravitropism).
  • Fruit development: Auxins are involved in the initiation and development of fruits.

Examples:

  • Indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) is the most common naturally occurring auxin.

Use in Agriculture:

  • Rooting hormone: Used in plant propagation to induce root formation.
  • Herbicides: Synthetic auxins (e.g., 2,4-D) are used as herbicides to control broadleaf weeds.

2. Gibberellins

Definition:

Gibberellins are a group of plant hormones that promote stem elongation, seed germination, and flowering.

Sources:

  • Produced mainly in young tissues such as growing stems, roots, and developing seeds.

Functions:

  • Stem elongation: Gibberellins promote the growth of stems by stimulating cell elongation.
  • Seed germination: Gibberellins are essential for breaking seed dormancy and triggering germination.
  • Flowering: Some gibberellins can induce flowering in certain plants.
  • Fruit development: Gibberellins are involved in the development of seedless fruits.
  • Leaf expansion: Gibberellins promote the expansion of leaves.

Examples:

  • Gibberellic acid (GA) is the most well-known gibberellin.

Use in Agriculture:

  • Increase fruit size: Used to increase the size of fruits like grapes and apples.
  • Breaking dormancy: Used to promote germination in seeds that are difficult to germinate.
  • Inducing flowering: Used in floriculture to induce flowering in certain plants.

3. Cytokinins

Definition:

Cytokinins are plant hormones that promote cell division (cytokinesis) and are involved in the regulation of growth and development.

Sources:

  • Cytokinins are produced mainly in the roots and are transported to other parts of the plant via the xylem.

Functions:

  • Cell division and differentiation: Cytokinins stimulate cell division and play a key role in the formation of tissues.
  • Delay of senescence: Cytokinins delay aging in plant tissues (leaf senescence).
  • Shoot initiation: Cytokinins are involved in the initiation of shoots and the formation of lateral buds.
  • Nutrient mobilization: Cytokinins help in mobilizing nutrients to the growing parts of the plant.
  • Root-shoot balance: Cytokinins help maintain a balance between root and shoot growth.

Examples:

  • Zeatin and Kinetin are examples of naturally occurring cytokinins.

Use in Agriculture:

  • Tissue culture: Cytokinins are widely used in plant tissue culture to promote shoot formation and cell division.
  • Delay senescence: Cytokinins can be used to extend the shelf life of cut flowers and fruits.

4. Ethylene

Definition:

Ethylene is a gaseous plant hormone that is involved in various aspects of plant growth, particularly in the process of fruit ripening, flowering, and senescence.

Sources:

  • Ethylene is produced in all parts of the plant, especially in ripening fruits, flowers, and wounded tissues.

Functions:

  • Fruit ripening: Ethylene is the key hormone responsible for the ripening of fruits like bananas, tomatoes, and apples.
  • Senescence: Ethylene promotes aging and senescence in flowers, leaves, and fruit.
  • Abscission: Ethylene stimulates the shedding of leaves, flowers, and fruits (abscission).
  • Stress response: Ethylene is involved in the plant's response to stress, such as wounding or pathogen attack.
  • Germination: Ethylene plays a role in the germination of some seeds, especially those that need to overcome dormancy.

Examples:

  • Ethylene gas itself is the hormone, though it is often synthesized from compounds like methionine.

Use in Agriculture:

  • Ripening control: Ethylene is used to control the ripening of fruits in storage and transportation, especially for bananas and tomatoes.
  • Promoting flower shedding: Ethylene is used to induce flower shedding in certain crops.
  • Inhibiting fruit drop: Ethylene can be used to inhibit premature fruit drop in crops like citrus.

Summary of Functions of Key Plant Hormones

Hormone Main Functions Key Processes Involved
Auxins Cell elongation, apical dominance, root initiation, phototropism, fruit development Growth, development, and photoreception
Gibberellins Stem elongation, seed germination, flowering, fruit development Growth, germination, and flowering
Cytokinins Cell division, shoot initiation, delay of senescence, nutrient mobilization Cell growth, tissue formation, senescence regulation
Ethylene Fruit ripening, senescence, abscission, stress response Ripening, aging, stress responses, and flower drop

2. Photoperiodism and Vernalization

Both photoperiodism and vernalization are important physiological processes in plants that influence growth, development, and flowering. These processes are responses to environmental factors like light and temperature. Let's explore each of them in detail:

1. Photoperiodism

Definition:

Photoperiodism is the response of plants to the length of day and night (light/dark cycle). It determines the timing of flowering and other developmental processes in plants.

Types of Photoperiodism:

Plants can be categorized based on their response to the length of the light and dark periods:

  • Short-Day Plants (SDPs):
    • Definition: These plants flower when the day length is shorter than a critical value. They require long nights (short days) to flower.
    • Examples: Chrysanthemums, poinsettias, soybeans.
    • Environmental Requirement: A long period of darkness is needed for flowering. These plants typically flower as the days get shorter in autumn.
  • Long-Day Plants (LDPs):
    • Definition: These plants flower when the day length is longer than a critical value. They require short nights (long days) to flower.
    • Examples: Spinach, lettuce, clover, and some wheat varieties.
    • Environmental Requirement: They typically flower during summer when days are long.
  • Day-Neutral Plants:
    • Definition: These plants do not have a specific requirement for day length. They can flower regardless of the length of day or night.
    • Examples: Tomatoes, cucumbers, and cotton.
    • Environmental Requirement: The flowering of these plants is primarily determined by other factors like age, size, or environmental conditions rather than photoperiod.

Mechanism of Photoperiodism:

Plants detect the length of day and night through special pigments called phytochromes and cryptochromes. These photoreceptors help plants measure light and dark periods.

  • Phytochrome System: Phytochromes exist in two interconvertible forms (Pr and Pfr), which allow the plant to detect changes in the light environment. The relative levels of these two forms help the plant determine the length of day and night.

Significance:

  • Photoperiodism is important for synchronizing flowering with favorable environmental conditions for pollination and seed dispersal.
  • It is also used in agriculture to manipulate the flowering time of crops to increase yield and quality (e.g., manipulating light exposure in greenhouses).

2. Vernalization

Definition:

Vernalization is the process by which some plants require a period of cold temperature (usually during winter) to induce or promote flowering in the subsequent growing season.

Why Vernalization Occurs:

Some plants need exposure to low temperatures (typically 0-10°C) for a certain period (often weeks or months) in order to initiate flowering or improve seed production. This process ensures that flowering occurs during the most suitable time of the year, often in spring.

Types of Vernalization:

  • Obligate Vernalization:
    • Definition: Some plants require a cold period in order to flower. Without vernalization, these plants cannot flower.
    • Examples: Winter wheat, biennial plants like carrots and cabbage.
    • Environmental Requirement: A specific duration of cold exposure is essential for flowering.
  • Facultative Vernalization:
    • Definition: These plants may flower after vernalization but can also flower without cold exposure, although the flowering may not be as robust.
    • Examples: Certain varieties of winter wheat, Arabidopsis.
    • Environmental Requirement: Cold exposure enhances flowering but is not strictly necessary.

Mechanism of Vernalization:

Vernalization affects the gene expression related to flowering. The cold exposure causes changes in the plant's genetic material, often through epigenetic changes (modifications that do not alter the DNA sequence itself but affect gene expression). Cold temperatures activate specific genes that make the plant competent to flower once the temperatures rise in spring.

Significance:

  • Vernalization helps plants sync their flowering with favorable climatic conditions, ensuring they flower after the cold winter when environmental conditions (temperature, sunlight) are optimal for reproduction.
  • In agriculture, vernalization is important for controlling the flowering of crops like wheat and barley, which require a cold period for optimal growth and yield.

Summary of Photoperiodism and Vernalization

Process Description Examples Environmental Requirement
Photoperiodism Response to the length of day and night, regulating flowering and other processes Short-day plants, long-day plants, day-neutral plants Day length and night length determine flowering.
Vernalization Requirement of a cold period for some plants to induce flowering in the next season Winter wheat, biennial plants (carrots, cabbage) Exposure to cold temperatures (0-10°C) for a certain duration.
