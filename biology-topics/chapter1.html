Chapter 1 - Biodiversity and Classification

1. Biodiversity and Classification

Biodiversity

  • Definition: Biodiversity refers to the variety and variability of life on Earth, including the diversity of species, ecosystems, and genetic material within species. It is essential for maintaining ecological balance and providing vital services such as clean air, water, food, and medicine.

Levels of Biodiversity

  • Species Diversity: The variety of species within an ecosystem or on Earth. Example: The Amazon Rainforest is home to millions of species of plants, animals, and microorganisms.
  • Genetic Diversity: The variety of genetic material within species, allowing populations to adapt to changing environments and providing resilience against diseases.
  • Ecosystem Diversity: The variety of ecosystems such as forests, deserts, wetlands, and oceans, each with its unique community of organisms and environmental conditions.

Importance of Biodiversity

  • Ecological Balance: Biodiversity ensures ecosystem stability, with species playing specific roles (e.g., pollinators, predators, decomposers) in maintaining balance.
  • Economic Value: Biodiversity provides raw materials for food, medicine, and other products, supporting industries like agriculture, forestry, and tourism.
  • Cultural Importance: Many cultures derive their traditions, values, and practices from the biodiversity in their environment.
  • Environmental Services: Biodiversity provides services like air and water purification, climate regulation, and pollination.

Threats to Biodiversity

  • Habitat Destruction: Urbanization, deforestation, and pollution lead to the loss of natural habitats for many species.
  • Climate Change: Changing climate conditions affect species distribution, behavior, and survival.
  • Overexploitation: Overhunting, overfishing, and overharvesting of natural resources lead to population declines.
  • Invasive Species: Non-native species that outcompete or prey on native species, disrupting ecosystems.

Classification of Living Organisms

  • Biological Classification (Taxonomy): The process of grouping organisms based on their similarities and differences, helping scientists organize the diversity of life into hierarchical categories for better understanding.

Hierarchy of Classification

  • Domain: The highest level of classification. There are three domains:
    • Archaea: Single-celled organisms with no nucleus, often found in extreme environments.
    • Bacteria: Single-celled organisms, some of which can cause diseases.
    • Eukarya: Organisms with cells that have a nucleus, including animals, plants, fungi, and protists.
  • Kingdom: Below the domain. Examples include:
    • Animalia (animals)
    • Plantae (plants)
    • Fungi (fungi)
    • Protista (single-celled organisms like amoeba, algae)
    • Monera (bacteria, archaea)
  • Phylum: Groups organisms based on major body plans or structure (e.g., Chordata for animals with a backbone, Arthropoda for insects, spiders).
  • Class: Further division within a phylum based on shared characteristics (e.g., Mammalia for mammals, Aves for birds).
  • Order: A group of related families (e.g., Carnivora for carnivorous mammals like lions and tigers).
  • Family: Groups species with similar characteristics (e.g., Felidae for cats, lions, tigers).
  • Genus: A group of closely related species (e.g., Panthera for lions, tigers, and leopards).
  • Species: The most specific level of classification, representing a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. The species name is binomial, consisting of the genus and species name (e.g., Homo sapiens for humans).

Binomial Nomenclature

  • Developed by Carl Linnaeus, this system gives every organism a two-part Latin name: the first part is the genus name, and the second part is the species name. Example: Homo sapiens for humans.

Modern Classification (Phylogenetic Classification)

  • Phylogeny: The evolutionary history and relationships among species or groups. Organisms are classified based on shared ancestry.
  • Cladistics: A method of classification that groups organisms based on common ancestry and the presence of shared derived traits (called synapomorphies).

2. The Three Domains of Life

The Three Domains of Life is a system of classification that categorizes all living organisms into three large, distinct groups based on genetic, biochemical, and cellular characteristics. This system was introduced by Carl Woese in 1990, based on differences in ribosomal RNA (rRNA) sequences, which allowed for a more accurate understanding of evolutionary relationships.

The Three Domains

  1. Archaea (Archaebacteria)

    Characteristics:

    • Archaea are single-celled prokaryotes (organisms without a nucleus).
    • They have unique genetic sequences in their ribosomal RNA.
    • Found in extreme environments like hot springs, deep-sea hydrothermal vents, and salt lakes (e.g., the Great Salt Lake).
    • They have cell walls, but unlike bacteria, the cell wall does not contain peptidoglycan.
    • They can be anaerobic (live without oxygen) or aerobic (can tolerate oxygen).

    Examples:

    • Methanogens: Produce methane gas, found in swampy areas or the intestines of ruminants.
    • Halophiles: Thrive in extremely salty environments.
    • Thermophiles: Live in extremely hot environments (e.g., hot springs).
  2. Bacteria (Eubacteria)

    Characteristics:

    • Bacteria are single-celled prokaryotes with no nucleus.
    • They have a cell wall made of peptidoglycan, which is different from archaea and eukaryotes.
    • They are found in a wide variety of environments, including soil, water, and the human body.
    • Bacteria can be aerobic or anaerobic.
    • Some bacteria are harmful and cause diseases (e.g., Salmonella, E. coli), while others are beneficial (e.g., lactic acid bacteria in yogurt production).

    Examples:

    • Cyanobacteria: Photosynthetic bacteria that produce oxygen, often called "blue-green algae."
    • Escherichia coli (E. coli): A common bacterium that can be both beneficial and harmful, depending on the strain.
  3. Eukarya (Eukaryotes)

    Characteristics:

    • Eukarya includes all organisms whose cells have a nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles (e.g., mitochondria, chloroplasts).
    • These organisms can be unicellular (like protists) or multicellular (like animals, plants, and fungi).
    • The genetic material in eukaryotes is organized into chromosomes within the nucleus.
    • Eukaryotes exhibit more complex internal structure and functionality compared to prokaryotes (archaea and bacteria).

    Examples:

    • Protists: Mostly unicellular organisms like amoeba, paramecium, and algae.
    • Fungi: Includes organisms like mushrooms, yeasts, and molds.
    • Plantae: Multicellular, photosynthetic organisms like trees, flowers, and grasses.
    • Animalia: Multicellular organisms that are generally heterotrophic (cannot produce their own food), such as humans, dogs, fish, and birds.

Key Differences Between the Domains

Feature Archaea Bacteria Eukarya
Cell Type Prokaryotic (no nucleus) Prokaryotic (no nucleus) Eukaryotic (has a nucleus)
Cell Wall No peptidoglycan (varied composition) Peptidoglycan No peptidoglycan (in plants, cellulose or chitin in fungi)
Ribosomal RNA Unique sequence Unique sequence Similar to eukaryotes, distinct from prokaryotes
Reproduction Asexual (binary fission) Asexual (binary fission) Asexual or sexual (mitosis, meiosis)
Examples Methanogens, halophiles, thermophiles Cyanobacteria, E. coli, Salmonella Humans, plants, fungi, protists

3. Five Kingdom Classification

The Five Kingdom Classification system was proposed by Robert Whittaker in 1969. This system divides all living organisms into five major kingdoms based on differences in their cellular structure, mode of nutrition, and reproduction. The five kingdoms are:

The Five Kingdoms

  1. Monera (Prokaryotes)

    Characteristics:

    • Composed of prokaryotic cells (cells without a nucleus).
    • Organisms are unicellular.
    • Lack membrane-bound organelles.
    • They have a cell wall, but the composition differs between bacteria and archaea.
    • Can be autotrophic (synthesize their own food) or heterotrophic (consume other organisms for food).

    Examples:

    • Bacteria (e.g., Escherichia coli, Salmonella).
    • Archaea (e.g., Methanogens, Halophiles).
  2. Protista (Protists)

    Characteristics:

    • Made up of eukaryotic cells (cells with a nucleus).
    • Can be unicellular or multicellular (though most are unicellular).
    • They have membrane-bound organelles.
    • Protists exhibit various modes of nutrition: some are autotrophic (e.g., algae), while others are heterotrophic (e.g., protozoans).

    Examples:

    • Protozoans (e.g., Amoeba, Paramecium).
    • Algae (e.g., Chlamydomonas, Spirulina).
    • Slime molds.
  3. Fungi

    Characteristics:

    • Composed of eukaryotic cells (cells with a nucleus).
    • They are heterotrophic, meaning they absorb nutrients from other organisms (e.g., decomposers).
    • Have a cell wall made of chitin.
    • Can be unicellular (e.g., yeasts) or multicellular (e.g., mushrooms, molds).
    • Reproduce through spores.

    Examples:

    • Yeasts (e.g., Saccharomyces cerevisiae).
    • Mushrooms (e.g., Agaricus).
    • Molds (e.g., Rhizopus).
  4. Plantae (Plants)

    Characteristics:

    • Composed of eukaryotic cells.
    • They are multicellular and autotrophic, meaning they produce their own food through photosynthesis.
    • Have cell walls made of cellulose.
    • Contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis.
    • Exhibit a complex structure, including roots, stems, and leaves.

    Examples:

    • Mosses (e.g., Bryophytes).
    • Ferns (e.g., Pteridophytes).
    • Flowering plants (e.g., Angiosperms).
    • Conifers (e.g., Gymnosperms).
  5. Animalia (Animals)

    Characteristics:

    • Composed of eukaryotic cells.
    • Multicellular organisms.
    • Heterotrophic (consume other organisms for food).
    • Lack cell walls.
    • They exhibit complex nervous and muscular systems (in most).
    • Reproduce mainly through sexual reproduction (though some can reproduce asexually).

    Examples:

    • Invertebrates (e.g., sponges, worms, insects).
    • Vertebrates (e.g., fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals).

Summary of the Five Kingdoms

Kingdom Cell Type Cellular Organization Mode of Nutrition Examples
Monera Prokaryotic Unicellular Autotrophic or Heterotrophic Bacteria, Archaea
Protista Eukaryotic Unicellular or multicellular Autotrophic or Heterotrophic Amoeba, Paramecium, Algae, Slime molds
Fungi Eukaryotic Unicellular or multicellular Heterotrophic (Decomposers) Yeasts, Mushrooms, Molds
Plantae Eukaryotic Multicellular Autotrophic (Photosynthesis) Mosses, Ferns, Flowering Plants
Animalia Eukaryotic Multicellular Heterotrophic (Ingest food) Invertebrates (e.g., Insects), Vertebrates (e.g., Humans)

4. Taxonomic Hierarchy and Binomial Nomenclature

Taxonomic Hierarchy

The taxonomic hierarchy is a system used to classify and organize living organisms based on shared characteristics and evolutionary relationships. It consists of several levels, each representing a rank, from the broadest to the most specific classification. The hierarchy follows a descending order of inclusiveness.

The Main Taxonomic Ranks

  • Domain – The highest rank in the classification system. There are three domains: Archaea, Bacteria, and Eukarya.
  • Kingdom – The second highest rank. Organisms in each kingdom share major features and life processes. Example kingdoms include Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, and Protista.
  • Phylum – Groups organisms based on major body plans or structural features. For example, Chordata (vertebrates) and Arthropoda (insects, spiders).
  • Class – A group within a phylum. For instance, within Chordata, there are classes such as Mammalia (mammals) and Aves (birds).
  • Order – Groups related families within a class. For example, within the class Mammalia, the order Carnivora includes animals like lions, tigers, and bears.
  • Family – Organisms within a family share similar structural features and behaviors. For instance, the family Felidae includes lions, tigers, and domestic cats.
  • Genus – A group of closely related species. For example, Panthera is the genus that includes lions, tigers, and leopards.
  • Species – The most specific level of classification. Organisms within a species can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. The scientific name of a species is based on its genus and species name (binomial nomenclature). Example: Homo sapiens (humans).

Binomial Nomenclature

Binomial nomenclature is the formal system of naming species by using two Latin-based names: the genus name followed by the species name. This system was introduced by Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century and helps provide unique and universally accepted names for species.

Key Points about Binomial Nomenclature:

  • Genus Name: The first part of the name is the genus, which is always capitalized and italicized or underlined. It represents a group of closely related species.
  • Species Name: The second part of the name is the species, which is always written in lowercase and italicized or underlined. It represents a specific organism within the genus.
  • Universal: The scientific names are standardized and used globally, helping avoid confusion that may arise from common names.

Examples:

  • Homo sapiens: Homo is the genus, and sapiens is the species (humans).
  • Panthera leo: Panthera is the genus, and leo is the species (lion).
  • Canis lupus: Canis is the genus, and lupus is the species (gray wolf).

Importance of Taxonomic Hierarchy and Binomial Nomenclature:

  • Universal Identification: Helps scientists around the world recognize the same species with the same name.
  • Classification and Evolution: Provides a system for grouping organisms based on evolutionary relationships and shared characteristics.
  • Communication: Avoids confusion caused by local or common names, which may vary across languages and regions.

Example of Taxonomic Classification

For the Tiger (Panthera tigris), the taxonomic classification would be:

  • Domain: Eukarya
  • Kingdom: Animalia
  • Phylum: Chordata
  • Class: Mammalia
  • Order: Carnivora
  • Family: Felidae
  • Genus: Panthera
  • Species: Panthera tigris

And the binomial nomenclature for the tiger is Panthera tigris.

5. Tools for Taxonomy: Herbaria and Botanical Gardens

Herbaria

A herbarium is a collection of preserved plant specimens (usually dried plants or parts of plants) that are used for taxonomic research. These specimens are typically mounted on paper sheets and labeled with information about the plant’s identity, habitat, collection date, and the name of the collector.

Purpose of Herbaria:

  • Taxonomic Study: Herbaria are used to study plant morphology, structure, and evolution. They allow taxonomists to compare specimens from different regions and make accurate species identifications.
  • Reference: They act as a reference point for scientists to check the validity of newly described species or to confirm the identity of specimens.
  • Documentation: Provide a historical record of plant species found in certain regions, helping to track changes in plant distribution over time.

Features of Herbaria:

  • Specimens are carefully collected, dried, and pressed to preserve them.
  • Each specimen is mounted on a sheet of paper, with detailed labels that include the plant's scientific name, place of collection, date, and collector.
  • Many herbaria contain voucher specimens (examples of species used in scientific studies).

Examples of Herbaria:

  • Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew (UK): One of the largest and most important herbaria in the world.
  • The Field Museum Herbarium (USA): Home to a vast collection of plant specimens.

Botanical Gardens

A botanical garden is a place where plants are grown and scientifically studied. Botanical gardens often have living plant collections, which include rare, exotic, and native species. These gardens serve as an open-air laboratory for studying plants in their natural environment.

Purpose of Botanical Gardens:

  • Conservation: Botanical gardens play a vital role in conserving endangered plant species by maintaining living collections and engaging in breeding programs.
  • Education and Public Awareness: They educate the public about plant diversity, the importance of plants, and environmental conservation.
  • Research and Study: Serve as centers for research on plant biology, horticulture, and taxonomy. They provide scientists with access to a variety of plant species for study and classification.

Features of Botanical Gardens:

  • Contain a diverse collection of plants arranged based on taxonomy, geography, or habitat.
  • Often feature themed gardens (e.g., medicinal plants, tropical plants, desert plants) for educational purposes.
  • Many botanical gardens work closely with universities and research institutions for plant conservation and scientific study.

Examples of Botanical Gardens:

  • Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew (UK): A world-renowned botanical garden with a significant research and conservation program.
  • Missouri Botanical Garden (USA): One of the oldest botanical gardens, focusing on plant conservation, research, and education.
  • Singapore Botanic Gardens (Singapore): Known for its tropical plant collection and biodiversity research.

Importance of Herbaria and Botanical Gardens in Taxonomy

Tool Role in Taxonomy
Herbaria - Store preserved specimens for taxonomic research and identification.
- Allow scientists to compare plant specimens to describe new species or confirm identifications.
- Provide a permanent record of plant species, helping track changes in distribution and aiding historical research.
Botanical Gardens - Facilitate live plant study in their natural habitat for classification, research, and educational purposes.
- Conserve rare and endangered species, ensuring their survival.
- Provide data and material for plant research, breeding, and sustainable horticultural practices.
