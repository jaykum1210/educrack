Chapter 7 - Cell Cycle and Cell Division

Mitosis and Meiosis: Phases and Significance

Both mitosis and meiosis are processes of cell division, but they differ in their purpose, mechanisms, and outcomes. Hereâ€™s a detailed explanation of both processes:

1. Mitosis

Mitosis is a type of cell division in eukaryotic cells that results in the formation of two genetically identical daughter cells. It is responsible for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction in organisms.

Phases of Mitosis:

Mitosis occurs in four main stages: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase. These stages are preceded by interphase, during which the cell prepares for division.

Interphase (not technically part of mitosis, but important):

  • G1 Phase (Gap 1): Cell growth and normal functions.
  • S Phase (Synthesis): DNA replication occurs, resulting in two identical sets of chromosomes.
  • G2 Phase (Gap 2): Final preparations for mitosis, such as protein synthesis.

Prophase:

  • Chromosomes condense and become visible.
  • The nuclear membrane begins to break down.
  • The mitotic spindle (made of microtubules) starts to form.
  • Centrioles move to opposite poles of the cell.

Metaphase:

  • Chromosomes line up at the metaphase plate (the center of the cell).
  • Spindle fibers attach to the centromeres of chromosomes at the kinetochore.

Anaphase:

  • Sister chromatids are pulled apart toward opposite poles of the cell.
  • The centromere splits, and each chromatid is now considered a separate chromosome.

Telophase:

  • Chromatids reach the poles of the cell and start to de-condense into chromatin.
  • The nuclear envelope re-forms around each set of chromosomes, creating two distinct nuclei.
  • Spindle fibers disappear.

Cytokinesis:

  • The cytoplasm divides, resulting in two genetically identical daughter cells.
  • In animal cells, the membrane pinches in the middle (cleavage furrow), while in plant cells, a new cell wall forms (cell plate).

Significance of Mitosis:

  • Growth and Development: Mitosis allows multicellular organisms to grow and develop.
  • Cell Repair: It helps replace damaged or dead cells (e.g., skin cells).
  • Asexual Reproduction: In unicellular organisms, mitosis is the mechanism for asexual reproduction (e.g., binary fission in bacteria).
  • Genetic Consistency: Mitosis ensures that each daughter cell has the same number of chromosomes and genetic information as the parent cell.

2. Meiosis

Meiosis is a specialized form of cell division that reduces the chromosome number by half, resulting in four non-identical daughter cells, each with half the chromosome number of the original cell. This is essential for sexual reproduction, as it produces gametes (sperm and eggs in animals, pollen and ovules in plants).

Phases of Meiosis:

Meiosis consists of two sequential divisions: Meiosis I and Meiosis II, each with similar phases as mitosis.

Meiosis I (Reduction Division):

Prophase I:
  • Chromosomes condense and become visible.
  • Homologous chromosomes pair up in a process called synapsis.
  • Crossing over occurs, where sections of chromatids are exchanged between homologous chromosomes, leading to genetic variation.
  • The nuclear membrane dissolves, and spindle fibers begin to form.
Metaphase I:
  • Homologous chromosome pairs align at the metaphase plate.
  • Spindle fibers attach to the centromeres of each chromosome.
Anaphase I:
  • Homologous chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids) are separated and pulled toward opposite poles of the cell.
  • Unlike mitosis, the sister chromatids remain together during this phase.
Telophase I:
  • Chromosomes reach the poles of the cell.
  • The nuclear envelope re-forms, and the cell divides by cytokinesis, resulting in two daughter cells.

Meiosis II (Equational Division):

Prophase II:
  • Chromosomes condense again (no replication of DNA between Meiosis I and Meiosis II).
  • A new spindle apparatus forms in each of the two daughter cells.
Metaphase II:
  • Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate in each of the two cells.
Anaphase II:
  • The centromeres of each chromosome split, and sister chromatids are separated and pulled to opposite poles.
Telophase II:
  • Chromatids reach the poles, and the nuclear envelope re-forms.
  • Cytokinesis occurs, resulting in four non-identical daughter cells, each with half the original chromosome number.

Significance of Meiosis:

  • Genetic Diversity: Meiosis generates genetic variation through processes like crossing over and independent assortment of chromosomes. This diversity is crucial for evolution and adaptation.
  • Halving of Chromosome Number: Meiosis reduces the chromosome number by half, ensuring that when gametes (sperm and eggs) fuse during fertilization, the resulting zygote has the correct number of chromosomes.
  • Formation of Gametes: Meiosis is essential for sexual reproduction, producing gametes (sperm and eggs) in animals, and pollen and ovules in plants.

Comparison of Mitosis and Meiosis

Aspect Mitosis Meiosis
Purpose Growth, repair, asexual reproduction Sexual reproduction (production of gametes)
Number of Divisions 1 division (PMAT) 2 divisions (Meiosis I and Meiosis II)
Chromosome Number Daughter cells have the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell (diploid to diploid) Daughter cells have half the chromosome number (diploid to haploid)
Number of Daughter Cells 2 daughter cells 4 daughter cells
Genetic Variation No genetic variation; cells are identical to the parent Genetic variation due to crossing over and independent assortment
Role in Organism Growth, repair, and asexual reproduction Production of gametes for sexual reproduction
