Chapter 9 - Photosynthesis and Respiration

1. Photosynthesis: Light and Dark Reactions, C3 & C4 Pathways

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants convert light energy into chemical energy, producing glucose and oxygen from carbon dioxide and water. It takes place in the chloroplasts of plant cells and involves two main stages: light reactions and dark reactions (also known as the Calvin Cycle). Additionally, there are two main types of photosynthetic pathways: C3 pathway and C4 pathway.

i. Light Reactions (Photochemical Reactions)

Location:

Occurs in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts.

Process:

  • The light reactions are light-dependent, meaning they require sunlight to proceed.
  • When chlorophyll absorbs light energy, it excites electrons, initiating a series of events.
  • Photolysis: Water molecules are split into oxygen (O₂), protons (H⁺), and electrons (e⁻). The oxygen is released as a byproduct.
  • The excited electrons travel through the electron transport chain (ETC), generating a flow of energy.
  • ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) and NADPH (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide Phosphate) are produced by the light reactions and are used in the dark reactions.

Key Products:

  • ATP: Energy carrier molecule.
  • NADPH: Reducing agent used in the dark reactions.
  • Oxygen (O₂): Released as a byproduct from the splitting of water.

ii. Dark Reactions (Calvin Cycle / Light-Independent Reactions)

Location:

Occurs in the stroma of the chloroplasts (fluid-filled space surrounding the thylakoid membranes).

Process:

  • The dark reactions do not require light directly but depend on the ATP and NADPH produced in the light reactions.
  • The primary purpose of the dark reactions is to fix carbon dioxide (CO₂) into an organic molecule (usually glucose).
  • The Calvin Cycle consists of a series of enzyme-controlled steps that convert carbon dioxide into glucose.
  • The key enzyme involved is RuBisCO (Ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase-oxygenase), which catalyzes the fixation of CO₂ onto ribulose bisphosphate (RuBP), resulting in the formation of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA).
  • Through several steps, 3-PGA is reduced to form G3P (Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate), which can be used to produce glucose and other carbohydrates.

Key Products:

  • Glucose: The primary sugar produced, which can be used immediately for energy or stored as starch.
  • Other carbohydrates: These can be used for plant growth and development.

iii. C3 Pathway (The Calvin Cycle)

Location:

Occurs in the mesophyll cells of leaves.

Process:

  • In the C3 pathway, carbon dioxide (CO₂) is fixed directly by RuBisCO in the Calvin Cycle to form a 3-carbon compound (3-phosphoglycerate, 3-PGA), which is then converted into G3P.
  • The name "C3" comes from the fact that the first stable product of CO₂ fixation is a 3-carbon molecule.
  • This pathway is most common in temperate plants and plants that thrive in moderate climates.

Advantages:

  • Efficient in moderate temperatures and when there is sufficient water.
  • It requires less energy than C4 and CAM pathways.

Disadvantages:

  • Inefficient under high temperatures and low CO₂ concentrations due to photorespiration (when RuBisCO binds to oxygen instead of carbon dioxide, wasting energy and reducing sugar production).

Examples:

  • Wheat, rice, and most trees.

iv. C4 Pathway

Location:

Occurs in mesophyll cells and bundle sheath cells of leaves.

Process:

  • The C4 pathway involves an additional step before the Calvin Cycle. CO₂ is initially fixed into a 4-carbon compound, usually oxaloacetate, in the mesophyll cells by the enzyme PEP carboxylase (which has a higher affinity for CO₂ than RuBisCO).
  • The 4-carbon compound is then transported to the bundle sheath cells, where it is converted back into CO₂, which enters the Calvin Cycle for glucose production.
  • This separation of carbon fixation and the Calvin Cycle into different cells reduces the chances of photorespiration, making it more efficient at higher temperatures and lower CO₂ concentrations.

Advantages:

  • More efficient in hot and dry climates.
  • Less photorespiration, as CO₂ is concentrated around RuBisCO, leading to higher sugar production.

Disadvantages:

  • Requires more energy (ATP) than the C3 pathway.
  • More complex than the C3 pathway.

Examples:

  • Corn, sugarcane, and sorghum.

v. Comparison of C3 and C4 Pathways

Feature C3 Pathway C4 Pathway
First stable product 3-carbon compound (3-PGA) 4-carbon compound (oxaloacetate)
Enzyme involved RuBisCO PEP carboxylase (in mesophyll cells) and RuBisCO (in bundle sheath cells)
Location of CO₂ fixation Mesophyll cells (Calvin Cycle) Mesophyll cells (initial fixation), Bundle sheath cells (Calvin Cycle)
Efficiency in hot climates Low (due to photorespiration) High (no photorespiration)
Energy requirement Lower Higher (requires more ATP)
Examples Wheat, rice, barley, trees Corn, sugarcane, sorghum, millet

vi. CAM Pathway (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism)

Location:

Occurs in the mesophyll cells of plants, especially in succulents and cacti.

Process:

  • The CAM pathway is an adaptation to very hot, dry conditions.
  • At night, CAM plants open their stomata to absorb CO₂, which is fixed into a 4-carbon compound (such as malate) by PEP carboxylase.
  • During the day, when the stomata are closed to conserve water, CO₂ is released from the stored 4-carbon compounds and enters the Calvin Cycle to produce glucose.

Advantages:

  • Minimizes water loss by opening stomata at night when it is cooler and more humid.
  • Efficient in arid environments with limited water availability.

Examples:

  • Cacti, pineapple, and aloe.

2. Cellular Respiration: Glycolysis, Krebs Cycle, and Electron Transport Chain

Cellular respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose (or other organic molecules) to produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the energy currency of the cell. It consists of three main stages: Glycolysis, the Krebs cycle (also known as the citric acid cycle), and the Electron Transport Chain (ETC).

1. Glycolysis (The Breakdown of Glucose)

Location:

Cytoplasm of the cell.

Process:

  • Glycolysis is the first step in the breakdown of glucose (a 6-carbon sugar) and occurs in all living cells.
  • It is an anaerobic process, meaning it does not require oxygen.
  • In glycolysis, one molecule of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) is broken down into two molecules of pyruvate (C₃H₄O₃), with the production of a small amount of energy.

Steps in Glycolysis:

Glucose Activation:

Glucose is phosphorylated (phosphate groups are added) using 2 ATP molecules, forming fructose-1,6-bisphosphate.

Cleavage:

The fructose-1,6-bisphosphate is then split into two 3-carbon molecules: glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P).

Energy Generation:

Each G3P molecule is converted into pyruvate through a series of steps, producing:

  • 2 ATP molecules (net gain, 4 ATP produced, but 2 ATP were consumed).
  • 2 NADH molecules (used in the electron transport chain).

Net Products of Glycolysis:

  • 2 ATP (net gain).
  • 2 NADH (which will carry electrons to the ETC).
  • 2 Pyruvate molecules (enter the Krebs cycle for further processing).
  • 2 H₂O molecules.
  • 2 protons (H⁺).

Significance of Glycolysis:

  • It is the first step in both aerobic (with oxygen) and anaerobic (without oxygen) respiration.
  • It provides an immediate source of ATP in cells.
  • It produces pyruvate, which is further processed in the Krebs cycle.

2. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle or TCA Cycle)

Location:

Mitochondrial matrix.

Process:

  • The Krebs cycle occurs after glycolysis if oxygen is available (aerobic conditions). It takes place in the mitochondria and is crucial for the complete oxidation of glucose.
  • Each pyruvate produced during glycolysis is converted into Acetyl-CoA before entering the Krebs cycle.

Steps in the Krebs Cycle:

Formation of Acetyl-CoA:

Pyruvate (3 carbons) is decarboxylated (one carbon is removed as CO₂) to form Acetyl-CoA (2 carbons). This step also produces 1 NADH.

Citric Acid Formation:

Acetyl-CoA (2 carbons) combines with oxaloacetate (4 carbons) to form citric acid (6 carbons).

Series of Reactions:

Citric acid undergoes a series of reactions where it loses 2 carbon atoms as CO₂ and is gradually oxidized, producing:

  • 3 NADH.
  • 1 FADH₂ (another electron carrier).
  • 1 ATP (via substrate-level phosphorylation).

Regeneration of Oxaloacetate:

The cycle ends by regenerating oxaloacetate, enabling the cycle to repeat with another molecule of Acetyl-CoA.

Net Products Per Cycle (Per Acetyl-CoA):

  • 3 NADH.
  • 1 FADH₂.
  • 1 ATP.
  • 2 CO₂ (released as waste).
  • Oxaloacetate (regenerated to combine with another Acetyl-CoA).

Net Products Per Glucose Molecule:

  • 6 NADH.
  • 2 FADH₂.
  • 2 ATP.
  • 4 CO₂.

Significance of the Krebs Cycle:

  • The Krebs cycle produces high-energy electron carriers NADH and FADH₂, which are used in the electron transport chain to generate more ATP.
  • It also produces ATP directly via substrate-level phosphorylation.
  • It plays a key role in oxidizing acetyl-CoA to CO₂ while transferring high-energy electrons to carrier molecules.

3. Electron Transport Chain (ETC) and Oxidative Phosphorylation

Location:

Inner mitochondrial membrane.

Process:

  • The Electron Transport Chain is the final stage of cellular respiration. It is an aerobic process, requiring oxygen.
  • The electron carriers NADH and FADH₂, produced in both glycolysis and the Krebs cycle, deliver electrons to the ETC, which is a series of protein complexes and other molecules embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane.

Steps in the ETC:

Electron Donation:

NADH and FADH₂ donate their electrons to the electron transport chain. NADH donates electrons to Complex I, and FADH₂ donates electrons to Complex II.

Electron Transfer:

The electrons move through the complexes in the ETC (Complex I, II, III, IV) via a series of redox reactions, releasing energy at each step. This energy is used to pump protons (H⁺) from the mitochondrial matrix into the intermembrane space, creating an electrochemical gradient.

Oxygen as the Final Electron Acceptor:

Oxygen (O₂) acts as the final electron acceptor at Complex IV, combining with electrons and protons to form water (H₂O).

ATP Synthesis:

The electrochemical gradient created by proton pumping across the membrane drives protons back into the matrix through ATP synthase. The flow of protons through ATP synthase provides the energy for the phosphorylation of ADP to ATP, a process known as oxidative phosphorylation.

Net Products of the Electron Transport Chain:

  • 34 ATP molecules (from oxidative phosphorylation).
  • Water (H₂O) (byproduct from the combination of electrons, protons, and oxygen).

Significance of the Electron Transport Chain:

  • The ETC generates the majority of ATP produced during cellular respiration.
  • Oxygen is essential as the final electron acceptor, allowing the entire chain to function efficiently.
  • The process also produces water as a byproduct, which is exhaled by the organism.
