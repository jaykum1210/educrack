Chapter 3 - Plant Morphology and Anatomy

1. Root, Stem, Leaf, Flower, Fruit, Seed (Structure & Modifications)

i. Root

Definition

  • The root anchors the plant to the soil and absorbs water and nutrients.

Structure of the Root

  • Root Cap: Protects the growing tip of the root.
  • Root Hair: Tiny hair-like structures that increase surface area for absorption.
  • Root Apex: The growing tip, where cell division occurs.
  • Mature Region: Where the root hairs are present and absorption of water and minerals takes place.
  • Xylem and Phloem: Vascular tissues that transport water, nutrients, and food.

Modifications of Root

  • Tap Root: A large primary root with smaller lateral roots (e.g., Carrot, Radish).
  • Fibrous Root: A mass of fine roots emerging from the base (e.g., Wheat, Grass).
  • Adventitious Root: Roots that arise from parts of the plant other than the radicle (e.g., Banyan tree, Ivy).

Modified Roots

  • Storage Roots: Store food (e.g., Sweet potato, Beetroot).
  • Prop Roots: Support the plant (e.g., Maize).
  • Climbing Roots: Help in climbing (e.g., Money plant).
  • Aerating Roots: Enable respiration (e.g., Mangroves).

ii. Stem

Definition

  • The stem supports the plant, conducts water, nutrients, and food, and facilitates the growth of leaves and flowers.

Structure of the Stem

  • Node: The point where leaves are attached.
  • Internode: The space between two nodes.
  • Apical Bud: The growing tip of the stem.
  • Lateral Buds: Buds that develop into branches or flowers.
  • Xylem and Phloem: Vascular tissues for transport.
  • Cortex and Epidermis: Outer protective layers of the stem.

Modifications of Stem

  • Underground Stems:
    • Rhizomes: Horizontal underground stems (e.g., Ginger).
    • Tubers: Storage organs (e.g., Potato).
    • Bulbs: Modified for storage (e.g., Onion).
  • Aerial Stems:
    • Climbing Stems: Use tendrils for support (e.g., Grapevine).
    • Thorny Stems: Modified for defense (e.g., Cactus).
    • Flattened Stems: Perform photosynthesis (e.g., Opuntia).

Stem Modifications for Storage

  • Stem Tubers: Store food (e.g., Potato).
  • Corms: Thickened underground stems for storage (e.g., Gladiolus).

iii. Leaf

Definition

  • The leaf is the primary site for photosynthesis, transpiration, and gas exchange.

Structure of the Leaf

  • Petiole: The stalk that attaches the leaf to the stem.
  • Lamina (Leaf Blade): The flat, green part of the leaf where photosynthesis occurs.
  • Midrib: The central vein of the leaf.
  • Veins: Vascular tissue (xylem and phloem) for transport.
  • Stomata: Small openings for gas exchange.
  • Cuticle: A waxy layer that prevents water loss.

Modifications of Leaf

  • Tendrils: Modified for climbing (e.g., Pea plant).
  • Spines: Modified for defense (e.g., Cactus).
  • Storage Leaves: Modified to store water (e.g., Onion).
  • Insectivorous Leaves: Modified to trap and digest insects (e.g., Venus flytrap).
  • Bracts: Modified leaves that are often colorful and attract pollinators (e.g., Poinsettia).

iv. Flower

Definition

  • The flower is the reproductive organ of the plant and is responsible for pollination and seed formation.

Structure of the Flower

  • Sepals: Protect the bud before it opens.
  • Petals: Often colorful and attract pollinators.
  • Stamens: Male reproductive organs consisting of the anther (produces pollen) and filament.
  • Pistil (Carpel): Female reproductive organ consisting of the stigma, style, and ovary.
  • Ovary: Contains ovules, which develop into seeds after fertilization.
  • Receptacle: The base of the flower that supports all the floral parts.

Modifications of Flower

  • Unisexual Flowers: Only one sex (either male or female) is present (e.g., Cucumber).
  • Bisexual Flowers: Contain both male and female reproductive parts (e.g., Lily).
  • Inflorescences: Groupings of flowers (e.g., Dandelion, Lotus).
  • Zygomorphic Flowers: Symmetrical in one plane (e.g., Orchid).
  • Actinomorphic Flowers: Radially symmetrical (e.g., Tulip).

v. Fruit

Definition

  • The fruit develops from the fertilized ovary of a flower and contains seeds.

Structure of the Fruit

  • Pericarp (Fruit Wall): The outer covering of the fruit, which is usually divided into three layers: exocarp (skin), mesocarp (flesh), and endocarp (inner layer around the seed).
  • Seed: Contains the embryo, endosperm, and seed coat.
  • Placenta: The part of the ovary wall to which the ovules (seeds) are attached.

Modifications of Fruit

  • Fleshy Fruits: Have a soft, edible pericarp (e.g., Apple, Peach).
  • Dry Fruits: The pericarp becomes dry and hard at maturity (e.g., Peanut, Cotton).
  • Dehiscent Fruits: Open when mature to release seeds (e.g., Poppy).
  • Indehiscent Fruits: Do not open to release seeds (e.g., Acorn).

vi. Seed

Definition

  • The seed is the reproductive unit of plants, containing the embryo and stored food, which germinates to form a new plant.

Structure of the Seed

  • Seed Coat: The protective outer layer of the seed.
  • Embryo: The young plant inside the seed, which includes the radicle (develops into the root) and the plumule (develops into the shoot).
  • Cotyledons: Seed leaves that store food (either dicotyledons with two cotyledons, or monocotyledons with one).
  • Endosperm: The food reserve used during germination (present in monocots).
  • Micropyle: Small opening through which the seed absorbs water.

Modifications of Seed

  • Dormancy: Seeds may remain inactive for a period of time until conditions are favorable for germination.
  • Dispersal Mechanisms: Seeds may be dispersed by wind, water, or animals (e.g., Dandelion seeds have a pappus for wind dispersal).

2. Anatomy of Dicot and Monocot Plants

Dicot Plants (Dicotyledons)

Dicotyledons are plants that have two cotyledons (seed leaves) in their seeds. The vascular tissues (xylem and phloem) in dicots are arranged in a specific manner, and these plants generally have broad leaves and a more complex structure.

Anatomical Features of Dicot Plants

Seed:

  • Two cotyledons (seed leaves).
  • Examples: Beans, Sunflower, Pea.

Root Anatomy:

  • Tap Root System: Dicot roots develop from the primary root, and lateral roots arise from the primary root, forming a tap root system.
  • Vascular Tissue: The xylem and phloem are arranged in a central column, forming a vascular cylinder. The xylem is typically arranged in a star-like pattern, with phloem in between the arms of the xylem.
  • Root Cap: Protects the growing tip of the root.

Stem Anatomy:

  • Vascular Bundles: Arranged in a ring in the stem, forming a distinct pattern. The vascular bundles consist of xylem (inside) and phloem (outside), and there is often a vascular cambium (lateral meristem) between them, which allows for secondary growth (growth in thickness).
  • Cortex: The area between the epidermis and vascular bundles, filled with parenchyma cells.
  • Pith: Central region of the stem, consisting of soft, spongy parenchyma cells, mainly involved in storage.

Leaf Anatomy:

  • Venation: Typically reticulate (net-like) venation, where veins are branched and form a network.
  • Stomata: Found in the epidermis of leaves; responsible for gas exchange.
  • Mesophyll: Divided into palisade parenchyma (closer to the upper epidermis, where photosynthesis occurs) and spongy parenchyma (below the palisade layer, involved in gas exchange).
  • Xylem and Phloem: The vascular bundles are arranged in a central vein and are usually surrounded by a bundle sheath.

Flower Anatomy:

  • Typically have floral parts in multiples of 4 or 5 (e.g., 4 or 5 petals, sepals, etc.).
  • Pentamerous structure (i.e., floral parts are in multiples of 4 or 5).

Monocot Plants (Monocotyledons)

Monocotyledons are plants that have only one cotyledon in their seeds. The vascular tissues in monocots are arranged differently, and they generally have long, narrow leaves and simple flowers.

Anatomical Features of Monocot Plants

Seed:

  • One cotyledon (seed leaf).
  • Examples: Wheat, Rice, Onion, Lily.

Root Anatomy:

  • Fibrous Root System: Monocot roots develop from the base of the stem (adventitious roots), forming a dense network of roots.
  • Vascular Tissue: The vascular bundles in the root are arranged in a circular pattern (in some monocots) or spread out in a cluster. The xylem and phloem are arranged in a more scattered manner within the vascular cylinder, and there is usually no vascular cambium.

Stem Anatomy:

  • Vascular Bundles: Vascular bundles are scattered throughout the stem, without any distinct pattern or arrangement in a ring. The xylem and phloem are scattered randomly throughout the stem. There is no vascular cambium, so monocots do not have secondary growth.
  • Cortex: Similar to dicots, but typically less developed.
  • Pith: The central area is often filled with parenchyma cells, similar to dicots but with a more uniform arrangement.

Leaf Anatomy:

  • Venation: Monocot leaves exhibit parallel venation, where veins run parallel to each other across the length of the leaf.
  • Stomata: Located on the leaf surface; usually present in equal numbers on both sides.
  • Mesophyll: Generally does not have a distinct differentiation between palisade and spongy parenchyma. It is often more homogeneous, with cells not as specialized.
  • Xylem and Phloem: Vascular bundles are scattered across the leaf with no clear organization into a central vein.

Flower Anatomy:

  • Typically have floral parts in multiples of 3 (e.g., 3 petals, 3 sepals).
  • Trimerous structure (i.e., floral parts are in multiples of 3).

Comparison of Dicot and Monocot Anatomy:

Feature Dicots Monocots
Cotyledons Two cotyledons One cotyledon
Leaf Venation Reticulate (net-like) Parallel
Root System Tap root system Fibrous root system
Vascular Bundles in Stem Arranged in a ring Scattered throughout the stem
Vascular Cambium Present, allowing secondary growth Absent
Flower Parts Floral parts in multiples of 4 or 5 Floral parts in multiples of 3
Xylem and Phloem Vascular tissue forms distinct patterns Vascular tissue scattered without distinct arrangement
Pith Present and differentiated Less defined or absent

3. Plant Tissues and Their Functions

Plant tissues are organized into different types based on their structure and function. These tissues can be categorized into meristematic tissues (responsible for growth) and permanent tissues (responsible for specific functions like transport, support, and protection).

i. Meristematic Tissues

Meristematic tissues are the tissues responsible for the growth of the plant. These tissues consist of actively dividing cells.

Types of Meristematic Tissues:

  • Apical Meristem: Located at the tips of roots and shoots, responsible for the primary growth (increase in length).
  • Lateral Meristem: Found in the sides of the plant (e.g., vascular cambium and cork cambium), responsible for secondary growth (increase in thickness).
  • Intercalary Meristem: Found at the nodes (junctions between the stem and leaves) and in some monocots, responsible for regenerative growth (lengthening of internodes).

Functions:

  • Responsible for the growth of the plant.
  • Apical meristem helps in elongation, while lateral meristem aids in thickening the plant's stem and roots.

ii. Permanent Tissues

Permanent tissues are made up of cells that have lost their ability to divide. These are classified into simple tissues and complex tissues based on their structure and function.

A. Simple Tissues

Simple tissues are composed of only one type of cell. These tissues perform basic functions like storage, support, and conduction.

Parenchyma:

  • Structure: Made up of thin-walled, living cells with large intercellular spaces.
  • Function:
    • Storage: Stores food (e.g., starch in roots).
    • Photosynthesis: Contains chloroplasts (in green parts like leaves).
    • Gas Exchange: Helps in the exchange of gases in leaves and stems.

Collenchyma:

  • Structure: Living cells with unevenly thickened walls.
  • Function:
    • Provides support and flexibility to growing parts of the plant.
    • Found in petioles, young stems, and leaf veins.

Sclerenchyma:

  • Structure: Cells with thick, lignified walls; often dead at maturity.
  • Function: Provides rigidity and mechanical support to the plant.
  • Types: Fibers (long, slender cells) and Sclereids (short, rounded cells).
  • Found in mature, non-growing parts like stems, seed coats, and nutshells.

B. Complex Tissues

Complex tissues are made up of more than one type of cell and perform specific functions, such as transport.

Xylem (Water-Conducting Tissue):

  • Structure: Composed of several cell types—tracheids, vessel elements, xylem fibers, and xylem parenchyma.
  • Function:
    • Transports water and minerals from roots to other parts of the plant.
    • Provides support due to the lignin in cell walls.
  • Types of Xylem:
    • Primary Xylem: Formed during primary growth.
    • Secondary Xylem: Formed during secondary growth, contributes to the thickening of the stem.

Phloem (Food-Conducting Tissue):

  • Structure: Composed of sieve tubes, companion cells, phloem fibers, and phloem parenchyma.
  • Function:
    • Transports food (sugars and amino acids) from the leaves to other parts of the plant.
    • Sieve Tubes: Specialized cells with pores that allow the flow of sap.
    • Companion Cells: Assist sieve tubes in transporting nutrients.

iii. Epidermal Tissue

Epidermal tissue forms the outer protective layer of the plant and prevents water loss and damage.

Structure:

  • Composed of a single layer of tightly packed cells, often covered with a waxy cuticle to prevent water loss.

Functions:

  • Protects the plant from physical damage and water loss.
  • Stomata: Small pores that allow gas exchange (CO₂ in and O₂ out) and transpiration (loss of water vapor).
  • Trichomes: Hair-like structures that help in reducing water loss and protecting the plant from herbivores.

iv. Protective Tissues

These tissues protect the plant from injury and environmental factors.

Cork (Phellem):

  • Formed by the cork cambium during secondary growth, provides a waterproof barrier to prevent water loss and infection.

Bark:

  • The outer layer of woody stems formed by cork and cork cambium.

v. Secretory Tissues

These tissues are specialized for secreting substances such as resins, oils, and nectar.

Nectar Glands:

  • Produce nectar to attract pollinators.

Resin Ducts:

  • Found in certain plants like pines, responsible for secreting resin for protection against herbivores and pathogens.

Laticifers:

  • Produce latex in plants like rubber trees.

Summary of Plant Tissues and Their Functions

Tissue Type Structure Function
Meristematic Tissue Actively dividing cells (apical, lateral, intercalary) Growth of the plant (primary and secondary growth)
Parenchyma Thin-walled, living cells Storage, photosynthesis, and gas exchange
Collenchyma Unevenly thickened cell walls Support and flexibility in growing parts
Sclerenchyma Thick, lignified walls, often dead at maturity Mechanical support and rigidity
Xylem Dead, thick-walled cells (tracheids, vessels) Transport of water and minerals, support
Phloem Living cells (sieve tubes, companion cells) Transport of food (sugars and nutrients)
Epidermal Tissue Single layer of cells Protection against water loss and physical damage
Cork Formed by cork cambium, dead cells Waterproof barrier, protection from pathogens
