Chapter 11 - 3D Geometry

1. Distance, Section Formula, and Direction Cosines

Definition

  • Distance refers to the length of the shortest path between two points in space.
  • Section Formula is used to find the coordinates of a point dividing a line segment in a given ratio.
  • Direction Cosines represent the cosines of the angles made by a vector with the coordinate axes.

Section Formula

  • The section formula is used to find the coordinates of a point dividing a line segment in a given ratio.
  • Internal Division (in 2D): If a point divides the line segment joining (x1, y1) and (x2, y2) in the ratio m:n, the coordinates of the point are: (x, y) = m x2 + n x1 m + n , m y2 + n y1 m + n
  • External Division: If a point divides the line segment joining (x1, y1) and (x2, y2) externally in the ratio m:n, the coordinates of the point are: (x, y) = m x2 - n x1 m - n , m y2 - n y1 m - n

Direction Cosines

  • Direction cosines of a vector represent the cosines of the angles the vector makes with the coordinate axes.
  • If the vector is r = (x, y, z), the direction cosines are given by: (cos α, cos β, cos γ) where α, β, γ are the angles made with the x, y, and z axes, respectively.
  • These direction cosines satisfy the relation: cos2α + cos2β + cos2γ = 1

2. Lines

i. Equation of a Line

  • General Form: The equation of a line is given by: A x + B y + C = 0
  • Slope-Intercept Form: The equation of a line in slope-intercept form is: y = m x + c where m is the slope and c is the y-intercept.
  • Point-Slope Form: The equation of a line using the point-slope form is: y - y 1 = m (x - x 1) where (x1, y1) is a point on the line and m is the slope.

ii. Slope of a Line

  • The slope of a line is the ratio of the change in y to the change in x: m = y 2 - y 1 x 2 - x 1
  • Parallel Lines: Lines with the same slope m1 = m2.
  • Perpendicular Lines: Lines where the product of their slopes is -1: m 1 × m 2 = -1

iii. Distance of a Point from a Line

  • The distance from a point (x1, y1) to a line Ax + By + C = 0 is given by: Distance = | A x 1 + B y 1 + C | A 2 + B 2

iv. Angle Between Two Lines

  • The angle θ between two lines with slopes m1 and m2 is given by: tan θ = | m1 - m2 | 1 + m1 m2

v. Intercept Form of a Line

  • The equation of a line in intercept form is: x 1 a + y 1 b = 1 where a is the x-intercept and b is the y-intercept.

vi. Collinearity of Points

  • Three points (x1, y1), (x2, y2), (x3, y3) are collinear if the area of the triangle formed by them is zero. The condition for collinearity is:
    Area = (1/2) * | x1(y2 - y3) + x2(y3 - y1) + x3(y1 - y2) | = 0

3. Plane

Definition

  • The equation of a plane represents a flat, two-dimensional surface in three-dimensional space.

Forms of Plane Equation

  • General Form:

    Equation: A x + B y + C z + D = 0

  • Point-Normal Form:

    Equation: r ⋅ n = d

  • Vector Form:

    Equation: r ⋅ n = a ⋅ n

Distance from a Point to a Plane

  • The perpendicular distance from a point to a plane can be found using the formula: Distance = | A x + B y + C z + D | A A 2 + B B 2 + C C 2

Angle Between Two Planes

  • The angle between two planes is determined by the angle between their normal vectors. The formula is: cos θ = | A 1 A 2 + B 1 B 2 + C 1 C 2 | A 2 + B 2 + C 2 ⁢ A 2 + B 2 + C 2

4. Intersection

Intersection of Two Planes

  • The intersection of two planes generally results in a line.
  • For two planes with equations: A 1 x + B 1 y + C 1 z + D 1 = 0 A 2 x + B 2 y + C 2 z + D 2 = 0
  • The direction ratios of the line of intersection can be found using the cross product of the normal vectors of the planes:
    • Normal vector of the first plane: n ₁ = < A1 , B1 , C1 >
    • Normal vector of the second plane: n ₂ = < A2 , B2 , C2 >
    • Direction vector of the line of intersection: L = n ₁ × n ₂
  • To find the equation of the line, a point on the line must be determined by solving the system of equations for x, y, and z.

Intersection of Three Planes

  • The intersection of three planes can result in:
    • A single point if the planes are not parallel and their normal vectors are not proportional.
    • A line if two planes are parallel and the third intersects them along a line.
    • No intersection if the planes are parallel or form a skew configuration.
  • To find the point of intersection of three planes, solve the system of three equations:
    • A 1 x + B 1 y + C 1 z + D 1 = 0
    • A 2 x + B 2 y + C 2 z + D 2 = 0
    • A 3 x + B 3 y + C 3 z + D 3 = 0
  • The solution to this system gives the coordinates (x, y, z) of the point of intersection.

Parallel Planes

  • If two planes are parallel, they will not intersect. The normal vectors of the planes are proportional:
    • n ₁ ∥ n ₂
  • The planes will either be distinct or coincident if the constant terms are the same. Example of two parallel planes:
    • A 1 x + B 1 y + C 1 z + D 1 = 0
    • A 2 x + B 2 y + C 2 z + D 2 = 0

Coincident Planes

  • If two planes are coincident, they are the same plane. The equations of the two planes are either identical or proportional:
    • A 1 x + B 1 y + C 1 z + D 1 = 0
    • k ( A 1 x + B 1 y + C 1 z + D 1 ) = 0

Parallel and Non-Intersecting Planes

  • If the planes are neither parallel nor coincident, they will intersect along a straight line. To find the line of intersection:
    1. Write the equations of the two planes:
      • A 1 x + B 1 y + C 1 z + D 1 = 0
      • A 2 x + B 2 y + C 2 z + D 2 = 0
    2. Find the direction ratios of the line by taking the cross product of the normal vectors of the planes.
    3. Find a point on the line by solving the system of two plane equations for two variables, then substitute to find the third variable.
    4. Write the parametric equation of the line:
      • x = x0 + t Lx
      • y = y0 + t Ly
      • z = z0 + t Lz
