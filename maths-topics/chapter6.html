Chapter 6 - Sequences & Series

1. Arithmetic Progression (AP)

What is an Arithmetic Progression (AP)?

An Arithmetic Progression (AP) is a sequence of numbers in which the difference between consecutive terms is constant. This constant difference is called the common difference.

General Form of an AP

The general form of an arithmetic progression is:

a, a + d, a + 2d, a + 3d, …

  • a is the first term,
  • d is the common difference,
  • n is the number of terms.

nth Term of an AP

The nth term of an arithmetic progression can be expressed by the formula:

Tn = a + (n - 1) ⋅ d

  • Tn is the nth term of the AP,
  • a is the first term,
  • d is the common difference,
  • n is the term number.

Sum of the First n Terms of an AP

The sum of the first n terms of an AP, denoted as Sn, is given by the formula:

Sn = (n / 2) ⋅ (2a + (n - 1) ⋅ d)

Alternatively, the sum can also be written as:

Sn = (n / 2) ⋅ (a + l)

  • n is the number of terms,
  • a is the first term,
  • d is the common difference,
  • l is the last term (the nth term).

Properties of an Arithmetic Progression

  • Constant difference: The difference between consecutive terms is constant, i.e., Tn+1 - Tn = d.
  • Midpoint of terms: The middle term (if the number of terms is odd) is the average of the first and last terms.
  • Sum of odd/even number of terms:
    • The sum of an odd number of terms is given by the product of the middle term and the number of terms.
    • For an even number of terms, the sum is the average of the first and last term multiplied by the number of terms.

Example of an Arithmetic Progression

Consider the AP: 3, 7, 11, 15, 19, …

The first term a = 3, and the common difference d = 4 (since 7 - 3 = 4).

Finding the nth Term:

To find the 6th term, use the formula:

Tn = a + (n - 1) ⋅ d

T6 = 3 + (6 - 1) ⋅ 4 = 3 + 5 ⋅ 4 = 3 + 20 = 23

So, the 6th term is 23.

Finding the Sum of the First 6 Terms:

We can use the formula:

Sn = (n / 2) ⋅ (2a + (n - 1) ⋅ d)

S6 = (6 / 2) ⋅ (2 ⋅ 3 + (6 - 1) ⋅ 4) = 3 ⋅ (6 + 20) = 3 ⋅ 26 = 78

So, the sum of the first 6 terms is 78.

2. Geometric Progression (GP)

What is a Geometric Progression (GP)?

A Geometric Progression (GP) is a sequence of numbers in which each term after the first is found by multiplying the previous term by a constant called the common ratio.

General Form of a GP

The general form of a geometric progression is:

a, ar, ar², ar³, ar⁴, …

  • a is the first term,
  • r is the common ratio (i.e., the factor by which each term is multiplied to get the next term),
  • n is the number of terms.

nth Term of a GP

The nth term of a geometric progression is given by the formula:

Tn = a ⋅ rn - 1

  • Tn is the nth term of the progression,
  • a is the first term,
  • r is the common ratio,
  • n is the term number.

Sum of the First n Terms of a GP

The sum of the first n terms of a geometric progression, denoted as Sn, is given by the formula:

Sn = a ⋅ (1 - rn) / (1 - r) (for r ≠ 1)

If the common ratio r = 1, the sum is simply:

Sn = n ⋅ a

Infinite Geometric Series

If the common ratio |r| < 1, the sum of an infinite geometric series (i.e., as n → ∞) is given by:

S∞ = a / (1 - r)

  • a is the first term,
  • r is the common ratio.

Properties of a Geometric Progression

  • Constant ratio: The ratio between consecutive terms is constant, i.e., Tn+1 / Tn = r.
  • Exponential growth/decay: If r > 1, the terms grow exponentially. If 0 < r < 1, the terms decrease exponentially.
  • Multiplicative nature: Each term is a product of the previous term and the common ratio.

Example of a Geometric Progression

Consider the GP: 2, 6, 18, 54, …

The first term a = 2, and the common ratio r = 3 (since 6 / 2 = 3).

Finding the nth Term:

To find the 5th term, use the formula:

Tn = a ⋅ rn - 1

T5 = 2 ⋅ 35 - 1 = 2 ⋅ 34 = 2 ⋅ 81 = 162

So, the 5th term is 162.

Finding the Sum of the First 4 Terms:

We can use the sum formula:

Sn = a ⋅ (1 - rn) / (1 - r)

S4 = 2 ⋅ (1 - 34) / (1 - 3)

S4 = 2 ⋅ (1 - 81) / -2 = 2 ⋅ (-80) / -2 = 80

So, the sum of the first 4 terms is 80.

3. AM-GM Inequality (Arithmetic Mean - Geometric Mean Inequality)

What is the AM-GM Inequality?

The AM-GM inequality is a fundamental inequality in mathematics that relates the Arithmetic Mean (AM) and Geometric Mean (GM) of a set of non-negative numbers. The inequality states that the Arithmetic Mean of a set of non-negative numbers is always greater than or equal to their Geometric Mean.

Statement of the AM-GM Inequality

For any set of n non-negative real numbers x₁, x₂, …, xₙ, the AM-GM inequality states:

x₁ + x₂ + ⋯ + xₙ / n ≥ x₁ ⋅ x₂ ⋅ ⋯ ⋅ xₙ1/n

  • x₁, x₂, …, xₙ are non-negative real numbers.
  • Arithmetic Mean (AM) is (x₁ + x₂ + ⋯ + xₙ) / n.
  • Geometric Mean (GM) is (x₁ ⋅ x₂ ⋅ ⋯ ⋅ xₙ)1/n.

Equality Condition

Equality holds if and only if x₁ = x₂ = ⋯ = xₙ. That is, the arithmetic mean equals the geometric mean only when all the numbers are equal.

Example of AM-GM Inequality

Let's consider the numbers 4 and 16.

Arithmetic Mean (AM):

AM = (4 + 16) / 2 = 10

Geometric Mean (GM):

GM = √(4 × 16) = √64 = 8

According to the AM-GM inequality:

AM = 10 ≥ GM = 8

So, the AM is greater than the GM, which follows the AM-GM inequality.

Equality Condition in Action

For equality to hold, all the numbers must be the same. For example, if we take the numbers 5 and 5, then both the arithmetic and geometric means will be equal:

Arithmetic Mean (AM):

AM = (5 + 5) / 2 = 5

Geometric Mean (GM):

GM = √(5 × 5) = √25 = 5

In this case, AM = GM = 5.

Applications of the AM-GM Inequality

  • Optimization Problems: The AM-GM inequality is widely used to solve optimization problems, especially those involving product and sum constraints.
  • Inequalities: It is used to prove many other inequalities in mathematics, such as the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality and Holder's inequality.
  • Economics: In economics, the AM-GM inequality is applied in the analysis of production and consumption.

Generalized AM-GM Inequality

For n non-negative numbers x₁, x₂, …, xₙ, the AM-GM inequality can be generalized as follows:

p₁x₁ + p₂x₂ + ⋯ + pₙxₙ / (p₁ + p₂ + ⋯ + pₙ) ≥ (x₁p₁ ⋅ x₂p₂ ⋅ ⋯ ⋅ xₙpₙ)1/n

Where p₁, p₂, …, pₙ are positive real numbers.

4. Special Series

Special series are certain types of series that have well-known formulas and patterns. These series frequently appear in various fields of mathematics, such as calculus, number theory, and algebra. Understanding these series and their properties is essential for solving complex problems.

i. Arithmetic Series (AP)

An Arithmetic Progression (AP) is a sequence of numbers where the difference between any two consecutive terms is constant. The sum of the first n terms of an arithmetic series is called an Arithmetic Series.

Formula for Sum of Arithmetic Series:

Sₙ = n / 2 (2a + (n - 1)d)

Where:

  • n is the number of terms,
  • a is the first term,
  • d is the common difference between terms.

Alternatively, if the last term l is known:

Sₙ = n / 2 (a + l)

ii. Geometric Series (GP)

A Geometric Progression (GP) is a sequence where each term after the first is found by multiplying the previous term by a constant called the common ratio.

Formula for Sum of Geometric Series:

For the first n terms:

Sₙ = a (1 - rⁿ) / (1 - r), for r ≠ 1

Where:

  • a is the first term,
  • r is the common ratio,
  • n is the number of terms.

For an infinite geometric series (if |r| < 1):

Sₙ = a / (1 - r)

iii. Sum of Natural Numbers

The sum of the first n natural numbers is:

Sₙ = n(n + 1) / 2

This is an example of an Arithmetic Series where the common difference is 1.

iv. Sum of Squares of Natural Numbers

The sum of the squares of the first n natural numbers is:

Sₙ = n(n + 1)(2n + 1) / 6

This formula helps in evaluating the sum of squared terms in a sequence.

v. Sum of Cubes of Natural Numbers

The sum of the cubes of the first n natural numbers is:

Sₙ = (n(n + 1) / 2)²

This is a special series where the sum of cubes follows a perfect square pattern.

vi. The Binomial Expansion (Binomial Theorem)

The Binomial Theorem provides the expansion of powers of a binomial expression (a + b)ⁿ. The expansion is:

(a + b)ⁿ = ∑(k = 0 to n) (n C k) a^(n-k) bᵏ

Where:

  • (n C k) are the binomial coefficients.

The series is used to expand expressions like (a + b)ⁿ.

vii. Harmonic Series

The Harmonic Series is the sum of the reciprocals of the natural numbers:

Hₙ = 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + ⋯ + 1/n

This series is important in number theory and calculus. Note that the harmonic series diverges, meaning it grows without bound as n increases, but it does so very slowly.

viii. Exponential Series

The Exponential Series represents the exponential function eˣ as an infinite series:

eˣ = 1 + x + x² / 2! + x³ / 3! + x⁴ / 4! + …

This series is crucial in calculus, especially in solving differential equations and modeling growth.

ix. Sine and Cosine Series (Maclaurin Series)

Sine Function:

sin(x) = x - x³ / 3! + x⁵ / 5! - x⁷ / 7! + …

Cosine Function:

cos(x) = 1 - x² / 2! + x⁴ / 4! - x⁶ / 6! + …

These series are used for approximating the sine and cosine functions, particularly in numerical methods and physics.

x. Infinite Series of Powers of 2

The sum of the first n terms of powers of 2 (i.e., 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, …) is:

Sₙ = 1 + 2 + 4 + 8 + ⋯ + 2ⁿ⁻¹ = 2ⁿ - 1

This is a Geometric Series with the common ratio r = 2.

xi. Sum of Terms Involving Factorials

Some special series involve factorials. For instance, the exponential series is an example where factorials appear in the denominator:

eˣ = ∑(n = 0 to ∞) xⁿ / n!

xii. Fibonacci Series

The Fibonacci Series is a sequence where each term is the sum of the two preceding terms, starting with 0 and 1. The sequence looks like:

0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, …

This sequence is important in various areas, including combinatorics, number theory, and even in nature.

5. Arithmetico-Geometric Series (AG Series)

Definition

  • An Arithmetico-Geometric Series is a series that combines elements of both an Arithmetic Progression (AP) and a Geometric Progression (GP).
  • In this series, each term is the product of a term from an arithmetic progression and a term from a geometric progression.

General Form

  • The general form of an Arithmetico-Geometric series is:

    • S = a + a + d r + a + 2d r 2 + a + 3d r 3 +...

  • Where:
    • a = first term of the arithmetic progression.
    • d = common difference of the arithmetic progression.
    • r = common ratio of the geometric progression.

Summation of Arithmetico-Geometric Series

  • The sum of an Arithmetico-Geometric series can be calculated by multiplying the series by powers of r and subtracting to simplify.
  • Example:

    • S - rS = a + a + d r + a + 2d r 2 + ...

Sum Formula

  • The sum can be expressed as:

    • S = a (1 - r) 2 + d (1 - r) 2

Applications

  • Physics: Describing systems that have both constant and exponentially changing components (e.g., oscillations with a linear term).
  • Economics: Modeling financial situations where a constant term is added to a growing sum (e.g., growing investments with fixed contributions).
  • Engineering: In systems where signals grow or decay while an additional constant is added over time (e.g., in control systems).

Example Calculation

  • Consider the following series:

    • S = 2 + 5x + 8x² + 11x³ + ...

    • a = 2, d = 3, r = x
    • Apply the summation formula to find the sum for specific values of \(r\).
